# Geometry: The Shapes All Around Us

**Grade 3 · Mathematics**

---

## Hello, Young Mathematician!

Look up from this book for just a moment and take a slow look around the room you are in. What do you see? Perhaps there is a window with straight sides and square corners. Perhaps there is a clock on the wall, round and smooth all the way around. Maybe there is a box of crayons, a tin of milk, a ball, or a slice of bread sitting nearby. Every single one of these things has a **shape**, and the study of shapes is called **geometry**.

Geometry is one of the oldest and most useful parts of mathematics, and the wonderful thing about it is that you do not need to go anywhere special to find it. Shapes are everywhere. They are in the windows of your house and the doors you walk through. They are in the road signs you pass on the way to school. They are in the football you kick, the box your shoes came in, the ice-cream cone you enjoy on a hot day, and the orange you peel for lunch.

In this chapter, you are going to become a shape detective. You will learn the names of the flat shapes and the solid shapes, and you will learn to describe them carefully using their **sides** and their **corners**. You will make patterns with shapes, you will fold paper to discover something special called **symmetry**, and you will even learn how to measure the **area** of a flat surface by covering it. By the time you reach the last page, you will never look at the world in quite the same way again, because you will see the geometry hiding in everything. Let us begin.

---

## Learning Goals

In this chapter you will learn to:

- Name the flat (2D) shapes — the square, the rectangle, the triangle, and the circle — and describe each one by its sides and corners.
- Name the solid (3D) shapes — the cube, the cuboid or box, the cylinder, the cone, and the sphere — and find them in everyday objects.
- Describe in words how shapes are the same and how they are different.
- Make and continue patterns using shapes.
- Understand points, lines, and simple paths.
- Find a **line of symmetry** by folding a shape so that the two halves match.
- Understand **area** as the amount of flat surface a shape covers, and measure it by counting squares.

## Our Big Question

> **How can I use shapes to describe and understand the world around me?**

This is the big question that holds our whole chapter together. We will answer it one step at a time. As you meet each new shape, you will learn its name and the words that describe it. As you look around your home, your school, and your community, you will start to spot those shapes in real things. By the end, you will be able to look at almost any object and describe it like a true young mathematician — naming its shape, its sides, its corners, and even its lines of symmetry.

## Word Power

Here are the important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about learning them all at once. Each one is explained carefully in the lessons, and these short notes are here to help you whenever you need them.

- **Shape** — the form of an object: how its outline looks. *Example:* the front of a clock has a round shape.
- **Side** — a straight edge of a flat shape. *Example:* a square has four sides.
- **Corner** — the point where two sides meet. *Example:* a rectangle has four corners.
- **Flat shape (2D shape)** — a shape that is flat, like a drawing on paper, with only length and width. *Example:* a triangle drawn on your page is a flat shape.
- **Solid shape (3D shape)** — a shape you can hold, that takes up space and has thickness. *Example:* a ball is a solid shape.
- **Square** — a flat shape with four equal straight sides and four square corners. *Example:* the face of a number cube.
- **Rectangle** — a flat shape with four straight sides and four square corners, where the opposite sides are equal. *Example:* a door.
- **Triangle** — a flat shape with three straight sides and three corners. *Example:* a slice of pizza or a yield road sign.
- **Circle** — a flat shape that is perfectly round, with no sides and no corners. *Example:* the top of a tin.
- **Cube** — a solid shape with six square faces, all the same size. *Example:* a dice or a number block.
- **Cylinder** — a solid shape with two flat circle ends and a curved middle. *Example:* a tin of milk.
- **Cone** — a solid shape with one flat circle end and a point at the top. *Example:* an ice-cream cone.
- **Sphere** — a solid shape that is perfectly round all over, like a ball. *Example:* an orange.
- **Pattern** — things arranged in an order that repeats. *Example:* circle, square, circle, square, circle, square.
- **Symmetry** — when two halves of a shape match each other exactly. *Example:* a butterfly's two wings.
- **Line of symmetry** — the line you can fold a shape along so that both halves fit exactly on top of each other. *Example:* a straight fold down the middle of a heart shape.
- **Area** — the amount of flat surface that a shape covers. *Example:* the number of square tiles it takes to cover the floor.

# Flat Shapes: The Shapes on Paper

We are going to begin with **flat shapes**, because they are the easiest shapes to draw and to see. A flat shape is exactly what its name says: it is flat. You can draw it on a page, you can trace it with your finger, and you can cut it out of paper. A flat shape has length and width, but it has no thickness — you cannot pick it up off the page and hold it in your hand.

Grown-up mathematicians have a special name for flat shapes. They call them **2D shapes**. The little "2D" is short for "two dimensions," which simply means the shape goes two ways: across and up-and-down. Do not let that long word worry you. For now, just remember that a flat shape and a 2D shape are the very same thing.

When we describe a flat shape, we look at two important things: its **sides** and its **corners**. A **side** is a straight edge of the shape. A **corner** is the point where two sides meet. If you count the sides and the corners of a shape, you can almost always tell exactly which shape it is. Let us meet the four most important flat shapes now.

## The Square

The first flat shape we will meet is the **square**, and it is a very tidy and even shape indeed. A square has **four straight sides**, and here is the special thing about it: all four sides are exactly the **same length**. Not one of them is longer or shorter than the others. A square also has **four corners**, and every corner is a **square corner** — a neat, perfect corner, just like the corner of this page.

Because a square is so even, it looks the same whichever way you turn it. Stand it on one side, turn it round, stand it on another side, and it still looks like a square. That is the gift of having four equal sides.

> **Look Around You:** The face of a dice is a square. So is a slice of cheese cut into a neat block, the front of many floor tiles, and the squares on a draughts board or a ludo board. How many squares can you find in your classroom right now?

## The Rectangle

Next comes the **rectangle**, which is a close cousin of the square. Like the square, a rectangle has **four straight sides** and **four square corners**. But there is one important difference. In a rectangle, the sides are **not** all the same length. Instead, the two long sides are equal to each other, and the two short sides are equal to each other. So a rectangle is usually longer than it is wide, like a stretched-out square.

This makes the rectangle one of the most useful shapes of all. Look at a door: it is tall and not very wide, so it is a rectangle. Look at the cover of this book, the top of a desk, a Jamaican flag, or a sheet of writing paper. Every one of them is a rectangle.

> **Think About It:** A square and a rectangle both have four sides and four square corners. So how can you tell them apart just by looking? (Hint: look carefully at the lengths of the sides.)

The curriculum asks you to think hard about the square and the rectangle together, so let us do that now. Here are four ways the square and the rectangle are **the same**: both have four straight sides; both have four corners; every corner in both shapes is a square corner; and both are flat shapes. And here are two ways they are **different**: in a square all four sides are equal, but in a rectangle only the opposite sides are equal; and a square looks the same whichever way you turn it, while a rectangle usually looks longer one way than the other.

> **Quick Check:** Without looking back, can you say one way a square and a rectangle are the same, and one way they are different?

## The Triangle

The **triangle** is an easy shape to remember, because its very name gives you a clue. "Tri" means three, and a triangle has **three straight sides** and **three corners**. That is all it takes to make a triangle: three straight sides joined together to make a closed shape.

Not all triangles look exactly alike. Some triangles are tall and thin, while others are short and wide. Some sit flat on one side, and others balance on a point. But no matter how they look, every single triangle has exactly three straight sides and three corners. If you count three sides, you know at once that you are looking at a triangle.

> **Did You Know?** Triangles are some of the strongest shapes in the whole world. That is why builders use them in roofs, in bridges, and in the frames of gates. The three sides hold each other firmly and stop the shape from bending out of place.

> **Look Around You:** Where do you see triangles? Look for a slice of pizza, a slice of bread cut from corner to corner, the roof of a small house, the sail of a boat, a piece of bunting, and the warning road signs that point a driver to slow down.

## The Circle

The last of our four flat shapes is the **circle**, and it is quite different from the others. A circle is **perfectly round** all the way around its edge. It has **no straight sides** at all, and it has **no corners** at all. Its edge is one smooth, curving line that never stops and never bends sharply.

Because it is so smooth and round, a circle can roll. Try to roll a square or a triangle and it will tumble and stop, because of its corners. But a circle rolls along easily, which is exactly why wheels are made round.

> **Look Around You:** Circles are everywhere once you start looking. The face of a clock is a circle. So is the top of a tin of milk, a coin, a button, a steering wheel, the bottom of a cup, and the full moon in the night sky.

> **Remember:** A circle is the only one of our four flat shapes with no sides and no corners. If a shape is round all the way around, it is a circle.

## Points, Lines, and Paths

Before we leave flat shapes, let us learn three small but important ideas: the point, the line, and the path. Every shape is really made out of these.

A **point** is just a single tiny spot, like the dot you make when you touch your pencil to the paper. A point does not have any size — it simply marks a place.

A **line** is straight and goes on without bending. When we draw just a small piece of a line, with a starting point and a stopping point, we call it a **line segment**. The sides of a square, a rectangle, and a triangle are all line segments, because each side is a straight piece with two ends.

A **path** is the way you travel from one place to another. A path can be straight, or it can turn and bend. When a path starts at a point, travels all the way around, and comes back to where it began without crossing itself, we call it a **simple closed path**. Every flat shape we have learned is made by a simple closed path. The three line segments of a triangle, joined end to end, make a simple closed path — and that path traces out the triangle.

> **Let's Explore:** Use your finger to trace the path around the edge of this book. You started at one corner, travelled along each side, and came back to where you began. That is a simple closed path, and it traces out a rectangle.

# Solid Shapes: The Shapes You Can Hold

Flat shapes live on paper, but the real world is full of shapes you can pick up and hold in your two hands. A ball, a box, a tin, an ice-cream cone — these are not flat. They take up space, they have thickness, and you can turn them all the way around. These are called **solid shapes**.

Just as flat shapes have the special name "2D shapes," solid shapes have the special name **3D shapes**. The "3D" is short for "three dimensions," which means the shape goes three ways: across, up-and-down, and front-to-back. A solid shape is one you could wrap up in paper or hold behind your back. Let us meet five important solid shapes and find them in everyday Jamaican objects.

## The Cube

A **cube** is a solid shape that is built entirely out of squares. It has **six flat faces**, and every single face is a square of exactly the same size. A "face" is just one of the flat sides of a solid shape, like one wall of a tiny room. Because all six faces are equal squares, a cube is the same length, the same width, and the same height — it is perfectly even, like a square that has grown thick.

> **Look Around You:** A dice is a perfect cube. So is a child's wooden building block, a square box of cheese, and many of the boxes you stack in a cupboard. Pick one up and count the faces — you will find six, and each one is a square.

## The Cuboid, or Box

A **cuboid** is shaped like a box, and in fact most people simply call it a box. Like the cube, a cuboid has **six flat faces** and they are all flat rectangles. But unlike the cube, the faces of a cuboid are **not** all the same size. Some are longer and some are shorter, in the very same way that a rectangle is longer than a square.

If a cube is a thick square, then a cuboid is a thick rectangle. This is one of the most common solid shapes of all, because so many things we use are packed into box shapes.

> **Look Around You:** A cereal box, a box of matches, a brick, a book lying closed, a tissue box, and a shoebox are all cuboids. Look how each one is shaped like a box, with flat rectangle faces.

> **Quick Check:** A cube and a cuboid both have six flat faces. What is the one big difference between them? (Think about the faces: are they all the same size, or not?)

## The Cylinder

A **cylinder** is a tall, round solid shape. It has **two flat ends**, and each of those ends is a **circle**. Joining the two circle ends together is a smooth, curved surface that wraps all the way around the middle. A cylinder has the round smoothness of a circle but the thickness of a solid.

Because its two ends are flat circles, a cylinder can stand up straight on one end. And because its middle is round and curved, a cylinder can also be laid on its side and rolled along, just like a wheel.

> **Look Around You:** A tin of milk, a tin of corned beef, a roll of tissue, a drinking glass, and a candle are all cylinders. Notice the flat round circle at the top and the bottom, with the smooth curved side in between.

## The Cone

A **cone** is the shape of an ice-cream cone, and that is the easiest way to remember it. A cone has **one flat end**, which is a **circle**, and it rises up from that circle to a single **point** at the top. So a cone is wide and round at the bottom and narrows to a sharp tip.

You will see cones used whenever something needs to come to a point. They make a shape that is steady at the bottom and pointed at the top.

> **Look Around You:** An ice-cream cone is the most famous cone of all. You will also see cones in a party hat, in the orange road cones that workers place on the street, and in a funnel used to pour liquid into a bottle.

## The Sphere

A **sphere** is a solid shape that is perfectly round all over, like a ball. It has no flat faces at all, no corners, and no straight edges — just one smooth, curving surface in every direction. If a circle were to puff up and become solid and round on every side, it would become a sphere.

Because it is round all over, a sphere will roll whichever way you push it. It cannot stand still on a flat table the way a cube or a cylinder can, unless you hold it or rest it in a dip.

> **Look Around You:** A football, a cricket ball, a marble, an orange, and a round fruit like a naseberry are all spheres. The Earth we live on is very nearly a giant sphere too.

> **Remember:** Flat shapes lie on paper, but solid shapes take up space and can be held. A circle is flat, but a ball is a sphere — and a sphere is a solid you can hold in your hand.

## Telling Solid Shapes Apart

When you want to name a solid shape, it helps to ask yourself a few simple questions. Is it round all over? Then it is a **sphere**. Does it have flat square faces? Then it is a **cube**. Does it have flat rectangle faces like a box? Then it is a **cuboid**. Does it have two flat circle ends and a curved middle? Then it is a **cylinder**. Does it have one circle end and rise to a point? Then it is a **cone**. With those few questions, you can name almost any solid shape you meet.

> **Think About It:** A tin of milk and a ball are both round in some way. But one of them can stand up flat on a table and one of them cannot. Which is which, and why?

# Shape Patterns

One of the most enjoyable things you can do with shapes is to make a **pattern**. A pattern is a group of things arranged in an order that **repeats** again and again. Once you know the order, you can always tell what comes next, because the same thing happens over and over.

Patterns are all around you. The tiles on a floor often make a pattern. The stripes on a flag make a pattern. The beads on a necklace can make a pattern. And you can make beautiful patterns of your very own using the shapes you have learned in this chapter.

## Making a Pattern

To make a shape pattern, you simply choose some shapes and place them in an order, then repeat that order. Here is a simple one made with a circle and a square:

**circle, square, circle, square, circle, square, …**

Can you see the order? It goes circle, then square, then circle, then square. That little group — circle, square — repeats again and again. Because the order keeps repeating, you can tell exactly what comes next: after circle, square, circle, square, circle, square, the next shape must be a circle, then a square once more.

Here is a slightly longer pattern, made with three shapes:

**triangle, triangle, circle, triangle, triangle, circle, …**

This time the repeating group is triangle, triangle, circle. Once you spot the group that repeats, you can always continue the pattern as far as you like.

## Continuing a Pattern

Continuing a pattern means looking carefully to find the part that repeats, and then carrying it on. The trick is always the same: find the repeating group, and then keep adding it on the end.

> **Let's Explore:** Look at this pattern: square, circle, circle, square, circle, circle, square, circle, circle. What is the repeating group? What three shapes should come next? (The repeating group is square, circle, circle — so next come square, circle, circle.)

> **Quick Check:** Continue this pattern by saying the next two shapes: circle, triangle, square, circle, triangle, square, circle, triangle, square, …

> **Did You Know?** People have used shape patterns for thousands of years to decorate cloth, baskets, pottery, and walls. The next time you see a printed cloth or a tiled floor, look closely — you will very likely find a repeating pattern of shapes hiding in it.

# Symmetry and Lines

Now we come to one of the most magical ideas in all of geometry: **symmetry**. A shape has symmetry when its two halves match each other exactly. If you could fold the shape right down the middle, one half would lie perfectly on top of the other half, with no part sticking out and no part missing.

Think of a butterfly. Its left wing and its right wing match each other exactly, like a mirror picture. If you folded the butterfly down the middle of its body, the two wings would fit perfectly on top of each other. We say the butterfly is **symmetrical**, because it has matching halves.

## The Line of Symmetry

The special fold line that splits a shape into two matching halves has a name. It is called the **line of symmetry**. The line of symmetry is the line you could fold along so that both halves fit exactly on top of each other.

The very best way to test for a line of symmetry is to **fold**. This is exactly what the curriculum asks you to do. If you cut a shape out of paper and fold it along a line, then check whether the two halves match perfectly, you will know at once whether that fold is a line of symmetry. If the halves match exactly, the fold **is** a line of symmetry. If one half pokes out past the other, then it is **not** a line of symmetry.

> **Let's Explore:** Cut a square out of paper. Fold it exactly down the middle, from top to bottom. Do the two halves match? Yes! That fold is a line of symmetry. Now open it out and fold it from side to side. Do the halves match again? Yes! A square has more than one line of symmetry.

Some shapes have one line of symmetry, some have several, and some have none at all. A circle is the most symmetrical shape of all, because you can fold it through its centre in any direction and the two halves will always match. A square has lines of symmetry both up-and-down and side-to-side. A rectangle has a line of symmetry down its middle each way. Many letters of the alphabet are symmetrical too — fold the letter A down the middle, and the two halves match.

> **Think About It:** Write the capital letter "A" on a piece of paper and fold it down the middle from top to bottom. Do the two halves match? Now try the letter "B" folded the same way — what about across the middle? Which fold gives a line of symmetry?

> **Look Around You:** Many things in nature are symmetrical. A leaf often has matching halves on each side of its middle vein. Your own face is nearly symmetrical, with one eye and one ear to match on each side. A flower, a butterfly, and a starfish all show symmetry.

> **Remember:** To check for a line of symmetry, fold the shape along the line. If the two halves match exactly, you have found a line of symmetry.

# Area: Covering a Flat Surface

Our last big idea in this chapter is **area**. Area is the amount of flat surface that a shape covers. When you want to know how much surface something takes up — the top of a desk, the floor of a room, the cover of a book — you are asking about its area.

Here is an easy way to think about it. Imagine you want to cover the whole top of your desk with small square tiles, with no gaps and no overlaps. The number of tiles it takes to cover the desk tells you the **area** of the desk top. Area is all about **covering** a flat surface completely.

## Measuring Area by Counting Squares

To measure area in a simple way, we use squares. We cover the flat shape with equal squares, and then we count how many squares it takes. The number of squares is the area.

Picture a rectangle drawn on grid paper, sitting neatly on top of the little squares. To find its area, you simply count the squares inside it. If the rectangle covers 6 little squares, then its area is 6 squares. If a different shape covers 10 little squares, its area is 10 squares. The bigger the surface, the more squares it takes to cover it, and so the larger its area.

> **Let's Explore:** Look at a tiled floor or a checked tablecloth. Choose a small area, perhaps the space covered by a book lying on it, and count how many tiles or checks the book covers. The number you count is the area of the book's cover, measured in tiles.

> **Quick Check:** One shape is covered by 8 squares, and another shape is covered by 12 squares. Which shape has the larger area, and how do you know?

> **Remember:** Area is the amount of flat surface a shape covers. We can measure area by counting how many equal squares it takes to cover the shape with no gaps and no overlaps.

## Chapter Summary

In this chapter, you became a shape detective and discovered the geometry hiding all around you.

You met the four flat (2D) shapes and learned to describe them by their sides and corners: the **square** with four equal sides and four square corners, the **rectangle** with four sides where the opposite sides are equal, the **triangle** with three sides and three corners, and the **circle** that is round with no sides and no corners. You learned about **points**, **lines**, **line segments**, and **simple closed paths**, and you saw how every flat shape is traced out by a path.

You then met the solid (3D) shapes that you can hold in your hand: the **cube**, the box-shaped **cuboid**, the **cylinder** with its two circle ends, the pointed **cone**, and the perfectly round **sphere**. You learned to find each one in everyday objects like dice, boxes, tins, ice-cream cones, and balls.

You learned how to make and continue **patterns** by finding the group of shapes that repeats. You discovered **symmetry**, and you learned to find a **line of symmetry** by folding a shape so its two halves match. Finally, you learned that **area** is the amount of flat surface a shape covers, and that you can measure it by counting squares.

## Remember

- A **flat (2D) shape** lies on paper and has length and width but no thickness; a **solid (3D) shape** takes up space and can be held.
- A **square** has four equal sides and four square corners; a **rectangle** has four sides with the opposite sides equal.
- A **triangle** has three straight sides and three corners; a **circle** is round, with no sides and no corners.
- The five solid shapes to know are the **cube**, the **cuboid (box)**, the **cylinder**, the **cone**, and the **sphere**.
- A **pattern** repeats a group of shapes again and again; find the repeating group to continue it.
- A shape has **symmetry** when its two halves match; the fold that makes them match is the **line of symmetry**.
- **Area** is the amount of flat surface a shape covers, and we can measure it by counting equal squares.
