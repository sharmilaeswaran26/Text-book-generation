Flat Shapes: The Shapes on Paper

Look around the room you are in and you will see shapes everywhere — in windows, doors, road signs, clocks, and books. The study of shapes is called geometry, and it is one of the oldest and most useful parts of mathematics. We will begin with flat shapes, because they are the easiest shapes to draw and to see.

A flat shape is exactly what its name says: it is flat. You can draw it on a page, trace it with your finger, and cut it out of paper. It has length and width, but no thickness — you cannot lift it off the page and hold it. Mathematicians call flat shapes 2D shapes, which simply means the shape goes two ways: across and up-and-down. A flat shape and a 2D shape are the very same thing.

When we describe a flat shape, we look at two things: its sides and its corners. A side is a straight edge of the shape. A corner is the point where two sides meet. If you count the sides and corners of a shape, you can almost always tell exactly which shape it is.

Look Around You

  • How many square windows or tiles can you see in your classroom right now?
  • Find one door — notice it is a rectangle, taller than it is wide.
  • Look for a clock or a coin — its round face is a circle.
  • Spot a slice of bread cut corner to corner — it makes two triangles.

Lesson 1: The Square and the Rectangle

The square is a very tidy shape. It has four straight sides, and all four sides are exactly the same length. It also has four corners, and every one is a square corner, just like the corner of this page. Because a square is so even, it looks the same whichever way you turn it. The face of a dice and a floor tile are squares.

The rectangle is a close cousin of the square. Like the square, it has four straight sides and four square corners. But its sides are not all the same length: the two long sides are equal, and the two short sides are equal. So a rectangle is usually longer than it is wide, like a stretched-out square. A door, a desktop, and a sheet of paper are rectangles.

The curriculum asks us to compare them carefully. Four ways they are the same: both have four straight sides; both have four corners; every corner in both is a square corner; and both are flat shapes. Two ways they are different: in a square all four sides are equal, but in a rectangle only the opposite sides are equal; and a square looks the same whichever way you turn it, while a rectangle usually looks longer one way than the other.

Quick Check

  • Without looking back, say one way a square and a rectangle are the same.
  • Now say one way they are different. (Hint: look at the lengths of the sides.)

Lesson 2: The Triangle and the Circle

The triangle gives you a clue in its very name. "Tri" means three, and a triangle has three straight sides and three corners. Some triangles are tall and thin, others short and wide, but every triangle has exactly three sides and three corners. A slice of pizza, the roof of a small house, and the sail of a boat are triangles.

The circle is quite different. It is perfectly round all the way around. It has no straight sides and no corners — just one smooth, curving edge. Because it is so round, a circle can roll, which is exactly why wheels are made round. A clock face, a coin, a button, and the top of a tin are circles.

Did You Know?

Triangles are strong: builders use triangles in roofs, bridges, and gate frames because the three sides hold each other firmly and stop the shape from bending.
The circle is special: it is the only one of our four flat shapes with no sides and no corners at all.

Lesson 3: Points, Lines, and Paths

Every shape is really made out of points, lines, and paths. A point is a single tiny spot, like the dot you make when you touch your pencil to the paper. A line is straight and goes on without bending; when we draw just a small piece of a line, with a start and a stop, we call it a line segment. The sides of a square, rectangle, and triangle are all line segments.

A path is the way you travel from one place to another, and it can be straight or it can bend. When a path starts at a point, travels all the way around, and comes back to where it began without crossing itself, we call it a simple closed path. Every flat shape is made by a simple closed path.

Let's Explore

Use your finger to trace the path around the edge of this book. You started at one corner, travelled along each side, and came back to where you began. That is a simple closed path, and it traces out a rectangle.

Let's Practice

Shape Hunt: Look around your classroom or home and find a real object for each flat shape.

  1. Find one square and write down the object.
  2. Find one rectangle and write down the object.
  3. Find one triangle and write down the object.
  4. Find one circle and write down the object.
  5. For each one, say how many sides and how many corners it has.

Solid Shapes: The Shapes You Can Hold

Flat shapes live on paper, but the real world is full of shapes you can pick up and hold in your two hands. A ball, a box, a tin, an ice-cream cone — these are not flat. They take up space, they have thickness, and you can turn them all the way around. These are called solid shapes.

Just as flat shapes are called "2D shapes," solid shapes are called 3D shapes, which means the shape goes three ways: across, up-and-down, and front-to-back. A solid shape is one you could wrap up in paper or hold behind your back. The flat sides of a solid shape are called its faces.

Look Around You

  • Find a dice or a wooden block — it is a cube with six square faces.
  • Find a cereal box or a brick — it is a cuboid, shaped like a box.
  • Find a tin of milk — it is a cylinder, round with flat circle ends.
  • Find an ice-cream cone or a party hat — it is a cone, with a point on top.
  • Find a ball or an orange — it is a sphere, round all over.

Lesson 1: Cubes and Cuboids (Boxes)

A cube is built entirely out of squares. It has six flat faces, and every face is a square of exactly the same size. Because all six faces are equal squares, a cube is the same length, width, and height — like a square that has grown thick. A dice and a wooden building block are perfect cubes.

A cuboid is shaped like a box, and most people simply call it a box. Like the cube, it has six flat faces, but they are flat rectangles and they are not all the same size. If a cube is a thick square, then a cuboid is a thick rectangle. A cereal box, a brick, a closed book, and a shoebox are all cuboids.

Quick Check

  • A cube and a cuboid both have six flat faces. What is the one big difference between them?
  • Think about the faces: are they all the same size, or not?

Lesson 2: Cylinders, Cones, and Spheres

A cylinder is a tall, round solid. It has two flat ends, and each end is a circle, joined by a smooth curved surface around the middle. A cylinder can stand up straight on one end, and it can also be laid on its side and rolled. A tin of milk, a drinking glass, and a candle are cylinders.

A cone is the shape of an ice-cream cone. It has one flat circle end and rises up to a single point at the top. So it is wide and round at the bottom and narrows to a sharp tip. A party hat and the orange road cones on the street are cones.

A sphere is perfectly round all over, like a ball. It has no flat faces, no corners, and no straight edges — just one smooth, curving surface in every direction. Because it is round all over, a sphere rolls whichever way you push it. A football, a marble, and an orange are spheres.

Remember

Flat shapes lie on paper, but solid shapes take up space and can be held. A circle is flat, but a ball is a sphere — and a sphere is a solid you can hold in your hand.

Lesson 3: Telling Solid Shapes Apart

To name a solid shape, ask yourself a few simple questions. Is it round all over? Then it is a sphere. Does it have flat square faces? Then it is a cube. Does it have flat rectangle faces like a box? Then it is a cuboid. Does it have two flat circle ends and a curved middle? Then it is a cylinder. Does it have one circle end and rise to a point? Then it is a cone. With those few questions, you can name almost any solid shape you meet.

Think About It

  • A tin of milk and a ball are both round in some way.
  • One of them can stand up flat on a table and one cannot. Which is which, and why?

Let's Practice

Solid Shape Hunt: Find a real, everyday object for each solid shape and write it down.

  1. Find one cube.
  2. Find one cuboid (box).
  3. Find one cylinder.
  4. Find one cone.
  5. Find one sphere.

Patterns, Symmetry & Area

Now that you know your shapes, you can do three wonderful things with them: make repeating patterns, discover symmetry by folding, and measure area by covering a surface with squares. Let us explore each one.

Lesson 1: Shape Patterns

A pattern is a group of things arranged in an order that repeats again and again. Once you know the order, you can always tell what comes next. Here is a simple pattern made with a circle and a square: circle, square, circle, square, circle, square, … The little group "circle, square" repeats over and over, so the next shape must be a circle, then a square.

Patterns can use more shapes too: triangle, triangle, circle, triangle, triangle, circle, … Here the repeating group is "triangle, triangle, circle." To continue any pattern, find the group that repeats, then keep adding it on the end. Patterns are all around you — in floor tiles, in printed cloth, and on flags.

Let's Explore

Look at this pattern: square, circle, circle, square, circle, circle, square, circle, circle. The repeating group is square, circle, circle — so the next three shapes are square, circle, circle.

Quick Check

  • Continue this pattern by saying the next two shapes:
  • circle, triangle, square, circle, triangle, square, circle, triangle, square, …

Lesson 2: Symmetry and Lines of Symmetry

A shape has symmetry when its two halves match each other exactly. Think of a butterfly: its left wing and right wing match like a mirror picture. If you folded the butterfly down the middle, the two wings would fit perfectly on top of each other. We say the butterfly is symmetrical.

The special fold line that splits a shape into two matching halves is called the line of symmetry. The best way to test for it is to fold, just as the curriculum asks. If you cut a shape out of paper and fold it along a line, and the two halves match perfectly, the fold is a line of symmetry. If one half pokes out past the other, it is not.

Some shapes have one line of symmetry, some have several, and some have none. A circle is the most symmetrical of all — fold it through its centre any way and the halves always match. A square has lines of symmetry both up-and-down and side-to-side. Many letters, like A, are symmetrical too.

Let's Explore

Cut a square out of paper. Fold it down the middle from top to bottom — the two halves match, so that fold is a line of symmetry. Now open it and fold from side to side — the halves match again. A square has more than one line of symmetry!

Think About It

  • Write the capital letter "A" and fold it down the middle from top to bottom. Do the halves match?
  • Which fold gives a line of symmetry, and which does not?

Lesson 3: Area — Covering a Flat Surface

Area is the amount of flat surface that a shape covers. When you want to know how much surface something takes up — the top of a desk, the floor of a room, the cover of a book — you are asking about its area.

Imagine covering the top of your desk with small square tiles, with no gaps and no overlaps. The number of tiles it takes tells you the area of the desk top. To measure area in a simple way, we cover the flat shape with equal squares and count how many it takes. If a rectangle covers 6 little squares, its area is 6 squares. The bigger the surface, the more squares it takes, and so the larger its area.

Remember

Area is the amount of flat surface a shape covers. We measure area by counting how many equal squares it takes to cover the shape with no gaps and no overlaps.

Quick Check

  • One shape is covered by 8 squares, and another is covered by 12 squares.
  • Which shape has the larger area, and how do you know?

Let's Practice

Make and Fold: Try these three tasks.

  1. Make your own repeating pattern using three different shapes. Draw or describe the first nine shapes.
  2. Cut a rectangle out of paper. Fold it to find a line of symmetry, then fold a different way to find another. How many can you find?
  3. Place a book on a tiled floor or checked cloth and count how many tiles it covers. That number is the area of the book's cover.

Word Power

Here are the important geometry words from this chapter. Each one is explained simply, with an everyday example to help you remember it.

Shapes and Their Parts

Shape: the form of an object — how its outline looks. Example: the front of a clock has a round shape.
Side: a straight edge of a flat shape. Example: a square has four sides.
Corner: the point where two sides meet. Example: a rectangle has four corners.
Flat shape (2D shape): a flat shape with length and width but no thickness. Example: a triangle drawn on paper.
Solid shape (3D shape): a shape you can hold, that takes up space and has thickness. Example: a ball.

Flat Shapes

Square: a flat shape with four equal straight sides and four square corners. Example: the face of a number cube.
Rectangle: a flat shape with four straight sides and four square corners, where opposite sides are equal. Example: a door.
Triangle: a flat shape with three straight sides and three corners. Example: a slice of pizza.
Circle: a flat shape that is perfectly round, with no sides and no corners. Example: the top of a tin.

Solid Shapes

Cube: a solid shape with six square faces, all the same size. Example: a dice.
Cuboid (box): a solid shape with six flat rectangle faces, shaped like a box. Example: a cereal box.
Cylinder: a solid shape with two flat circle ends and a curved middle. Example: a tin of milk.
Cone: a solid shape with one flat circle end and a point at the top. Example: an ice-cream cone.
Sphere: a solid shape that is perfectly round all over, like a ball. Example: an orange.

Patterns, Symmetry, Lines, and Area

Pattern: things arranged in an order that repeats. Example: circle, square, circle, square.
Symmetry: when two halves of a shape match each other exactly. Example: a butterfly's two wings.
Line of symmetry: the line you can fold a shape along so both halves fit exactly on top of each other. Example: a fold down the middle of a heart shape.
Point: a single tiny spot that marks a place. Example: the dot made by your pencil tip.
Line segment: a straight piece of a line with two ends. Example: one side of a triangle.
Simple closed path: a path that goes all the way around and comes back to its start without crossing itself. Example: tracing the edge of a book.
Area: the amount of flat surface that a shape covers. Example: the number of square tiles it takes to cover a floor.

Answer Key

Here are the answers to the Quick Checks and the Practice tasks from each lesson. Try the questions yourself first, then check your work here.

Flat Shapes — Answers

Square and rectangle (same): both have four straight sides; both have four corners; every corner is a square corner; both are flat shapes.

Square and rectangle (different): a square has four equal sides, but a rectangle has only its opposite sides equal; a square looks the same whichever way you turn it, while a rectangle usually looks longer one way.

Sides and corners: square — 4 sides, 4 corners; rectangle — 4 sides, 4 corners; triangle — 3 sides, 3 corners; circle — no sides, no corners.

Why a circle rolls but a square does not: a circle has no corners and a smooth round edge, so it rolls easily; a square has straight sides and corners that catch and stop it.

Solid Shapes — Answers

Cube vs cuboid: a cube has six square faces all the same size; a cuboid has six rectangle faces that are not all the same size. Example of a cube: a dice. Example of a cuboid: a cereal box.

Six square faces: a cube — an everyday example is a dice or a wooden block.

Tin vs ball (Think About It): the tin of milk is a cylinder; it can stand up flat on a table because it has a flat circle end. The ball is a sphere; it cannot stand still because it is round all over and rolls.

Cylinder vs cone: a cylinder has two flat circle ends with a curved middle; a cone has only one flat circle end and rises to a point at the top.

Patterns, Symmetry & Area — Answers

Pattern (circle, triangle, square, …): the next two shapes are circle, triangle. (The repeating group is circle, triangle, square.)

Pattern (square, circle, circle, …): the next three shapes are square, circle, circle.

Line of symmetry: it is the line you can fold a shape along so the two halves fit exactly on top of each other. To test for one, cut the shape from paper and fold it along the line; if the halves match exactly, it is a line of symmetry.

The letter "A": folded down the middle from top to bottom, the two halves match — so that fold is a line of symmetry.

Area (8 squares vs 12 squares): the shape covered by 12 squares has the larger area, because it takes more squares to cover it.

Meaning of area: area is the amount of flat surface a shape covers; we measure it by counting how many equal squares cover the shape with no gaps and no overlaps.
