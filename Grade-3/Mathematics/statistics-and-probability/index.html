Collecting and Organising Data

Have you ever wanted to find out something about your class? Perhaps you have wondered which fruit your friends like best, or how most children get to school in the morning. There is a clever way to answer questions like these: you can collect information. When you ask a question and gather everyone's answers, you are collecting little pieces of information that mathematicians call data. In this chapter you will become a real data detective.

What You Will Learn

  • Ask a question and collect information (data) about your class.
  • Tell the difference between attribute data (words) and numeric data (numbers).
  • Record your data neatly using tally marks.
  • Organise your data in a table with clear headings.

Lesson 1: Collecting Data

Every good investigation begins with a question. Before you can find out anything, you must decide exactly what you want to know. Imagine you want to find out the favourite fruit of the children in your class. Your question might be: "What is your favourite fruit — mango, banana, or orange?" This question is clear, and it even gives a few choices, called categories, so everyone knows what kind of answer you are looking for.

Once you have your question, the next step is to gather the answers. You could ask each child, one at a time. Each answer a child gives is one little piece of information. When you put all those pieces together, you have your data.

The data you collect can be of two kinds. Sometimes you collect words, such as colours, types of cars, or favourite movies. We call this attribute data, because it describes what something is like. Other times you collect numbers, such as how many brothers and sisters each child has. We call this numeric data, because it counts amounts. Both kinds can be sorted and shown in graphs.

Think About It

If you wanted to find out how the children in your class travel to school, what question would you ask? What categories, or choices, might you give them — walk, bus, car, bicycle?

Did You Know?

Grown-ups collect data all the time. Shopkeepers count which sweets sell the most, nurses record how tall children grow, and weather watchers gather data about rain and sunshine. Collecting data helps people make good decisions.

Lesson 2: Tally Charts and Tables

When you ask many children a question, the answers come quickly, one after another. How can you keep count without getting muddled? The answer is to use tally marks.

A tally mark is a simple upright line. Each time someone gives an answer, you make one mark next to that answer. But here is the clever part: when you reach five, you do not draw five separate lines. Instead, you draw four upright lines and then a fifth line straight across them, like a little gate. This makes a neat bundle of five that is very easy to count. Counting in bundles of five is a great trick, because you can count your bundles quickly — five, ten, fifteen, twenty.

Suppose you asked your class, "What is your favourite fruit?" and made a tally mark for each answer. When you finished, your tally chart looked like this:

Fruit Tally Number
Mango ||||̶ ||| 8
Banana ||||̶ 5
Orange ||| 3

Look carefully. For mango there is one full bundle of five and three more single marks, which makes 5 + 3 = 8. For banana there is one bundle of five, so that is 5. For orange there are three single marks, so that is 3. A chart like this, with neat rows and columns and clear headings, is called a table. Notice the heading at the top of each column — "Fruit," "Tally," and "Number" — so that anyone reading it knows exactly what each part means.

Let's Explore

Make a tally chart of your own. Ask ten classmates whether they like cricket, football, or netball best. Make one tally mark for each answer, remembering to bundle in fives. Then count up and write the total number for each sport.

Quick Check

In a tally chart, how many marks are in one full bundle? And how would you show the number seven using tally marks?

Remember

Tally marks help you count one at a time without losing your place, and bundling them in fives makes them quick and easy to add up.

Let's Practice

Choose one question to ask your class, such as "What is your favourite colour?" Decide on three or four categories before you begin. Then go around and collect everyone's answer, making one tally mark for each and bundling them in fives. Copy your tally chart into a neat table and write the total number for each category in the last column. Finally, talk about your data: which colour was chosen the most, which the least, and how many children were asked in all.

Showing and Reading Data

A table is neat and useful, but there is an even more exciting way to show your data: you can turn it into a picture. When you do this, anyone can look at your data and understand it in a single glance. There are two favourite ways to picture data — pictographs and bar graphs — and you are going to learn both, and then learn how to read them.

What You Will Learn

  • Make and label a pictograph using a picture and a key.
  • Make and label a bar graph using bars of different lengths.
  • Read a table, a pictograph, and a bar graph to answer questions.
  • Find which has the most, which has the least, and how many more or fewer.

Lesson 3: Pictographs and Bar Graphs

A pictograph uses a small picture or symbol to stand for an amount. To make one, you decide on one picture to use, and then decide how many that one picture will stand for. This is called the key, and it is very important. For our favourite-fruit data, imagine we choose a small circle to stand for the children, and we decide that one circle stands for 2 children. That is our key. Mango had 8 children, so we draw 4 circles, because 4 twos make 8.

Every pictograph needs three things to be complete: a title that tells what the graph is about, a label for each row, and a key that tells how much one picture is worth.

A bar graph uses bars of different lengths to show amounts. A long bar means a large amount, and a short bar means a small amount. This makes it very easy to see, just by looking, which thing has the most and which has the least. To make a bar graph of our favourite fruits, we draw a line going up the side with numbers on it, and along the bottom we write the name of each fruit. For each fruit we draw a bar that rises up to the right number. Because mango's bar is the tallest, you can tell in an instant that mango is the most popular fruit, without reading a single number.

Like a pictograph, a good bar graph must be labelled. It needs a title at the top, a label on the bottom telling what the bars stand for, and a label on the side telling what the numbers mean. When everything is labelled, your graph tells its story clearly.

Did You Know?

A bar graph can stand up tall, with bars rising upward — we call that a vertical bar graph. Or it can lie on its side, with bars stretching out to the right — we call that a horizontal bar graph. Both show exactly the same data; they just face different ways.

Look Around You

Bar graphs and pictographs appear in many places — in newspapers showing how many runs a cricketer scored, on posters in the clinic, and in television weather reports. Once you know how to read them, you will spot them everywhere.

Lesson 4: Reading Graphs

Making a graph is only half the fun. The real magic happens when you read a graph to answer questions. When you read a bar graph or a pictograph, you can find how many (look at one bar and find the number it reaches), which has the most (find the longest bar), which has the least (find the shortest bar), and how many more or fewer (find the difference between two bars).

Here is a table showing how the children in a Grade 3 class travel to school:

Way to travel Number of children
Walk7
Bus10
Car4
Bicycle2

Let us read it like data detectives. How many children ride the bus? We look at the "Bus" row and see 10, so ten children ride the bus. Which way is the most popular? The largest number is 10, beside "Bus," so the bus is the most popular. Which way is the least popular? The smallest number is 2, beside "Bicycle." How many more children walk than ride in a car? Seven walk and four come by car, and 7 − 4 = 3, so three more children walk than ride in a car.

Think About It

Why do you think a bar graph makes it so easy to spot the most popular and the least popular answer? What is it about the bars that helps your eyes find the answer fast?

Quick Check

Using the travel table above, how many children in all are in the class? (Hint: add up every number.) And how many more children ride the bus than ride a bicycle?

Remember

To find "the most," look for the longest bar or biggest number. To find "the least," look for the shortest bar or smallest number. To compare, find the difference by subtracting.

Let's Practice

Here is a small table of pets owned by children in a group: Dog — 5, Cat — 3, Fish — 6. Use it to answer: Which pet is owned by the most children? Which by the fewest? How many more children own a fish than own a cat? How many children are in the group altogether? Then think about how you would draw this data as a bar graph, and which bar would be the tallest.

Chance and Probability

Some things in life are sure to happen. Some things might happen. And some things can never happen at all. When mathematicians talk about how likely something is to happen, they are talking about probability, which is just a grown-up word for chance. In this part you will learn the four special chance words and carry out your own probability experiments.

What You Will Learn

  • Use the words certain, likely, unlikely, and impossible to describe chance.
  • Carry out a probability experiment, such as tossing a coin or rolling a die.
  • Predict an outcome before an experiment begins.
  • Record outcomes with tally marks and compare them with your prediction.

Lesson 5: The Four Chance Words

To talk about chance, we use four special words. Learning these is like learning a secret code for describing the future.

The first word is certain. Something is certain when it is sure to happen, every single time. It is certain that the sun will rise in the morning. It is certain that you will grow older.

The second word is likely. Something is likely when it has a good chance of happening, even though it is not completely sure. It is likely to be hot in Jamaica at midday.

The third word is unlikely. Something is unlikely when it has only a small chance of happening. It is unlikely that it will snow in Kingston.

The fourth word is impossible. Something is impossible when it can never, ever happen. It is impossible for a cat to fly on its own. It is impossible to count to the very last number, because numbers never end.

Let's Explore

Sort these into certain, likely, unlikely, or impossible: "A fish will walk to school." "The bell will ring at the end of the day." "I will see a green car on the way home." "Tomorrow will come after today." Talk about your answers with a partner — you may not always agree, and that is part of the fun!

Lesson 6: Doing a Probability Experiment

The best way to understand chance is to try it for yourself. When you do something to see what happens, that is called an experiment. What actually happens is called the outcome.

Think about tossing a coin. A coin has two sides: heads and tails. When you toss it, it must land on one side or the other, so there are exactly two possible outcomes: heads or tails. Before you toss, you cannot be certain which one you will get, but you know it will be one of those two. Each side has the same chance, so we say heads and tails are equally likely.

A good data detective always does three things. First, you predict — you make a sensible guess about what will happen before you begin. Then you do the experiment and record the outcome, writing down what really happened, often with a tally mark. Afterwards, you compare your prediction with the real outcomes to see how close your guess was. Suppose you toss a coin ten times and record each outcome:

Outcome Tally Number
Heads||||̶ |6
Tails||||4

In this experiment, heads came up 6 times and tails came up 4 times. That makes 6 + 4 = 10 tosses in all, which is exactly how many times we tossed — so we know we counted correctly. Heads and tails each have an equal chance, yet they did not come out perfectly even this time. That is the nature of chance: over just a few tosses, one outcome may happen a little more than the other. You can also roll a die and record which of the numbers 1 to 6 comes up, or pick a number from a bag and record what you draw. Each time, predict first, record with tally marks, then compare.

Did You Know?

Here is a probability game you can play with a partner. Write the numbers 1 to 12 on a sheet of paper. Place twelve counters on the numbers any way you like — you may even put more than one on the same number. Take turns rolling two dice and adding the two numbers together. If you have a counter on that sum, remove one. The first player to remove all their counters wins! Afterwards, talk about why some sums, like 6, 7, and 8, came up more often than 2 or 12.

Think About It

Before rolling one die, predict: are you more likely to roll a number less than 7, or a number greater than 7? Look at the numbers 1 to 6 on the die and explain your answer.

Remember

Certain means it will always happen, impossible means it can never happen, and likely and unlikely sit in between. In an experiment, always predict first, record the outcomes, then compare.

Let's Practice

Toss a coin twenty times. Before you start, predict how many heads and how many tails you think you will get. Make a tally chart with two rows, "Heads" and "Tails," and record each toss with a tally mark, bundling in fives. When you finish, count the totals and check that they add up to twenty. Then compare your results with your prediction. Were you close?

Word Power

Here are the important words you met in this chapter. Each one comes with its meaning and an example to help you remember it.

Data: Pieces of information that you collect. If you ask ten friends their favourite colour and write down each answer, all those answers together are your data.
Information: The facts you find out by looking, asking, or counting. "Six children walk to school" is a piece of information.
Tally: A small mark you make to count one thing. We draw four upright marks, then a fifth mark across them to make a group of five.
Table: A neat way to organise data in rows and columns with headings. A table can list each fruit in one column and how many children chose it in the next.
Pictograph: A graph that uses a picture or symbol to stand for an amount. One small apple might stand for two children who like apples.
Bar graph: A graph that uses bars of different lengths to show amounts. The longer the bar, the larger the amount.
Probability: The chance that something will happen. The probability of getting heads when you toss a coin is one chance out of two.
Certain: Sure to happen, every single time. It is certain that the sun will rise tomorrow.
Likely: Has a good chance of happening, but is not completely sure. It is likely to be hot in Jamaica at noon.
Unlikely: Has only a small chance of happening. It is unlikely that it will snow in Kingston.
Impossible: Can never happen, no matter what. It is impossible for a cat to fly on its own.
Outcome: What actually happens when you do an experiment. When you toss a coin, "heads" is one possible outcome and "tails" is the other.

Probability — Think About It

On one die the numbers are 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. The numbers less than 7 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 — that is every number on the die. The numbers greater than 7 are none. So it is certain you will roll a number less than 7, and impossible to roll a number greater than 7.
