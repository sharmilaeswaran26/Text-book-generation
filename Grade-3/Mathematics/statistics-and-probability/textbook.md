# Statistics and Probability: Become a Data Detective!

**Grade 3 · Mathematics**

---

## Hello, Young Mathematician!

Have you ever wanted to find out something about your class? Perhaps you have wondered which fruit your friends like best, or how most of the children get to school in the morning. Do more of them walk, ride the bus, or come in a car? These are wonderful questions, and there is a clever way to answer them.

The secret is this: you can collect **information**. When you ask a question and gather everyone's answers, you are collecting little pieces of information that mathematicians call **data**. Once you have gathered your data, you can sort it, count it, and even turn it into a picture that tells a story at a glance.

In this chapter you are going to become a real data detective. You will learn how to ask a good question and gather answers, how to keep a neat count using tally marks, and how to draw your data as a pictograph or a bar graph. You will learn how to read these graphs to find out which thing has the most and which has the least. And right at the end, you will explore the exciting world of **chance**, where you will toss coins, roll dice, and learn the special words mathematicians use to talk about what is likely to happen. Let us begin our investigation!

---

## Learning Goals

In this chapter you will learn to:

- Ask a question and collect information (data) about your class and the world around you.
- Sort and record your data neatly using tally marks and tables.
- Show your data in pictures and graphs, by making and labelling pictographs and simple bar graphs.
- Read and interpret a table, a pictograph, and a bar graph to find how many, which has the most, and which has the least.
- Use the probability words *certain*, *likely*, *unlikely*, and *impossible* to describe chance.
- Carry out simple probability experiments, such as tossing a coin or rolling a die, and record the outcomes.

## Our Big Question

Here is the big question that will guide our whole chapter:

> **How can we collect information, show it as a picture, and use it to answer questions and make good guesses?**

We will answer this question step by step. First we will collect data by asking questions. Then we will organise that data into tally charts and tables. Next we will turn the data into pictographs and bar graphs that anyone can read. After that we will practise reading graphs to find answers. And finally we will explore chance and probability, learning to predict what might happen and to check our predictions with real experiments.

## Word Power

Here are the important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about learning them all at once. Each one is explained again carefully inside the lessons, so you will understand them fully as you read.

- **Data** — pieces of information that you collect. *Example:* if you ask ten friends their favourite colour and write down each answer, all those answers together are your data.
- **Information** — the facts you find out by looking, asking, or counting. *Example:* "Six children walk to school" is a piece of information.
- **Tally** — a small mark you make to count one thing. We draw four upright marks, then a fifth mark across them to make a group of five. *Example:* |||| with a line across stands for five.
- **Table** — a neat way to organise data in rows and columns with headings. *Example:* a table can list each fruit in one column and how many children chose it in the next column.
- **Pictograph** — a graph that uses a picture or symbol to stand for an amount. *Example:* one small apple drawing might stand for two children who like apples.
- **Bar graph** — a graph that uses bars of different lengths to show amounts. The longer the bar, the larger the amount. *Example:* a tall bar for "bus" shows that many children ride the bus.
- **Probability** — the chance that something will happen. *Example:* the probability of getting heads when you toss a coin is one chance out of two.
- **Certain** — sure to happen, every single time. *Example:* it is certain that the sun will rise tomorrow.
- **Likely** — has a good chance of happening, but is not completely sure. *Example:* it is likely to be hot in Jamaica at noon.
- **Unlikely** — has only a small chance of happening. *Example:* it is unlikely that it will snow in Kingston.
- **Impossible** — can never happen, no matter what. *Example:* it is impossible for a cat to fly on its own.
- **Outcome** — what actually happens when you do an experiment. *Example:* when you toss a coin, "heads" is one possible outcome and "tails" is the other.

# Part One: Collecting and Organising Data

## Collecting Data

Every good investigation begins with a question. Before you can find out anything, you must decide exactly what you want to know. A clear question is the very first tool of a data detective.

Imagine you want to find out the favourite fruit of the children in your class. Your question might be: *"What is your favourite fruit — mango, banana, or orange?"* Notice that this question is clear and easy to answer. It even gives a few choices, called **categories**, so that everyone knows what kinds of answers you are looking for.

Once you have your question, the next step is to **gather the answers**. You could go around the class and ask each child, one at a time, "What is your favourite fruit?" Each answer a child gives is one little piece of **information**. When you put all those pieces of information together, you have your **data**.

The data you collect can be of two kinds. Sometimes you collect words, such as colours, types of cars, or favourite movies. We call this **attribute data**, because it describes what something is like. Other times you collect numbers, such as how many brothers and sisters each child has. We call this **numeric data**, because it counts amounts. Both kinds are useful, and both can be sorted and shown in graphs.

> **Think About It:** If you wanted to find out how the children in your class travel to school, what question would you ask? What categories, or choices, might you give them — walk, bus, car, bicycle?

> **Did You Know?** Grown-ups collect data all the time. Shopkeepers count which sweets sell the most, nurses record how tall children grow, and weather watchers gather data about rain and sunshine. Collecting data helps people make good decisions.

## Tally Charts and Tables

When you ask many children a question, the answers come quickly, one after another. How can you keep count without getting muddled? The answer is to use **tally marks**.

A tally mark is a simple upright line, like this: |. Each time someone gives an answer, you make one tally mark next to that answer. But here is the clever part. When you reach five, you do not draw five separate lines. Instead, you draw four upright lines and then a fifth line straight across them, like a little gate. This makes a neat bundle of five that is very easy to count: ||||̸ .

Counting in bundles of five is a great trick, because you can count your bundles quickly — five, ten, fifteen, twenty — instead of counting every single line.

**Let's look at an example.** Suppose you asked your class, "What is your favourite fruit?" and you made a tally mark each time someone answered. When you finished, your tally chart looked like this:

| Fruit | Tally | Number |
|-------|-------|--------|
| Mango | \|\|\|\|̸ \|\|\| | 8 |
| Banana | \|\|\|\|̸ | 5 |
| Orange | \|\|\| | 3 |

Look carefully at the chart. For mango, there is one full bundle of five and three more single marks, which makes 5 + 3 = 8. For banana there is one bundle of five, so that is 5. For orange there are three single marks, so that is 3. The last column, where we have written the total number for each fruit, turns our tally marks into tidy numbers.

A chart like this, with neat rows and columns and clear headings, is called a **table**. A table is one of the best ways to organise data, because everything is in its proper place and easy to find. Notice that the table has a heading at the top of each column — "Fruit," "Tally," and "Number" — so that anyone reading it knows exactly what each part means.

> **Let's Explore:** Make a tally chart of your own. Ask ten classmates whether they like cricket, football, or netball best. Make one tally mark for each answer, remembering to bundle in fives. Then count up and write the total number for each sport.

> **Quick Check:** In a tally chart, how many marks are in one full bundle? And how would you show the number seven using tally marks?

> **Remember:** Tally marks help you count one at a time without losing your place, and bundling them in fives makes them quick and easy to add up.

# Part Two: Showing and Reading Data

## Pictographs and Bar Graphs

A table is neat and useful, but there is an even more exciting way to show your data: you can turn it into a **picture**. When you do this, anyone can look at your data and understand it in a single glance. There are two favourite ways to picture data, and you are going to learn both.

### Making a Pictograph

A **pictograph** uses a small picture or symbol to stand for an amount. To make one, you must decide on one picture to use, and then decide how many that one picture will stand for. This is called the **key**, and it is very important.

For our favourite-fruit data, imagine we choose a small circle to stand for the children, and we decide that **one circle stands for 2 children**. That is our key. Then we draw the right number of circles beside each fruit. Mango had 8 children, so we draw 4 circles, because 4 twos make 8. Banana is harder, because 5 is not an even number of twos — we would draw 2 circles and then half a circle to show the leftover one. To keep things simple at first, pictographs work best when each picture stands for a small, friendly number.

Every pictograph needs three things to be complete: a **title** that tells what the graph is about, a **label** for each row so you know what each line stands for, and a **key** that tells how much one picture is worth. Without these, a reader would be lost.

### Making a Bar Graph

A **bar graph** uses **bars** of different lengths to show amounts. A long bar means a large amount, and a short bar means a small amount. This makes it very easy to see, just by looking, which thing has the most and which has the least.

To make a bar graph of our favourite fruits, we draw a line going up the side with numbers on it: 0, 1, 2, 3, and so on. Then, along the bottom, we write the name of each fruit. For each fruit we draw a bar that rises up to the right number. Mango rises all the way up to 8, banana up to 5, and orange up to 3. Because mango's bar is the tallest, you can tell in an instant that mango is the most popular fruit, without having to read a single number.

Like a pictograph, a good bar graph must be **labelled**. It needs a title at the top, a label on the bottom telling what the bars stand for (the fruits), and a label on the side telling what the numbers mean (the number of children). When everything is labelled, your graph tells its story clearly to anyone who sees it.

> **Did You Know?** A bar graph can stand up tall, with bars rising upward — we call that a *vertical* bar graph. Or it can lie on its side, with bars stretching out to the right — we call that a *horizontal* bar graph. Both show exactly the same data; they just face different ways.

> **Look Around You:** Bar graphs and pictographs appear in many places — in newspapers showing how many runs a cricketer scored, on posters in the clinic, and in television weather reports. Once you know how to read them, you will spot them everywhere.

## Reading Graphs

Making a graph is only half the fun. The real magic happens when you **read** a graph to answer questions. A graph is full of answers waiting to be found, if you know how to look.

When you read a bar graph or a pictograph, here are the kinds of questions you can answer:

- **How many?** Look at one bar or one row, and find the number it reaches. This tells you the amount for that one thing.
- **Which has the most?** Find the longest bar, or the row with the most pictures. That is the one with the greatest amount.
- **Which has the least?** Find the shortest bar, or the row with the fewest pictures. That is the one with the smallest amount.
- **How many more or fewer?** Compare two bars by finding the difference between their numbers.

**Let's look at an example.** Here is a table showing how the children in a Grade 3 class travel to school:

| Way to travel | Number of children |
|---------------|--------------------|
| Walk | 7 |
| Bus | 10 |
| Car | 4 |
| Bicycle | 2 |

Now let us read it like data detectives. *How many children ride the bus?* We look at the "Bus" row and see the number 10, so ten children ride the bus. *Which way is the most popular?* The largest number is 10, beside "Bus," so the bus is the most popular way to travel. *Which way is the least popular?* The smallest number is 2, beside "Bicycle," so the bicycle is the least popular. *How many more children walk than ride in a car?* Seven children walk and four come by car, and 7 − 4 = 3, so three more children walk than ride in a car. See how many answers were hiding inside the data!

> **Think About It:** Why do you think a bar graph makes it so easy to spot the most popular and the least popular answer? What is it about the bars that helps your eyes find the answer fast?

> **Quick Check:** Using the travel table above, how many children in all are in the class? (Hint: add up every number.) And how many more children ride the bus than ride a bicycle?

> **Remember:** To find "the most," look for the longest bar or biggest number. To find "the least," look for the shortest bar or smallest number. To compare, find the difference by subtracting.

# Part Three: Chance and Probability

## Chance and Probability

Some things in life are sure to happen. Some things might happen. And some things can never happen at all. When mathematicians talk about how likely something is to happen, they are talking about **probability**, which is just a grown-up word for **chance**.

To talk about chance, we use four special words. Learning these four words is like learning a secret code for describing the future.

The first word is **certain**. Something is certain when it is sure to happen, every single time. *It is certain that the sun will rise in the morning.* *It is certain that you will grow older.* No matter what, these things will happen.

The second word is **likely**. Something is likely when it has a good chance of happening, even though it is not completely sure. *It is likely to be hot in Jamaica at midday.* It usually is hot, so we say it is likely — but just now and then a cool, cloudy day comes along.

The third word is **unlikely**. Something is unlikely when it has only a small chance of happening. It could happen, but it probably will not. *It is unlikely that it will snow in Kingston.* It almost never does, so we say it is unlikely.

The fourth word is **impossible**. Something is impossible when it can never, ever happen, no matter what. *It is impossible for a cat to fly on its own.* *It is impossible to count to the very last number, because numbers never end.* These things simply cannot be.

> **Let's Explore:** Sort these into *certain*, *likely*, *unlikely*, or *impossible*: "A fish will walk to school." "The bell will ring at the end of the day." "I will see a green car on the way home." "Tomorrow will come after today." Talk about your answers with a partner — you may not always agree, and that is part of the fun!

### Doing a Probability Experiment

The best way to understand chance is to **try it** for yourself. When you do something to see what happens, that is called an **experiment**. What actually happens is called the **outcome**.

Think about tossing a coin. A coin has two sides: heads and tails. When you toss it, it must land on one side or the other. So there are exactly two possible outcomes: **heads** or **tails**. Before you toss, you cannot be certain which one you will get, but you do know it will be one of those two. Each side has the same chance, so we say heads and tails are *equally likely*.

A good data detective always does two things in an experiment. First, you **predict** — you make a sensible guess about what will happen before you begin. Then you do the experiment and **record the outcome**, writing down what really happened, often with a tally mark. Afterwards, you **compare** your prediction with the real outcomes to see how close your guess was.

**Let's look at an example.** Suppose you toss a coin ten times and use a tally chart to record each outcome:

| Outcome | Tally | Number |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Heads | \|\|\|\|̸ \| | 6 |
| Tails | \|\|\|\| | 4 |

In this experiment, heads came up 6 times and tails came up 4 times. That makes 6 + 4 = 10 tosses in all, which is exactly how many times we tossed — so we know we counted correctly. Heads and tails each have an equal chance, yet they did not come out perfectly even this time. That is the nature of chance: over just a few tosses, one outcome may happen a little more than the other. The more times you toss, the closer to even they usually become.

You can do experiments like this with other things too. You can **roll a die** and record which of the numbers 1 to 6 comes up. You can **pick a number** from a bag of folded papers and record what you draw. Each time, you predict first, then record the outcomes with tally marks, then compare. This is exactly how real scientists and mathematicians test their ideas.

> **Did You Know?** Here is a probability game you can play with a partner. Write the numbers 1 to 12 on a sheet of paper. Place twelve counters on the numbers any way you like — you may even put more than one counter on the same number. Take turns rolling two dice and adding the two numbers together. If you have a counter on that sum, remove one. The first player to remove all their counters wins! After playing, talk about why some sums, like 6, 7, and 8, came up more often than 2 or 12.

> **Think About It:** Before rolling one die, predict: are you more likely to roll a number less than 7, or a number greater than 7? Look at the numbers 1 to 6 on the die and explain your answer.

> **Remember:** *Certain* means it will always happen, *impossible* means it can never happen, and *likely* and *unlikely* sit in between. In an experiment, always predict first, record the outcomes, then compare.

## Practice Together

Let us practise as a class, step by step. Your teacher will help you work through these together.

1. Choose one question to ask the class, such as "What is your favourite colour?" Decide on three or four categories before you begin.
2. Go around and collect everyone's answer, making one tally mark for each. Remember to bundle your tallies in fives.

## Chapter Summary

In this chapter, you became a data detective and learned how to collect, organise, show, and read information, and how to talk about chance.

You learned that **data** is the information we collect when we ask a question and gather answers, and that a clear question with good categories is the first step. You learned to record your answers neatly using **tally marks**, bundling them in fives, and to organise them in a **table** with headings.

You then learned two ways to picture data: a **pictograph**, which uses a symbol with a key to stand for an amount, and a **bar graph**, which uses bars of different lengths. You discovered that both must have a title and clear labels, and that a graph lets you see at a glance which thing has the most and which has the least. You practised **reading** tables and graphs to answer questions about how many, which has the most, which has the least, and how many more or fewer.

Finally, you explored **probability**, the chance that something will happen. You learned the four chance words — **certain**, **likely**, **unlikely**, and **impossible** — and you carried out **experiments** like tossing a coin and rolling a die, learning to predict an outcome, record it, and compare your prediction with what really happened.

## Remember

- **Data** is the information we collect by asking, looking, and counting.
- **Tally marks** help us count one at a time; we bundle them in fives to add up quickly.
- A **table** organises data in neat rows and columns with clear headings.
- A **pictograph** uses a symbol and a key; a **bar graph** uses bars — and both need a title and labels.
- To read a graph: the longest bar or biggest number is the **most**, and the shortest bar or smallest number is the **least**.
- **Probability** is the chance of something happening: *certain*, *likely*, *unlikely*, or *impossible*.
- In a probability experiment, **predict** first, **record** the outcomes, then **compare**.
