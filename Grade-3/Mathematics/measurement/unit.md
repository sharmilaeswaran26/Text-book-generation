# Grade 3 — Mathematics — Content Standard: Measurement

> **Faithful curriculum extract** from the Jamaica National Standards Curriculum (NSC), Grade 3, Mathematics, Content Standard: Measurement (Standard 2). This file preserves the curriculum as written for teachers. The student-facing lesson is in `textbook.md`.

**Standard 2:** Students will use non-standard and/or standard metric (SI) units, instruments, and procedures to estimate and measure quantities of different attributes, and to compute and solve problems.

**Attainment Target (AT-6):** Estimate, compare and use various types of measurements.

---

## Benchmark 3.2.1 — Estimate and measure distance

### Objectives
- **3.6.4** Differentiate between the use of metre and centimetre in measurement situations.
- **3.6.5** Estimate, measure, and compare distances in metres and/or centimetres.
- **(NEW)** Recognize measuring instruments that are used to measure distances: ruler, metre stick, tape measure, and know how to use them.
- **(NEW)** Use the word metre or centimetre and their symbols 'm' and 'cm' to describe and record distances.
- **3.6.6** Know that 100 cm = 1 m.
- **(NEW)** Use the knowledge in 3.6.6 to tell how many centimetres will be exactly the same length as a whole number of metres.
- **3.6.10** Measure and/or compute the perimetre of various objects and polygons. (DO NOT use a formula)
- **3.6.11** Establish a reference measure for one kilometre.
- **3.6.12** Use the word kilometre and its symbol km to describe the appropriate unit length.
- **3.6.13** Differentiate between the use of the metre, the centimetre and the kilometre in various measurement situations.
- **3.6.14** Know that 1000 m = 1 km.
- *Also see computation with measures under Number 1.9 e and f.*

### Illustrative Examples
1. Which metric unit is most often used to measure the distance:
   a) Along a roadway?
   b) On a racing track?
2. What are possible measures for these distances?
   a) The length of a new pencil? (15 cm, 15 m, 15 km)
   b) The distance from Kingston to Ocho Rios? (100 cm, 100 m, 100 km)
   c) The height of a doorway? (2 cm, 2 m, 2 km)
3. How many centimetres are the same as 5 metres?
4. Present an object (it would be good to have a watermelon or other objects for students to see and touch) and ask students:
   a) What can we measure about the watermelon? (Distance from one end to the other, distance around, how heavy it is...)
   b) What is the distance around the watermelon?
5. A wall goes all the way around Mrs. Ham's land. How long is the wall if the land looks like the diagram below? (Sides shown: 5 m, 8 m, 7 m, 8 m)

## Benchmark 3.2.2 — Estimate and measure time, using standard units

### Objectives
- **3.6.3 (Modified)** Read a calendar to tell a) month, b) day of week, and date.
- **(NEW)** Know relationships between month, year, week, day.
- **(NEW)** Associate the passage of years with a person's age and concepts of younger, older.
- **3.6.4** Calculate age in years and months.
- **(NEW)** Know that 60 minutes = 1 hour.
- **3.6.5** Tell or show the time on the clock (picture or model of) in minute intervals using the format minutes to / past.
- **2.6.3 (Modified)** Solve problems that compute elapsed time ... How many months until; how many hours until.
- **(NEW)** Compute the difference between two times given in hours and minutes, both before or both after 12 noon, or 12 am.
- **(NEW)** Add and subtract amounts of time, e.g. 5 hrs 45 minutes + 15 minutes.

### Illustrative Examples
1. Does your birthday fall on the same day of the week each year? Explain.
2. Use the calendar to answer the following:
   a) What day of the week is August 1?
   b) What is the date of the 2nd Monday in July?
   c) Which months have 30 days?
3. What time is shown on the clock?

## Benchmark 3.2.3 — Estimate and measure liquid capacity or volume

### Objectives
- **3.6.17** Know that 1000 mL = 1 L.
- **3.6.18** Estimate, measure and record the capacity of various containers in litres (L) or millilitres (mL).
- **(NEW)** Use the symbols L and mL when writing measures of volume or capacity.
- **3.6.19** Differentiate between the use of the litre and the millilitre in measurement situations.
- **(NEW)** Know what attribute of an object or quantity is measured using litres.
- **(NEW)** Recognize measuring instruments that are used to measure volume or capacity: measuring cup, teaspoon, and know how to use them.

### Illustrative Examples
1. Which of these are measured using litres?
2. Which is about one litre?
   a) A bottle of medicine for a baby.
   b) A bucket of water to wash a car.
   c) A jug of orange juice for breakfast.

## Benchmark 3.2.4 — Estimate and measure mass, using non-standard and standard metric units

### Objectives
- **3.6.15** Differentiate between the use of the kilogram and the gram in various measurement situations.
- **3.6.16** Know that 1000 g = 1 kg.
- **2.6.8** Use the symbols kg and g; or words kilogram, gram when recording measures of mass.
- **(NEW)** Know what attribute of an object or quantity is measured using grams or kilograms.
- **2.6.6** Recognize measuring instruments that are used to measure mass: balance, scale, and know how to use them.

### Illustrative Examples
1. Is the mass of this object measured in kilograms?
2. Mister Joel is using this instrument.
   a) What is the name of the instrument? Name of instrument: ___________
   b) What is he finding out? He is finding out: ___________
3. Which of these are best measured in grams?
   a) The mass of a spoon of salt.
   b) The mass of a truckload of potatoes.

## Benchmark 3.2.5 — Understand the concept, estimate and measure temperature, using standard metric units

### Objectives
- **3.6.20** Associate a given temperature with a) a hot or cool day, b) normal body temperature, c) a fever, d) the freezing and e) boiling point of water.
- **3.6.21** Record a given temperature using symbols, e.g. 28 °C.
- **2.6.10** Use the thermometre to read temperatures at different times of the day and year and record these readings.

### Illustrative Examples
3. A kettle begins to boil. What is the most likely temperature of the water in the kettle? A) 0 °C, B) 30 °C, C) 100 °C
4. Keep a class log of the temperature each day for a week, at different places in the school.

## Benchmark 3.2.6 — Associate the measurement of a quantity (distance, volume/capacity, mass) with the units and instruments best used

### Objectives
- **2.6.23** Select the unit (metre, centimetre, litre, kilogram) best used in a given situation.
- **2.6.24** Tell the measurement situation(s) in which a given unit (metre, centimetre, litre, and kilogram) is best used.

### Illustrative Examples
Tell which is the best unit of measure for the following:
- a) length of the classroom
- b) length of a leaf
- c) distance between two towns
- d) the weight of a watermelon
- e) the weight of an envelope
- f) jug of lemonade

## Benchmark 3.2.7 — Understand the measure: dozen

### Objectives
- **3.6.22** Identify twelve (12) objects as one dozen.
- **3.6.23** Identify commodities usually sold by the dozen.
- **(NEW)** Identify the number of objects in half and quarter dozen.
- **3.6.24** Use 'dozen' in problem solving situations.

### Illustrative Examples
1. One dozen roses are in the vase, and one dozen eggs are in the box. How many things make one dozen?
2. Which of these is usually sold and priced by the dozen? (When you ask the price, the seller says: ..... per dozen) — Mark Yes (✓) or No (✗): Rice, eggs, ackee, potatoes, cars.

## Benchmark 3.2.8 — Understand the concept: area; use informal units, and a grid to determine the area of objects

> (This is the very first introduction to area, and these examples show how to give a practical idea of area, before going to shapes and formula.)

### Objectives
- **3.6.25** Identify the surface (coverage) of any object, as its area.
- **3.6.26a** Find the area of irregular and regular flat surfaces using non-standard unit regions (e.g. leaves or circles).
- **3.6.26b** Find the area of flat shapes (actual, pictures of or diagrams of) when placed on a grid. (Do NOT use formula) (A Geoboard would be useful here.)

### Illustrative Examples
1. Compare the pictures of these objects (hula hoop, wheel, plate).
   a) How is the hula-hoop like the wheel and the plate?
   b) How is the plate different from the wheel and the hula-hoop?
   (Also: rail, window, door)
   c) How is the rail like the window and door?
   d) How are the window and the door alike, different?
2. Plates, windows, doors, floors all have a lot of area. What is area?
3. Show me the area of: a) the top of a table, b) the legs of a table, c) the wall, d) the classroom floor, e) the page of a book, f) the football field.
4. Find how much area a) the top of a table, b) a sheet of newspaper has.
   a) Find a table or a sheet of newspaper. Collect many leaves that have fallen from a tree. Paste as many of these leaves as you can to cover the paper or put them to cover the table top.
   b) How many leaves covered the paper?
   c) Are there any spaces?
5. Cut out shapes and use them to cover a larger flat object or sheet of paper. Students could work in groups, with each group being given a different shape or a different sized shape, to find out how many of the smaller shapes will cover the larger shape or flat object.
6. Find out: How many sheets of newspaper will cover the floor in the classroom?
7. A grid helps us to see how many smaller shapes is covered. Squares are usually preferred to using rectangles, triangles or circles. Look at the grid and respond.
   a) A rag is shown shaded on the grid. What is the area of the rag?
   b) Draw a mat shaped like a rectangle that covers 10 squares.
