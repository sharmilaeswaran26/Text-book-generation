# Measurement: How Big, How Heavy, How Much?

**Grade 3 · Mathematics**

---

## Hello, Young Mathematician!

Have you ever stood against a wall while someone made a little pencil mark above your head to see how tall you have grown? Have you watched your mother scoop flour onto a scale when she is baking, or filled a bottle with water right up to the top? Have you looked at the clock to find out how long it is until lunchtime, or counted out coins to pay for a patty at the shop?

If you have done any of these things, then you already know something wonderful: you have been **measuring**. Measuring is one of the most useful skills in the whole world, and you use it almost every day without even thinking about it.

In this chapter, you are going to become a real measuring expert. You will learn how to measure how **long** things are, how **heavy** they are, and how **much** a container can hold. You will learn to tell the **time**, to read a **calendar**, to count out **money** at the market, to read a **thermometer**, and to find the **area** of a flat shape by counting squares. You will even learn how to measure the distance all the way **around** a shape.

By the end of the chapter, you will look at the world around you and see numbers, units, and measurements everywhere. Let us begin!

---

## Learning Goals

In this chapter you will learn to:

- measure **length** using centimetres (cm) and metres (m), and choose the right unit and tool for the job;
- estimate a length first, and then measure it to check;
- remember that **100 cm = 1 m**, and use it to change metres into centimetres;
- measure **mass** in grams (g) and kilograms (kg), and remember that **1000 g = 1 kg**;
- measure **capacity** in millilitres (mL) and litres (L), and remember that **1000 mL = 1 L**;
- tell the **time** to the hour, half-hour, quarter-hour, and minute, and use a **calendar**;
- count **Jamaican money**, add up prices, and give the correct change;
- read a **thermometer** and talk about hot and cold;
- find the **area** of a flat shape by counting squares;
- find the **perimeter** by measuring all the way around a shape.

## Our Big Question

> **How can we use numbers to measure the world around us — how long, how heavy, how much, how hot, and how far?**

We will answer this big question one step at a time. Each topic in this chapter takes one kind of measuring — length, mass, capacity, time, money, temperature, area, and perimeter — and shows you the right **unit** and the right **tool** to use, with plenty of examples to practise. Put all the topics together, and you will be able to measure almost anything!

## Word Power

Here are some important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about learning them all at once. Each one is explained again carefully inside the lessons.

- **Measure** – to find out the size, amount, or how much of something there is. *Example: we measure how tall a plant has grown.*
- **Unit** – the special amount we use when we measure, such as a centimetre, a gram, or a litre. *Example: the unit we use to measure a short pencil is the centimetre.*
- **Estimate** – a careful, sensible guess made before measuring. *Example: I estimate the door is about 2 m tall, then I measure to check.*
- **Length** – how long something is from one end to the other. *Example: the length of your desk.*
- **Centimetre (cm)** – a small unit of length, about the width of your littlest fingernail. *Example: a new crayon is about 9 cm long.*
- **Metre (m)** – a larger unit of length, about the width of a doorway. *Example: a door is about 2 m tall.*
- **Mass** – how heavy something is, or how much "stuff" is in it. *Example: the mass of a bag of sugar.*
- **Gram (g)** – a small unit of mass, about as heavy as a paper clip. *Example: a spoon of salt has a mass of a few grams.*
- **Kilogram (kg)** – a larger unit of mass. *Example: a big pumpkin has a mass of about 3 kg.*
- **Capacity** – how much a container can hold when it is full. *Example: the capacity of a water bottle.*
- **Millilitre (mL)** – a small unit of capacity. *Example: a spoonful of medicine is about 5 mL.*
- **Litre (L)** – a larger unit of capacity. *Example: a carton of juice holds 1 L.*
- **Temperature** – how hot or how cold something is. *Example: the temperature on a sunny day.*
- **Area** – the amount of flat surface a shape covers. *Example: the area of the top of your table.*
- **Perimeter** – the distance all the way around the edge of a shape. *Example: the perimeter of the school yard fence.*
- **Dozen** – a group of twelve (12) things. *Example: a dozen eggs is 12 eggs.*

# Part One: Measuring Length

## What Is Length?

**Length** tells us how long something is, from one end all the way to the other end. The length of your pencil, the length of your classroom, the height of a door, and the distance from your home to school are all lengths. When we measure length, we are answering the question: *"How long is it?"* or *"How far is it?"*

To measure length neatly and fairly, everyone must use the same **units**. Long ago, people measured using their feet or their hands, but my foot and your foot are not the same size! So today we use units that are exactly the same for everyone. The two main units you will use this year are the **centimetre** and the **metre**.

## The Centimetre and the Metre

A **centimetre** is a small unit of length. We write it for short as **cm**. One centimetre is about as wide as the nail on your smallest finger. We use centimetres to measure small things, like the length of a leaf, a crayon, or a book.

A **metre** is a much bigger unit of length. We write it for short as **m**. One metre is about the width of a doorway, or about as long as a big step. We use metres to measure bigger things, like the height of a door, the length of the classroom, or the height of a tree.

> **Word Power:** When we write a measurement, the number comes first and the unit comes after, with no full stop: we write **15 cm** and **2 m**, not "15 cm." or "2 metres point."

## Choosing the Right Unit

A good mathematician always picks the unit that fits. You would not measure the length of a tiny leaf in metres — the answer would be a tiny part of a metre, and that is hard to say. And you would not measure the length of a football field in centimetres — the number would be enormous!

> **Think About It:** Which unit would you choose to measure these — centimetres or metres?
> a) The length of your pencil
> b) The height of your teacher
> c) The length of a small leaf
> d) The length of the school hall

The sensible answers are: a) centimetres, b) metres, c) centimetres, and d) metres. Small things go in centimetres; big things go in metres.

There is also an even bigger unit, called the **kilometre** (written **km**), used for very long distances, like the distance between two towns. The distance from Kingston to Ocho Rios is best measured in kilometres. You do not need to measure in kilometres yourself yet, but it is good to know the word.

## The Right Tool for the Job

To measure length, we use special tools:

- A **ruler** is a short, flat measuring tool, usually 30 cm long. It is perfect for measuring small things like a book or a pencil.
- A **metre stick** is exactly 1 m long. It is good for measuring the height of a door or a child.
- A **tape measure** is a long, bendy strip that can be rolled up. It is good for measuring longer things, and it can even bend around corners.

When you measure with a ruler, always line up the **start** of the object with the **0** mark on the ruler — not with the edge of the ruler. Then read the number at the other end.

## Estimate First, Then Measure

A clever habit is to **estimate** before you measure. To estimate means to make a careful, sensible guess. After you guess, you measure to see how close you were. This makes you better and better at judging length.

> **Let's Explore:** Look at your pencil. Estimate its length in centimetres — make your best guess. Now lay it along a ruler, line up the end with 0, and read the number. How close was your estimate?

**Worked Example 1 — Reading a ruler.**
Tara lays her crayon on a ruler. One end is at the 0 mark. The other end reaches the 9 mark.
*How long is the crayon?*
The crayon stretches from 0 to 9, so its length is **9 cm**. We write the number (9) and then the unit (cm).

## Comparing Lengths

When we **compare** lengths, we find out which is longer and which is shorter. The best way is to measure both, then look at the numbers.

**Worked Example 2 — Comparing.**
A green ribbon is 24 cm long. A blue ribbon is 30 cm long.
*Which ribbon is longer, and by how much?*
The blue ribbon is longer, because 30 is more than 24.
To find *how much* longer, we subtract:
30 cm − 24 cm = **6 cm**.
So the blue ribbon is **6 cm longer** than the green ribbon.

## How Centimetres and Metres Are Linked

Here is one of the most important facts in this whole chapter:

> **Remember:** **100 cm = 1 m.** One metre is the same length as one hundred centimetres.

This means a metre is just a tidy way of saying "100 centimetres." Knowing this lets us change metres into centimetres. Each metre is worth 100 cm, so we count in hundreds.

**Worked Example 3 — Changing metres to centimetres.**
*How many centimetres are the same as 5 metres?*
Each metre is 100 cm. So for 5 metres, we add 100 five times, or we count: 100, 200, 300, 400, 500.
5 m = 5 × 100 cm = **500 cm**.
So 5 metres is exactly the same length as 500 centimetres.

Let us look at a few more so the pattern is clear:
- 1 m = 100 cm
- 2 m = 200 cm
- 3 m = 300 cm
- 10 m = 1000 cm

> **Quick Check:** How many centimetres are the same as 4 metres? *(Count in hundreds: 100, 200, 300, 400. Answer: 400 cm.)*

> **Did You Know?** The metre stick and the ruler are like a team. If you laid 100 little centimetre marks in a row, you would have exactly one metre. That is why a metre stick has 100 small marks on it!

> **Look Around You:** Find three things in your classroom you would measure in centimetres, and three things you would measure in metres. Which group has the bigger things?

# Part Two: Measuring Mass

## What Is Mass?

**Mass** tells us how heavy something is — how much "stuff" is packed inside it. When you lift a feather, it feels light, so it has a small mass. When you lift a big stone, it feels heavy, so it has a large mass. When we measure mass, we are answering the question: *"How heavy is it?"*

We measure mass with two main units: the **gram** and the **kilogram**.

## The Gram and the Kilogram

A **gram** is a small unit of mass. We write it for short as **g**. One gram is very light — about as heavy as a paper clip, or a single raisin. We use grams to measure light things, like a spoon of salt, a letter, or a few seeds.

A **kilogram** is a much bigger unit of mass. We write it for short as **kg**. We use kilograms to measure heavier things, like a bag of rice, a pumpkin, or a baby. A bag of sugar from the shop often has a mass of 1 kg or 2 kg.

> **Think About It:** Which unit would you use — grams or kilograms?
> a) A spoon of salt
> b) A truckload of potatoes
> c) An envelope
> d) A watermelon
> Light things use grams: a) salt and c) envelope. Heavy things use kilograms: b) potatoes and d) watermelon.

## The Right Tool for Mass

To measure mass, we use a **balance** or a **scale**. A balance has two pans: you put the object in one pan and small weights in the other, and you add weights until the two pans are level. A scale has a dial or numbers that show the mass when you place an object on it. The scale at the market that weighs your yam, and the scale at the doctor that weighs you, both measure mass.

## How Grams and Kilograms Are Linked

> **Remember:** **1000 g = 1 kg.** One kilogram is the same as one thousand grams.

A kilogram is a big "bundle" of 1000 grams. So if you have 2 kg, that is 2 bundles of 1000, which is 2000 g.

**Worked Example 4 — Changing kilograms to grams.**
*A bag of flour has a mass of 2 kg. How many grams is that?*
Each kilogram is 1000 g.
2 kg = 2 × 1000 g = **2000 g**.

**Worked Example 5 — Comparing mass.**
A pawpaw has a mass of 900 g. A pineapple has a mass of 1 kg.
*Which is heavier?*
First, change the kilogram into grams so the units match: 1 kg = 1000 g.
Now compare: 1000 g is more than 900 g.
So the **pineapple is heavier** than the pawpaw, by 1000 g − 900 g = **100 g**.

> **Quick Check:** A bunch of bananas has a mass of 3 kg. How many grams is that? *(3 × 1000 = 3000 g.)*

> **Did You Know?** When you hold a 1 kg bag of sugar in your hand, you are holding exactly 1000 grams. That is a useful weight to remember, so you can guess the mass of other things by comparing them to it.

# Part Three: Measuring Capacity

## What Is Capacity?

**Capacity** tells us how much a container can hold when it is full. A small cup holds only a little, so it has a small capacity. A big bucket holds a lot, so it has a large capacity. When we measure capacity, we are answering the question: *"How much does it hold?"*

We measure capacity with two main units: the **millilitre** and the **litre**.

## The Millilitre and the Litre

A **millilitre** is a small unit of capacity. We write it for short as **mL**. A millilitre is a tiny amount. A spoonful of medicine for a baby is only about 5 mL. We use millilitres to measure small amounts of liquid.

A **litre** is a much bigger unit of capacity. We write it for short as **L**. A carton of juice or a bottle of water often holds 1 L. We use litres to measure larger amounts of liquid, like a jug of lemonade or a bucket of water.

> **Think About It:** Which is about one litre — a) a bottle of medicine for a baby, b) a bucket of water to wash a car, or c) a jug of orange juice for breakfast? The bucket holds far more than a litre, and the tiny medicine bottle holds far less. The **jug of orange juice** is about one litre.

## The Right Tool for Capacity

To measure capacity, we use a **measuring cup** or a **measuring jug** with lines and numbers marked on the side. We pour the liquid in and read the number at the top of the liquid. For very small amounts, we use a **teaspoon** or a **measuring spoon**.

## How Millilitres and Litres Are Linked

> **Remember:** **1000 mL = 1 L.** One litre is the same as one thousand millilitres.

**Worked Example 6 — Changing litres to millilitres.**
*A bottle holds 2 L of water. How many millilitres is that?*
Each litre is 1000 mL.
2 L = 2 × 1000 mL = **2000 mL**.

**Worked Example 7 — Adding capacity.**
Marcus pours 1 L of juice into a big jug. Then he adds 500 mL of water.
*How much liquid is in the jug now?*
First make the units match. 1 L = 1000 mL.
Now add: 1000 mL + 500 mL = **1500 mL**.
That is the same as **1 L and 500 mL**, or one and a half litres.

> **Quick Check:** A pot holds 3 L of soup. How many millilitres is that? *(3 × 1000 = 3000 mL.)*

> **Look Around You:** Look at bottles and cartons at home. Find the number printed on the side that tells the capacity. Can you find one that says 1 L, and one that uses mL?

# Part Four: Measuring Time

## Telling the Time on a Clock

A **clock** helps us measure **time** — it tells us what part of the day it is. A clock face has the numbers 1 to 12 around the edge, and two hands. The **short hand** points to the **hour**. The **long hand** points to the **minutes**.

The long hand travels all the way around the clock once every hour. As it goes, it passes the minute marks. There are **60 minutes in 1 hour**.

> **Remember:** **60 minutes = 1 hour.**

## Hours, Half-Hours and Quarter-Hours

Let us learn to read four important kinds of time.

**O'clock (the hour).** When the long hand points straight up to the 12, the time is an exact hour. If the short hand points to the 3, the time is **3 o'clock**.

**Half past (the half-hour).** When the long hand points straight down to the 6, half of an hour (30 minutes) has gone by. If the short hand is between 3 and 4, the time is **half past 3**, which we can also call 3:30.

**Quarter past (the quarter-hour).** A quarter of an hour is 15 minutes. When the long hand points to the 3 (one quarter of the way around), it is **quarter past** the hour. If the short hand is just past 3, the time is **quarter past 3**, or 3:15.

**Quarter to (the quarter-hour).** When the long hand points to the 9 (three quarters of the way around), there is a quarter of an hour, 15 minutes, left until the next hour. We say **quarter to**. If the long hand is on 9 and the short hand is nearly at 4, the time is **quarter to 4**, or 3:45.

**Worked Example 8 — Reading minutes past.**
The long hand points to the 4. Each number on the clock counts 5 minutes (because 60 minutes ÷ 12 numbers = 5 minutes for each number). So to reach the 4, we count: 5, 10, 15, 20. The long hand shows **20 minutes**. If the short hand is just past 7, the time is **20 minutes past 7**.

> **Quick Check:** The long hand points to the 6 and the short hand is between 8 and 9. What is the time? *(The 6 means 30 minutes, so it is half past 8.)*

## Days, Weeks, Months and the Calendar

Time is also measured in longer pieces. Here are the ones you must know:

- There are **7 days in 1 week**: Sunday, Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, Saturday.
- There are **12 months in 1 year**: January, February, March, April, May, June, July, August, September, October, November, December.
- Most months have 30 or 31 days. February is the short one, with 28 days (or 29 in a leap year).

A **calendar** is a chart that shows all the days of a month, sorted into weeks. We use it to find dates and days of the week.

> **Did You Know?** The months with 30 days are April, June, September, and November. There is an old rhyme to remember it: *"Thirty days has September, April, June, and November."* All the rest have 31, except February.

**Worked Example 9 — Using a calendar.**
Suppose a calendar shows that July 1 is a Monday.
*What is the date of the second Monday in July?*
The first Monday is July 1. The next Monday is one week (7 days) later: 1 + 7 = **July 8**.
So the second Monday in July is **July 8**.

## How Long Does It Take? (Duration)

The amount of time an activity takes, from when it starts to when it ends, is called its **duration**. We can find a duration by counting on from the start time to the end time.

**Worked Example 10 — Finding duration.**
Maths class starts at 9:00 and ends at 10:00.
*How long is the class?*
From 9:00 to 10:00 is one hour. So the class lasts **1 hour**, which is **60 minutes**.

**Worked Example 11 — Adding time.**
A bus ride takes 45 minutes. Then you walk for 15 more minutes.
*How long is the whole trip?*
Add the minutes: 45 minutes + 15 minutes = 60 minutes.
And 60 minutes = 1 hour. So the whole trip takes **1 hour**.

> **Think About It:** About how long do you think it takes to brush your teeth — a few seconds, a few minutes, or a few hours? *(A few minutes is the sensible answer.)*

# Part Five: Measuring with Money

## Jamaican Money

We use **money** to buy the things we need. In Jamaica, our money is the **Jamaican dollar**, and we write it with the sign **$**. Money comes in two kinds:

- **Coins**, made of metal, for smaller amounts: the $1, $5, $10, and $20 coins.
- **Notes**, made of paper, for larger amounts: the $50, $100, $500, $1000, and more.

When we go to the shop or the market, we use money to pay, and the seller may give us back **change** if we paid more than the price.

## Making Up Amounts

We can make the same amount of money in different ways, by putting coins and notes together.

**Worked Example 12 — Making up an amount.**
*How can you make $30 using coins?*
Here are two ways:
- One $20 coin and one $10 coin: 20 + 10 = $30.
- Three $10 coins: 10 + 10 + 10 = $30.
Both ways make exactly **$30**.

## Adding Prices

When you buy more than one thing, you **add** the prices together to find the total cost.

**Worked Example 13 — Adding prices at the shop.**
At the shop, a patty costs $80 and a box drink costs $50.
*How much do they cost altogether?*
Add the two prices: $80 + $50 = **$130**.
So the patty and the box drink cost **$130** together.

## Giving Change

If you pay with more money than the price, the seller gives you back the extra. The extra money is called **change**. To find the change, we **subtract** the price from the money paid.

**Worked Example 14 — Giving change.**
A bun costs $70. You pay with a $100 note.
*How much change should you get?*
Subtract the price from what you paid: $100 − $70 = **$30**.
So your change is **$30**.

**Worked Example 15 — A bigger shopping trip.**
You buy a loaf of bread for $250 and a tin of milk for $200. You pay with a $500 note.
*What is the total, and what is your change?*
First, add the prices to find the total: $250 + $200 = $450.
Next, find the change: $500 − $450 = **$50**.
So the total is **$450**, and your change is **$50**.

> **Quick Check:** A mango costs $40. You pay with a $50 coin. How much change do you get? *($50 − $40 = $10.)*

> **Did You Know?** Some things at the market are sold by the **dozen**. A dozen means **twelve (12)**. So a dozen eggs is 12 eggs. Eggs, oranges, and bananas are often sold by the dozen, while rice is sold by weight and cars are certainly not sold by the dozen!

# Part Six: Measuring Temperature

## Hot and Cold

**Temperature** tells us how hot or how cold something is. A cup of hot chocolate has a high temperature. A glass of iced water has a low temperature. On a sunny day in Jamaica it feels hot, and the temperature is high. On a cool, breezy morning the temperature is lower.

## Reading a Thermometer

To measure temperature exactly, we use a tool called a **thermometer**. A thermometer has numbers up the side, and a coloured line inside that rises when things get hotter and falls when things get cooler. We read the number that the top of the line reaches.

Temperature is measured in **degrees**, and we use the little sign **°C** (degrees Celsius). For example, we write a warm day as **28 °C**.

Here are some temperatures that are good to know:

- Water **freezes** into ice at **0 °C** — that is very cold.
- A comfortable, warm day is about **28 °C** to **30 °C**.
- Your body's normal temperature is about **37 °C**.
- When someone is sick with a **fever**, their body is hotter than normal.
- Water **boils** in a hot kettle at **100 °C** — that is very, very hot.

> **Think About It:** A kettle begins to boil. What is the most likely temperature of the water — 0 °C, 30 °C, or 100 °C? *(Boiling water is 100 °C. 0 °C is freezing, and 30 °C is only warm.)*

> **Look Around You:** Is today a hot day or a cool day? If your class has a thermometer, read the temperature in the morning and again at midday. Did the number go up or down?

# Part Seven: Measuring Area

## What Is Area?

**Area** is the amount of flat surface a shape covers. Think of the top of your desk, the page of your book, the classroom floor, or a window. Each of these is a flat surface, and the amount of space it covers is its area. When we measure area, we are answering the question: *"How much surface does it cover?"*

A door covers more surface than a window, so the door has a larger area. A football field covers a huge surface, so it has a very large area. The page of a book covers only a little surface, so it has a small area.

## Finding Area by Counting Squares

One easy way to find the area of a flat shape is to cover it with **squares** that are all the same size, and then **count the squares**. We use squares because they fit together neatly with no gaps. When a shape is drawn on a **grid** of equal squares, we just count how many squares it covers.

**Worked Example 16 — Counting squares.**
A rag is drawn on a grid. It covers 2 rows of squares, and each row has 4 squares.
*What is the area of the rag?*
Count all the squares: 4 in the top row and 4 in the bottom row.
4 + 4 = **8 squares**.
So the area of the rag is **8 squares**.

**Worked Example 17 — Counting another shape.**
A shape on the grid covers 3 rows, and each row has 5 squares.
*What is its area?*
Count: 5 + 5 + 5 = 15.
The area is **15 squares**.

> **Let's Explore:** On grid paper, draw a mat shaped like a rectangle that covers exactly **10 squares**. There is more than one way to do it! You could draw 2 rows of 5 squares, or 1 row of 10 squares. Count to check that you have exactly 10.

> **Did You Know?** When a shape has a bumpy or curved edge, sometimes a square is only partly covered. Then we count the squares that are mostly covered to estimate the area. But for shapes drawn neatly on a grid, we can count every full square exactly.

> **Quick Check:** A floor mat covers 4 rows of squares with 3 squares in each row. What is its area? *(3 + 3 + 3 + 3 = 12 squares.)*

# Part Eight: Measuring Perimeter

## What Is Perimeter?

The **perimeter** is the distance all the way **around** the edge of a flat shape. Imagine a tiny ant walking right around the very edge of a shape until it comes back to where it started. The whole distance the ant walks is the perimeter.

We find the perimeter by measuring the length of every side, and then **adding all the sides together**.

**Worked Example 18 — Perimeter of a rectangle.**
A garden is shaped like a rectangle. Its sides are 5 m, 3 m, 5 m, and 3 m.
*What is the perimeter?*
Add all four sides:
5 m + 3 m + 5 m + 3 m = **16 m**.
So the perimeter of the garden is **16 m**.

**Worked Example 19 — Mrs. Ham's land.**
A wall goes all the way around Mrs. Ham's land. The sides of the land are 5 m, 8 m, 7 m, and 8 m.
*How long is the wall?*
The wall is the perimeter, so we add all the sides:
5 m + 8 m + 7 m + 8 m.
Add them step by step: 5 + 8 = 13, then 13 + 7 = 20, then 20 + 8 = **28 m**.
So the wall around Mrs. Ham's land is **28 m** long.

> **Quick Check:** A square tile has four sides, each 2 cm long. What is its perimeter? *(2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 8 cm.)*

> **Remember:** **Area** is the surface a shape covers (count the squares inside). **Perimeter** is the distance around the edge (add up all the sides). They are not the same thing!

And in every topic, you learned to choose the right **unit** and the right **tool**, and to **estimate** before you measure.

## Remember

- To **measure** is to find the size or amount of something, using the same **units** for everyone.
- **Length**: use cm for small things and m for big things. **100 cm = 1 m**.
- **Mass**: use g for light things and kg for heavy things. **1000 g = 1 kg**.
- **Capacity**: use mL for small amounts and L for large amounts. **1000 mL = 1 L**.
- **Time**: **60 minutes = 1 hour**; 7 days = 1 week; 12 months = 1 year. Use a clock and a calendar.
- **Money**: add prices to find the total; subtract to find the change.
- **Temperature**: read a thermometer in degrees (°C). Water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C.
- **Area** is the surface a shape covers — count the squares. **Perimeter** is the distance around — add the sides.
- Always **estimate first**, choose the **right unit**, and pick the **right tool**.

## Review Questions

1. Name two units we use to measure length, and say which one you would use for a leaf and which for a doorway.
2. How many centimetres are the same as 7 metres?
3. A red string is 40 cm and a blue string is 55 cm. Which is longer, and by how much?
4. A bag of sugar is 1 kg. How many grams is that?
5. Which unit, grams or kilograms, would you use for a truckload of potatoes? Why?
6. A bottle of water holds 3 L. How many millilitres is that?
7. How many minutes are there in one hour? How many days are there in one week?
8. If July 1 is a Monday, what date is the second Monday in July?
9. A pencil costs $60 and an eraser costs $40. You pay with a $100 note. What is your change?
10. Is the boiling water in a kettle about 0 °C or about 100 °C?
11. A shape covers 4 rows of squares with 5 squares in each row. What is its area?
12. A rectangle has sides 6 m, 4 m, 6 m, and 4 m. What is its perimeter?

## Fun Activity

**Measuring Treasure Hunt!** With a ruler or a tape measure, go around your classroom or your home and find:

- one thing shorter than 10 cm;
- one thing about 1 metre long;
- something that holds about 1 litre;
- something with a mass of about 1 kilogram.

Before you measure each one, write down your **estimate** first. Then measure to check how close you were. The more you practise estimating, the sharper your measuring eye will become. Happy measuring, young mathematician!
