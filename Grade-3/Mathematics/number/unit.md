# Grade 3 — Mathematics — Content Standard: Number

> **Faithful curriculum extract** from the Jamaica National Standards Curriculum (NSC), Grade 3, Mathematics, Content Standard: Number. This file preserves the curriculum as written for teachers — Attainment Targets, Benchmarks, Objectives, and Illustrative Examples. The student-facing lesson is in `textbook.md`.

**Standard 1:** Students will demonstrate an understanding of numbers, types of numbers, numeration systems, and the relationship among numbers, and apply number theory concepts to compute fluently and solve problems.

---

## Attainment Target 1 — Demonstrate an understanding of the ideas of sets

### Benchmark 3.1.1 — Demonstrate understanding of groups or sets using actual objects or pictures of objects

**Objectives**
- 2.1.1 Compare the relative sizes of two or more sets of objects and tell "more", "less", "same as".
- 2.1.2 Identify sets with the same number of objects (equivalent sets).
- (NEW) Manipulate sets of objects by joining two or more unequal sets; taking out parts of a set.
- (NEW) Manipulate sets of objects by joining sets of the same number; taking out several equivalent sets.
- (NEW) Divide a set of objects into two or more equal parts.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Which of these sets has the same number of members?
2. Look at the marbles in the picture.
   - a) Put all the marbles together.
   - b) Separate the marbles in different ways: e.g. those with a hole in the middle, same shape, same colour, inside or outside the bowl.
3. Practically share 16 dog bones for 3 dogs, one at a time. After sharing, note how many each dog has. Are these equal amounts? Are there any left over?

### Benchmark 3.1.2 — Associate number with the members in a set of objects, representations of objects or symbols

**Objectives**
- 1.1.5 (Extended): Identify the number of members in a set of up to 9999 members.
- (NEW) Place up to 9999 objects into groups of tens, hundreds (tens of tens) and/or thousands (tens of hundreds) as a preliminary to writing numerals.
- (NEW) Experience larger numbers — thousands of objects (a truck load of oranges, persons at a function, a game, etc.).

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Use the number blocks or bundles of tens to show: a) 20  b) 47  c) 178.
2. What number do these number blocks show altogether?
3. Draw or find a picture of a truck full of oranges going to the market.

## Attainment Target 2 — Know the value of numerals and associate them with their names, numbers and ordinals

### Benchmark 3.1.3 — Read and write number words and numerals using the Hindu-Arabic place value system, and associate both with number of objects up to 9999 (at least)

**Objectives**
- 1.2.2 (Extended): Name numbers up to 9999 using (standard) numerals, e.g. 1259.
- 1.2.3 (Extended): Combine number words from Grade 1 and read and write up to at least nine thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine.
- 2.2.6 (Extended): Interpret a numeral for a 4-digit whole number using thousands, hundreds, tens and ones.
- 3.3.1 Read and write 3-digit and 4-digit numbers using expanded notation and simplified form of hundreds, tens and ones (e.g. 7392 = 7000 + 300 + 90 + 2).
- (NEW) Rename a 4-digit number showing more than nine hundreds, e.g. 1798 as 17 hundreds, 9 tens, 8 ones.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Write the standard numeral (e.g. 1134) for: a) Three thousand four hundred;  b) Five thousand, three hundred and sixty-two.
2. Write the number names (words) and standard numerals for a numeral shown in a Thousands / Hundreds / Tens / Ones table.
3. Write the following numbers using hundreds instead of thousands (1 thousand is 10 hundreds):
   - a) How many hundreds is the same as 2 thousand?
   - b) How many hundreds is the same as 5 thousand 3 hundreds?

### Benchmark 3.1.4 — Demonstrate an understanding of the magnitude (size) of numbers and use this to compare and to place whole numbers and/or fractional numbers in order of size, including use of the number line

**Objectives**
- 3.3.4 (Extended): Identify the greatest and least of any set of 4-digit whole numbers, at least.
- 3.3.5 Identify numbers just before or just after a given whole number.
- 2.3.3 (Extended): Place in serial order any set of numbers up to 9999 (at least).
- 2.3.4 (Extended): Name a missing number in a sequence of any set of whole numbers, counting by 1's, 10's, 100's, etc.
- (NEW) Order and compare 3- and 4-digit numbers on a number line using different scales: e.g. 1 unit = 50; 1 unit = 100; 1 unit = 500.
- 3.3.14 Place in serial order unit fractions (e.g. 1/2, 1/3, ...) and fractions having equal denominators.
- 3.3.16 Place in serial order fractions with the same numerator.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Which shows the most? 2567; 1789; 9871.
2. Put in order beginning with the least: 376, 290, 760, 237.
3. Which number comes next when counting in 100's? 1726, 1826, 1926, ___.
4. Place these numbers along the number line shown: 1250, 1300, 1200, 1350 and 1400.
5. Which fraction of the same apple is bigger: 1/4 or 1/2?

### Benchmark 3.1.5 — Demonstrate an understanding of the use of number to tell positions (ordinals) and other properties and types of number

**Objectives**
- 1.2.1 (Extended): Use ordinal numbers to 20th at least.
- (NEW) Identify and give examples of even, odd, whole, counting numbers.
- (NEW) Understand the idea of prime numbers, factors, products.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. When two numbers are multiplied the product is 16. What are the numbers called? a) fractions  b) factors  c) multiples.
2. What number is a factor of 6?

### Benchmark 3.1.6 — Use fractional numbers to name equal parts of an object or a set of objects

**Objectives**
- 3.3.17b Change whole and mixed numbers to improper fractions and vice versa.
- 2.3.30 Identify different names for a fraction (equivalent fractions).
- 3.3.18 (Modified): Generate a fraction equivalent to another fraction on denominator families of: a) 2, 3, 6, 8, 10, 12;  b) 3, 6, 9, 12;  c) 4, 8, 12. e.g. 1/2 = ?/8.
- 3.3.19 Identify a fraction in its simplest form (using equivalent fractions).

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Complete by filling in the boxes to generate equivalent fractions (the multiplication-by-1 technique):
   - a) 1/4 × (n/n) = ?/12, so 1 is the same as 4/4.
   - b) 2/3 × (n/n) = ?/(answer), so the fraction is renamed.

### Benchmark 3.1.7 — Identify the value of notes and coins in the Jamaican currency and apply these values to everyday situations such as purchases

**Attainment Target 5 — Demonstrate an understanding of the use and value of money.**

**Objectives**
- 3.5.1 (Money work begun without formal explanation at this time.)
- 3.5.2 Count change from notes/coins in common use as a cashier would in a purchasing situation.
- 3.5.3a Tell the worth of a set of coins or notes using combinations up to $500 and $1000 notes.
- (NEW) Identify Jamaican notes and coins up to $1000, and show $500 or $1000 as a combination of other notes and coins.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Exchange a $1000 note for the following: a) $100 notes;  b) $20 coins;  c) $10 coins. Tell how many of each was used.
2. What is the value of this set of notes?
3. Henry paid for a bag of oranges priced at $180 with a $500 note. Count out the change as a cashier would.
   - Step 1: Give an amount to make 200, i.e. $20 (since $180 plus $20 is $200).
   - Step 2: Count out $100 notes adding to $200 until you reach the amount used to pay ($500).
   - Step 3: The change is $320.

## Attainment Target 3 — Use the basic operations with numbers and number patterns

### Benchmark 3.1.9 — Compute with whole numbers quickly and accurately; use these skills to find answers in realistic (problem) situations

#### a) Addition — add whole numbers up to 9999 (at least)

**Objectives**
- 3.3.10a Memorize and recall addition of 2-digit numbers (without using pencil and paper).
- 3.3.11 Add whole numbers up to 4 digits (9,999) at least, in vertical or horizontal format, renaming ones, tens, hundreds — any two, or all three.
- (NEW) Solve problems that require the addition of up to 4-digit whole numbers.
- 3.3.64 Generate number patterns using addition and represent these on the addition chart.
- 3.3.47a Differentiate between addition and subtraction in problem situations (include oral response).
- 3.3.47b Differentiate between addition and multiplication in problem situations (include oral response).
- 3.3.56 Transfer data from one problem situation to another in order to solve the problem.
- 3.3.65 Lay out presentations logically and use the correct label for the answer to a problem.
- 3.3.66 Identify the data missing from a problem which makes it impossible to solve.
- 3.3.67 Identify data given which is not necessary in the solving of a problem.
- 3.3.68 Solve problems which require the use of addition and subtraction in the same problem.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. What number when added to 320 will make 500?
   - a) Use the inverse of addition: 320 + ? = 500; so 500 − 320 = 180.
   - b) Counting on from 320 until you get to 500: 320 + 20 + 20 + 20 + 20 = 400; 400 + 100 = 500. The answer is 180.
2. Add 5273 + 792 + 54 (the headings TH, H, T, O may be used to help align, but students should be able to add without relying on the headings).
3. Peta's father has 870 marbles. He gives Peta 650 and gives her brother John 150. How many does he have left?

#### b) Subtraction — subtract from whole numbers up to 9999 (at least)

**Objectives**
- 3.3.6 Check answers in subtraction examples by adding.
- 3.3.10b Memorize and recall subtraction of 2-digit numbers (without using pencil and paper).
- (NEW) Subtract from up to 9999, a 1-, 2-, 3-, or 4-digit number, given in horizontal or vertical format, renaming tens, hundreds and/or thousands — any two positions or all three positions.
- (NEW) Subtract from multiples of ten, hundred, thousand or numbers with zero in any position.
- (NEW) Show how to check answers to subtraction by adding.
- 3.3.47c Differentiate between addition and subtraction in problem situations (include oral response).
- 3.3.47d Differentiate between subtraction and division in problem situations (include oral response).
- 3.3.68 Solve problems which require the use of addition and subtraction in the same problem (2 steps).

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Subtract: a) 3692 − 157;  b) 7198 − 1526;  c) 2300 − 27;  d) 6000 − 730;  e) 3865 − 498.
2. How many times can a group of 8 be subtracted from 126?
3. Can this situation be solved? What data is missing? "Joy collects bottles. She needs 50 more to exchange them for a bottle of soda. How many bottles does she now have?"
4. Do the following subtraction and check your answer by adding: 9276 − 378 = 8898; CHECK: 8898 + 378 = 9276.

#### c) Multiplication — multiply up to a 3-digit whole number by 0 through 5

**Objectives**
- 3.3.20 State, using multiplication ideas, the idea of repeated addition of the same number (e.g. moving from 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 to 4 × 3).
- 3.3.22 Use the terms "multiply", "product", and "factor" correctly.
- 3.3.23 Use an array to discover or show multiplication and division facts.
- 3.3.24 Write multiplication sentences in various ways (e.g. four 3's = 12; 4 × 3 = 12; or 12 ÷ 4 = 3).
- 3.3.25 (Modified): Discover, memorize and recall pairs of multiplication facts with 2, 3, 4, 5 or 10 as a factor.
- 3.3.27 Show the correct multiplication facts for a repeated addition (e.g. by use of a number line picture or an array).
- 3.3.30 Use arrays, number line or pictures to demonstrate multiplication facts with one factor 3.
- 3.3.31 Multiply any number by one.
- 3.3.32 Identify pairs of related multiplication facts (same product for the result).
- 3.3.33 Multiply a 2-digit number by 2, 3 or 4 without renaming.
- 3.3.34 Check by addition the answers for multiplication examples.
- 3.3.35 Find the product when one factor is zero.
- 3.3.36a (Modified): Multiply a 2-digit number (including money) by 0 to 5, renaming the partial product of ones.
- 3.3.36b Multiply a 2-digit number (including money) by a 1-digit number, renaming the partial product of ones.
- 3.3.37 Compare products and identify the greatest and lesser product.
- 3.3.38a (Modified): Multiply a 2- or 3-digit number (including money) by 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 or 5 with or without renaming the partial product of ones.
- 3.3.38b Multiply a 2-digit or 3-digit number (including zero and money) by 0 to 5 with or without renaming the partial product of ones, or tens.
- 3.3.40 Use the commutative property of multiplication.
- 3.3.41 (Modified): Multiply a 3-digit number by 0 to 5, renaming the partial product of tens.
- 3.3.49a Recall the multiplication facts with one factor (2, 3, 4, or 5) related to a given division fact using the inverse relationship.
- 3.3.54a Write pairs of multiplication facts from an array or from a given product and factors.
- 3.3.61 Assess whether or not a given response to a multiplication problem is correct.
- 3.3.62 Organize multiplication facts into a chart.
- 3.3.47e Differentiate between addition and multiplication in problem situations (include oral response).
- 3.3.47f Differentiate between multiplication and division in problem situations (include oral response).
- 3.3.57a Write, from given data, story problems using multiplication to solve them.
- (NEW, 3.3.68 Modified) Solve problems which require the use of multiplication and subtraction in the same problem (2 steps).
- (NEW, 3.3.68) Solve problems which require the use of multiplication and addition in the same problem (2 steps).

**Illustrative Examples**
1. What do these arrays show?
2. Draw an array to show the answer to 2 × 9.
3. How many of the spaces in this grid can you fill in correctly in one minute? (Use your memory.) — a multiplication grid with × across 4, 6, 9 and down 1, 2, 3, 5.
4. Show how to find the answer to 72 ÷ 3 by a) adding  b) multiplying.
5. Multiply: a) 31 × 3;  b) 300 × 3;  c) 473 × 5;  d) 136 × 2.
6. There are 23 rows of chairs with 4 chairs in each row in a room. Do you have enough chairs to seat 90 persons? Explain.
7. Each table holds 4 chairs. How many chairs are needed for 45 tables?
8. What information do you need to find the answer? "A box holds 200 packs of flashlight batteries. How many single batteries are in the box?"
9. (Challenge) Corn is planted by placing 3 seeds in each hole. A field has 5 rows with 6 holes in each row. How many seeds are planted in all?

#### d) Division — divide whole numbers by 2, 3, 4 or 5

**Objectives**
- 3.3.42 Partition objects into equivalent sets of a given number (e.g. put 32 oranges in groups of two until none remain; associate this partitioning with division).
- (NEW) Share objects into a given number of equivalent sets (e.g. place 15 mangoes in 3 groups; associate this partitioning with division).
- 3.3.44 Write division examples using the signs.
- 3.3.46 Show division as a) repeated subtraction  b) the inverse of multiplication.
- 3.3.48 Use known division facts to find unknown factors.
- 3.3.49b Recall division facts with one factor 2, 3, 4 or 5, and the related multiplication fact using the inverse relationship.
- 3.3.53 Divide any number by one.
- 3.3.54b Write pairs of division facts from an array or from a given product and factors.
- 3.3.58a Divide by 2, 3, 4 or 5 a 2- or 3-digit number where each digit is a multiple of the divisor (e.g. divide a 3-digit number whose digits are multiples of the divisor, e.g. 164 ÷ 4, 255 ÷ 5).
- 3.3.58b Divide 2-digit multiples of ten.
- (NEW) Divide numbers close to a division fact to get a remainder.
- 3.3.63 Divide numbers having up to 4 digits by 2, 3, 4 and 5 (OPTIONAL).
- 3.3.50 Use subtraction to check division examples.
- 3.3.60 Use multiplication to check (verify) the answer for a division example.
- 3.3.61b Assess whether or not a given answer to a division example is reasonable.
- 3.3.45 Solve problems which require the use of division.
- 3.3.47g Differentiate between multiplication and division in problem situations (include oral response).
- 3.3.57a Write, from given data, story problems using division to solve them.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Donna has 27 oranges. a) Circle or group the oranges so that there are 3 oranges in each group.  b) Write a number in the empty box to complete the division sentence: 27 ÷ ___.
2. Write the related division for this multiplication sentence: 7 × 5 = 35.
3. Divide (a set of division facts).
4. John was asked to divide 46 by 2; he gave an answer. Is the answer reasonable? Explain.
5. Use multiplication to find out if the answer to each division is correct: a) 35 ÷ 5 = 7;  b) 24 ÷ 4 = 10.
6. What does the remainder in a division show?
7. What is the answer including the remainder in each of these divisions? (Remember that zero is sometimes a remainder.) a) 18 ÷ 4 = ___ Rem ___;  b) 40 ÷ 5 = ___ Rem ___;  c) 245 ÷ 4 = ___ Rem ___.
8. The teacher has 48 small toy cars. She gives 5 children in the class the same number of cars. a) How many cars did she give each child?  b) How many cars will be left over? Show or tell how you got the answers.

#### e) Add and subtract amounts of money and measures

**Objectives**
- 3.3.11 Add whole numbers showing money in $ or cents (not both) up to 4 digits (9,999) at least, in vertical or horizontal format, renaming ones, tens, hundreds — any two, or all three.
- (NEW) Subtract from a whole number up to 9999, a 1-, 2-, 3-, or 4-digit number, showing money in $ or cents (not both), in horizontal or vertical format, renaming tens, hundreds and/or thousands.
- (NEW) Subtract from whole numbers showing money (in $ or cents, not both) which are multiples of ten, hundred, thousand or numbers with zero in any position.
- 3.6.8 Add or subtract simple measurements which use whole numbers of metres or centimetres.
- 3.6.10 (Measurement difference — calculating the difference.)

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Subtract: a) $609 − $350;  b) $1678 − $588;  c) $500 − $350.
2. How long is it from one end of the line to the other, using the measures given (e.g. 40 metres, 75 metres)?
3. A table measures 8 metres and a bench measures 12 metres. What is the difference between the length of the table and the length of the bench?

#### f) Multiply amounts of money and measures

**Objectives**
- 3.3.36b Multiply a 2-digit number (including money) by a 1-digit number, renaming the partial product of ones.
- 3.3.38b Multiply a 2-digit or 3-digit number (including zero and money) by 0 to 5 with or without renaming the partial product of ones, or tens.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Multiply: a) $12 × 4;  b) $230 × 5.
2. Tickets to the fair cost $50. How much will 5 tickets cost?

### Benchmark 3.1.10 — Compute with fractional numbers quickly and accurately; use these skills to find answers in realistic (problem) situations

#### a) Add and subtract fractions on like (same) denominators

**Objectives**
- 2.3.32 (Modified): Add and subtract fractions with the same denominator up to 12ths.
- (NEW) Model the addition and subtraction of fractions using fraction pieces or shading a grid.
- (NEW) Subtract a proper fraction from whole numbers less than 4.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. The grid below shows 5 equal parts. Shade 3 parts in red, shade 1 part in blue. How many parts in all are shaded? Complete: 3/5 + 1/5 = ___.
2. There are two pies. Each pie has been cut into thirds. a) How many thirds are there in all?  b) John eats one of the thirds. How many thirds are left?

### Benchmark 3.1.11 — Use approximation and estimation with numbers

#### a) Round whole numbers

**Objectives**
- 3.3.1 Round a 2-digit number to the nearest number of tens.
- (NEW) Round a 3-digit number to the nearest ten, or hundred, using a number line or counting in tens, hundreds.
- (Optional, NEW) Round a 4-digit number to the nearest hundred or thousand, using a number line or counting in tens, hundreds.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Draw a number line and use it to tell what 48 is when rounded to the nearest ten (read as: nearest multiple of ten). a) Where is 48 on the number line?  b) Which multiple of ten is it closer to: 40 or 50?
2. Which multiple of ten is closer to these numbers? a) 32  b) 56  c) 81.
3. Use the number line to show that 792 is closer to 800 than to 700, so 792 rounded to the nearest hundred is 800.

#### b) Use rounded numbers to estimate answers when computing and/or solving problems

**Objectives**
- 3.3.1 Use rounded numbers to estimate answers for addition and subtraction examples using 2-digit numbers.
- 3.3.8 Estimate and check whether or not responses for addition and subtraction are reasonable.
- 3.3.9 Use rounded numbers to estimate products.
- (NEW) Estimate quotients (answers) when dividing.
- 3.3.70 Use estimation in problem solving.

**Illustrative Examples**
1. Lenora went to the supermarket with $500. She wants to buy a bag of cornmeal for $183 and a tin of milk for $239. Estimate and tell: a) whether or not she has enough money to buy these items;  b) how much will be left?
