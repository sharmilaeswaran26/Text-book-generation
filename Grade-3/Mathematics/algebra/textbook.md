## Hello, Young Mathematician!

Do you like puzzles and mysteries? Do you enjoy spotting what comes next, or finding a clue that nobody else has noticed? If you do, then you are going to love this chapter, because algebra is full of puzzles and mysteries, and you are exactly the right person to solve them.

Algebra might sound like a big, grown-up word, but it is really about something very simple and very fun. It is about numbers that follow patterns, and it is about finding numbers that are hiding. Imagine you see this: **7 + ▢ = 12**. Some sneaky number is hiding inside that box. Your job, like a clever detective, is to find out which number it is. When you work it out, you will feel that lovely little spark of "Aha! I got it!"

In this chapter you will hunt for hidden numbers, you will read patterns and tell what comes next, you will use a box or a letter to stand for a number you do not know yet, and you will decide whether number sentences are telling the truth or telling a fib. Sharpen your pencil, switch on your thinking, and let us begin to solve some mysteries.

## Learning Goals

By the time you reach the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- Look at a number pattern, find its rule, and tell what number comes next.
- Find a missing number in a number sentence, such as **5 + ▢ = 9**.
- Use a box (▢) or a letter (like *n*) to stand for a number you do not know yet.
- Decide whether a number sentence is **true** or **false**.
- Solve "If … then …" puzzles by putting a number in place of a letter.

## Our Big Question

> **How can I find a number that is hidden, and how do I know when a number sentence is true?**

We will answer this big question one small step at a time. First we will warm up our pattern-spotting eyes. Then we will learn the detective trick for finding a hidden number. After that we will meet boxes and letters that stand for unknown numbers, we will learn to test whether a sentence is true or false, and finally we will solve "If … then …" puzzles. Each lesson gives you another tool, and by the end you will have a whole toolbox for solving number mysteries.

## Word Power

Here are some special words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about learning them all by heart right now. You will understand each one fully as you read, because every word is explained with an example.

> **Word Power:**
> - **Pattern** — a set of things that follow a rule, repeating in an orderly way. *Example:* 2, 4, 6, 8 is a pattern. The numbers keep going up by 2.
> - **Rule** — the secret instruction that tells you how a pattern is made or how to find the next number. *Example:* the rule for 5, 10, 15 is "add 5 each time."
> - **Number sentence** — a line of mathematics that uses numbers and signs, like a sentence made of maths instead of words. *Example:* 6 + 3 = 9 is a number sentence.
> - **Variable (unknown)** — a box ▢ or a letter, like *n*, that stands for a number we do not know yet. *Example:* in 4 + ▢ = 10, the box is the unknown. We must find what number it stands for.
> - **Equals (=)** — the sign that means "is the same as" or "has the same value as." *Example:* in 3 + 2 = 5, the equals sign tells us 3 + 2 is the same amount as 5.
> - **True** — a number sentence is true when both sides are equal and the sentence is correct. *Example:* 6 + 3 = 9 is true.
> - **False** — a number sentence is false when the two sides are not equal and the sentence is not correct. *Example:* 6 + 3 = 10 is false.

## Number Patterns

Let us begin with patterns, because patterns are all around you, and your eyes are already very good at spotting them.

A **pattern** is a set of numbers that follow a rule. Look at this row of numbers:

**2, 4, 6, 8, …**

Do you notice something? Each number is a little bigger than the one before it. Let us find out exactly how much bigger. From 2 to 4 we go up by 2. From 4 to 6 we go up by 2. From 6 to 8 we go up by 2 again. So the secret instruction, which we call the **rule**, is "add 2 each time." Once you know the rule, you can tell what comes next. After 8, we add 2 more, and we get **10**.

> **Let's Explore:** Count the wheels on bicycles in a row: one bike has 2 wheels, two bikes have 4 wheels, three bikes have 6 wheels. The wheels make the pattern 2, 4, 6, 8 — the same one we just learned!

Here is the wonderful thing about patterns: once you have found the rule, you can keep the pattern going as long as you like. Let us try another one.

**5, 10, 15, ▢**

First, find the rule. From 5 to 10 we add 5. From 10 to 15 we add 5. So the rule is "add 5 each time." To find the missing number, we add 5 to the last number we can see: 15 + 5 = **20**. The pattern is 5, 10, 15, **20**.

> **Let's Explore:** Think about counting money. One five-dollar coin is 5, two coins make 10, three coins make 15, four coins make 20. Counting in fives is a pattern you already use!

Patterns do not only go up. Some patterns go **down**. Look at this one:

**20, 18, 16, 14, …**

This time the numbers are getting smaller. From 20 to 18 we go down by 2. From 18 to 16 we go down by 2. So the rule is "subtract 2 each time," or "take away 2 each time." The next number is 14 − 2 = **12**.

**Here is how a detective finds the rule of any pattern, step by step:**

1. Look at the first two numbers. Are the numbers getting bigger or smaller?
2. Work out how much they change by. (Add or subtract to find the gap.)
3. Check that the same change happens between the next numbers too.
4. Use the rule to find the missing number.

**Worked Example:** Find the missing number in **3, 6, 9, ▢, 15**.

- Step 1: The numbers are getting bigger.
- Step 2: From 3 to 6 the gap is 3. From 6 to 9 the gap is 3. So the rule is "add 3 each time."
- Step 3: After 9, we add 3 to get the missing number: 9 + 3 = **12**.
- Step 4: Check it works: 12 + 3 = 15. Yes! It matches the last number.

So the missing number is **12**, and the full pattern is 3, 6, 9, **12**, 15.

> **Quick Check:** What is the rule for the pattern 10, 20, 30, 40? What number comes next? *(Answer: the rule is "add 10 each time," and the next number is 50.)*

> **Look Around You:** Patterns are everywhere — the days of the week, the tiles on a floor, the way you skip-count to 100. See how many number patterns you can spot on your way home today.

## The Missing Number

Now we come to the heart of our mystery: finding a number that is hiding. This is one of the most useful skills in all of mathematics.

Look at this number sentence:

**7 + ▢ = 12**

The box ▢ is hiding a number. We must be detectives and find out which number it is. The sentence says, "7 plus some number equals 12." So we ask ourselves a clever question: *what must I add to 7 to make 12?*

Let us think. 7 plus 1 is 8. 7 plus 2 is 9. 7 plus 3 is 10. 7 plus 4 is 11. 7 plus 5 is 12. There it is! The hidden number is **5**.

So **▢ = 5**, and the full true sentence is 7 + 5 = 12.

> **Did You Know?** There is a faster detective trick. To find a missing number that is being added, you can **subtract**. Adding and subtracting are opposites, like opening and closing a door. So for 7 + ▢ = 12, we work out 12 − 7 = 5. The hidden number is 5. Subtracting "undoes" the adding and reveals the secret!

Let us use the subtracting trick on a new puzzle.

**Worked Example:** Find the missing number in **▢ + 6 = 15**.

- The sentence says "some number plus 6 equals 15."
- Adding and subtracting are opposites, so we subtract: 15 − 6 = **9**.
- Check it: 9 + 6 = 15. Yes! The sentence is true.

So **▢ = 9**.

Missing numbers can also hide in a subtraction sentence. Let us look at one.

**Worked Example:** Find the missing number in **10 − ▢ = 4**.

- The sentence says "10 take away some number leaves 4."
- We ask: what must I take away from 10 to be left with 4?
- 10 take away 6 leaves 4, because 10 − 6 = 4.
- Check it: 10 − 6 = 4. Yes!

So **▢ = 6**.

> **Think About It:** Mishka has 12 mangoes. She gives some to her friend and has 8 left. How many did she give away? We can write this as 12 − ▢ = 8. What number is hiding in the box? *(Think: 12 − 4 = 8, so she gave away 4 mangoes.)*

Here is a friendly way to remember the detective trick:

> **Remember:** To find a missing number that is **added**, you **subtract** to undo it. To find a missing number that is **taken away** from the start, you ask "what take-away gives this answer?" Always **check** your answer by putting it back into the sentence.

> **Quick Check:** What is the missing number in 8 + ▢ = 14? *(Answer: 14 − 8 = 6, so ▢ = 6.)*

## Letters and Boxes for Unknowns

So far we have used a box, ▢, to stand for the hidden number. In algebra, we can also use a **letter** to do the very same job. A letter like *n* simply stands for a number we do not know yet. When a box or a letter stands for an unknown number, we call it a **variable**.

Why use a letter instead of a box? Because letters are easy to write and easy to talk about. Instead of saying "the number in the box," we can just say "*n*." It means exactly the same thing.

Look at these two number sentences side by side:

- **5 + ▢ = 9**
- **5 + n = 9**

They are asking the very same question: *what number, added to 5, makes 9?* Whether we call it ▢ or *n*, the answer is the same. We work out 9 − 5 = 4, so the hidden number is **4**. We can write **▢ = 4** or **n = 4**. Both are correct.

> **Did You Know?** Mathematicians often use the letter *n* for an unknown number, but any letter will do. You could use *a*, *b*, *x*, or even the first letter of your own name. The letter is just a name for the number you are looking for, like a label on a box before you open it.

Letters are very helpful when we tell a story with numbers. Let us look at a money story, because algebra is wonderful for keeping track of money.

**Worked Example:** Kemar saves $30. Then he earns some more money, which we will call *n*. Now he has $50 altogether. How much did he earn?

- We write the story as a number sentence: 30 + n = 50.
- The letter *n* stands for the money he earned.
- To find *n*, we subtract: 50 − 30 = **20**.
- Check it: 30 + 20 = 50. Yes!

So **n = 20**. Kemar earned $20.

> **Look Around You:** When you save your pocket money, you are using algebra without even knowing it. "I have $15 saved, and I need $25 for the book. How much more must I get?" That is 15 + n = 25, and the answer is n = 10.

> **Quick Check:** In the sentence n + 7 = 10, what number does *n* stand for? *(Answer: 10 − 7 = 3, so n = 3.)*

## True or False Sentences

A **number sentence** is a line of maths that uses numbers and signs, like a sentence built out of mathematics. Here are some number sentences:

- 6 + 3 = 9
- 4 + 5 = 8
- 10 − 2 = 8

But here is an important question a good mathematician always asks: *is the sentence telling the truth?* A number sentence is **true** when both sides are equal. It is **false** when the two sides are not equal.

Let us test the first one: **6 + 3 = 9**. We work out the left side: 6 + 3 = 9. The right side says 9. Both sides are 9, so they are equal. This sentence is **true**.

Now let us test another: **6 + 3 = 10**. We work out the left side: 6 + 3 = 9. But the right side says 10. Is 9 the same as 10? No, it is not. The two sides are not equal, so this sentence is **false**.

> **Did You Know?** The equals sign, =, is like a balance scale. For a sentence to be true, both sides must hold the same amount, so the scale stays level. If one side is heavier than the other, the scale tips, and the sentence is false.

**Here is how to test whether any number sentence is true or false:**

1. Work out the value of the left side.
2. Work out the value of the right side.
3. Compare them. If they are equal, the sentence is **true**. If they are not equal, the sentence is **false**.

**Worked Example:** Is **4 + 5 = 8** true or false?

- Left side: 4 + 5 = 9.
- Right side: 8.
- Is 9 the same as 8? No. So the sentence is **false**.

**Worked Example:** Is **12 − 4 = 8** true or false?

- Left side: 12 − 4 = 8.
- Right side: 8.
- Is 8 the same as 8? Yes. So the sentence is **true**.

> **Think About It:** Without working hard, can you tell that 100 + 100 = 5 is false? Of course you can — adding two big numbers could never give such a small answer. Good mathematicians use thinking like this to spot false sentences quickly.

> **Quick Check:** Is 7 + 2 = 9 true or false? Is 10 − 3 = 6 true or false? *(Answers: 7 + 2 = 9 is true; 10 − 3 = 7, not 6, so 10 − 3 = 6 is false.)*

## If-Then Puzzles

Our last mystery is the most fun of all: the "If … then …" puzzle. In these puzzles, someone tells you what a letter stands for, and you use it to finish the sentence. Telling what a letter stands for, and putting it into a sentence, is called **substituting** — it just means swapping the letter for its number.

Here is how it works. Suppose I tell you:

**If n = 40, then 90 − n = ?**

The first part tells me a secret: *n* stands for 40. So everywhere I see *n*, I can swap it for 40. The sentence 90 − n becomes 90 − 40. Now I just work it out: 90 − 40 = **50**.

So if n = 40, then 90 − n = **50**. Mystery solved!

> **Remember:** "Substitute" means swap the letter for the number you were given. Once the letter is gone and only numbers are left, you work out the answer just as you always do.

Let us try another, which shows a clever maths idea.

**Worked Example:** If n = 28, then n + n = ?

- We are told *n* stands for 28.
- So n + n becomes 28 + 28.
- Work it out: 28 + 28 = **56**.

So if n = 28, then n + n = **56**.

> **Did You Know?** Did you notice that n + n is the same as adding n two times? That is exactly the same as 2 × n! So n + n is the same as 2 × 28, which is also 56. Adding a number to itself and multiplying it by 2 give the same answer. This is how adding again and again turns into multiplying.

Let us do one more "If … then …" puzzle together, using a single value of *n* in two different sentences.

**Worked Example:** If n = 25, what is:
- a) 625 − n
- b) 135 − n

We are told *n* stands for 25, so we swap *n* for 25 in each part.

- a) 625 − n becomes 625 − 25 = **600**.
- b) 135 − n becomes 135 − 25 = **110**.

So when n = 25, we get 625 − n = **600** and 135 − n = **110**.

> **Quick Check:** If n = 10, then n + n = ? *(Answer: 10 + 10 = 20.)*

## Practice Together

Let us solve a few mysteries side by side. Read each one, try it in your head or on paper, and then check the worked answer.

**1. Continue the pattern.** What number comes next in 4, 8, 12, 16, ▢?
The rule is "add 4 each time." The next number is 16 + 4 = **20**.

**2. Find the missing number.** 9 + ▢ = 16.
Subtract to undo the adding: 16 − 9 = **7**. Check: 9 + 7 = 16. True!

**3. Use a letter.** In n + 5 = 12, what is *n*?
Subtract: 12 − 5 = **7**. So n = 7. Check: 7 + 5 = 12. True!

**4. True or false?** Is 8 + 8 = 16 true or false?
Left side: 8 + 8 = 16. Right side: 16. They are equal, so it is **true**.

**5. If … then …** If n = 30, then 70 − n = ?
Swap *n* for 30: 70 − 30 = **40**.

## Chapter Summary

In this chapter, you became a number detective and learned how to solve some wonderful mathematical mysteries.

You learned that a **pattern** is a set of numbers that follow a **rule**, and that once you find the rule you can tell what number comes next. You learned the detective trick for finding a **missing number** in a number sentence, and that adding and subtracting are opposites that can undo each other. You discovered that a box ▢ or a letter like *n* can stand for an unknown number, and we call this a **variable**. You learned to test whether a **number sentence** is **true** or **false** by working out both sides and comparing them. And finally, you solved "If … then …" puzzles by **substituting** a number for a letter. Most importantly, you learned to always check your answers, just as a careful detective checks every clue.

## Remember

- A **pattern** follows a **rule**, like "add 2 each time" or "subtract 5 each time." Find the rule, then you can find the next number.
- To find a missing number that is **added**, you **subtract** to undo it. Adding and subtracting are opposites.
- A box ▢ or a letter like *n* is a **variable** — it stands for a number you do not know yet.
- A number sentence is **true** when both sides are equal, and **false** when they are not.
- For an "If … then …" puzzle, **substitute** (swap) the letter for its number, then work out the answer.
- Always **check** your answer by putting it back into the sentence.

## Fun Activity

**The Hidden Number Game.** Play this with a partner or with your family.

1. One player thinks of a secret number and keeps it hidden. This is the unknown.
2. That player writes a number sentence with a box for the secret number, such as **▢ + 4 = 11**.
3. The other player is the detective and must find the hidden number. (Here, 11 − 4 = 7, so the secret number is 7.)
4. Check together by putting the number back into the sentence: 7 + 4 = 11. True!
5. Now swap roles and play again. Try using a letter like *n* instead of a box, and try making patterns for each other to continue.

The player who solves the most mysteries is the Number Detective Champion. Have fun, and keep your detective eyes sharp!
