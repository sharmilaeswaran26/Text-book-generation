Speaking & Listening

Long before children could read or write, people shared poems out loud. A poem is meant to be heard, not only seen. When a poem is read aloud, its words seem to dance — and that is why listening to poems is such a treat. In this strand you will learn to listen to poems, talk about how they make you feel, and speak clearly so that everyone understands you.

Listening to a Poem

When you listen to a poem, listen with your whole self. Sit still, keep your ears open, and let the words paint pictures in your mind. A good listener notices the rhythm — the beat the words make, a little like the beat of a drum or a clap. A good listener also notices the rhyming words, the ones that end with the same sound, like cat and hat, or sun and fun.

Read these lines slowly and listen to how they sound:

The rain comes down, drip-drop, drip-drop,
It will not stop, it will not stop.
It taps the roof and fills the street,
And cools my hot and tired feet.

Did you hear the beat? Did you notice that drop rhymes with stop, and street rhymes with feet? That gentle music is one of the things that makes a poem a poem.

Did You Know?

Many of the ring games and clapping rhymes you played as a little child — like "Brown Girl in the Ring" — are really poems set to music. You have been enjoying poems your whole life without even knowing it!

Talking About How a Poem Makes You Feel

Every poem gives you a feeling. Some poems make you feel happy and want to skip. Some make you feel calm and sleepy. Some make you feel a little sad, and some make you feel excited.

After you listen to a poem, it is good to talk about it. You might say, "This poem made me feel happy, because it talked about playing in the sun." There is no wrong answer, because a poem can make different people feel different things. What matters is that you can say how you feel and why you feel that way.

Think About It

  • Think of a song you really love. How does it make you feel when you hear it? Can you say why?

Speaking Clearly

Whether you are sharing how a poem made you feel or reading a poem aloud, you want people to understand you. To speak clearly, remember three things. First, do not rush — speak slowly enough that every word can be heard. Second, speak loudly enough to reach the back of the room, but not so loudly that you are shouting. Third, look at the people you are speaking to, and say each word carefully, all the way to the end.

When you read a poem aloud, you can read with expression. This means letting your voice show the feeling of the poem. If the poem is happy, let your voice sound bright and cheerful. If it is sad, let your voice grow soft and slow. Reading with expression turns a plain poem into a real treat for everyone who is listening.

Let's Practice

Read the rain poem above out loud. First, read it in a flat, sleepy voice. Then read it again with lots of expression, tapping your hand softly like falling rain. Which one sounds more like a poem? Talk with a partner about how the two readings were different.

Word Skills

When you read, you will often meet a word you have never seen before. This used to feel scary, but not anymore — because you are about to become a word detective. A word detective uses clues to solve the mystery of a new word, all by themselves.

Using Context Clues

The first and best tool a word detective has is called context clues. The word context means the other words and sentences around the tricky word. Very often, those other words leave little clues that help you guess what the tricky word means.

Look at this sentence: She penned a letter to her aunt. Imagine you do not know the word penned. The clues are that she did something with a letter, and she did it to her aunt. What can you do to a letter? You can write it! So penned must mean wrote.

Here is another: The weary traveller sat down to rest after walking all day. The clues are that the traveller sat down to rest after walking all day. A person who walks all day and needs to rest must be very tired. So weary means tired.

Quick Check

  • Use context clues to guess the underlined word: The soup was so scrumptious that I asked for a second bowl. Does "scrumptious" mean tasty or horrible? How do you know?

Inflectional Endings

Sometimes a word looks tricky only because a small ending has been added to a word you already know. These small endings are called inflectional endings. The most common ones are -s, -es, -ed, and -ing.

The endings -s and -es usually mean more than one: one book becomes two books; one box becomes two boxes. The ending -ed usually tells us something already happened: today I play, but yesterday I played. The ending -ing usually tells us something is happening right now: I am playing at this very moment.

So if you meet the word jumping, just cover the -ing with your finger. Underneath, you see jump — a word you know well! Then add the ending back, and you understand the whole word.

Remember

When a word looks long and tricky, look for a small ending like -s, -es, -ed, or -ing. Cover the ending and find the smaller word you already know hiding inside.

Reading Fluently with Expression

A good reader does not only get the words right — a good reader reads fluently, which means smoothly and with feeling, so the words flow like water in a river instead of stopping and starting in bumps. Poems are wonderful for practising fluent reading, because their rhythm helps to carry you along.

Mango, mango, sweet and round,
The ripest one falls to the ground.
I pick it up and take a bite —
Yellow, juicy, pure delight!

Read it once to get the words. Then read it again, a little smoother. Then read it a third time with happy expression, as if you are truly enjoying that sweet mango.

Did You Know?

Words that pop up very often when we read — like the, and, said, they, because, and little — are called sight words. The more sight words you know at a glance, the more smoothly and quickly you can read.

Let's Practice

Read these words and find the smaller word hiding inside each one by covering the ending: walking, boxes, jumped, singing, dishes. Then use each word in a sentence of your own.

Comprehension

Now we come to one of the most delightful parts of this whole unit — reading and enjoying poems. A poem is a little box of treasures. Once you know how to open it, you will find wonderful things inside: pictures, feelings, and big ideas.

Imagery: Word-Pictures

The first treasure inside a poem is imagery. Imagery is a special word for the word-pictures a poet paints inside your mind. Good imagery makes you see, hear, smell, taste, or feel something, even though you are only reading words on a page.

The golden sun peeped over the hill,
The cool blue river was calm and still,
I heard the birds sing sweet and clear,
And smelled the roses blooming near.

Did you see the golden sun and the blue river? Did you hear the birds singing? Did you almost smell the roses? That is imagery at work. The poet did not just say "it was a nice morning" — instead, the poet used careful words so that you could picture the morning for yourself.

Let's Explore

Read these lines: The cold ice cream melted on my tongue, sweet and sticky in the hot sun. Which senses does this imagery use? Can you feel the cold, taste the sweetness, and feel the hot sun?

Mood: The Feeling of the Poem

The second treasure is the poem's mood — the feeling that the whole poem gives you. Read these two short poems and notice how different they feel:

Hooray, hooray, it's market day!
We laugh and skip along the way.
With baskets full and spirits bright,
We sing and dance with all our might.

That poem has a happy, excited mood. The words hooray, laugh, skip, and dance fill it with joy. Now read this one:

The empty house stood dark and still,
No sound was heard upon the hill.
The cold wind sighed a lonely tune,
Beneath the pale and silent moon.

This second poem has a quiet, lonely mood. The words empty, dark, cold, lonely, and silent give it a sad and still feeling. The poet chooses words carefully to create the mood, just as an artist chooses colours to paint a picture.

Quick Check

  • If a poem used words like bright, giggle, sunshine, and play, what kind of mood do you think it would have — happy or sad?

Theme: What the Poem Is About

The third treasure is the theme — what the poem is mostly about, its big idea. After you read a poem, ask yourself, "What is this poem really about?" The answer is the theme. A poem might have a theme of friendship, nature, family, or being brave.

When I fall down and hurt my knee,
My mother runs to comfort me.
She holds me close and dries my eyes,
And sings to me sweet lullabies.

What is this poem mostly about? It is about a mother caring for her child. So the theme is a mother's love, or family. The imagery and the warm mood both help us to find the theme.

Finding the Main Idea

When you read any piece of writing — not only a poem — it helps to find the main idea, the most important thing the writer wants to tell you. To find it, ask, "What is this mostly about?" Read this paragraph:

Dogs make wonderful pets. They are loyal and will stay by your side. They can be trained to do clever tricks. They also keep you safe by barking when a stranger comes near.

This paragraph is mostly about why dogs make wonderful pets — that is the main idea. The other sentences are little details that help to prove it.

Remember

To find the main idea or the theme, ask yourself one simple question: "What is this mostly about?"

Let's Practice

Read a short poem chosen by your teacher. In small groups, talk about (1) the imagery — what word-pictures you can see, hear, or feel; (2) the mood — the feeling the poem gives; and (3) the theme — what the poem is mostly about. Then share your answers with the class.

Grammar

Words are like building blocks. When you understand how they fit together, you can build sentences that are clear, interesting, and fun to read. In this strand, we will play with some clever kinds of words.

Synonyms: Words That Mean the Same

A synonym is a word that means the same, or almost the same, as another word. Synonyms let you choose just the right word, and help you avoid using the same word over and over. Instead of always saying happy, you could say glad, cheerful, or joyful.

WordSynonym (same meaning)
biglarge
smalltiny
fastquick
prettybeautiful
coldchilly

So if you write, "The food was nice," you could swap nice for the synonym delicious: "The food was delicious!"

Word Power

Synonym: a word that means the same as another word. Think "same" starts with s, just like synonym.

Antonyms: Words That Mean the Opposite

An antonym is a word that means the opposite of another word. Antonyms come in pairs and are everywhere once you start looking.

WordAntonym (opposite meaning)
bigsmall
hotcold
happysad
daynight
openclosed

Look at this sentence: The ______ man gave all his money away. What kind of man gives all his money away? A rich man! So the antonym of poor fits: The rich man gave all his money away.

Quick Check

  • Can you name the antonym of each of these words? Fast, big, happy, up.

Homonyms: Words That Sound the Same

Homonyms are words that sound the same but have a different meaning, and sometimes a different spelling. The most famous homonyms are to, too, and two:

  • two means the number 2. I have two hands.
  • too means also or very. I want to come too. The soup is too hot.
  • to shows direction and many other things. I am going to school.

Other homonyms include sea (the ocean) and see (to look) — I see the sea; and their (belonging to them) and there (a place) — Their house is over there. Because homonyms sound alike, you must think about the meaning of your sentence to choose the right one.

Did You Know?

Some words are spelled exactly the same but have two different meanings, like back (the part of your body) and back (the opposite of front). You have to look at the rest of the sentence to know which meaning is being used.

Adjectives: Describing Words

An adjective is a describing word. Adjectives tell us more about a person, place, or thing — they are like a box of bright crayons that colour in your sentences. The word mango is plain, but a sweet, juicy, yellow mango makes you almost taste it! The words sweet, juicy, and yellow are adjectives.

Adjectives can tell us about colour (red, blue), size (big, tiny), shape (round, square), or how something feels, sounds, or tastes (soft, loud, sour). We can also use adjectives to compare. To compare two things, we often add -er and use than: My mango is sweeter than yours. For longer adjectives, we use more...than: Nadine's house is more beautiful than Gary's.

Look Around You

  • Look at something near you right now — maybe your bag, a tree, or the sky. Can you think of three adjectives to describe it?

Conjunctions: Joining Words

A conjunction is a joining word — a little hook that links two ideas together so you can build longer, more interesting sentences. The three most useful conjunctions are and, but, and because.

  • and joins ideas that go together: I like rice and peas.
  • but joins ideas that are different or surprising: I wanted to play, but it was raining.
  • because tells us the reason: I was happy because it was my birthday.

Conjunctions help you join two short, choppy sentences into one smooth one. Instead of I like mangoes. I like oranges. you can write I like mangoes and oranges.

Let's Practice

Join these two sentences with the conjunction but: "The dog was small. It barked loudly." Then join these two with because: "I went to bed early. I was tired." Write your new sentences and read them aloud.

Writing

Now it is time to use everything you have learned and become a writer yourself. In this strand, you will learn to plan your work, to write a friendly letter, and to write simple poems of your own.

Planning Your Writing

Good writing rarely happens by accident. Before they write, smart writers stop to plan — to think about their ideas and get them in order. One excellent way to plan is to use a graphic organiser, a simple drawing or chart that helps you sort your ideas. You might draw a web, with your main idea in a circle in the middle and your other ideas branching out around it. Or you might simply make a list of all the things you want to say.

Remember

Plan first, write second. A few minutes of planning saves you from getting stuck later on.

Writing a Friendly Letter

A friendly letter is a warm letter you write to someone you know, such as a friend, a cousin, or a grandparent. A friendly letter has four main parts:

  1. The greeting (with the date): At the top you write the date. Then comes the greeting, the way you say hello, usually Dear and the person's name.
  2. The body: The main part, where you write your message in one or more paragraphs.
  3. The closing: A kind way to say goodbye, such as Your friend, or With love,
  4. The signature: Your name, written under the closing, so the reader knows who the letter is from.

Here is a small friendly letter that puts all four parts together. Read it and find each part:

12 Orange Street, Kingston
June 28, 2026

Dear Auntie Marie,

Thank you so much for the lovely birthday gift you sent me. The storybook is my favourite already, and I have read it twice! School is going well, and I am learning to write poems. I hope you can come and visit us soon.

Your loving niece,
Keisha

The date is at the top, the greeting is "Dear Auntie Marie," the body is the middle with all the news, the closing is "Your loving niece," and the signature is "Keisha."

Did You Know?

Friendly letters can be written for many reasons. You might write to invite someone to a party, to say thank you for a gift, or to request something you need. The parts of the letter stay the same — only your message changes.

Writing Organised Paragraphs

The body of your letter, and most of your writing, is made of paragraphs. A paragraph is a group of sentences that all talk about one main idea. A well-organised paragraph usually starts with a sentence that tells the main idea, and then the other sentences add details. Begin each new idea with a new paragraph, and use joining words like and, but, and because to link your sentences. Do not forget your full stops and commas — these little marks help your reader to understand you.

Think About It

  • Why do you think it is helpful to put all the sentences about one idea together in the same paragraph, instead of mixing everything up?

Writing Simple Poems

Now for the most exciting part — writing your very own poems! You do not even have to make it rhyme. Start by choosing your theme — what your poem will be about, such as the rain, your pet, or your favourite food. Then think of word-pictures (imagery) that help the reader see and feel your idea, and choose words that give the mood you want. If you would like it to rhyme, choose words that end with the same sound, like cat and hat.

My kite flies high up in the sky,
It dips and dances way up high.
The wind takes hold and pulls the string,
My happy kite, a lovely thing.

Notice the theme (a kite), the imagery (the kite dipping and dancing), and the happy mood. Start small, choose a theme you love, and let your words paint a picture.

Let's Practice

Write a short friendly letter to a friend or relative. Remember the four parts: the greeting (with date), the body, the closing, and your signature. Then write a simple four-line poem about something you love, and try to include some imagery so the reader can picture it.

Word Power

Poetry Words

Poem: a special kind of writing that often uses rhythm, rhyme, and word-pictures to share a feeling or idea
Rhythm: the beat or pattern of sounds in a poem, a little like the beat of a drum
Rhyme: words that end with the same sound, like cat and hat
Imagery: words that help you to see, hear, smell, taste, or feel something in your mind
Mood: the feeling that a poem gives you, such as happy, calm, or sad
Theme: what a poem is mostly about; its big idea
Expression: letting your voice show the feeling of a poem when you read it aloud

Word Skills

Context clues: the other words around a tricky word that help you guess its meaning
Inflectional ending: a small ending added to a word, such as -s, -es, -ed, or -ing
Fluently: reading smoothly and with feeling, so the words flow along
Sight words: common words we should know at a glance, like the, and, and said
Main idea: the most important thing a piece of writing is telling you

Grammar Words

Synonym: a word that means the same, or almost the same, as another word (glad / happy)
Antonym: a word that means the opposite of another word (big / small)
Homonym: a word that sounds the same as another but has a different meaning or spelling (to / too / two)
Adjective: a describing word that tells us more about a person, place, or thing (a juicy mango)
Conjunction: a joining word such as and, but, or because that links ideas together
Comparative adjective: a describing word used to compare, often ending in -er or using more...than

Writing Words

Friendly letter: a warm letter written to someone you know, such as a friend or relative
Greeting: the part of a letter that says hello, usually Dear and a name
Body: the main part of a letter, where the message is written
Closing: a kind way to say goodbye at the end of a letter, such as Your friend,
Signature: the writer's name, written at the end of a letter
Paragraph: a group of sentences that all talk about one main idea
Graphic organiser: a chart or drawing that helps a writer plan and sort ideas

Reading the rain poem: The reading with expression sounds more like a poem, because the voice shows the feeling and the rhythm of the falling rain.
