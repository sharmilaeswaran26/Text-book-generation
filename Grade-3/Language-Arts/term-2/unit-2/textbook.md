# Unit 2: The Magic of Words and Poems

**Grade 3 · Language Arts · Term 2**

---

## Hello, Young Learner!

Have you ever read a poem that made you feel happy, or perhaps a little sad? Have you ever written a letter to a friend or a grandparent, and waited eagerly for one to come back to you? Words are some of the most wonderful gifts we have. With words, we can paint pictures in another person's mind, share how we feel, and reach across the whole island of Jamaica — or even across the sea — to touch the heart of someone we love.

In this unit, you are going to become a true word explorer. You will listen to poems and talk about the feelings they give you. You will learn clever tricks to figure out tricky new words all by yourself. You will discover how a good poem can let you see, hear, and feel things just by reading it. You will play with words that mean the same and words that mean the opposite, and you will learn the proper way to write a friendly letter that someone will be delighted to receive.

By the end of this chapter, you will look at words in a brand-new way. So sharpen your pencil, open your ears, and let us begin our adventure with the magic of words and poems.

---

## Learning Goals

In this chapter you will learn to:

- Listen carefully to poems and talk about how they make you feel, speaking clearly so that others understand you.
- Use clues around a word — and the small endings added to words — to work out what new words mean.
- Read poems with expression, and understand their imagery, mood, and theme.
- Find the main idea of a piece of writing.
- Use synonyms, antonyms, and homonyms, and brighten your sentences with adjectives and conjunctions.
- Plan and write a friendly letter, and write simple poems of your own.

---

## Our Big Question

> **How do words help us share what we see and how we feel?**

Keep this question in your mind as you read. Whether you are reading a poem about the rain, figuring out a new word, or writing a letter to your best friend, you are using words to share what is inside your head and your heart with another person. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to answer this question in your very own words, with plenty of examples to prove it.

---

## Word Power

Here are some special words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about learning them all at once — each one is explained carefully in the lessons. This is just a friendly first meeting.

- **Poem** – a special kind of writing that often uses rhythm, rhyme, and word-pictures to share a feeling or idea. *Example: We read a lovely poem about the sea.*
- **Imagery** – words that help you to see, hear, smell, taste, or feel something in your mind. *Example: The poet used imagery to make me picture the golden sun.*
- **Mood** – the feeling that a poem gives you, such as happy, calm, or sad. *Example: The cheerful mood of the poem made me smile.*
- **Theme** – what a poem is mostly about; its big idea. *Example: The theme of the poem was friendship.*
- **Context clues** – the other words around a tricky word that help you guess its meaning. *Example: I used context clues to learn that "weary" means tired.*
- **Synonym** – a word that means the same, or almost the same, as another word. *Example: "Glad" is a synonym for "happy."*
- **Antonym** – a word that means the opposite of another word. *Example: "Big" is the antonym of "small."*
- **Homonym** – a word that sounds the same as another but has a different meaning or spelling. *Example: "To," "too," and "two" are homonyms.*
- **Adjective** – a describing word that tells us more about a person, place, or thing. *Example: The word "juicy" is an adjective in "a juicy mango."*
- **Conjunction** – a joining word, such as "and," "but," or "because," that links ideas together. *Example: I like rice and peas.*
- **Friendly letter** – a warm letter written to someone you know, such as a friend or relative. *Example: I wrote a friendly letter to my cousin in Montego Bay.*

# Part One: Listening to Poems and Speaking Clearly

Long before children could read or write, people shared poems out loud. A poem is meant to be heard, not only seen. When a poem is read aloud, its words seem to dance, and that is why listening to poems is such a treat.

## Listening to a Poem

When you listen to a poem, the most important thing is to listen with your whole self. Sit still, keep your ears open, and let the words paint pictures in your mind. A good listener notices the rhythm — the beat that the words make, a little like the beat of a drum or a clap. A good listener also notices the rhyming words, the ones that end with the same sound, like *cat* and *hat*, or *sun* and *fun*.

Read these lines slowly in your head, and listen to how they sound:

> *The rain comes down, drip-drop, drip-drop,*
> *It will not stop, it will not stop.*
> *It taps the roof and fills the street,*
> *And cools my hot and tired feet.*

Did you hear the beat as you read? Did you notice that *drop* rhymes with *stop*, and *street* rhymes with *feet*? That gentle music is one of the things that makes a poem a poem.

> **Did You Know?** Many of the ring games and clapping rhymes you played as a little child — like "Brown Girl in the Ring" — are really poems set to music. You have been enjoying poems your whole life without even knowing it!

## Talking About How a Poem Makes You Feel

Here is one of the best parts about poems: every poem gives you a feeling. Some poems make you feel happy and want to skip. Some make you feel calm and sleepy. Some make you feel a little sad, and some make you feel excited.

When you have listened to a poem, it is good to talk about it with others. You might say, "This poem made me feel happy, because it talked about playing in the sun." Or you might say, "This poem made me feel calm, because it was about the quiet night." There is no wrong answer, because a poem can make different people feel different things. What matters is that you can say *how* you feel and *why* you feel that way.

> **Think About It:** Think of a song you really love. How does it make you feel when you hear it? Can you say why?

## Speaking Clearly

Whether you are sharing how a poem made you feel, telling a friend about your weekend, or reading a poem aloud to the class, you want people to understand you. To speak clearly, remember three simple things. First, do not rush — speak slowly enough that every word can be heard. Second, speak loudly enough to reach the back of the room, but not so loudly that you are shouting. Third, look at the people you are speaking to, and say each word carefully, all the way to the end.

When you read a poem aloud, you can do something extra special. You can read with *expression*. This means letting your voice show the feeling of the poem. If the poem is happy, let your voice sound bright and cheerful. If it is sad, let your voice grow soft and slow. Reading with expression turns a plain poem into a real treat for everyone who is listening.

> **Let's Explore:** Read the rain poem from earlier out loud. First, read it in a flat, sleepy voice. Then read it again with lots of expression, tapping your hand softly like falling rain. Which one sounds more like a poem?

# Part Two: Becoming a Word Detective

When you read, you will often meet a word you have never seen before. This used to feel scary, but not anymore — because you are about to become a word detective. A word detective uses clues to solve the mystery of a new word, all by themselves.

## Using Context Clues

The first and best tool a word detective has is called **context clues**. The word *context* simply means the other words and sentences around the tricky word. Very often, those other words leave little clues that help you guess what the tricky word means.

Look at this sentence:

> *She penned a letter to her aunt.*

Imagine you do not know the word *penned*. Look at the clues around it: she did something with a *letter*, and she did it to her *aunt*. What can you do to a letter? You can write it! So *penned* must mean *wrote*. You solved the mystery using only the clues in the sentence — well done, detective!

Here is another one:

> *The weary traveller sat down to rest after walking all day.*

What does *weary* mean? The clues are that the traveller *sat down to rest* and had been *walking all day*. A person who walks all day and needs to rest must be very tired. So *weary* means *tired*.

> **Quick Check:** Read this sentence and use context clues to guess the meaning of the underlined word: *The soup was so scrumptious that I asked for a second bowl.* Does "scrumptious" mean tasty or horrible? How do you know?

## Inflectional Endings

Sometimes a word looks tricky only because a small ending has been added to a word you already know. These small endings are called **inflectional endings**. The most common ones are **-s**, **-es**, **-ed**, and **-ing**.

These little endings do important jobs. The endings **-s** and **-es** usually mean *more than one*. One *book* becomes two *books*. One *box* becomes two *boxes*. The ending **-ed** usually tells us that something already happened, in the past. Today I *play*, but yesterday I *played*. The ending **-ing** usually tells us something is happening right now. I am *playing* at this very moment.

So if you meet the word *jumping* and it looks new, just cover the **-ing** with your finger. Underneath, you see *jump* — a word you know well! Then add the ending back, and you understand: *jumping* means doing the action of jump right now. The same trick works for *jumps*, *jumped*, and many more.

> **Remember:** When a word looks long and tricky, look for a small ending like **-s**, **-es**, **-ed**, or **-ing**. Cover the ending and find the smaller word you already know hiding inside.

## Reading Fluently with Expression

A good reader does not only get the words right — a good reader reads *smoothly* and *with feeling*. We call this reading **fluently**. When you read fluently, the words flow along like water in a river, instead of stopping and starting in bumps.

Poems are wonderful for practising fluent reading, because their rhythm helps to carry you along. Read this little poem aloud and try to let it flow:

> *Mango, mango, sweet and round,*
> *The ripest one falls to the ground.*
> *I pick it up and take a bite —*
> *Yellow, juicy, pure delight!*

Read it once to get the words. Then read it again, a little smoother. Then read it a third time with happy expression, as if you are truly enjoying that sweet mango. Each time, your reading becomes more fluent and more fun to listen to.

> **Did You Know?** There are some words that pop up so often when we read that we should know them at a glance, without sounding them out. Words like *the*, *and*, *said*, *they*, *because*, and *little* are called **sight words**. The more sight words you know on sight, the more smoothly and quickly you can read.

# Part Three: The Joy of Reading Poems

Now we come to one of the most delightful parts of this whole unit — reading and enjoying poems. A poem is a little box of treasures. Once you know how to open it, you will find wonderful things inside: pictures, feelings, and big ideas. Let us learn how to open that box.

## Imagery: Word-Pictures

The first treasure inside a poem is **imagery**. Imagery is a special word for the word-pictures a poet paints inside your mind. Good imagery makes you *see*, *hear*, *smell*, *taste*, or *feel* something, even though you are only reading words on a page.

Read these lines and notice what happens inside your head:

> *The golden sun peeped over the hill,*
> *The cool blue river was calm and still,*
> *I heard the birds sing sweet and clear,*
> *And smelled the roses blooming near.*

Did you *see* the golden sun and the blue river? Did you *hear* the birds singing? Did you almost *smell* the roses? That is imagery at work! The poet did not just say "it was a nice morning." Instead, the poet used careful words so that you could picture the morning for yourself. That is the magic of imagery.

Imagery often speaks to your five senses: sight (what you see), hearing (what you hear), smell (what you smell), taste (what you taste), and touch (what you feel). When you read a poem, you can hunt for words that go with each of your senses.

> **Let's Explore:** Read these lines: *The cold ice cream melted on my tongue, sweet and sticky in the hot sun.* Which senses does this imagery use? Can you feel the cold, taste the sweetness, and feel the hot sun?

## Mood: The Feeling of the Poem

The second treasure inside a poem is its **mood**. The mood is the feeling that the whole poem gives you. Just as a sunny day can feel cheerful and a rainy evening can feel cosy or gloomy, every poem carries a feeling along with it.

Read these two short poems and notice how different they feel:

> *Hooray, hooray, it's market day!*
> *We laugh and skip along the way.*
> *With baskets full and spirits bright,*
> *We sing and dance with all our might.*

That first poem has a *happy* and *excited* mood, doesn't it? The words *hooray*, *laugh*, *skip*, and *dance* fill it with joy. Now read this one:

> *The empty house stood dark and still,*
> *No sound was heard upon the hill.*
> *The cold wind sighed a lonely tune,*
> *Beneath the pale and silent moon.*

This second poem has a *quiet* and *lonely* mood. The words *empty*, *dark*, *cold*, *lonely*, and *silent* give it a sad and still feeling. The poet chooses words carefully to create the mood, just like an artist chooses colours to paint a picture.

> **Quick Check:** If a poem used words like *bright*, *giggle*, *sunshine*, and *play*, what kind of mood do you think it would have — happy or sad?

## Theme: What the Poem Is About

The third treasure is the **theme**. The theme is what the poem is mostly about — its big idea. After you read a poem, you can ask yourself, "What is this poem really about?" The answer is the theme.

A poem might have a theme of friendship, or nature, or family, or being brave. The theme is not always written down in the poem; sometimes you have to think about it. Read this short poem:

> *When I fall down and hurt my knee,*
> *My mother runs to comfort me.*
> *She holds me close and dries my eyes,*
> *And sings to me sweet lullabies.*

What is this poem mostly about? It is about a mother caring for her child. So we could say the *theme* of this poem is a mother's love, or family. The imagery (the picture of the mother holding the child) and the mood (warm and gentle) both help us to find the theme.

> **Think About It:** Imagine a poem all about playing cricket with your friends on a Saturday. What do you think the theme of that poem would be?

## Finding the Main Idea

When you read any piece of writing — not only a poem — it helps to find the **main idea**. The main idea is the most important thing the writer wants to tell you. It is like the theme, but we use the words *main idea* for stories and paragraphs, and *theme* for poems.

To find the main idea, ask yourself, "What is this mostly about?" Read this short paragraph:

> *Dogs make wonderful pets. They are loyal and will stay by your side. They can be trained to do clever tricks. They also keep you safe by barking when a stranger comes near.*

What is this paragraph mostly about? It is mostly about why dogs make wonderful pets. That is the main idea. All the other sentences — about being loyal, doing tricks, and keeping you safe — are little details that help to prove the main idea.

> **Remember:** To find the main idea or the theme, ask yourself one simple question: "What is this mostly about?"

# Part Four: Playing With Words — Grammar and Conventions

Words are like building blocks. When you understand how they fit together, you can build sentences that are clear, interesting, and fun to read. In this part, we will play with some clever kinds of words.

## Synonyms: Words That Mean the Same

A **synonym** is a word that means the same, or almost the same, as another word. Synonyms are very useful, because they let you choose just the right word, and they help you avoid using the same word over and over.

For example, instead of always saying *happy*, you could say *glad*, *cheerful*, or *joyful* — these are all synonyms for *happy*. Here are some more pairs of synonyms:

- *big* and *large*
- *small* and *tiny*
- *fast* and *quick*
- *pretty* and *beautiful*
- *cold* and *chilly*

So if you write, "The food was nice," you could swap *nice* for the synonym *delicious*, and your sentence becomes much tastier: "The food was delicious!"

> **Word Power:** A **synonym** is a word that means the same as another word. Think "**s**ame" starts with **s**, just like **s**ynonym.

## Antonyms: Words That Mean the Opposite

An **antonym** is the very opposite. An antonym is a word that means the *opposite* of another word. Antonyms come in pairs, and they are everywhere once you start looking. Here are some:

- *big* and *small*
- *hot* and *cold*
- *up* and *down*
- *happy* and *sad*
- *day* and *night*
- *open* and *closed*

Look at this sentence with a blank in it: *The ______ man gave all his money away.* What kind of man gives all his money away? Not a poor man — a *rich* man! So the antonym of *poor* fits perfectly: *The rich man gave all his money away.*

> **Quick Check:** Can you name the antonym of each of these words? *Fast, big, happy, up.* (The answers are at the end of this section in your mind — try them first!)

## Homonyms: Words That Sound the Same

Now here is a tricky and fun group of words. **Homonyms** are words that *sound the same* but have a different meaning, and sometimes a different spelling. They can fool you if you are not careful!

The most famous homonyms are these three little words: **to**, **too**, and **two**. They all sound exactly the same, but watch how they mean very different things:

- **two** means the number 2. *I have two hands.*
- **too** means *also* or *very*. *I want to come too. The soup is too hot.*
- **to** is used in many ways, such as showing direction. *I am going to school.*

Here are some other homonyms to know:

- **sea** (the ocean) and **see** (to look). *I see the sea.*
- **their** (belonging to them) and **there** (a place). *Their house is over there.*
- **night** (when it is dark) and **knight** (a soldier of long ago).

Because homonyms sound alike, you must think about the *meaning* of your sentence to choose the right one.

> **Did You Know?** Some words are spelled the very same way but have two different meanings, like *back* (the part of your body) and *back* (the opposite of front). You have to look at the rest of the sentence to know which meaning is being used.

## Adjectives: Describing Words

An **adjective** is a describing word. Adjectives tell us more about a person, a place, or a thing. They are like a box of bright crayons that you use to colour in your sentences and make them come alive.

Think about the word *mango*. That is just a plain thing. But now add some adjectives: *a sweet, juicy, yellow mango*. Suddenly you can almost taste it! The words *sweet*, *juicy*, and *yellow* are all adjectives that describe the mango.

Adjectives can tell us about colour (*red*, *blue*), size (*big*, *tiny*), shape (*round*, *square*), or how something feels, sounds, or tastes (*soft*, *loud*, *sour*). Here are some plain nouns brightened up with adjectives:

- a *dog* → a *friendly, brown dog*
- a *day* → a *hot, sunny day*
- a *house* → a *tall, wooden house*

Sometimes we use adjectives to *compare* two things. When we compare, we often add **-er** to the adjective and use the word *than*. For example: *My mango is sweeter than yours.* For longer adjectives, we use the words *more...than* instead. For example: *Nadine's house is more beautiful than Gary's.*

> **Look Around You:** Look at something near you right now — maybe your bag, a tree, or the sky. Can you think of three adjectives to describe it?

## Conjunctions: Joining Words

A **conjunction** is a joining word. Conjunctions are like little hooks that link two ideas together so that you can build longer, more interesting sentences. The three most useful conjunctions are **and**, **but**, and **because**.

The conjunction **and** joins two ideas that go together: *I like rice and peas.* / *We sang and danced at the party.*

The conjunction **but** joins two ideas that are different or surprising: *I wanted to play, but it was raining.* / *The bag was heavy, but I carried it home.*

The conjunction **because** tells us the *reason* for something: *I was happy because it was my birthday.* / *She ran fast because she did not want to be late.*

Look how conjunctions help you join two short, choppy sentences into one smooth one. Instead of writing two small sentences — *I like mangoes. I like oranges.* — you can join them with a conjunction: *I like mangoes and oranges.* Much smoother!

> **Let's Explore:** Join these two sentences with the conjunction *but*: "The dog was small. It barked loudly." Then join these two with *because*: "I went to bed early. I was tired."

# Part Five: Becoming a Writer

Now it is time to use everything you have learned and become a writer yourself. In this part, you will learn to write a friendly letter and to write simple poems of your own. Before we write, though, every good writer makes a plan.

## Planning Your Writing

Good writing rarely happens by accident. Before they write, smart writers stop to *plan*. Planning means thinking about your ideas and getting them in order before you begin. One excellent way to plan is to use a **graphic organiser** — a simple drawing or chart that helps you sort your ideas.

You might draw a web, with your main idea in a circle in the middle and your other ideas branching out around it like the legs of a spider. Or you might simply make a list of all the things you want to say. Once your ideas are planned out, the writing becomes much easier, because you already know what you want to say.

> **Remember:** Plan first, write second. A few minutes of planning saves you from getting stuck later on.

## Writing a Friendly Letter

A **friendly letter** is a warm letter that you write to someone you know, such as a friend, a cousin, or a grandparent. A friendly letter is not just any jumble of words — it has special parts, and each part has its own place. There are four main parts to remember.

The first part is the **greeting** (along with the **date**). At the top, you write the date, so the reader knows when you wrote. Then comes the greeting, which is the way you say hello. A friendly letter usually starts with *Dear* and the person's name, like *Dear Grandma,*.

The second part is the **body**. This is the main part of the letter, where you write your message. The body is made up of one or more paragraphs, telling your news, asking questions, or sharing how you feel.

The third part is the **closing**. This is a kind way to say goodbye at the end, such as *Your friend,* or *With love,* or *Yours truly,*.

The fourth part is the **signature**. This is simply your name, written under the closing, so the reader knows who the letter is from.

Here is a small friendly letter that puts all four parts together. Read it and find each part:

> 12 Orange Street, Kingston
> June 28, 2026
>
> Dear Auntie Marie,
>
> Thank you so much for the lovely birthday gift you sent me. The storybook is my favourite already, and I have read it twice! School is going well, and I am learning to write poems. I hope you can come and visit us soon.
>
> Your loving niece,
> Keisha

Did you find all four parts? The *date* is at the top. The *greeting* is "Dear Auntie Marie." The *body* is the middle part with all the news. The *closing* is "Your loving niece," and the *signature* is "Keisha." When your friendly letter has all four parts, it is complete and ready to be sent.

> **Did You Know?** Friendly letters can be written for many reasons. You might write to *invite* someone to a party, to say *thank you* for a gift, or to *request* something you need. The parts of the letter stay the same — only your message changes.

## Writing Organised Paragraphs

The body of your letter, and indeed most of your writing, is made up of **paragraphs**. A paragraph is a group of sentences that all talk about one main idea. A well-organised paragraph usually starts with a sentence that tells the main idea, and then the other sentences add details about that idea.

To keep your writing neat and clear, remember to begin each new idea with a new paragraph, and to use joining words like *and*, *but*, and *because* to link your sentences smoothly. Do not forget your full stops at the end of sentences and your commas where you pause. These little marks help your reader to understand you.

> **Think About It:** Why do you think it is helpful to put all the sentences about one idea together in the same paragraph, instead of mixing everything up?

## Writing Simple Poems

Now for the most exciting part — writing your very own poems! You have already learned about imagery, mood, and theme, and now you get to use them yourself.

Writing a poem is simpler than you might think. You do not even have to make it rhyme if you do not want to. Start by choosing your theme — what your poem will be about. It could be the rain, your pet, your favourite food, or your family. Then think of word-pictures (imagery) that help the reader see and feel your idea. Choose words that give the feeling, or mood, you want.

If you would like your poem to rhyme, you can choose words that end with the same sound, like *cat* and *hat*, or *tree* and *bee*. Here is a simple poem a Grade 3 student might write about a kite:

> *My kite flies high up in the sky,*
> *It dips and dances way up high.*
> *The wind takes hold and pulls the string,*
> *My happy kite, a lovely thing.*

Notice the theme (a kite), the imagery (the kite dipping and dancing in the sky), and the happy mood. You can do this too! Start small, choose a theme you love, and let your words paint a picture.

> **Let's Explore:** Think of one thing you love — maybe the beach, a pet, or a special food. Say one sentence out loud that paints a word-picture of it. You have just made the first line of a poem!

## Practice Together

Let us practise some of what we have learned. Try these with a partner or with your class.

1. **Poem feelings.** Listen as someone reads a short poem aloud. Then take turns saying how the poem made you feel, and why.
2. **Word detective.** Read this sentence and use context clues to find the meaning of the underlined word: *The tiny puppy was timid and hid behind its mother.* Does *timid* mean brave or shy?
3. **Synonym and antonym swap.** For the word *happy*, say one synonym (a word that means the same) and one antonym (a word that means the opposite).
4. **Homonym choice.** Choose the correct homonym: *I want to come (to / too / two) the party.*
5. **Adjective hunt.** Look at a picture in your book. Find three adjectives to describe what you see.
6. **Join the sentences.** Use a conjunction to join these two sentences into one: *I was hungry. I ate a banana.*

## Try It Yourself

Here are some things to try on your own. Take your time and have fun.

1. Write a short friendly letter to a friend or a relative. Remember the four parts: the greeting (with the date), the body, the closing, and your signature.
2. Write a simple poem of four lines about something you love. Try to include some imagery so the reader can picture it.
3. Make a list of five pairs of antonyms (opposite words). Then make a list of five pairs of synonyms (same-meaning words).
4. Read a short poem aloud three times. Each time, try to read more smoothly and with more expression than before.
