# Unit 1: Word Detectives and Story Tellers

**Grade 3 · Language Arts · Term 2**

---

## Hello, Young Learner!

Have you ever played a guessing game with a friend? Maybe your friend held a sweet behind their back and gave you little clues until you guessed what it was. Reading and writing are full of games just like that. Every day, words give you clues, sounds, and little surprises, and your job is to be a clever detective who works them out.

Imagine you are sitting under a big mango tree with your friends. One of you is telling a story about a goat that got loose in the market. Everyone is listening closely. When the story is finished, someone asks, "But what happened first?" and you all try to remember the events in the right order. That is language at work — listening, speaking, remembering, and sharing.

In this unit, you are going to become a word detective and a story teller. You will learn special sounds that two letters make when they glide together. You will discover words that mean the same thing and words that mean the opposite. You will find out how to tell a story in the right order, how to find the most important idea in what you read, and how to write your very own paragraph. By the time you reach the last page, words will feel like good friends who share their secrets with you. Let us begin!

---

## Learning Goals

In this unit you will learn to listen carefully and speak clearly so that others understand you, and to retell a story by putting its events in the correct order. You will learn to hear and read special vowel sounds called diphthongs, and to use the clues around a word to work out what it means. You will learn to find the main idea in a story and the details that support it. You will learn about naming words called nouns, about words that mean the same and words that mean the opposite, about words that sound alike but are different, and about how to talk about things that happened in the past. Finally, you will learn to plan and write a neat, organised paragraph of your very own.

## Our Big Question

**How do words and stories help us understand each other?**

As you read, keep this question in your mind. You will answer it bit by bit — by learning how to speak and listen well, how to read words like a detective, how to find the meaning in a story, how to use words correctly, and how to write your ideas so that other people can enjoy them too.

## Word Power

Here are some important words you will meet in this unit. Do not worry about learning them all at once. Each one is explained gently inside the lessons, so you will understand them as you read.

- **Sequence** – to put things in the right order, from first to last. *We put the pictures in sequence to tell the story.*
- **Diphthong** – two vowel sounds that glide together into one sliding sound, like the *oi* in *coin*. *The word "cow" has a diphthong.*
- **Context clue** – a hint in the words around a hard word that helps you guess its meaning. *I used a context clue to work out the new word.*
- **Main idea** – the most important point that a story or paragraph is about. *The main idea of the story is helping others.*
- **Detail** – a small piece of information that tells us more about the main idea. *One detail is that the boy shared his lunch.*
- **Noun** – a naming word for a person, place, animal, or thing. *Teacher, school, dog, and book are all nouns.*
- **Synonym** – a word that means the same, or nearly the same, as another word. *"Big" and "large" are synonyms.*
- **Antonym** – a word that means the opposite of another word. *"Hot" and "cold" are antonyms.*
- **Homophone** – a word that sounds the same as another word but has a different meaning and spelling. *"Sea" and "see" are homophones.*
- **Past tense** – the form of a word we use to talk about something that already happened. *Yesterday I walked to school.*
- **Paragraph** – a group of sentences about one main idea. *I wrote a paragraph about my pet.*

# Part One: Speaking and Listening — Sharing Our Words

Before we ever read or write, we speak and we listen. Talking and listening are the very first ways we share our ideas with one another. In this first part, you will learn to listen with care, to speak so people understand you, and to tell a story in just the right order.

## Listening with Your Whole Self

Listening is more than just hearing. You hear many sounds all day — birds, cars, the school bell — but listening means really paying attention to what someone is saying so that you understand it.

A good listener does a few simple things. You look at the person who is speaking. You keep your mouth quiet and your ears open. You think about the words you hear. And you wait until the speaker is finished before you talk.

Imagine your teacher says, "Take out your blue book, turn to page ten, and underline the first word." If you are listening carefully, you can follow all three steps in the right order. But if your mind was wandering, you might only catch one part and get muddled. That is why good listening matters so much — it helps you follow instructions and learn new things.

> **Quick Check:** Name two things a good listener does. Which one do you find easiest to do?

## Speaking So Others Understand

When it is your turn to speak, your job is to help your listeners understand you. You do this by speaking clearly, not too fast and not too soft. You look at the people you are talking to. And you stay on your topic, so your listeners do not get lost.

Speaking clearly is a useful skill in many places. You might ask a question in class, give a vote of thanks at a school programme, or explain a game to a friend. Each time, the clearer you speak, the better people understand.

Here is something fun you can try with a partner. One of you draws a simple picture in secret — perhaps a house with a tree beside it. Then, without showing the picture, you give your partner careful instructions: "Draw a square in the middle. Put a triangle on top for the roof." Your partner draws only what you say. When you are done, compare the two pictures. If they look alike, you gave clear instructions and your partner listened well!

> **Let's Explore:** Think of a game you love to play. How would you explain its rules to someone who has never played it before? Practise saying it out loud.

## Asking and Answering Questions

Good talkers are also good question-askers. When you do not understand something, asking a question is a smart and brave thing to do. And when someone asks you a question, answering it kindly helps everyone learn.

Picture your class looking at a safety sign that shows a person slipping on a wet floor. One student might ask, "What does this sign warn us about?" Another answers, "It warns us that the floor is wet and slippery, so we must walk carefully." By asking and answering, the whole class understands the sign better than before.

> **Think About It:** What is a good question you could ask about a "No Smoking" sign? What might the answer be?

## Telling a Story in Order

One of the most important speaking skills is retelling a story by putting its events in **sequence**. The word *sequence* means the right order, from what happened first to what happened last.

Think about getting ready for school in the morning. First you wake up. Next you brush your teeth. Then you get dressed. After that you eat your breakfast. Finally you walk to school. These events happen in a sequence. If you mixed them up — eating breakfast before you woke up — the story would not make sense!

Stories work the same way. When you retell a story, words like **first**, **next**, **then**, **after that**, and **finally** are your helpers. They show your listeners the order of events.

Let us try it with a short story. *A little girl named Asha planted a seed. First, she dug a small hole in the soil. Next, she dropped in the seed and covered it. Then, every day, she watered it. After many days, a tiny green shoot pushed up. Finally, the plant grew tall and bloomed into a bright flower.*

Notice how the order words guide us from the beginning all the way to the end. That is good sequencing, and it makes a story easy to follow.

> **Let's Explore:** Retell what you did after you came home from school yesterday. Use the words *first, next, then,* and *finally* to keep your events in order.

> **Remember:** Listening carefully, speaking clearly, and telling events in the right order all help others understand you.

# Part Two: Word Recognition and Fluency — Becoming a Word Detective

Now we move from talking to reading words. Some words are easy to read at a glance, but others hold little puzzles inside them. In this part, you will learn special vowel sounds, you will learn how word endings change meaning, and you will learn the detective trick of using clues to work out new words.

## Diphthongs: When Two Vowels Glide Together

Say the word *coin* slowly. Listen to the middle sound: *oi*. Did you notice how your mouth seems to slide from one sound into another, all in one breath? That sliding sound is called a **diphthong**.

A diphthong happens when two vowel sounds glide together to make one smooth, sliding sound. Your mouth begins in one shape and slides into another. It is a little like a slide in the playground — you start at the top and glide to the bottom in one swoop.

Let us listen to some diphthongs you already use every day.

The **oi** and **oy** sound is in words like *coin*, *oil*, *soil*, *boy*, and *toy*. Say *boy* and *oil* — can you hear your mouth slide?

The **ou** and **ow** sound is in words like *cloud*, *mouth*, *house*, *cow*, *owl*, and *town*. Say *cloud* and *cow* slowly and feel that sliding sound.

> **Word Power:** A **diphthong** is two vowel sounds that glide together into one sliding sound, like the *ou* in *mouth* or the *oy* in *toy*.

Here is a fun way to practise. Read these silly sentences aloud and listen for the diphthongs: *The boy found a coin in the soil.* *A brown owl sat on the house and looked at the cloud.* Every time your mouth makes that sliding sound, you have found a diphthong!

> **Quick Check:** Which words in this list have a diphthong — *cat, cow, sun, oil, town*? (Hint: listen for the sliding sound.)

## Inflectional Endings: Little Endings That Change Words

Sometimes we add a small ending to the end of a word. These small endings are called **inflectional endings**, and they can change the meaning of a word in a helpful way.

Look at the word *play*. Now watch what happens when we add endings:
- *play* becomes *plays* — meaning one person does it now.
- *play* becomes *playing* — meaning it is happening right now.
- *play* becomes *played* — meaning it already happened.

The endings **-s**, **-ing**, and **-ed** are doing important work. They tell us *when* something happens and *how many* people are doing it. When you read a hard word, look for these endings. If you cover the ending with your finger, you might find a smaller word you already know hiding underneath. For example, in *jumping*, cover *-ing* and you find *jump*!

> **Did You Know?** When you see a long word like *walking* or *helped*, you can often find a shorter, easier word inside it. Spotting that little word helps you read the big one.

## Context Clues: Reading Like a Detective

What do you do when you are reading and you meet a word you do not know? You do not have to give up. A good word detective looks for **context clues** — hints hidden in the other words around the hard word.

Read this sentence: *The hungry dog gobbled up its food in seconds.* Suppose you did not know the word *gobbled*. Look at the clues: the dog was *hungry*, and it ate its food *in seconds*. Those clues help you guess that *gobbled* means to eat quickly and greedily. You solved the puzzle without even using a dictionary!

Here is another: *The path was so narrow that we had to walk one behind the other.* If *narrow* is new to you, the clue "walk one behind the other" tells you the path must be very thin. So *narrow* means thin or not wide.

> **Think About It:** Read this sentence: *The weather was so humid that our clothes felt damp and sticky.* What clues help you guess what *humid* means?

The clues around a word are like little arrows all pointing to the meaning. The more you practise looking for them, the better a reader you become. And of course, if you are still unsure, you can always check the meaning in a dictionary.

## Reading Smoothly and Knowing Your Sight Words

Some words appear so often that we should know them on sight, without sounding them out. We call these **sight words**. Words like *the*, *said*, *come*, *because*, *would*, and *friend* are sight words. When you know them instantly, your reading becomes smooth and quick, like a stream flowing over stones.

Reading smoothly is called reading with **fluency**. A fluent reader does not stop and start at every word. The reading flows, and the story comes alive. The best way to become fluent is the simplest one: read a little every day, and read your favourite parts aloud.

> **Look Around You:** Find a sign, a label, or a book near you. How many words can you read in one second, without sounding them out? Those are your sight words.

> **Remember:** A good word detective listens for diphthongs, looks for little endings, uses context clues, and reads sight words quickly to stay fluent.

# Part Three: Comprehension — Finding the Meaning

Reading words is only half the adventure. The other half is **comprehension**, which means understanding what you read. In this part, you will learn to find the most important idea in a story, to spot the details that support it, and to follow a story's events in order.

## The Main Idea

Every story and every paragraph is mostly about one big thing. That big thing is called the **main idea**. The main idea is like the trunk of a tree — it is the most important part that everything else grows from.

Read this short paragraph: *Dogs make wonderful pets. They are loyal and will protect your home. They love to play and keep you company. A dog will always be happy to see you when you come home.*

What is this paragraph mostly about? It is about dogs making wonderful pets. That is the main idea. To find the main idea, ask yourself one helpful question: "What is this mostly about?"

> **Word Power:** The **main idea** is the most important point that a story or paragraph is telling us. Ask, "What is this mostly about?" to find it.

## Details That Help

If the main idea is the tree trunk, then the **details** are the branches. Details are the smaller pieces of information that tell us more about the main idea and help to prove it.

Look again at the paragraph about dogs. The main idea is that dogs make wonderful pets. The details are the reasons that show this: they are loyal, they protect your home, they love to play, and they are happy to see you. Each detail supports the main idea, like branches supporting the leaves.

> **Quick Check:** In the dog paragraph, name one detail that tells us why dogs make wonderful pets.

## Sequencing a Story's Events

Just as you learned to retell a story in order when you speak, you must also follow events in order when you read. Putting story events in the right order is called **sequencing**. Remember the helper words: *first, next, then, after that,* and *finally*.

Read this little story and notice the order: *Marcus wanted to make a kite. First, he gathered some sticks, paper, and string. Next, he tied the sticks into a cross. Then, he glued the paper over the frame. After that, he attached a long tail. Finally, he ran across the field and watched his kite soar into the sky.*

If someone asked, "What did Marcus do right after he tied the sticks?" you could look back and answer: "He glued the paper over the frame." Following the sequence helps you understand and remember the whole story.

> **Think About It:** In the kite story, what was the very first thing Marcus did, and what was the very last thing?

## Enjoying a Poem

Not everything we read is a story. Sometimes we read **poems**. A poem uses fewer words than a story, and many poems have words at the ends of lines that rhyme, or sound alike. Poems often paint a picture in your mind and make you feel something.

Read this little poem aloud:

> *The rain comes down, pitter-patter,*
> *Splashing puddles, what a clatter!*
> *I jump and play and laugh and shout,*
> *Then run inside when sun comes out.*

Did you hear the rhyming words — *patter* and *clatter*, *shout* and *out*? Even in a short poem, you can find a main idea (a child enjoying the rain) and a sequence of events (the rain falls, the child plays, then the sun comes out). Reading poems is a lovely way to enjoy words.

> **Let's Explore:** Read the rain poem again. Which two pairs of words rhyme? How does the poem make you feel?

> **Remember:** To understand what you read, find the main idea by asking "What is this mostly about?", look for the details that support it, and follow the events in order.

# Part Four: Grammar and Conventions — Using Words Correctly

Words follow rules, just as games do. When we use these rules, our writing and speaking become clear and correct. In this part, you will learn about naming words, about words that mean the same and the opposite, about words that sound alike, and about how to talk about the past.

## Nouns: Naming Words

A **noun** is a naming word. It names a person, a place, an animal, or a thing. Almost everything around you has a name, and that name is a noun.

Look around and you will find nouns everywhere. *Teacher, doctor, mother,* and *friend* are nouns that name people. *School, market, beach,* and *Jamaica* are nouns that name places. *Dog, goat, fish,* and *bird* are nouns that name animals. *Book, pencil, table,* and *mango* are nouns that name things.

> **Word Power:** A **noun** is a naming word for a person, place, animal, or thing, such as *girl, town, cat,* or *chair*.

Here is a quick game. Read this sentence and find the nouns: *The boy gave a mango to his teacher at school.* The nouns are *boy, mango, teacher,* and *school* — they all name a person, a thing, or a place.

> **Look Around You:** Look up from this page. Can you name three things you can see? Each one is a noun. Can you also name one person and one place? Those are nouns too.

## Synonyms: Words That Mean the Same

Sometimes two different words mean the same thing, or very nearly the same thing. These are called **synonyms**. Synonyms are like two friends who agree with each other.

For example, the word *big* and the word *large* mean the same thing. So do *happy* and *glad*. So do *small* and *little*, *fast* and *quick*, and *pretty* and *beautiful*.

Why are synonyms useful? They let us choose just the right word and keep our writing interesting. Instead of saying *happy* over and over, you might say *glad*, or *cheerful*, or *joyful*.

> **Quick Check:** Can you think of a synonym for the word *cold*? (Hint: when ice cream is very cold, we might also call it...)

## Antonyms: Words That Mean the Opposite

Now meet the opposites. An **antonym** is a word that means the opposite of another word. If synonyms are friends who agree, antonyms are like two ends of a seesaw — they point in opposite directions.

Think of *hot* and *cold*. They are opposites, so they are antonyms. So are *up* and *down*, *big* and *small*, *day* and *night*, *happy* and *sad*, and *fast* and *slow*.

Antonyms help us describe the world clearly. When you say the soup is *hot*, everyone understands it is not *cold*.

> **Think About It:** What is the antonym (the opposite) of *open*? What about the opposite of *tall*?

## Homophones: Words That Sound Alike

Here is a tricky and fun group of words. Some words sound exactly the same when you say them, but they have different meanings and different spellings. These are called **homophones** (you may also hear them called homonyms).

The most famous example is the little word that sounds like "too." It can be spelled three different ways:
- **to** — as in *I am going to the shop.*
- **too** — meaning also, or very, as in *I want to come too.*
- **two** — the number, as in *I have two hands.*

All three sound the same, but each means something different! Here are more pairs that sound alike:
- **sea** (the ocean) and **see** (to look with your eyes).
- **sun** (the bright star in the sky) and **son** (a boy child).
- **here** (in this place) and **hear** (to listen with your ears).

> **Did You Know?** Because homophones sound the same, the only way to know which one to use is to think about the meaning of your sentence. Is it the *sea* full of water, or *see* with your eyes?

> **Quick Check:** Which word fits — *I want to eat ice cream, (too / two)*? Which word fits — *She has (to / two) cats*?

## Past Tense: Talking About Yesterday

When something happened in the past — yesterday, last week, or a moment ago — we use the **past tense** of a verb. A verb is an action word, like *walk* or *jump*. To talk about the past, the verb often changes.

For many verbs, we simply add **-ed** to show the past. *Walk* becomes *walked*. *Play* becomes *played*. *Jump* becomes *jumped*. *Help* becomes *helped*. So today I *walk* to school, but yesterday I *walked* to school.

But here is something to watch out for: some verbs do not follow this rule. They change in their own special way, and we just have to learn them. *Run* becomes *ran*, not "runned." *Eat* becomes *ate*. *Go* becomes *went*. *See* becomes *saw*. So today I *eat* my lunch, but yesterday I *ate* my lunch.

> **Word Power:** The **past tense** is the form of a verb we use for something that already happened. Many verbs add *-ed* (*played*), but some change in their own way (*ate, ran, went*).

Read these two sentences and feel the difference. *Today I play in the yard.* *Yesterday I played in the yard.* The little ending *-ed* tells us the second one already happened.

> **Look Around You:** Think of something you did this morning. Say it using a past tense verb, like "I *brushed* my teeth" or "I *ate* breakfast."

> **Remember:** Nouns name things; synonyms mean the same; antonyms mean the opposite; homophones sound alike but differ in meaning; and past tense verbs tell us about what already happened.

# Part Five: Writing — Putting Our Ideas on Paper

Now you get to be a story teller in writing! Writing lets you share your ideas with people you may never even meet. In this part, you will learn to plan your ideas, to write a neat paragraph, and to write events in order. You can even write a simple poem.

## Planning Before You Write

A wise writer plans before writing, just as a builder plans before building a house. Planning helps you decide what to say and in what order to say it.

One helpful planning tool is a **graphic organiser**. This is simply a chart or web that helps you gather your ideas. You might draw a big circle in the middle of your page with your topic inside it — say, "My Pet" — and then draw smaller circles around it, each holding one idea: "she is brown," "she likes to run," "she sleeps on my bed." Now your ideas are all gathered and ready to use. This gathering of ideas is sometimes called brainstorming or webbing.

> **Let's Explore:** Pick a topic you love — your family, your favourite food, or a fun day. Draw a circle, write your topic inside, and add three idea-bubbles around it.

## Writing a Paragraph

A **paragraph** is a group of sentences that all tell about one main idea. A good paragraph has three parts, and they fit together neatly.

First comes the **topic sentence**. This is the opening sentence that tells the reader what the whole paragraph will be about. For example: *My dog Brownie is the best pet in the world.*

Next come the **detail sentences**. These give more information about the topic. They prove or explain the topic sentence. For example: *She is gentle and never bites. She loves to chase a ball in the yard. Every evening she waits for me by the gate.*

Finally comes the **closing sentence**, which gently ends the paragraph. For example: *I am so happy that Brownie is my pet.*

Put together, the whole paragraph reads smoothly:

> *My dog Brownie is the best pet in the world. She is gentle and never bites. She loves to chase a ball in the yard. Every evening she waits for me by the gate. I am so happy that Brownie is my pet.*

Notice how every sentence is about Brownie. That is what makes it one good paragraph — all the sentences share one main idea.

> **Did You Know?** A paragraph is like a sandwich. The topic sentence is the top slice of bread, the details are the filling in the middle, and the closing sentence is the bottom slice that holds it all together.

## Writing Events in Order

When you write about something that happened, remember to put your events in **sequence**, just as you learned to do when speaking and reading. Use your order words — *first, next, then, after that,* and *finally* — to guide your reader from beginning to end.

Here is a short paragraph that tells events in order: *Yesterday my class made fruit salad. First, we washed our hands carefully. Next, we cut up bananas, oranges, and pawpaw. Then, we mixed all the fruit in a big bowl. After that, we shared it into small cups. Finally, we sat together and enjoyed our tasty treat.*

Because the events are in order, the reader can picture exactly what happened, step by step.

> **Think About It:** Think of something you made or did recently. What happened first, and what happened last?

## Writing a Simple Poem

Writing a poem is a playful kind of writing. A simple poem does not need to be long. Many short poems have lines that rhyme, where the last words sound alike.

Here is a tiny poem about the sea:

> *Down by the sea I love to play,*
> *Splashing in the waves all day.*
> *I build a castle in the sand,*
> *The finest castle in the land.*

Notice the rhyming pairs: *play* and *day*, *sand* and *land*. To write your own, pick a topic you enjoy, think of a few words that rhyme, and build short lines around them. There is no wrong way to enjoy a poem.

> **Let's Explore:** Try writing two rhyming lines about something you like. For example: *I have a little yellow cat, / She likes to sleep upon my mat.*

> **Remember:** Good writing starts with a plan, grows into a neat paragraph with a topic sentence, details, and a closing, and uses order words when telling events in sequence.
