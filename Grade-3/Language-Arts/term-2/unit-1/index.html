Speaking & Listening

Listening with Your Whole Self

Listening is more than just hearing. You hear many sounds all day — birds, cars, the school bell — but listening means really paying attention to what someone is saying so that you understand it.

A good listener does a few simple things. You look at the person who is speaking. You keep your mouth quiet and your ears open. You think about the words you hear. And you wait until the speaker is finished before you talk.

Imagine your teacher says, "Take out your blue book, turn to page ten, and underline the first word." If you are listening carefully, you can follow all three steps in the right order. But if your mind was wandering, you might only catch one part and get muddled. That is why good listening helps you follow instructions and learn new things.

Quick Check

Name two things a good listener does. Which one do you find easiest to do?

Speaking So Others Understand

When it is your turn to speak, your job is to help your listeners understand you. You do this by speaking clearly, not too fast and not too soft. You look at the people you are talking to. And you stay on your topic, so your listeners do not get lost.

Speaking clearly is useful in many places. You might ask a question in class, give a vote of thanks at a school programme, or explain a game to a friend. Each time, the clearer you speak, the better people understand.

Here is something fun to try with a partner. One of you draws a simple picture in secret — perhaps a house with a tree beside it. Then, without showing the picture, you give your partner careful instructions: "Draw a square in the middle. Put a triangle on top for the roof." Your partner draws only what you say. When you are done, compare the two pictures. If they look alike, you gave clear instructions and your partner listened well!

Let's Explore

Think of a game you love to play. How would you explain its rules to someone who has never played it before? Practise saying it out loud.

Asking and Answering Questions

Good talkers are also good question-askers. When you do not understand something, asking a question is a smart and brave thing to do. And when someone asks you a question, answering it kindly helps everyone learn.

Picture your class looking at a safety sign that shows a person slipping on a wet floor. One student might ask, "What does this sign warn us about?" Another answers, "It warns us that the floor is wet and slippery, so we must walk carefully." By asking and answering, the whole class understands the sign better than before.

Think About It

What is a good question you could ask about a "No Smoking" sign? What might the answer be?

Telling a Story in Order

One of the most important speaking skills is retelling a story by putting its events in sequence. The word sequence means the right order, from what happened first to what happened last.

Think about getting ready for school. First you wake up. Next you brush your teeth. Then you get dressed. After that you eat your breakfast. Finally you walk to school. These events happen in a sequence. If you mixed them up — eating breakfast before you woke up — the story would not make sense!

Stories work the same way. When you retell a story, words like first, next, then, after that, and finally are your helpers. They show your listeners the order of events. For example: A little girl named Asha planted a seed. First, she dug a small hole. Next, she dropped in the seed and covered it. Then, every day, she watered it. After many days, a tiny green shoot pushed up. Finally, the plant bloomed into a bright flower.

Remember

Listening carefully, speaking clearly, and telling events in the right order all help others understand you.

Let's Practice

Retell what you did after you came home from school yesterday. Use the words first, next, then, and finally to keep your events in order. Then tell it to a partner and see if they can picture each step.

Word Skills

Diphthongs: When Two Vowels Glide Together

Say the word coin slowly. Listen to the middle sound: oi. Did you notice how your mouth seems to slide from one sound into another, all in one breath? That sliding sound is called a diphthong.

A diphthong happens when two vowel sounds glide together to make one smooth, sliding sound. It is a little like a slide in the playground — you start at the top and glide to the bottom in one swoop.

The oi and oy sound is in words like coin, oil, soil, boy, and toy. The ou and ow sound is in words like cloud, mouth, house, cow, owl, and town. Read these aloud and listen for the slide: The boy found a coin in the soil. A brown owl sat on the house and looked at the cloud.

Word Power

Diphthong: Two vowel sounds that glide together into one sliding sound, like the ou in mouth or the oy in toy.

Quick Check

Which words have a diphthong — cat, cow, sun, oil, town? (Hint: listen for the sliding sound.)

Inflectional Endings: Little Endings That Change Words

Sometimes we add a small ending to the end of a word. These small endings are called inflectional endings, and they can change the meaning of a word.

Look at the word play. It becomes plays (one person does it now), playing (it is happening right now), and played (it already happened). The endings -s, -ing, and -ed tell us when something happens and how many people are doing it.

When you read a hard word, look for these endings. If you cover the ending with your finger, you might find a smaller word you already know hiding underneath. For example, in jumping, cover -ing and you find jump!

Did You Know?

When you see a long word like walking or helped, you can often find a shorter, easier word inside it. Spotting that little word helps you read the big one.

Context Clues: Reading Like a Detective

What do you do when you meet a word you do not know? You do not have to give up. A good word detective looks for context clues — hints hidden in the other words around the hard word.

Read this sentence: The hungry dog gobbled up its food in seconds. Suppose you did not know gobbled. The clues are that the dog was hungry and it ate in seconds. So gobbled must mean to eat quickly and greedily. You solved the puzzle without a dictionary!

Here is another: The path was so narrow that we had to walk one behind the other. The clue "walk one behind the other" tells you the path must be very thin. So narrow means thin or not wide. If you are still unsure, you can always check a dictionary.

Think About It

Read this sentence: The weather was so humid that our clothes felt damp and sticky. What clues help you guess what humid means?

Reading Smoothly and Knowing Your Sight Words

Some words appear so often that we should know them on sight, without sounding them out. We call these sight words. Words like the, said, come, because, would, and friend are sight words. When you know them instantly, your reading becomes smooth and quick.

Reading smoothly is called reading with fluency. A fluent reader does not stop and start at every word — the reading flows, and the story comes alive. The best way to become fluent is the simplest one: read a little every day, and read your favourite parts aloud.

Look Around You

Find a sign, a label, or a book near you. How many words can you read in one second, without sounding them out? Those are your sight words.

Let's Practice

Say these words aloud and clap once each time you hear a diphthong — that sliding vowel sound: boy, cat, cloud, sun, oil, town, dog, mouth. Then use the clues to guess the hard word: The tiny kitten was so timid that it hid under the bed whenever a visitor came. What do you think timid means?

Comprehension

The Main Idea

Every story and every paragraph is mostly about one big thing. That big thing is called the main idea. The main idea is like the trunk of a tree — it is the most important part that everything else grows from.

Read this paragraph: Dogs make wonderful pets. They are loyal and will protect your home. They love to play and keep you company. A dog will always be happy to see you when you come home. What is this paragraph mostly about? It is about dogs making wonderful pets. That is the main idea. To find it, ask yourself: "What is this mostly about?"

Word Power

Main idea: The most important point a story or paragraph is telling us. Ask, "What is this mostly about?" to find it.

Details That Help

If the main idea is the tree trunk, then the details are the branches. Details are the smaller pieces of information that tell us more about the main idea and help to prove it.

Look again at the paragraph about dogs. The main idea is that dogs make wonderful pets. The details are the reasons that show this: they are loyal, they protect your home, they love to play, and they are happy to see you. Each detail supports the main idea.

Quick Check

In the dog paragraph, name one detail that tells us why dogs make wonderful pets.

Sequencing a Story's Events

Just as you retell a story in order when you speak, you must follow events in order when you read. Putting story events in the right order is called sequencing. Remember the helper words: first, next, then, after that, and finally.

Read this story: Marcus wanted to make a kite. First, he gathered some sticks, paper, and string. Next, he tied the sticks into a cross. Then, he glued the paper over the frame. After that, he attached a long tail. Finally, he ran across the field and watched his kite soar into the sky. If someone asked, "What did Marcus do right after he tied the sticks?" you could answer: "He glued the paper over the frame."

Think About It

In the kite story, what was the very first thing Marcus did, and what was the very last thing?

Enjoying a Poem

Not everything we read is a story. Sometimes we read poems. A poem uses fewer words than a story, and many poems have words at the ends of lines that rhyme, or sound alike. Poems often paint a picture in your mind.

Read this poem aloud: The rain comes down, pitter-patter, / Splashing puddles, what a clatter! / I jump and play and laugh and shout, / Then run inside when sun comes out. Did you hear the rhyming words — patter and clatter, shout and out? Even a short poem has a main idea (a child enjoying the rain) and a sequence of events (the rain falls, the child plays, then the sun comes out).

Remember

To understand what you read, find the main idea by asking "What is this mostly about?", look for the details that support it, and follow the events in order.

Let's Practice

Read this paragraph and find the main idea together: The library is a special place. It is full of books on every subject. It is quiet, so you can read in peace. The librarian helps you find just what you need. What is this paragraph mostly about? Now name two details that support the main idea.

Grammar

Nouns: Naming Words

A noun is a naming word. It names a person, a place, an animal, or a thing. Almost everything around you has a name, and that name is a noun.

Teacher, doctor, mother, and friend are nouns that name people. School, market, beach, and Jamaica name places. Dog, goat, fish, and bird name animals. Book, pencil, table, and mango name things. In the sentence The boy gave a mango to his teacher at school, the nouns are boy, mango, teacher, and school.

Word Power

Noun: A naming word for a person, place, animal, or thing, such as girl, town, cat, or chair.

Look Around You

Look up from this page. Can you name three things you can see? Each one is a noun. Can you name one person and one place too?

Synonyms: Words That Mean the Same

Sometimes two different words mean the same thing, or very nearly the same. These are called synonyms. Synonyms are like two friends who agree with each other.

The word big and the word large mean the same thing. So do happy and glad, small and little, fast and quick, and pretty and beautiful. Synonyms let us choose just the right word and keep our writing interesting.

Quick Check

Can you think of a synonym for the word cold? (Hint: when ice cream is very cold, we might also call it...)

Antonyms: Words That Mean the Opposite

An antonym is a word that means the opposite of another word. If synonyms are friends who agree, antonyms are like two ends of a seesaw — they point in opposite directions.

Think of hot and cold. They are opposites, so they are antonyms. So are up and down, big and small, day and night, happy and sad, and fast and slow.

Think About It

What is the antonym (the opposite) of open? What about the opposite of tall?

Homophones: Words That Sound Alike

Some words sound exactly the same when you say them, but they have different meanings and different spellings. These are called homophones (you may also hear them called homonyms).

The most famous example sounds like "too" and is spelled three ways: to (I am going to the shop), too (meaning also, as in I want to come too), and two (the number, as in I have two hands). Here are more pairs: sea (the ocean) and see (to look); sun (the star in the sky) and son (a boy child); here (in this place) and hear (to listen).

Did You Know?

Because homophones sound the same, the only way to know which one to use is to think about the meaning of your sentence. Is it the sea full of water, or see with your eyes?

Past Tense: Talking About Yesterday

When something happened in the past, we use the past tense of a verb. A verb is an action word, like walk or jump. To talk about the past, the verb often changes.

For many verbs, we simply add -ed. Walk becomes walked, play becomes played, jump becomes jumped. But watch out: some verbs change in their own special way. Run becomes ran (not "runned"), eat becomes ate, go becomes went, and see becomes saw. So today I eat my lunch, but yesterday I ate my lunch.

Word Power

Past tense: The form of a verb we use for something that already happened. Many verbs add -ed (played), but some change in their own way (ate, ran, went).

Look Around You

Think of something you did this morning. Say it using a past tense verb, like "I brushed my teeth" or "I ate breakfast."

Let's Practice

Match each word to its synonym — big, happy, fast (choose from: quick, large, glad). Then match each word to its antonym — hot, up, day (choose from: night, cold, down). Finally, change these verbs to the past tense: play, jump, eat, run.

Writing

Planning Before You Write

A wise writer plans before writing, just as a builder plans before building a house. Planning helps you decide what to say and in what order to say it.

One helpful planning tool is a graphic organiser — simply a chart or web that helps you gather your ideas. You might draw a big circle in the middle of your page with your topic inside it — say, "My Pet" — and then draw smaller circles around it, each holding one idea: "she is brown," "she likes to run," "she sleeps on my bed." Now your ideas are gathered and ready to use. This is sometimes called brainstorming or webbing.

Let's Explore

Pick a topic you love — your family, your favourite food, or a fun day. Draw a circle, write your topic inside, and add three idea-bubbles around it.

Writing a Paragraph

A paragraph is a group of sentences that all tell about one main idea. A good paragraph has three parts. First comes the topic sentence, which tells the reader what the paragraph is about: My dog Brownie is the best pet in the world. Next come the detail sentences, which give more information: She is gentle and never bites. She loves to chase a ball in the yard. Every evening she waits for me by the gate. Finally comes the closing sentence: I am so happy that Brownie is my pet.

Put together, every sentence is about Brownie. That is what makes it one good paragraph — all the sentences share one main idea.

Did You Know?

A paragraph is like a sandwich. The topic sentence is the top slice of bread, the details are the filling, and the closing sentence is the bottom slice that holds it all together.

Writing Events in Order

When you write about something that happened, put your events in sequence, just as you do when speaking and reading. Use your order words — first, next, then, after that, and finally — to guide your reader from beginning to end.

Here is a paragraph that tells events in order: Yesterday my class made fruit salad. First, we washed our hands carefully. Next, we cut up bananas, oranges, and pawpaw. Then, we mixed all the fruit in a big bowl. After that, we shared it into small cups. Finally, we sat together and enjoyed our tasty treat.

Think About It

Think of something you made or did recently. What happened first, and what happened last?

Writing a Simple Poem

Writing a poem is a playful kind of writing. A simple poem does not need to be long. Many short poems have lines that rhyme, where the last words sound alike.

Here is a tiny poem about the sea: Down by the sea I love to play, / Splashing in the waves all day. / I build a castle in the sand, / The finest castle in the land. Notice the rhyming pairs: play and day, sand and land. To write your own, pick a topic you enjoy, think of a few words that rhyme, and build short lines around them.

Remember

Good writing starts with a plan, grows into a neat paragraph with a topic sentence, details, and a closing, and uses order words when telling events in sequence.

Let's Practice

Plan and write a short paragraph about your favourite food. First, draw a quick web of your ideas. Then begin with a topic sentence, add two or three detail sentences, and end with a closing sentence. Read it aloud to a partner when you are done.

Word Power

Important Words from This Unit

Sequence: To put things in the right order, from first to last. We put the pictures in sequence to tell the story.
Diphthong: Two vowel sounds that glide together into one sliding sound, like the oi in coin. The word "cow" has a diphthong.
Inflectional ending: A small ending added to a word, such as -s, -ing, or -ed, that changes its meaning. "Jump" becomes "jumped."
Context clue: A hint in the words around a hard word that helps you guess its meaning. I used a context clue to work out the new word.
Sight word: A word we know at a glance, without sounding it out, such as the, said, or friend.
Fluency: Reading smoothly and quickly, so the story flows. She read the page with fluency.
Main idea: The most important point a story or paragraph is about. The main idea of the story is helping others.
Detail: A small piece of information that tells us more about the main idea. One detail is that the boy shared his lunch.
Noun: A naming word for a person, place, animal, or thing. Teacher, school, dog, and book are all nouns.
Synonym: A word that means the same, or nearly the same, as another word. "Big" and "large" are synonyms.
Antonym: A word that means the opposite of another word. "Hot" and "cold" are antonyms.
Homophone: A word that sounds the same as another word but has a different meaning and spelling. "Sea" and "see" are homophones.
Past tense: The form of a verb we use to talk about something that already happened. Yesterday I walked to school.
Paragraph: A group of sentences about one main idea. I wrote a paragraph about my pet.
Topic sentence: The opening sentence of a paragraph that tells what it will be about.
Graphic organiser: A chart or web that helps you gather and arrange your ideas before writing.
