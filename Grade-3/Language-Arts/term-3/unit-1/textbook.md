# Unit 1: Word Wizards — Bringing It All Together

**Grade 3 · Language Arts · Term 3**

## Hello, Young Learner!

Can you believe how far you have come? Think back to when you first started school. Back then, the letters on a page may have looked like a row of little squiggles, and writing your own name felt like hard work. Look at you now! You can read whole stories, speak your mind in front of the class, and write down your thoughts so that other people can read them too.

This is your very last Language Arts unit in Grade 3, and it is a special one. It is like the grand finish to a long and wonderful journey. In this unit, you will not be learning everything from the very beginning. Instead, you will gather up all the clever word skills you have built over three years and bring them together, the way a builder gathers bricks to finish a fine, strong house.

You are going to listen carefully and speak clearly. You are going to crack open tricky words like a puzzle. You are going to tell the difference between something that is true for everyone and something that is just what one person feels. You are going to learn the rules that make your sentences neat and correct. And you are going to write clear, well-organised pieces that you can be proud of.

By the end of this unit, you will be a true Word Wizard, ready to step into Grade 4. So take a deep breath, sit up tall, and let us begin.

---

## Learning Goals

In this unit you will learn to:
- Listen carefully to others and speak clearly, asking and answering questions in a polite and friendly way.
- Read words with r-controlled vowels and diphthongs, and use word clues to work out the meaning of new words.
- Read smoothly and with feeling, recognising your sight words at a glance.
- Tell the difference between a fact and an opinion, and find the main idea in what you read.
- Use future tense verbs, make your subjects and verbs agree, write singular and plural nouns correctly, join ideas with conjunctions, and use punctuation marks the right way.
- Plan, write, and improve clear, well-punctuated sentences and paragraphs, and keep a journal.

## Our Big Question

Here is the big question to keep in your mind all through this unit:

**How can I use everything I have learned to read, speak, and write really well?**

You will answer this question little by little, lesson by lesson. Every time you sound out a hard word, listen kindly to a friend, spot an opinion, fix a sentence, or write a tidy paragraph, you are answering it. By the last page, you will see that all your word skills work together, just like the parts of a team, to help you understand the world and share your own ideas.

## Word Power

Here are some important words you will meet in this unit. Do not worry about learning them all at once. Each one is explained again inside the lessons.

> **Word Power:**
> - **Fact** – something that is true and can be checked, and is true for everyone. *Jamaica is an island.*
> - **Opinion** – what a person feels, thinks, or believes; others may not agree. *Mango is the best fruit.*
> - **Main idea** – what a story or paragraph is mostly about.
> - **Context clue** – a hint in the words around a new word that helps you guess its meaning.
> - **Diphthong** – two vowels that glide together to make one sliding sound, like *oi* in *coin.*
> - **R-controlled vowel** – a vowel whose sound is changed by the letter *r* next to it, like *ar* in *star.*
> - **Future tense** – a way of talking about what will happen later, using the word *will.* *I will play tomorrow.*
> - **Noun** – a naming word for a person, place, animal, or thing.
> - **Conjunction** – a joining word, such as *and, but, because,* and *so.*
> - **Punctuation** – the special marks, like the full stop and question mark, that help us read a sentence the right way.

# Part One: Speaking and Listening

Before we even open a book, let us talk about two skills you use every single day: listening and speaking. You listen to your teacher, your friends, and your family. You speak when you answer a question, share a story, or ask for help. Doing these two things well is one of the most useful skills in the whole world.

## Listening Carefully

Listening is more than just hearing. You hear birds singing in the background without really thinking about them. But listening means giving someone your full attention so that you truly understand what they are saying.

A good listener does a few simple things. You look at the person who is speaking. You keep your mouth quiet and your ears open. You think about the words you are hearing. And you wait for your turn before you speak. When you listen like this, you understand much more, and the speaker feels respected too.

Imagine your teacher is giving instructions for a fun activity. If you listen carefully, you will know exactly what to do. But if your mind wanders off to what you will eat at lunch, you might miss an important step and feel lost. Careful listening saves you from confusion.

> **Quick Check:** Name two things a good listener does with their eyes and ears.

## Speaking Clearly

When it is your turn to speak, the goal is to be understood. Speaking clearly means saying your words at a steady pace, not too fast and not in a mumble, and loudly enough for others to hear. It helps to stand or sit up tall and to look at the people you are speaking to.

In class, we practise speaking in Standard Jamaican English. This is the kind of English we use for school, for writing, and for talking to people we do not know well. At home and with friends you may speak in our warm Jamaican Creole, and that is a part of who we are. A clever speaker knows how to use the right one at the right time.

> **Think About It:** Think of a time you had to explain something to a younger child. How did you make your words easy for them to understand?

## Asking and Answering Questions Politely

A big part of speaking and listening is asking and answering questions. When a classmate finishes giving a talk, you might ask a question to learn more, like, "Why did you choose that topic?" That is called asking a *clarifying* question, because it helps make things clear.

When we ask and answer questions, we must always be polite and kind. This is called having sensitivity to your audience. You wait until the speaker is finished. You ask your question in a gentle voice. You say "please" and "thank you." And if you do not agree with someone, you can say so in a friendly way, without making them feel bad.

> **Let's Explore:** Suppose your friend says, "I think dogs make the best pets." You like cats better. A polite way to share your opinion would be: "That is a good point. I like cats best, but I understand why you love dogs." Notice how kind words keep everyone happy, even when we do not agree.

## Joining a Discussion and Giving Your Opinion

A discussion is when a group talks together about a topic, and everyone gets a chance to share. To be good at discussions, you make helpful contributions. That means you say something that adds to the talk, instead of saying something off-topic.

You can also give your opinion in a discussion, as long as you do it politely. After your group goes on a nature walk, you might say, "I think we should plant more trees in the schoolyard, because they give us shade." When you share an opinion, it is helpful to give a reason for it. That little word *because* makes your idea much stronger.

> **Remember:** Listen with kindness, speak clearly, and share your ideas politely. Everyone deserves a turn to be heard.

# Part Two: Word Recognition and Fluency

Now let us become word detectives. When you read, you meet hundreds of words. Some you know in a flash. Others are new and tricky. In this part, you will sharpen the skills that help you read every word smoothly and work out the ones you do not know.

## R-Controlled Vowels

You already know that the vowels are a, e, i, o, and u. Here is something interesting: when the letter **r** comes right after a vowel, it bosses that vowel around and changes its sound. We call these **r-controlled vowels**, and some people call the **r** a "bossy r."

There are five of them, and they are worth learning by heart:

- **ar** sounds like the *ar* in **star, car,** and **farm.**
- **er** sounds like the *er* in **fern, her,** and **water.**
- **ir** sounds like the *ir* in **girl, bird,** and **shirt.**
- **or** sounds like the *or* in **fork, corn,** and **storm.**
- **ur** sounds like the *ur* in **turn, hurt,** and **church.**

Did you notice something clever? The middle three — **er, ir,** and **ur** — all make almost the same sound, like the *er* in *her.* Say *fern, girl,* and *turn* one after the other and listen. They rhyme at the end! This is a handy thing to remember when you are reading.

> **Let's Explore:** Read these words aloud and listen for the bossy r: *market, garden, dirt, morning, burger.* Can you point to the r-controlled vowel in each one?

> **Quick Check:** Which two words have the same vowel sound: *bird, fork, turn*?

## Diphthongs

Here is a fun word: **diphthong.** It sounds funny, but the idea is simple. A diphthong is when two vowels work together and your mouth slides from one sound to another, all in one go. Say the word *coin* slowly and feel how your mouth glides. That sliding sound is a diphthong.

Two pairs of letters make a sound like the *oy* in *boy*:
- **oi** as in **coin, oil,** and **point.**
- **oy** as in **boy, toy,** and **enjoy.**

And two pairs make a sound like the *ow* in *cow*:
- **ou** as in **out, house,** and **mouse.**
- **ow** as in **cow, down,** and **flower.**

A good way to remember them is in little phrases: "the **boy** has a **coin**" and "the **cow** runs **out**."

> **Did You Know?** The word *diphthong* comes from old words meaning "two sounds." So a diphthong really is two vowel sounds gliding into one. Now you know a secret that even some grown-ups have forgotten!

## Inflectional Endings

Sometimes we add a little ending to a word to change it slightly. These endings are called **inflectional endings.** The most common ones are **-s, -ed,** and **-ing.**

Look at the base word *play.* Watch what happens when we add endings:
- *play* + **s** = **plays** (She plays every day.)
- *play* + **ed** = **played** (We played yesterday.)
- *play* + **ing** = **playing** (They are playing now.)

The base word *play* stays the same, and the ending tells us a little more. The ending **-ed** usually tells us the action already happened. The ending **-ing** usually tells us it is happening right now. When you meet a long word while reading, try covering the ending and reading the base word first. *Laughing* becomes *laugh* plus *ing.* *Smiles* becomes *smile* plus *s.* Breaking words into chunks like this makes them easy to read.

> **Quick Check:** Add **-ed** and **-ing** to the word *jump.* What two new words do you get?

## Context Clues

What do you do when you are reading and you bump into a word you have never seen before? You do not have to stop and give up. You can become a word detective and look for **context clues.** A context clue is a hint hidden in the other words around the tricky word.

Read this sentence: *The clever dog found a way to open the gate.* Suppose you did not know the word *clever.* The rest of the sentence tells you the dog was smart enough to open a gate. So *clever* must mean smart! The other words gave you a clue.

Here is another: *He was a good scholar who got straight A's.* Even if *scholar* is new to you, the words "good" and "straight A's" tell you a scholar must be a very good student. The clues are right there, waiting for you to notice them.

> **Let's Explore:** Read this sentence and guess the meaning of the underlined word from the clues: "The soup was so *scrumptious* that everyone asked for a second bowl." What do you think *scrumptious* means?

## Reading Fluently and Sight Words

Reading fluently means reading smoothly, at a comfortable pace, with feeling in your voice, the way you would speak. A fluent reader does not stop and stumble over every word. Their reading sounds like a flowing river, not a bumpy road.

How do you become fluent? One big helper is your **sight words.** Sight words are common little words like *the, said, was, come,* and *because* that pop up again and again. They appear so often that you learn to know them on sight, in a flash, without sounding them out. The more sight words you know by heart, the smoother your reading becomes, because your eyes can glide right over them.

The secret to becoming a fluent reader is simple: read often, and read aloud. The more you practise, the easier and smoother it gets, just like riding a bicycle.

> **Remember:** Use the bossy r and diphthongs to sound out words, break long words into chunks, hunt for context clues, and know your sight words by heart. These are your tools for reading anything!

# Part Three: Comprehension

Reading the words is only half the job. The other half is understanding what they mean. We call this **comprehension.** In this part, you will learn to find the main idea and, most importantly, to tell the difference between a fact and an opinion.

## Finding the Main Idea

Every story and every paragraph is mostly about one big thing. That one big thing is called the **main idea.** All the other sentences are like helpers that tell you more about it.

Think of a paragraph as a tree. The main idea is the strong trunk, and the other details are the branches that grow out from it. When you read, ask yourself, "What is this mostly about?" The answer is the main idea.

For example, read this little paragraph: *Mongoose are busy little animals. They wake up early and hunt for food all day. They eat insects, eggs, and even snakes. When they are tired, they curl up to sleep.* What is it mostly about? It is mostly about mongoose and what they do. That is the main idea. The other sentences are the details that fill it in.

> **Think About It:** When you tell a friend about a movie you saw, you usually tell them the main idea first, like "It was about a boy who could fly." Why is the main idea a good place to start?

## Fact and Opinion

This is one of the most important skills in the whole unit, so let us take our time with it.

A **fact** is something that is true and can be checked. A fact is true for everyone, no matter who they are or how they feel. For example, *Jamaica is an island* is a fact. You can check it by looking at a map, and it is true for every person in the world. Here are a few more facts:
- *A spider has eight legs.*
- *Water is wet.*
- *Kingston is the capital city of Jamaica.*

You could check each of these and find that it is true. Nobody can argue with a fact.

An **opinion** is different. An opinion is what a person feels, thinks, or believes. An opinion is not true for everyone, because different people feel different ways. For example, *Mango is the best fruit* is an opinion. You might love mango, but your friend might think otherwise. There is no way to check it and prove it for everyone. Here are a few more opinions:
- *Saturday is the most fun day of the week.*
- *Blue is a beautiful colour.*
- *Football is more exciting than cricket.*

Notice how these are all about what someone *feels.* Another person could disagree, and that would be perfectly fine. That is the big difference. A fact can be checked and is true for everyone. An opinion is what someone feels, and others may not agree.

> **Let's Explore:** Read each sentence and decide if it is a fact or an opinion. *(1) The sun is hot. (2) Summer is the nicest season. (3) A week has seven days. (4) Ice cream tastes better than cake.* Which ones can be checked, and which ones are just what someone feels?

A clue can help you spot opinions. Opinions often use feeling words like *best, nicest, prettiest, worst,* or *I think* and *I feel.* When you see those words, you are usually looking at an opinion.

> **Quick Check:** Make up one fact about your school and one opinion about your school.

## Using Context Clues for Meaning

Remember the context clues you learned about in Part Two? You use them in comprehension too. When you read a non-fiction text about a real subject, you will sometimes meet a new word. Use the words around it, and even the pictures, to help you work out what it means. Good readers are always using clues to gain meaning, page after page.

> **Remember:** Find the main idea by asking what the text is mostly about. A fact can be checked and is true for everyone. An opinion is what someone feels, and others may not agree.

# Part Four: Grammar and Conventions

Grammar is the set of rules that makes our sentences neat, clear, and correct. When you follow these rules, your reader understands you easily. In this part, you will learn about future tense, subject-verb agreement, plural nouns, conjunctions, and punctuation. Let us take them one at a time.

## The Future Tense

When you talk about something that has not happened yet, but will happen later, you are using the **future tense.** The magic word for the future tense is **will.** You simply put *will* in front of the action word.

Look how easy it is:
- *I will read a story tomorrow.*
- *We will visit Grandma on Sunday.*
- *She will play netball after school.*
- *They will plant a tree next week.*

Each of these talks about something that is going to happen in the time ahead. Whenever you make a plan or a promise about later, you reach for the word *will.*

> **Let's Explore:** Change these sentences to the future tense using *will.* "I eat lunch." → "I will eat lunch." Now try: "We sing a song." and "He cleans his room."

## Subject-Verb Agreement

Every sentence has a **subject** (the person or thing doing the action) and a **verb** (the action word). For a sentence to sound right, the subject and the verb must agree, like two friends marching in step.

Here is the rule for the present. When the subject is just one (like *he, she,* or *the dog*), the verb often takes an **-s**:
- *She walks to school.*
- *The bird sings.*

But when the subject is more than one, or is *we, you,* or *they*, the verb does not take the **-s**:
- *We walk to school.*
- *They sing.*
- *The birds sing.*

So we say *She walks* but *They walk.* We say *The cat plays* but *The cats play.* Reading your sentence aloud will often tell you if it sounds right.

> **Quick Check:** Choose the correct verb. "The boys (run / runs) in the yard." "My sister (read / reads) every night."

## Singular and Plural Nouns

A **noun** is a naming word for a person, place, animal, or thing. When there is just one, we say it is **singular.** When there is more than one, we say it is **plural.**

The simplest way to make a noun plural is to add an **-s**:
- one **cat** → two **cats**
- one **book** → three **books**
- one **dog** → many **dogs**

But the English language has a few tricks up its sleeve. When a word ends in *s, x, ch,* or *sh,* we add **-es** instead, because it is easier to say:
- one **box** → two **boxes**
- one **bus** → three **buses**
- one **brush** → many **brushes**

And here is a clever one. When a word ends in a *y* with a consonant before it, we change the *y* to *i* and add **-es**:
- one **baby** → two **babies**
- one **lady** → three **ladies**
- one **city** → many **cities**

So *baby* becomes *babies,* not "babys." Once you know these little patterns, you can make almost any noun plural.

> **Did You Know?** A few special nouns do not follow any of these rules at all. One *child* becomes two *children,* one *man* becomes two *men,* and one *foot* becomes two *feet.* These are old, old words that have kept their own special plurals for hundreds of years.

## Conjunctions: The Joining Words

**Conjunctions** are joining words. They are like little bridges that connect ideas and join short sentences into longer, smoother ones. Four of the most useful conjunctions are **and, but, because,** and **so.**

Here is how each one works:
- **and** joins two ideas that go together. *I like rice and I like peas.*
- **but** shows a difference or a surprise. *I wanted to play, but it started to rain.*
- **because** gives a reason. *I was happy because I won the race.*
- **so** shows what happened as a result. *It was raining, so I took my umbrella.*

Without conjunctions, our writing would sound choppy: *I wanted to play. It started to rain.* With a conjunction, it flows: *I wanted to play, but it started to rain.* Much nicer!

> **Let's Explore:** Join each pair of sentences with the best conjunction. "I was tired. I went to bed." (use *so*) "She is small. She is strong." (use *but*)

## Punctuation Marks

**Punctuation** marks are the small but mighty signs that tell your reader how to read a sentence. Let us meet the most important ones.

Every sentence must begin with a **capital letter.** We also use a capital letter for the names of people and places, like *Mary* and *Kingston,* and for the special word *I.*

At the end of a sentence, we use one of three marks:
- The **full stop** ( . ) goes at the end of a telling sentence. *The sky is blue.*
- The **question mark** ( ? ) goes at the end of an asking sentence. *What is your name?*
- The **exclamation mark** ( ! ) shows strong feeling, like excitement or surprise. *Watch out! Hurrah, we won!*

So the rule is easy to remember: if you are telling, use a full stop; if you are asking, use a question mark; and if you are feeling something strongly, use an exclamation mark.

> **Quick Check:** Which mark belongs at the end of each sentence? "Where are my shoes___" "I found a dollar___" (excited!) "We had rice for dinner___"

> **Remember:** Use *will* for the future, make your subjects and verbs agree, add *-s, -es,* or *-ies* to make nouns plural, join ideas with conjunctions, and end every sentence with the right punctuation mark.

# Part Five: Writing

Now comes the part where you put everything together: writing. Writing is how you share your ideas on paper so that others can read them, even when you are not there to speak. A good writer plans first, then drafts, then makes the writing better. Let us learn how.

## Writing Clear, Correct Sentences

A good piece of writing is built from good sentences, the way a wall is built from good bricks. A clear sentence tells one idea, begins with a capital letter, and ends with the right punctuation mark.

Look at the difference. A weak sentence might be: *me and tim went the shop* — it has no capital letter, no full stop, and the words are jumbled. A clear, correct sentence would be: *Tim and I went to the shop.* It begins with a capital, ends with a full stop, and the words are in good order. When you write each sentence with care, your whole piece becomes easy and pleasant to read.

> **Think About It:** Why is it important to use capital letters and full stops? Imagine reading a whole page with no stops at all. How would that feel?

## Organising a Paragraph

A **paragraph** is a group of sentences that are all about one main idea. A good paragraph usually starts with a topic sentence that tells the reader what it is about. Then come a few detail sentences that tell more. Then it ends with a closing thought.

Here is a tidy little paragraph: *My favourite place is the beach. I love to feel the warm sand under my feet. I splash in the cool blue water and look for pretty shells. The beach always makes me feel happy.* See how the first sentence tells the main idea, the middle sentences give details, and the last sentence wraps it up? That is a well-organised paragraph.

> **Look Around You:** Look at a page in any of your reading books. Notice how the writing is split into paragraphs, with each new paragraph starting on a new line. Writers do this to keep their ideas neat and easy to follow.

## Plan, Draft, and Improve

The best writers do not write a perfect piece on the very first try. Nobody does! Instead, they work in three friendly steps.

First comes **planning.** Before you write, you think about your topic and jot down your ideas. You might brainstorm a list of words, or make quick notes. This is like drawing a map before a journey.

Next comes the **draft.** Now you write your sentences and paragraphs, turning your plan into real writing. Do not worry about making it perfect yet. Just get your ideas down.

Last comes **improving,** which we also call revising and editing. Now you read over your draft and make it better. You check that each sentence is clear. You fix any capital letters, full stops, and spelling mistakes. You might add a detail to make an idea richer. This is the step that turns good writing into great writing.

> **Did You Know?** Even famous authors who write books for grown-ups go back and improve their writing many, many times before it is finished. Improving your work is not a sign that you did badly. It is a sign that you are a real writer!

## Writing in a Journal

A **journal** is your very own special notebook where you write down your thoughts, your feelings, and the things that happen to you. Writing in a journal is one of the best ways to become a stronger writer, because the more you write, the easier writing becomes.

In your journal, you can write about your day, a dream you had, something that made you laugh, or a wish for the future. There are no marks and no wrong answers in a journal. It is just for you. You might write, *Today was a good day. I will play football with my cousin on the weekend, and I cannot wait!* Notice how that journal entry even uses the future tense and an exclamation mark. Everything you have learned comes together when you write.

> **Remember:** Write clear sentences with capital letters and the right punctuation. Group your sentences into tidy paragraphs. Plan, draft, and improve. And write in your journal often to grow stronger every day.
