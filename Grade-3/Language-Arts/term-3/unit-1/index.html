Speaking and Listening

You listen and speak every single day. You listen to your teacher, your friends, and your family, and you speak when you answer a question, share a story, or ask for help. Doing these two things well is one of the most useful skills in the whole world, and in this unit you will become even better at both.

Listening Carefully

Listening is more than just hearing. You hear birds singing in the background without really thinking about them. But listening means giving someone your full attention so that you truly understand what they are saying.

A good listener looks at the person who is speaking, keeps quiet and waits for a turn, and thinks carefully about the words. Imagine your teacher is giving instructions for a fun activity. If you listen carefully, you will know exactly what to do. But if your mind wanders off, you might miss an important step and feel lost. Careful listening saves you from confusion.

Quick Check

  • Name two things a good listener does with their eyes and ears.

Speaking Clearly

When it is your turn to speak, the goal is to be understood. Speaking clearly means saying your words at a steady pace, not too fast and not in a mumble, and loudly enough for others to hear. It helps to sit up tall and to look at the people you are speaking to.

In class, we practise speaking in Standard Jamaican English, which we use for school and for talking to people we do not know well. At home and with friends you may speak in our warm Jamaican Creole, and that is a part of who we are. A clever speaker knows how to use the right one at the right time.

Think About It

  • Think of a time you had to explain something to a younger child. How did you make your words easy for them to understand?

Asking and Answering Questions Politely

When a classmate finishes giving a talk, you might ask a question to learn more, like, "Why did you choose that topic?" That is called a clarifying question, because it helps make things clear.

When we ask and answer questions, we must always be polite and kind. This is called having sensitivity to your audience. You wait until the speaker is finished, you ask your question in a gentle voice, and you remember to say "please" and "thank you." If you do not agree with someone, you can say so in a friendly way, without making them feel bad.

Let's Explore

Suppose your friend says, "I think dogs make the best pets," but you like cats better. A polite way to share your opinion would be: "That is a good point. I like cats best, but I understand why you love dogs." Kind words keep everyone happy, even when we do not agree.

Joining a Discussion and Giving Your Opinion

A discussion is when a group talks together about a topic, and everyone gets a turn to share. To be good at discussions, you make helpful contributions. That means you say something that adds to the talk, instead of saying something off-topic.

You can also give your opinion, as long as you do it politely. After a nature walk, you might say, "I think we should plant more trees in the schoolyard, because they give us shade." When you share an opinion, it helps to give a reason for it. That little word because makes your idea much stronger.

Remember

Listen with kindness, speak clearly, and share your ideas politely. Everyone deserves a turn to be heard.

Let's Practice

With a partner, take turns. One person speaks for one minute about something special to them, while the other listens carefully without interrupting. Then the listener asks one polite clarifying question, and the speaker answers. Swap roles and try again. Remember to look at each other, use clear words, and be kind.

Word Skills

When you read, you meet hundreds of words. Some you know in a flash, and others are new and tricky. In this part, you will sharpen the skills that help you read every word smoothly and work out the ones you do not know. Let us become word detectives.

R-Controlled Vowels

When the letter r comes right after a vowel, it bosses that vowel around and changes its sound. We call these r-controlled vowels, and some people call the r a "bossy r." There are five of them, and they are worth learning by heart.

Sound Example Words
arstar, car, farm
erfern, her, water
irgirl, bird, shirt
orfork, corn, storm
urturn, hurt, church

Here is a clever thing to notice: the middle three — er, ir, and ur — all make almost the same sound. Say fern, girl, and turn one after the other and listen. They rhyme at the end!

Quick Check

  • Read aloud and find the bossy r: market, garden, dirt, morning, burger.
  • Which two words have the same vowel sound: bird, fork, turn?

Diphthongs

A diphthong is when two vowels work together and your mouth slides from one sound to another, all in one go. Say the word coin slowly and feel how your mouth glides. That sliding sound is a diphthong.

Two pairs make a sound like the oy in boy: oi (coin, oil, point) and oy (boy, toy, enjoy). Two pairs make a sound like the ow in cow: ou (out, house, mouse) and ow (cow, down, flower). Remember them with little phrases: "the boy has a coin" and "the cow runs out."

Did You Know?

The word diphthong comes from old words meaning "two sounds." So a diphthong really is two vowel sounds gliding into one. Now you know a secret that even some grown-ups have forgotten!

Inflectional Endings

Sometimes we add a little ending to a word to change it slightly. These endings are called inflectional endings, and the most common ones are -s, -ed, and -ing. Look at the base word play: play + s = plays, play + ed = played, play + ing = playing.

The base word stays the same, and the ending tells us a little more. The ending -ed usually tells us the action already happened, and -ing usually tells us it is happening right now. When you meet a long word, try covering the ending and reading the base word first. Laughing becomes laugh plus ing.

Quick Check

  • Add -ed and -ing to the word jump. What two new words do you get?

Context Clues

When you bump into a word you have never seen, you do not have to give up. You can look for context clues — hints hidden in the other words around the tricky word. Read this: The clever dog found a way to open the gate. Even if clever is new to you, the rest of the sentence shows the dog was smart enough to open a gate. So clever must mean smart!

Here is another: He was a good scholar who got straight A's. The words "good" and "straight A's" tell you a scholar must be a very good student. The clues are right there, waiting for you to notice them.

Let's Explore

Guess the meaning of the underlined word from the clues: "The soup was so scrumptious that everyone asked for a second bowl." What do you think scrumptious means?

Reading Fluently and Sight Words

Reading fluently means reading smoothly, at a comfortable pace, with feeling in your voice. A fluent reader does not stop and stumble over every word; their reading sounds like a flowing river, not a bumpy road.

One big helper is your sight words — common little words like the, said, was, come, and because that pop up again and again. You learn to know them on sight, in a flash, without sounding them out. The more sight words you know by heart, the smoother your reading becomes. The secret is simple: read often, and read aloud.

Remember

Use the bossy r and diphthongs to sound out words, break long words into chunks, hunt for context clues, and know your sight words by heart. These are your tools for reading anything!

Let's Practice

Read these words aloud and sort them into two groups — words with an r-controlled vowel and words with a diphthong: star, coin, fork, cow, girl, boy, turn, house. Then choose two of the words and use each one in a sentence of your own.

Comprehension

Reading the words is only half the job. The other half is understanding what they mean. We call this comprehension. In this part, you will learn to find the main idea and to tell the difference between a fact and an opinion.

Finding the Main Idea

Every story and every paragraph is mostly about one big thing. That one big thing is called the main idea, and all the other sentences are helpers that tell you more about it. Think of a paragraph as a tree: the main idea is the strong trunk, and the details are the branches.

Read this little paragraph: Mongoose are busy little animals. They wake up early and hunt for food all day. They eat insects, eggs, and even snakes. When they are tired, they curl up to sleep. What is it mostly about? It is mostly about mongoose and what they do. That is the main idea.

Think About It

  • When you tell a friend about a movie, you usually tell them the main idea first, like "It was about a boy who could fly." Why is the main idea a good place to start?

Fact and Opinion

A fact is something that is true and can be checked. A fact is true for everyone, no matter who they are or how they feel. For example, Jamaica is an island is a fact — you can check it on a map, and it is true for every person in the world. Here are more facts: A spider has eight legs. Water is wet. Kingston is the capital city of Jamaica.

An opinion is different. An opinion is what a person feels, thinks, or believes, and it is not true for everyone. For example, Mango is the best fruit is an opinion — you might love mango, but your friend might think otherwise. Here are more opinions: Saturday is the most fun day. Blue is a beautiful colour. Football is more exciting than cricket.

So here is the big difference: a fact can be checked and is true for everyone, while an opinion is what someone feels, and others may not agree. A clue can help you: opinions often use feeling words like best, nicest, prettiest, worst, or I think and I feel.

Let's Explore

Decide if each sentence is a fact or an opinion: (1) The sun is hot. (2) Summer is the nicest season. (3) A week has seven days. (4) Ice cream tastes better than cake. Which ones can be checked, and which are just what someone feels?

Quick Check

  • Make up one fact about your school and one opinion about your school.

Using Context Clues for Meaning

When you read a non-fiction text about a real subject, you will sometimes meet a new word. Use the words around it, and even the pictures, to help you work out what it means. Good readers are always using clues to gain meaning, page after page.

Remember

Find the main idea by asking what the text is mostly about. A fact can be checked and is true for everyone. An opinion is what someone feels, and others may not agree.

Let's Practice

Read each sentence and write F for fact or O for opinion: (1) A cow gives milk. (2) Cricket is the best game. (3) The Jamaican flag has black, green, and gold. (4) Reading is more fun than maths. Then write one fact and one opinion of your own.

Grammar

Grammar is the set of rules that makes our sentences neat, clear, and correct. When you follow these rules, your reader understands you easily. In this part, you will learn about the future tense, subject-verb agreement, plural nouns, conjunctions, and punctuation.

The Future Tense

When you talk about something that has not happened yet, but will happen later, you are using the future tense. The magic word is will. You simply put will in front of the action word: I will read a story tomorrow. We will visit Grandma on Sunday. She will play netball after school. Whenever you make a plan or a promise about later, you reach for the word will.

Let's Explore

Change these sentences to the future tense using will: "I eat lunch." becomes "I will eat lunch." Now try: "We sing a song." and "He cleans his room."

Subject-Verb Agreement

Every sentence has a subject (the person or thing doing the action) and a verb (the action word). For a sentence to sound right, they must agree. When the subject is just one (like he, she, or the dog), the verb often takes an -s: She walks to school. The bird sings. But when the subject is more than one, or is we, you, or they, the verb does not take the -s: We walk to school. They sing. The birds sing.

So we say She walks but They walk; The cat plays but The cats play. Reading your sentence aloud will often tell you if it sounds right.

Quick Check

  • Choose the correct verb: "The boys (run / runs) in the yard." "My sister (read / reads) every night."

Singular and Plural Nouns

A noun is a naming word for a person, place, animal, or thing. When there is just one, it is singular; when there is more than one, it is plural. The simplest way to make a noun plural is to add an -s: one cat becomes two cats, one book becomes three books.

When a word ends in s, x, ch, or sh, we add -es instead: one box becomes two boxes, one bus becomes three buses. And when a word ends in a y with a consonant before it, we change the y to i and add -es: one baby becomes two babies, one city becomes many cities.

Did You Know?

A few special nouns do not follow any rule. One child becomes children, one man becomes men, and one foot becomes feet. These old words have kept their own special plurals for hundreds of years.

Conjunctions: The Joining Words

Conjunctions are joining words, like little bridges that connect ideas. Four of the most useful are and, but, because, and so. And joins ideas that go together (I like rice and I like peas). But shows a difference (I wanted to play, but it started to rain). Because gives a reason (I was happy because I won). So shows a result (It was raining, so I took my umbrella).

Without conjunctions, writing sounds choppy. With one, it flows. Much nicer!

Let's Explore

Join each pair with the best conjunction: "I was tired. I went to bed." (use so) and "She is small. She is strong." (use but).

Punctuation Marks

Punctuation marks are the small but mighty signs that tell your reader how to read a sentence. Every sentence must begin with a capital letter. We also use capitals for names of people and places, like Mary and Kingston, and for the special word I.

At the end of a sentence, we use one of three marks. The full stop ( . ) ends a telling sentence (The sky is blue.). The question mark ( ? ) ends an asking sentence (What is your name?). The exclamation mark ( ! ) shows strong feeling (Watch out! Hurrah, we won!).

Quick Check

  • Which mark belongs at the end? "Where are my shoes ___" "I found a dollar ___" (excited!) "We had rice for dinner ___"

Remember

Use will for the future, make your subjects and verbs agree, add -s, -es, or -ies to make nouns plural, join ideas with conjunctions, and end every sentence with the right punctuation mark.

Let's Practice

Write three sentences about what you will do this weekend. Use a future tense verb with will in each one. Make sure each sentence begins with a capital letter and ends with the correct punctuation mark, and try to use a conjunction (and, but, because, so) in at least one of them.

Writing

Writing is how you share your ideas on paper so that others can read them, even when you are not there to speak. A good writer plans first, then drafts, then makes the writing better. Let us learn how.

Writing Clear, Correct Sentences

A good piece of writing is built from good sentences, the way a wall is built from good bricks. A clear sentence tells one idea, begins with a capital letter, and ends with the right punctuation mark.

Look at the difference. A weak sentence might be: me and tim went the shop — no capital letter, no full stop, words jumbled. A clear, correct sentence would be: Tim and I went to the shop. It begins with a capital, ends with a full stop, and the words are in good order.

Think About It

  • Why is it important to use capital letters and full stops? Imagine reading a whole page with no stops at all. How would that feel?

Organising a Paragraph

A paragraph is a group of sentences that are all about one main idea. A good paragraph usually starts with a topic sentence that tells the reader what it is about, then a few detail sentences that tell more, then a closing thought.

Here is a tidy little paragraph: My favourite place is the beach. I love to feel the warm sand under my feet. I splash in the cool blue water and look for pretty shells. The beach always makes me feel happy. The first sentence tells the main idea, the middle sentences give details, and the last sentence wraps it up.

Look Around You

  • Look at a page in any of your reading books. Notice how the writing is split into paragraphs, each one starting on a new line. Writers do this to keep their ideas neat and easy to follow.

Plan, Draft, and Improve

The best writers do not write a perfect piece on the first try. Instead, they work in three friendly steps. First comes planning: before you write, you think about your topic and jot down ideas, like drawing a map before a journey. Next comes the draft: you write your sentences and paragraphs, turning your plan into real writing, without worrying about making it perfect yet.

Last comes improving, which we also call revising and editing. You read over your draft and make it better — checking that each sentence is clear, fixing capital letters, full stops, and spelling, and adding a detail to make an idea richer. This is the step that turns good writing into great writing.

Did You Know?

Even famous authors who write books for grown-ups go back and improve their writing many, many times. Improving your work is not a sign that you did badly. It is a sign that you are a real writer!

Writing in a Journal

A journal is your very own special notebook where you write down your thoughts, your feelings, and the things that happen to you. The more you write, the easier writing becomes. You can write about your day, a dream you had, or a wish for the future. There are no marks and no wrong answers in a journal — it is just for you.

You might write: Today was a good day. I will play football with my cousin on the weekend, and I cannot wait! Notice how that entry even uses the future tense and an exclamation mark. Everything you have learned comes together when you write.

Remember

Write clear sentences with capital letters and the right punctuation. Group your sentences into tidy paragraphs. Plan, draft, and improve. And write in your journal often to grow stronger every day.

Let's Practice

Write a short, tidy paragraph (three or four sentences) about your favourite food. Begin with a topic sentence, give two details, and end with a closing thought. When you finish, read it over and improve it: check your capital letters, your full stops, and your spelling.

Word Power

Speaking and Listening

Listening: Giving someone your full attention so that you truly understand what they say.
Discussion: When a group talks together about a topic and everyone gets a turn to share.
Clarifying question: A question you ask to make something clearer.
Standard Jamaican English (SJE): The kind of English used for school, writing, and speaking to people we do not know well.

Word Skills

R-controlled vowel: A vowel whose sound is changed by the letter r next to it, like ar in star.
Diphthong: Two vowels that glide together to make one sliding sound, like oi in coin.
Inflectional ending: A small ending such as -s, -ed, or -ing added to a base word.
Base word: The main word before any ending is added, like play in playing.
Context clue: A hint in the words around a new word that helps you guess its meaning.
Sight word: A common word you know in a flash without sounding it out, like the or said.
Fluency: Reading smoothly, at a comfortable pace, with feeling in your voice.

Comprehension

Comprehension: Understanding what you read.
Main idea: What a story or paragraph is mostly about.
Fact: Something that is true, can be checked, and is true for everyone. Jamaica is an island.
Opinion: What a person feels, thinks, or believes; others may not agree. Mango is the best fruit.

Grammar and Conventions

Future tense: A way of talking about what will happen later, using the word will. I will play tomorrow.
Subject: The person or thing in a sentence that does the action.
Verb: An action word, like run, sing, or read.
Subject-verb agreement: Making the subject and verb match, like She walks but They walk.
Noun: A naming word for a person, place, animal, or thing.
Singular: Just one of something. one cat
Plural: More than one of something. two cats, boxes, babies
Conjunction: A joining word, such as and, but, because, and so.
Punctuation: The marks that help us read a sentence the right way.
Full stop: The mark ( . ) at the end of a telling sentence.
Question mark: The mark ( ? ) at the end of an asking sentence.
Exclamation mark: The mark ( ! ) that shows strong feeling.

Writing

Paragraph: A group of sentences that are all about one main idea.
Topic sentence: The sentence that tells the reader what a paragraph is about.
Draft: A first try at a piece of writing, before you improve it.
Revising and editing: Reading over your writing to make it clearer and to fix mistakes.
Journal: Your own special notebook for writing your thoughts and feelings.
