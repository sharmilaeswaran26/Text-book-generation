## Hello, Young Learner!

One morning, Maya woke up and heard her grandmother calling from the kitchen, "Come and get your breakfast before it gets cold!" Maya listened carefully, jumped out of bed, and hurried downstairs. At the table, she told her little brother all about the dream she had — about a clever mongoose and a sleepy crab — and she told it so well that he begged to hear it again. Later that day at school, she read a story from her reader, raised her hand to answer a question, and wrote three neat sentences about caring for her teeth.

Did you notice something? In just one morning, Maya did four very important things. She **listened**, she **spoke**, she **read**, and she **wrote**. These four skills are the magic keys of Language Arts, and you use them every single day, often without even thinking about it.

In this chapter, you are going to become better at all four. You will learn how to listen like a detective, how to read tricky words you have never seen before, how to speak clearly so everyone understands you, and how to write down your own real-life stories. Along the way, we will talk about something close to home — your wonderful body and the healthy foods that keep it strong. So sit up tall, open your ears and your eyes, and let us begin.

## Learning Goals

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:
- Follow the rules for good listening and good speaking, and retell a story in the right order.
- Listen carefully to find the problem in a story and how it was solved.
- Read new words by stretching out long vowel sounds, consonant blends, and r-controlled vowels.
- Break long words into syllables and find smaller words hiding inside big words.
- Notice the endings -s, -ed, and -ing, and read your grade-level sight words.
- Use context clues and helpful questions to understand what you read.
- Use negative words like *isn't* and *don't*, join opposite ideas with *but*, and compare things with words like *big, bigger, biggest*.
- Plan and write your own simple Language Experience Story about something real you did.

## Our Big Question

**How can I become a better listener, reader, speaker, and writer?**

That is the one big question this whole chapter will help you answer. As you read each part, you will pick up a new skill, and each skill is like another step up a ladder. By the last page, you will be standing much higher than where you began — using language with more confidence than ever before.

## Word Power

Here are some important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about learning them all at once. Each one is explained again, gently, inside the lessons.

- **Vowel** — the letters *a, e, i, o, u*. Vowels are special helper letters. Every word needs at least one. *Example: The word* **cat** *has the vowel* **a**.
- **Long vowel** — a vowel that "says its own name", like the *a* in *cake*. The vowel sounds just like its letter name. *Example: The* **i** *in* **kite** *says its name, "i".*
- **Consonant blend** — two consonants next to each other whose sounds you can still hear, like *bl* in *black*. The two sounds blend together but neither one disappears. *Example: You can hear both the* **s** *and the* **t** *in* **stop**.
- **Syllable** — a beat or "chunk" in a word. Clap once for each beat. *Example:* **pen-cil** *has two syllables.*
- **Context clue** — the other words around a hard word that help you guess its meaning. They give you hints, like clues in a treasure hunt. *Example: "The* **enormous** *elephant was the biggest animal at the zoo" — "biggest" tells you* **enormous** *means very big.*
- **Conjunction** — a joining word that links two ideas, like *and* or *but*. It is the bridge between two parts of a sentence. *Example: "I like mango,* **but** *I love banana."*
- **Adjective** — a describing word that tells us more about a person, place, or thing. *Example: A* **juicy, yellow** *banana.*
- **Negative** — a word or phrase that says "no" or "not", like *isn't* or *don't*. *Example: "I* **do not** *like cold porridge."*
- **Sight word** — a common word you learn to read in a flash, without sounding it out, like *the, was, said*.
- **Language Experience Story** — a piece of writing about something real that truly happened to you. *Example: writing about the day you went to the market.*

# Part One: Becoming a Great Listener and Speaker

Before you can read or write, you first learned to listen and to speak. These two skills are partners, like a pencil and paper. In this part, you will learn how to listen well, how to speak clearly, and how to retell stories and give reports that everyone enjoys.

## The Rules of Good Listening

Listening is much more than just hearing. You hear many sounds all day — birds, cars, a fan whirring — but you do not really *listen* to all of them. To listen means to pay close attention with your whole body, so that you understand and remember what someone is saying.

Good listeners follow a few simple rules. They look at the person who is speaking. They keep their lips closed and wait quietly for their turn. They keep their hands and feet still so they are not distracted. And most of all, they think about the words they are hearing, holding onto them in their minds.

> **Did You Know?** Your ears never stop working, even while you sleep. But truly *listening* takes more than ears — it takes your attention. That is why a good listener turns their eyes, ears, and mind all toward the speaker at the same time.

Think about your own classroom. When your teacher says, "Open your books to page ten," a good listener hears every word and turns to the right page at once. A poor listener, who was busy looking out the window, opens to the wrong page and feels lost. Listening well keeps you from getting lost.

> **Let's Explore:** Play this quiet game with a friend. One of you gives three instructions in a row, such as: "Touch your nose, clap two times, then sit down." The other must do all three, in the right order, from memory. Then swap. This is how good listeners hold onto what they hear.

## The Rules of Good Speaking

Speaking is how you share what is inside your mind. When you speak well, people understand you easily and enjoy listening to you. When you speak in a mumble, or too softly, or all in a rush, your listener has to work very hard to follow you.

Good speakers do a few things. They speak clearly, saying each word fully. They speak loudly enough to be heard, but not so loudly that they shout. They speak at a gentle, steady speed — not too fast, not too slow. And they look at the people they are talking to.

In school, and when we are speaking to people we do not know well, we try to use **Standard Jamaican English**. This is the language you read in your books and hear on the news. At home, with family and friends, you might use the warm, everyday language of Jamaica, and that language is precious too. A good speaker learns to choose the right one for the right place.

> **Remember:** A good speaker and a good listener take turns. When one person speaks, the other listens. When it is your turn to speak, the others listen to you. This is how a real conversation works, like throwing a ball gently back and forth.

> **Think About It:** Have you ever tried to tell someone something exciting, but they were not listening at all? How did it make you feel? Now you can understand why listening is a gift you give to a speaker.

## Listening and Retelling a Story in Order

One of the best ways to show that you listened well is to **retell** what you heard. To retell a story means to tell it again in your own words. But here is the tricky part — you must tell it in the right **order**, from the beginning, to the middle, to the end.

Imagine someone told you a story like this: "First, a little girl planted a seed. Next, she watered it every day. Then, after many days, a beautiful sunflower grew." If you wanted to retell that story, you would have to keep the order. It would sound very strange to say, "A sunflower grew, then she planted a seed!" The order would be all mixed up.

Helpful order words make retelling easy. Words like **first, next, then, after that,** and **finally** act like stepping stones, guiding your listener through the story one step at a time.

> **Let's Explore:** Think about how you got ready for school this morning. Can you retell it in order using order words? For example: "First I woke up. Next I brushed my teeth. Then I ate my breakfast. After that I put on my uniform. Finally I walked to school." Notice how the order words keep everything tidy.

When you retell, try to use Standard Jamaican English, speak clearly, and keep the events in the order they happened. Do that, and your listener will be able to picture the whole story, just as you did.

## Finding the Problem and the Solution

Many stories you hear — on the radio, on the television, or read aloud by your teacher — have a **problem** in them. The problem is the trouble that a character runs into. And almost every story also has a **solution**, which is how that trouble is fixed in the end.

Listen to this short story. "Anansi the spider was very hungry, but all his food was at the top of a tall tree he could not climb. He thought and thought. At last, he spun a long, strong web, swung himself up to the very top, and ate until he was full." Can you find the problem? Anansi was hungry and could not reach his food. Can you find the solution? He spun a web to climb up to it.

> **Quick Check:** In the Anansi story, what was the problem, and how was it solved? Whisper your answer to yourself, then say it out loud to a partner.

Good listeners are like detectives. As they listen, they ask themselves, "What is the trouble here?" and "How did the character get out of it?" Sometimes you can even think of a *different* way the problem could have been solved. That is using your own clever mind.

> **Think About It:** If you were Anansi, can you think of another way to reach the food at the top of the tree? There is often more than one good solution to a problem.

## Listening and Following Instructions

Some of the most important listening you will ever do is listening to **instructions** — the steps that tell you how to do something. If you miss even one step, the whole thing can go wrong.

Let us say your teacher is showing you how to fold a paper boat. She says, "First, fold the paper in half. Next, fold the two top corners down. Then, fold up the bottom edges. After that, open it out and shape it into a boat." To make your boat, you must listen to each step and do it in order. Skip a step, and your boat will not float.

> **Let's Explore:** Try it yourself. Find a square of paper and listen carefully as a grown-up or a friend reads out the steps to fold something simple, like a paper fan or a paper hat. Do exactly what each step says, in order. Did your folding come out right? If it did, you are a careful listener.

> **Remember:** When you follow instructions, do not rush ahead. Listen to one step, do that step, and then listen for the next. Careful listeners make wonderful things.

## Giving an Oral Report

Sometimes you will be asked to give an **oral report**. This simply means standing up and telling the class about something you saw, did, or learned. It is your turn to be the speaker while everyone else listens.

A good oral report uses everything you have learned so far. You speak clearly and loudly enough. You tell things in order, using words like *first, next,* and *then*. You use Standard Jamaican English. And you look at your listeners.

For example, after the paper-folding activity, you might report: "Today my group folded a paper boat. First, it was hard to fold the corners neatly. Next, I helped my friend straighten his folds. Then, in the end, all three of us made boats that floated in a bowl of water. I felt very proud."

> **Did You Know?** When you give a report about something you really did, you are an *expert* on that topic, because no one knows your experience better than you. That makes reporting easier and more fun.

# Part Two: Becoming a Word Detective

When you read, you sometimes meet a word you have never seen before. Do not let it scare you! A good reader has a whole toolbox of tricks for working out new words. In this part, you will fill your own toolbox. Soon you will be able to unlock almost any word, all by yourself.

## Long Vowel Sounds

You already know the five vowels: *a, e, i, o, u*. Vowels are very busy letters, and they can make two kinds of sounds: short sounds and long sounds.

A **long vowel** is easy to remember, because a long vowel "says its own name". Listen to the *a* in **cake** — it says "ay", just like the letter name *A*. Listen to the *i* in **kite** — it says "eye", just like the letter name *I*. The vowel is telling you its own name out loud.

Here are five long vowel words to practise, one for each vowel:
- Long **a**: c**a**ke (the *a* says "ay")
- Long **e**: f**ee**t (the *e* says "ee")
- Long **i**: k**i**te (the *i* says "eye")
- Long **o**: r**o**pe (the *o* says "oh")
- Long **u**: m**u**le (the *u* says "you")

To read a word, you **blend** the sounds together smoothly. Take the word *rope*. Say each sound — /r/ /oh/ /p/ — then slide them together quickly: "rope". That is blending, and it is how readers turn letters into words.

> **Word Power:** To **blend** means to mix sounds together smoothly to make a whole word, the way you blend fruits together to make a smoothie.

> **Quick Check:** Read these long vowel words aloud, stretching out the long vowel: *cake, feet, kite, rope, mule*. Can you hear each vowel saying its own name?

> **Look Around You:** Long vowel words are everywhere. Look for the long *a* in *plate*, the long *e* in *green*, the long *o* in *home*. Can you spot a long vowel word on something in your classroom right now?

## Consonant Blends

All the letters that are not vowels are **consonants**. Sometimes two consonants stand side by side in a word, and you can still hear *both* of their sounds. When that happens, we call it a **consonant blend**.

Listen to the word **black**. At the start, you hear the /b/ and the /l/ blended together: *bl*. You did not lose either sound — they hold hands and slide together. Some blends come at the *start* of words, like these:
- **bl**ock, **bl**ue (the *bl* blend)
- **st**op, **st**ar (the *st* blend)
- **gr**ass, **gr**ow (the *gr* blend)

Other blends come at the *end* of words. Listen to the end of these words and you will hear two sounds:
- ha**nd**, frie**nd** (the *nd* blend)
- fa**st**, ne**st** (the *st* blend)
- la**mp**, ju**mp** (the *mp* blend)

> **Did You Know?** A blend is made of two sounds, and each sound in the blend can still be heard on its own. Try it: say *stop* slowly, and you can pull apart the /s/ and the /t/. Then push them back together. That pulling apart and pushing together is exactly what good readers do.

> **Let's Explore:** Look at these words and find the blend in each one: *first, think, past, lamp, friend, dusk*. Say the blend by itself, then read the whole word. Which blends are at the start, and which are at the end?

> **Quick Check:** Make your own blend words. Can you think of a word that starts with *gr* and a word that ends with *nd*? (Hint: *grass*... *sand*...)

## R-Controlled Vowels

Here is a curious thing about the letter *r*. When a vowel has the letter *r* right after it, the *r* is so powerful that it changes the way the vowel sounds! We call these **r-controlled vowels**, because the *r* is in control.

Listen to these examples:
- **ar** says "ar", as in c**ar**, st**ar**, sh**ar**p.
- **er** says "er", as in f**er**n, t**er**m.
- **ir** says "ir", as in b**ir**d, g**ir**l.
- **or** says "or", as in c**or**n, f**or**k.
- **ur** says "ur", as in h**ur**t, t**ur**n.

Notice that *er, ir,* and *ur* often sound very much alike — they all make a soft "ur" sound. That is just the powerful *r* at work.

> **Remember:** When a vowel is followed by the letter *r*, the vowel sound changes. The *r* "controls" the vowel. So when you see *ar, er, ir, or,* or *ur*, get ready for the special r-sound.

> **Look Around You:** R-controlled words are all around. Think of a *car* on the road, a *bird* in a tree, *corn* on your plate, the *dirt* in the garden. Can you say each one and hear the powerful *r*?

## Breaking Words into Syllables

When a word looks long and scary, here is a wonderful trick: break it into **syllables**. A syllable is a beat or a chunk of sound in a word. Every syllable has a vowel sound, and you can *clap* once for each one.

Clap as you say these words:
- *cat* — one clap, one syllable.
- *pen-cil* — two claps, two syllables.
- *ba-na-na* — three claps, three syllables.
- *but-ter-fly* — three claps, three syllables.

When you break a long word into chunks, each chunk is small and easy to read. Then you read the chunks one at a time and slide them together. *But-ter-fly* becomes *butterfly*. The long word was never scary at all — it was just little words holding hands.

There are some helpful rules. We never break a one-syllable word, like *girl* or *wall* — it is already one chunk. We never split up a blend or a digraph, so *think-ing* keeps its *th* together. And when two consonants sit between two vowels, we usually split between them, like *lit-tle* and *pret-ty*. We also split compound words between the two smaller words, like *sun-flower* and *cup-cake*.

> **Let's Explore:** Clap out these words and count the syllables: *mango, kitchen, elephant, basketball*. How many beats does each one have? (*Man-go* = 2, *kit-chen* = 2, *el-e-phant* = 3, *bas-ket-ball* = 3.)

## The Word Within Word Strategy

Here is one more clever trick for big words. Often, hiding inside a long word, there are smaller words you already know! When you find them, you can use them to read the whole word. We call this the **Word Within Word** strategy.

Look at the word **intelligent**. Hiding inside it, you can find the small words *in*, *tell*, and *gent*. Once you spot the words you know, you can work out the tricky part in the middle and then slide it all together: *in-tell-i-gent*, "intelligent". The big word cracks open like a coconut, and the smaller words spill out.

> **Quick Check:** Look at the word *suitable*. Can you find a smaller word hiding inside it? (Hint: it has to do with a *suit* of clothes... *suit*-able!) Finding little words inside big words makes reading so much easier.

## Word Endings: -s, -ed, and -ing

Some little endings get added to the back of words, and they change the word's meaning in important ways. We call them **inflectional endings**. The three you will meet most often are **-s, -ed,** and **-ing**.

- Adding **-s** often means *more than one*, or it tells us about *now*. *One friend* becomes *two friends*. *I walk* becomes *she walks*.
- Adding **-ed** usually means it happened in the *past*, already finished. *I walk today* becomes *I walked yesterday*.
- Adding **-ing** usually means it is happening *right now*. *I walk* becomes *I am walking*.

Read these word pairs aloud and listen to how the ending changes the word: *reach → reaches*, *friend → friends*, *walk → walking*, *prepare → prepares*. Each ending makes a new word with a new meaning.

> **Remember:** Always pronounce the ending when you read, because the ending changes the word and its meaning. Reading *walk* is different from reading *walked* or *walking*. The little ending makes a big difference.

## Sight Words

Some words pop up so very often that you should learn to read them in a flash, without sounding them out at all. We call these **sight words**, because you know them the moment you set eyes on them.

Words like *the, was, said, you, they,* and *come* are sight words. You will see them in almost every story you ever read. The more you practise them, the faster your reading becomes, because you are not stopping to sound them out.

> **Did You Know?** A great way to practise sight words is to play games with them. You can hide word cards around the room and hunt for them, or play "I Spy" with words on the wall. The more you play, the faster these words become your old friends.

# Part Three: Becoming a Strong Reader Who Understands

Reading is more than just saying the words. The real magic of reading is **understanding** what the words mean. We call this understanding **comprehension**. In this part, you will learn how to make sure you truly understand every story you read.

## Using Context Clues

Even strong readers meet words they do not know. When that happens, you do not always need a dictionary. You can often work out the meaning by looking at the other words around it. Those helpful words are called **context clues**, and they work just like clues in a treasure hunt.

Read this sentence: "The **enormous** elephant was the biggest animal at the whole zoo." Even if you did not know the word *enormous*, the clue "the biggest animal" tells you that *enormous* must mean *very, very big*. The other words solved the mystery for you.

Here is another: "After running the race, Sara was **exhausted**, so she lay down to rest." The clues "after running the race" and "lay down to rest" tell you that *exhausted* must mean *very tired*.

> **Let's Explore:** Try this one. "The puppy was so **playful** that it ran, jumped, and chased its tail all afternoon." What do you think *playful* means? The clues "ran, jumped, and chased its tail" should help you guess. ( It means *full of play, loving to play*.)

> **Look Around You:** The next time you read and meet a word you do not know, stop and look at the words around it. Do not give up right away. Be a word detective and hunt for the context clues hiding nearby.

## Asking Yourself Questions

Good readers do something clever and quiet inside their heads while they read: they ask themselves questions. This is called **self-questioning**, and it keeps your mind awake and thinking.

Before you read, you might ask, "What do I think this story will be about?" While you read, you might ask, "Wait — do I understand what just happened? Why did the character do that?" And after you read, you might ask, "What was this story really about? What was my favourite part?"

> **Think About It:** When you ask yourself questions as you read, you are checking that your mind is really following the story. If you ever answer "No, I do not understand," that is your signal to go back and read that part again. Good readers do this all the time.

When you notice you are confused, that is not a bad thing — it is actually a good thing! It means you are paying attention. You can go back, read the tricky part again more slowly, look for context clues, and keep going. That is what strong readers do.

## Finding the Key Information and Staying Organized

Stories and lessons are full of information, but some of it is more important than the rest. The most important pieces are called the **key information** — the things you really need to remember.

To keep track of key information, readers often use **graphic organizers** and **outlines**. A graphic organizer is a simple chart or picture that helps you sort your thoughts. For example, when you read a story, you might fill in a chart with four boxes: who the characters are, where it takes place (the setting), what the problem is, and how it is solved (the solution).

> **Quick Check:** Think of a story you know well, like a favourite folktale. Can you name the characters, the setting, the problem, and the solution? If you can fill in all four, you have understood the story's key information.

Using a chart like this helps you hold the whole story in your mind, neat and tidy, instead of letting the details slip away. It is like putting your toys in a box instead of leaving them all over the floor.

# Part Four: Becoming a Careful Writer of Sentences

Now that you can listen, speak, and read, it is time to look closely at how sentences are built. In this part, you will learn some grammar tools that make your sentences clear and correct. Grammar is simply the set of rules that helps our words work well together.

## Saying "No": Negative Forms

Sometimes we want to say that something is *not* true, or that we do *not* want to do something. To do this, we use **negative** words. The most important little word here is *not*.

Look at how *not* changes the verb **to be**:
- "She **is** happy" becomes "She **is not** happy", which we can shorten to "She **isn't** happy".
- "They **are** ready" becomes "They **are not** ready", which we can shorten to "They **aren't** ready".

And look at how *not* changes the verb **to do**:
- "I **do** like it" becomes "I **do not** like it", which we can shorten to "I **don't** like it".
- "He **does** know" becomes "He **does not** know", which we can shorten to "He **doesn't** know".

Notice the little mark in *isn't, aren't, don't,* and *doesn't*. That mark is called an apostrophe, and it shows where we squeezed two words together into one short word.

> **Word Power:** A **negative** sentence is one that tells us something is *not* so. We make it by adding the word *not* after a form of "to be" or "to do".

> **Quick Check:** Turn these positive sentences into negative ones. "The soup is hot." → "The soup ___ hot." "I do eat breakfast." → "I ___ eat breakfast." (Answers: *isn't*; *don't*.)

## Joining Opposite Ideas with "But"

A **conjunction** is a joining word. You already know the joining word *and*. Now meet a very useful one: **but**. We use *but* to join two ideas that are *opposite* or that *disagree* with each other.

Look at these sentences:
- "I wanted to play outside, **but** it started to rain." (Wanting to play and the rain disagree.)
- "Mango is sweet, **but** lime is sour." (Sweet and sour are opposites.)
- "Sam is small, **but** he is very strong." (We might expect a small person to be weak, so this surprises us.)

The word *but* warns your reader, "Get ready — something different is coming!" It is the perfect bridge between two ideas that pull in opposite directions.

> **Think About It:** Can you finish this sentence with an opposite idea? "I like to wake up early, **but** ___." Think of something that pulls the other way, such as "but my sister likes to sleep late."

## Describing and Comparing with Adjectives

An **adjective** is a describing word. It tells us more about a person, place, or thing — its size, shape, colour, or feel. In "a **juicy, yellow** banana", the words *juicy* and *yellow* are adjectives that paint a picture for us.

Adjectives have a special power: they can **compare** things. Watch how the word *big* changes when we compare:
- One thing on its own: "The dog is **big**."
- Comparing *two* things: "The cow is **bigger** than the dog." (We add **-er**.)
- Comparing *three or more* things: "The elephant is the **biggest** of them all." (We add **-est**.)

So the pattern is: **big, bigger, biggest**. We add *-er* when we compare two things, and *-est* when we compare three or more. Try it with other adjectives: *tall, taller, tallest*; *fast, faster, fastest*; *small, smaller, smallest*.

> **Let's Explore:** Look at three people in your family. Who is *tall*? Who is *taller*? Who is the *tallest*? Now try it with three things in your classroom and the word *long*.

> **Did You Know?** A few special adjectives do not follow the *-er / -est* rule at all. Instead of "gooder" and "goodest", we say *good, better, best*. And instead of "badder", we say *bad, worse, worst*. These special words you simply learn by heart.

## A Quick Word About Nouns, Pronouns, and Prepositions

Three more helpers make your sentences work. A **noun** is a naming word — it names a person (*girl*), a place (*market*), or a thing (*spoon*). A **pronoun** is a small word that stands in for a noun, so you do not have to repeat it — instead of "Maya likes Maya's hat", we say "Maya likes **her** hat". And a **preposition** is a little word that tells us *where* something is or *which way* it goes — words like *on, under, in, beside, over,* and *to*.

> **Look Around You:** Prepositions are easy to spot once you know them. Your book is **on** the desk. Your bag is **under** the chair. Your friend sits **beside** you. Can you make a sentence about your classroom using a preposition?

# Part Five: Becoming a Writer of My Own Stories

The most exciting skill of all is writing your very own stories. In this last part, you will learn how to plan and write a **Language Experience Story** — a true story about something that really happened to you.

## What Is a Language Experience Story?

A **Language Experience Story** is writing about a real experience from your own life. It is not made-up or pretend. It is the true tale of something you did, somewhere you went, or something that happened to you.

You have hundreds of these stories already inside you! The day you lost your first tooth. The morning you helped to cook breakfast. The time you went to the market with your grandmother. The afternoon you and your friends folded paper boats. Every one of these is a Language Experience Story waiting to be written down.

> **Think About It:** Close your eyes and think of one thing you did this week that you enjoyed. That memory could become your very own written story.

## Planning Before You Write

Good writers do not just start scribbling. First, they **plan**. Planning before writing is called **pre-writing**, and it helps you gather your ideas so your story comes out neat and clear.

A wonderful way to plan is with a **graphic organizer**, just like the ones you used for reading. You might make a simple chart and fill in your ideas: Who was there? Where did it happen? What happened first, next, and last? How did you feel?

For example, suppose you want to write about going to the market. Your plan might look like this:
- **Who:** Me and my grandmother.
- **Where:** The market in town.
- **What happened first:** We bought a dozen oranges.
- **What happened next:** We chose two pounds of yam.
- **What happened last:** We carried the heavy bags home and made juice.
- **How I felt:** Tired but happy.

> **Let's Explore:** Pick one real thing you did and fill in a plan like the one above in your head. Who was there? Where were you? What happened, in order? Once you have your plan, the writing is already half done.

## Writing Your First Draft

Once your plan is ready, you write your **first draft**. A first draft is your very first try at writing the story out in full sentences. It does not have to be perfect. Real writers always write a first draft, then go back and make it better later.

Using the market plan from above, your first draft might read like this:

> *On Saturday I went to the market with my grandmother. First, we bought a dozen sweet oranges. Next, we chose two pounds of yam. Then we carried the heavy bags all the way home. After that, we made fresh orange juice. I was tired, but I was very happy.*

Do you see how the plan turned into a real story? Notice the order words — *first, next, then, after that*. Notice the conjunction *but* joining "tired" and "happy". Notice the adjectives *sweet* and *heavy*. You have used the skills from this whole chapter, all in one little story!

> **Remember:** Writing has steps, just like folding a paper boat. First you plan, then you write a draft, and later you read it over and fix it up. Take it one step at a time, and you can write anything.

## Practice Together

Let us work through some examples together, with the book showing you the way.

**1. Long vowels.** Look at the word *rope*. Which vowel makes the long sound? The vowel *o*, and it says its own name, "oh". Now you try: in the word *feet*, which vowel makes the long sound? *(The* e*, saying "ee".)*

**2. Finding the problem and solution.** Listen: "Tom's kite got stuck high in a tree. He fetched a long stick and gently knocked it free." What is the problem? *(Tom's kite got stuck in the tree.)* What is the solution? *(He used a long stick to knock it free.)*

**3. Making a negative.** Change this to a negative sentence: "She is afraid of the dark." We add *not* after *is*: *(She is not afraid of the dark, or "She isn't afraid of the dark.")*

**4. Comparing with adjectives.** Fill in the blanks: "A puppy is small. A kitten is ___ than a horse. The mouse is the ___ of all three." *(smaller; smallest.)*

**5. Joining with "but".** Join these two opposite ideas with *but*: "I like to read. My eyes feel tired." *(I like to read, but my eyes feel tired.)*

What a journey you have taken! In this chapter, you grew stronger in all four magic keys of Language Arts.

## Remember

- A good listener looks, stays quiet, and thinks about the speaker's words; a good speaker speaks clearly and takes turns.
- Long vowels say their own names; blends keep both sounds; the letter *r* changes the vowel before it.
- Break big words into syllables, and look for smaller words hiding inside them.
- Context clues are the helpful words around a tricky word — use them like a detective.
- Add *not* to make a sentence negative; use *but* to join opposite ideas; add *-er* and *-est* to compare.
- Good writers plan first, then write a draft, then make it better.
