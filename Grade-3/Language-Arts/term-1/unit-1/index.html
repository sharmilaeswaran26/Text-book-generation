Speaking & Listening

The Rules of Good Listening

Listening is much more than just hearing. You hear many sounds all day — birds, cars, a fan whirring — but you do not really listen to all of them. To listen means to pay close attention with your whole body, so that you understand and remember what someone is saying.

Good listeners follow a few simple rules. They look at the person who is speaking. They keep their lips closed and wait quietly for their turn. They keep their hands and feet still so they are not distracted. And most of all, they think about the words they are hearing, holding onto them in their minds.

Think about your own classroom. When your teacher says, "Open your books to page ten," a good listener hears every word and turns to the right page at once. A poor listener, who was busy looking out the window, opens to the wrong page and feels lost. Listening well keeps you from getting lost.

Did You Know?

Your ears never stop working, even while you sleep. But truly listening takes more than ears — it takes your attention. That is why a good listener turns their eyes, ears, and mind all toward the speaker at the same time.

Let's Explore

Play this quiet game with a friend. One of you gives three instructions in a row, such as: "Touch your nose, clap two times, then sit down." The other must do all three, in the right order, from memory. Then swap. This is how good listeners hold onto what they hear.

The Rules of Good Speaking

Speaking is how you share what is inside your mind. When you speak well, people understand you easily and enjoy listening to you. When you speak in a mumble, or too softly, or all in a rush, your listener has to work very hard to follow you.

Good speakers do a few things. They speak clearly, saying each word fully. They speak loudly enough to be heard, but not so loudly that they shout. They speak at a gentle, steady speed — not too fast, not too slow. And they look at the people they are talking to.

In school, and when we are speaking to people we do not know well, we try to use Standard Jamaican English. This is the language you read in your books and hear on the news. At home, with family and friends, you might use the warm, everyday language of Jamaica, and that language is precious too. A good speaker learns to choose the right one for the right place.

Remember

A good speaker and a good listener take turns. When one person speaks, the other listens. When it is your turn to speak, the others listen to you. This is how a real conversation works, like throwing a ball gently back and forth.

Think About It

Have you ever tried to tell someone something exciting, but they were not listening at all? How did it make you feel? Now you can understand why listening is a gift you give to a speaker.

Listening and Retelling a Story in Order

One of the best ways to show that you listened well is to retell what you heard. To retell a story means to tell it again in your own words. But here is the tricky part — you must tell it in the right order, from the beginning, to the middle, to the end.

Imagine someone told you a story like this: "First, a little girl planted a seed. Next, she watered it every day. Then, after many days, a beautiful sunflower grew." If you wanted to retell that story, you would have to keep the order. It would sound very strange to say, "A sunflower grew, then she planted a seed!" The order would be all mixed up.

Helpful order words make retelling easy. Words like first, next, then, after that, and finally act like stepping stones, guiding your listener through the story one step at a time. When you retell, try to use Standard Jamaican English, speak clearly, and keep the events in the order they happened.

Let's Explore

Think about how you got ready for school this morning. Can you retell it in order? For example: "First I woke up. Next I brushed my teeth. Then I ate my breakfast. After that I put on my uniform. Finally I walked to school." Notice how the order words keep everything tidy.

Finding the Problem and the Solution

Many stories you hear — on the radio, on the television, or read aloud by your teacher — have a problem in them. The problem is the trouble that a character runs into. And almost every story also has a solution, which is how that trouble is fixed in the end.

Listen to this short story. "Anansi the spider was very hungry, but all his food was at the top of a tall tree he could not climb. He thought and thought. At last, he spun a long, strong web, swung himself up to the very top, and ate until he was full." The problem is that Anansi was hungry and could not reach his food. The solution is that he spun a web to climb up to it. Good listeners are like detectives, asking "What is the trouble here?" and "How did the character get out of it?"

Quick Check

In the Anansi story, what was the problem, and how was it solved? Whisper your answer to yourself, then say it out loud to a partner.

Think About It

If you were Anansi, can you think of another way to reach the food at the top of the tree? There is often more than one good solution to a problem.

Following Instructions and Giving a Report

Some of the most important listening you will ever do is listening to instructions — the steps that tell you how to do something. If you miss even one step, the whole thing can go wrong. Say your teacher is showing you how to fold a paper boat: "First, fold the paper in half. Next, fold the two top corners down. Then, fold up the bottom edges." To make your boat, you must listen to each step and do it in order. Skip a step, and your boat will not float.

Sometimes you will be asked to give an oral report. This means standing up and telling the class about something you saw, did, or learned. A good report uses everything you have learned: you speak clearly, tell things in order, use Standard Jamaican English, and look at your listeners. For example: "Today my group folded a paper boat. First, it was hard to fold the corners neatly. Next, I helped my friend. Then, in the end, our boats floated in a bowl of water. I felt very proud."

Remember

When you follow instructions, do not rush ahead. Listen to one step, do that step, and then listen for the next. Careful listeners make wonderful things.

Let's Practice

Listening detective: Listen to this short story read aloud by a friend or grown-up: "Tom's kite got stuck high in a tree. He fetched a long stick and gently knocked it free." Then answer: What was the problem? How was it solved?

Retell in order: Tell how you got ready for school this morning, using the order words first, next, then, after that, and finally.

Word Skills (Word Recognition & Fluency)

Long Vowel Sounds

You already know the five vowels: a, e, i, o, u. Vowels can make two kinds of sounds: short sounds and long sounds. A long vowel is easy to remember, because a long vowel "says its own name". Listen to the a in cake — it says "ay", just like the letter name A. Listen to the i in kite — it says "eye", just like the letter name I.

Here are five long vowel words, one for each vowel: long a in cake; long e in feet; long i in kite; long o in rope; long u in mule. To read a word, you blend the sounds together smoothly. Take the word rope: say each sound — /r/ /oh/ /p/ — then slide them together quickly: "rope".

Word Power

To blend means to mix sounds together smoothly to make a whole word, the way you blend fruits together to make a smoothie.

Quick Check

Read these long vowel words aloud, stretching out the long vowel: cake, feet, kite, rope, mule. Can you hear each vowel saying its own name?

Look Around You

Long vowel words are everywhere. Look for the long a in plate, the long e in green, the long o in home. Can you spot a long vowel word on something in your classroom right now?

Consonant Blends

All the letters that are not vowels are consonants. Sometimes two consonants stand side by side in a word, and you can still hear both of their sounds. When that happens, we call it a consonant blend. Listen to the word black: at the start you hear the /b/ and the /l/ blended together. You did not lose either sound.

Some blends come at the start of words: block, blue; stop, star; grass, grow. Other blends come at the end of words: hand, friend; fast, nest; lamp, jump.

Did You Know?

A blend is made of two sounds, and each sound can still be heard on its own. Try it: say stop slowly, and you can pull apart the /s/ and the /t/. Then push them back together. That pulling apart and pushing together is exactly what good readers do.

Let's Explore

Find the blend in each of these words: first, think, past, lamp, friend, dusk. Say the blend by itself, then read the whole word. Which blends are at the start, and which are at the end?

R-Controlled Vowels

Here is a curious thing about the letter r. When a vowel has the letter r right after it, the r is so powerful that it changes the way the vowel sounds! We call these r-controlled vowels, because the r is in control.

ar says "ar", as in car, star, sharp. er says "er", as in fern, term. ir says "ir", as in bird, girl. or says "or", as in corn, fork. ur says "ur", as in hurt, turn. Notice that er, ir, and ur often sound very much alike.

Remember

When a vowel is followed by the letter r, the vowel sound changes. The r "controls" the vowel. So when you see ar, er, ir, or, or ur, get ready for the special r-sound.

Look Around You

R-controlled words are all around. Think of a car on the road, a bird in a tree, corn on your plate, the dirt in the garden. Can you say each one and hear the powerful r?

Syllables and the Word Within Word

When a word looks long and scary, break it into syllables. A syllable is a beat or chunk of sound, and you can clap once for each one: cat (one clap), pen-cil (two claps), ba-na-na (three claps), but-ter-fly (three claps). Read the chunks one at a time and slide them together. A few rules help: never break a one-syllable word like girl or wall; never split a blend, so think-ing keeps its th together; split between two consonants, like lit-tle and pret-ty; and split compound words between the two smaller words, like sun-flower.

Often, hiding inside a long word, there are smaller words you already know. This is the Word Within Word strategy. Look at intelligent: hiding inside it you can find in, tell, and gent. Spot the words you know, work out the tricky middle, then slide it all together.

Quick Check

Look at the word suitable. Can you find a smaller word hiding inside it? (Hint: it has to do with a suit of clothes... suit-able!) Finding little words inside big words makes reading easier.

Word Endings and Sight Words

Some little endings get added to the back of words and change the meaning. We call them inflectional endings. Adding -s often means more than one, or tells about now (friend → friends). Adding -ed usually means it happened in the past (walk → walked). Adding -ing usually means it is happening right now (walk → walking). Read these pairs aloud: reach → reaches, friend → friends, walk → walking, prepare → prepares.

Some words pop up so often that you should learn to read them in a flash, without sounding them out. We call these sight words — words like the, was, said, you, they, and come. The more you practise them, the faster your reading becomes.

Remember

Always pronounce the ending when you read, because the ending changes the word and its meaning. Reading walk is different from reading walked or walking. The little ending makes a big difference.

Let's Practice

Long vowels: Read aloud and name the long vowel you hear: cake, kite, mule.

Blends: Find the consonant blend in each word: grass, hand, stop.

Syllables: Clap and count the syllables in banana, pencil, butterfly.

Comprehension

Using Context Clues

Reading is more than just saying the words. The real magic of reading is understanding what the words mean. We call this understanding comprehension. Even strong readers meet words they do not know. When that happens, you can often work out the meaning by looking at the other words around it. Those helpful words are called context clues, and they work just like clues in a treasure hunt.

Read this sentence: "The enormous elephant was the biggest animal at the whole zoo." Even if you did not know the word enormous, the clue "the biggest animal" tells you that enormous must mean very, very big. Here is another: "After running the race, Sara was exhausted, so she lay down to rest." The clues "after running the race" and "lay down to rest" tell you that exhausted must mean very tired.

Let's Explore

Try this one. "The puppy was so playful that it ran, jumped, and chased its tail all afternoon." What do you think playful means? The clues "ran, jumped, and chased its tail" should help you guess. (It means full of play, loving to play.)

Look Around You

The next time you read and meet a word you do not know, stop and look at the words around it. Do not give up right away. Be a word detective and hunt for the context clues hiding nearby.

Asking Yourself Questions

Good readers do something clever and quiet inside their heads while they read: they ask themselves questions. This is called self-questioning, and it keeps your mind awake and thinking.

Before you read, you might ask, "What do I think this story will be about?" While you read, you might ask, "Wait — do I understand what just happened? Why did the character do that?" And after you read, you might ask, "What was this story really about? What was my favourite part?" When you notice you are confused, that is actually a good thing — it means you are paying attention. You can go back, read the tricky part again more slowly, look for context clues, and keep going.

Think About It

When you ask yourself questions as you read, you are checking that your mind is really following the story. If you ever answer "No, I do not understand," that is your signal to go back and read that part again. Good readers do this all the time.

Finding Key Information and Staying Organized

Stories and lessons are full of information, but some of it is more important than the rest. The most important pieces are called the key information — the things you really need to remember.

To keep track of key information, readers often use graphic organizers and outlines. A graphic organizer is a simple chart or picture that helps you sort your thoughts. For example, when you read a story, you might fill in a chart with four boxes: who the characters are, where it takes place (the setting), what the problem is, and how it is solved (the solution). Using a chart like this helps you hold the whole story in your mind, neat and tidy, like putting your toys in a box instead of leaving them all over the floor.

Quick Check

Think of a story you know well, like a favourite folktale. Can you name the characters, the setting, the problem, and the solution? If you can fill in all four, you have understood the story's key information.

Let's Practice

Context clue: Read this sentence and guess the meaning of the bold word using the clues: "The soup was so delicious that everyone asked for a second bowl."

Story map: Choose a folktale you know. On paper, write its characters, setting, problem, and solution in four boxes.

Grammar & Conventions

Saying "No": Negative Forms

Sometimes we want to say that something is not true, or that we do not want to do something. To do this, we use negative words. The most important little word here is not.

Look at how not changes the verb to be: "She is happy" becomes "She is not happy", shortened to "She isn't happy". "They are ready" becomes "They are not ready", shortened to "They aren't ready". Now look at the verb to do: "I do like it" becomes "I do not like it", shortened to "I don't like it". "He does know" becomes "He does not know", shortened to "He doesn't know". The little mark in isn't, aren't, don't, and doesn't is an apostrophe; it shows where we squeezed two words into one.

Word Power

A negative sentence is one that tells us something is not so. We make it by adding the word not after a form of "to be" or "to do".

Quick Check

Turn these positive sentences into negative ones. "The soup is hot." → "The soup ___ hot." "I do eat breakfast." → "I ___ eat breakfast." (Answers: isn't; don't.)

Joining Opposite Ideas with "But"

A conjunction is a joining word. You already know the joining word and. Now meet a very useful one: but. We use but to join two ideas that are opposite or that disagree with each other.

Look at these sentences: "I wanted to play outside, but it started to rain." "Mango is sweet, but lime is sour." "Sam is small, but he is very strong." The word but warns your reader, "Get ready — something different is coming!" It is the perfect bridge between two ideas that pull in opposite directions.

Think About It

Can you finish this sentence with an opposite idea? "I like to wake up early, but ___." Think of something that pulls the other way, such as "but my sister likes to sleep late."

Describing and Comparing with Adjectives

An adjective is a describing word. It tells us more about a person, place, or thing — its size, shape, colour, or feel. In "a juicy, yellow banana", the words juicy and yellow are adjectives that paint a picture for us.

Adjectives can also compare things. Watch how big changes: one thing on its own — "The dog is big." Comparing two things — "The cow is bigger than the dog." (We add -er.) Comparing three or more things — "The elephant is the biggest of them all." (We add -est.) So the pattern is big, bigger, biggest. Try it: tall, taller, tallest; fast, faster, fastest.

Did You Know?

A few special adjectives do not follow the -er / -est rule. Instead of "gooder" and "goodest", we say good, better, best. And instead of "badder", we say bad, worse, worst. These special words you simply learn by heart.

Nouns, Pronouns, and Prepositions

Three more helpers make your sentences work. A noun is a naming word — it names a person (girl), a place (market), or a thing (spoon). A pronoun is a small word that stands in for a noun, so you do not have to repeat it — instead of "Maya likes Maya's hat", we say "Maya likes her hat". And a preposition is a little word that tells us where something is or which way it goes — words like on, under, in, beside, over, and to.

Look Around You

Prepositions are easy to spot once you know them. Your book is on the desk. Your bag is under the chair. Your friend sits beside you. Can you make a sentence about your classroom using a preposition?

Let's Practice

Make it negative: Change "She is afraid of the dark" into a negative sentence.

Compare: Fill in the blanks: "A puppy is small. A kitten is ___ than a horse. The mouse is the ___ of all three."

Join with "but": Join these opposite ideas with but: "I like to read. My eyes feel tired."

Writing

What Is a Language Experience Story?

The most exciting skill of all is writing your very own stories. A Language Experience Story is writing about a real experience from your own life. It is not made-up or pretend. It is the true tale of something you did, somewhere you went, or something that happened to you.

You have hundreds of these stories already inside you! The day you lost your first tooth. The morning you helped to cook breakfast. The time you went to the market with your grandmother. The afternoon you and your friends folded paper boats. Every one of these is a Language Experience Story waiting to be written down.

Think About It

Close your eyes and think of one thing you did this week that you enjoyed. That memory could become your very own written story.

Planning Before You Write

Good writers do not just start scribbling. First, they plan. Planning before writing is called pre-writing, and it helps you gather your ideas so your story comes out neat and clear. A wonderful way to plan is with a graphic organizer, just like the ones you used for reading.

For example, suppose you want to write about going to the market. Your plan might look like this: Who: Me and my grandmother. Where: The market in town. First: We bought a dozen oranges. Next: We chose two pounds of yam. Last: We carried the heavy bags home and made juice. How I felt: Tired but happy.

Let's Explore

Pick one real thing you did and fill in a plan like the one above in your head. Who was there? Where were you? What happened, in order? Once you have your plan, the writing is already half done.

Writing Your First Draft

Once your plan is ready, you write your first draft. A first draft is your very first try at writing the story out in full sentences. It does not have to be perfect. Real writers always write a first draft, then go back and make it better later.

Using the market plan, your first draft might read: "On Saturday I went to the market with my grandmother. First, we bought a dozen sweet oranges. Next, we chose two pounds of yam. Then we carried the heavy bags all the way home. After that, we made fresh orange juice. I was tired, but I was very happy." See how the plan turned into a real story? Notice the order words, the conjunction but joining "tired" and "happy", and the adjectives sweet and heavy.

Remember

Writing has steps, just like folding a paper boat. First you plan, then you write a draft, and later you read it over and fix it up. Take it one step at a time, and you can write anything.

Let's Practice

Plan your story: Use a graphic organizer in your head to plan a short Language Experience Story about something you did today. Decide who was there, where it happened, and what happened first, next, and last.

Write a draft: Write a first draft of three or four sentences, using at least one order word (first, next, then) and one adjective.

Word Power

Vowel: the letters a, e, i, o, u. Every word needs at least one. Example: the word cat has the vowel a.
Long vowel: a vowel that "says its own name", like the a in cake or the i in kite.
Consonant blend: two consonants next to each other whose sounds you can still hear, like bl in black or st in stop.
R-controlled vowel: a vowel whose sound is changed by an r right after it, as in car, bird, corn.
Syllable: a beat or "chunk" in a word. Clap once for each beat. Example: pen-cil has two syllables.
Sight word: a common word you learn to read in a flash, without sounding it out, like the, was, said.
Context clue: the other words around a hard word that help you guess its meaning, like clues in a treasure hunt.
Conjunction: a joining word that links two ideas, like and or but.
Adjective: a describing word that tells us more about a person, place, or thing. Example: a juicy, yellow banana.
Negative: a word or phrase that says "no" or "not", like isn't or don't.
Noun: a naming word for a person, place, or thing, like girl, market, spoon.
Pronoun: a small word that stands in for a noun, like her in "Maya likes her hat".
Preposition: a little word that tells where something is or which way it goes, like on, under, beside.
Language Experience Story: a piece of writing about something real that truly happened to you.
Retell: to tell a story again, in your own words and in the right order.
