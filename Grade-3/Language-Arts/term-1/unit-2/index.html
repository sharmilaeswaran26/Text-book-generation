Speaking & Listening

The Gift of Good Listening

Listening is much more than simply hearing. You hear many sounds all day — a dog barking, a car passing, the wind in the trees — without really paying attention to them. Listening is different. Listening means you give your full attention to what someone is saying, so that you truly understand them.

A good listener does a few simple things. You look at the person who is speaking. You keep your body still and your mouth quiet. You think carefully about the words you hear. And you wait politely for your turn before you speak. When you listen like this, two good things happen: you understand the speaker, and the speaker feels respected.

Think about story time in your classroom. When your teacher reads a story aloud, the children who listen well are the ones who can answer the questions afterwards. They remember what happened, and in what order. Good listening is like opening a door so that the ideas can come in.

Remember

A good listener looks at the speaker, stays quiet and still, thinks about the words, and waits for their turn to talk.

Retelling What You Hear

One of the best ways to show that you listened well is to retell what you heard. To retell a story means to say it again in your own words, in the right order, with the most important parts.

When you retell, it helps to put the events in the order they happened. We have some lovely little words that help us do this: first, after that, then, and finally. These are called order words, and they keep your retelling tidy.

Let's Explore

Suppose you listened to a story about a boy named Andre who lost his goat. You might retell it like this: "First, Andre's goat ran away into the bush. After that, he looked everywhere for it. Then, he heard a little bleating sound near the river. Finally, he found his goat eating sweet grass by the water." See how the order words hold the whole story together?

Speaking Clearly in Standard Jamaican English

When we speak so that everyone — at school, on the radio, or far away — can understand us easily, we use Standard Jamaican English, which we often call SJE for short. It is the kind of careful, clear English you use when you read aloud in class, give a talk, or speak to a guest.

At home and with friends, you may speak in our warm Jamaican Creole, and that is a precious part of who we are. But it is also important to learn to speak clearly in Standard Jamaican English, because it helps you to be understood by people everywhere. A clever speaker knows both, and chooses the right one for the right moment.

To speak clearly, remember these helpers: speak in a voice loud enough to be heard, but not shouting; say your words slowly and fully, not all rushed together; and look at the people you are speaking to.

Think About It

Imagine you are telling your whole class about your favourite game. How would you stand? How loudly would you speak? Would you look down at your shoes, or up at your friends?

Giving and Following Instructions

Sometimes speaking means giving an instruction — telling someone exactly what to do. Good instructions are short, clear, and given in a voice everyone can hear. Think of these classroom instructions: "Stop writing." "Close your book." "Stand up." "Sit and listen carefully." Each one is short and easy to follow.

Following instructions is just as important as giving them. When you listen carefully and do exactly what was asked, you show that you are a good listener. You have practised this many times in games like Simon Says, One-Two-Three Red Light, and Mother May I. In those games, the player who listens best is the player who wins.

Quick Check

If a friend says, "Hop on one foot, then clap twice," what are the two things you must do, and in what order?

Asking Good Questions

When you do not understand something, the best thing you can do is ask a question. Asking questions is a sign of a smart learner, not a silly one. If a guest speaker visits your class and you are unsure about something they said, you might politely ask, "Could you please explain that part again?" or "What did you mean when you said...?"

Good questions help you gain clarity, which means they help you understand more clearly. A good question often begins with one of these helpful words: who, what, when, where, why, or how. Keep these words in your mind — they will become very important again when you reach the writing lessons.

Did You Know?

The cleverest people in the world are not the ones who never have questions. They are the ones who are brave enough to ask them. Every question you ask helps your mind to grow.

Let's Practice

Have a friend or your teacher read you a short story. Then retell it using the order words first, after that, then, and finally. Try to include the most important parts and to speak clearly in Standard Jamaican English.

Word Skills

Using Context Clues

Imagine you are reading a story and you come to a word you do not know. What can you do? One of the very best tricks is to use context clues. The context is all the other words and sentences around the tricky word. Very often, those words give you clues about what the new word means, just like the clues in a mystery.

Let us try it together. Read this sentence: "The hungry dog gobbled up its food in just a few seconds." Even if you have never seen the word gobbled before, the other words help you. The dog was hungry, and it ate its food in just a few seconds. So gobbled must mean to eat something very quickly. You worked it out like a true detective, without even needing a dictionary.

Here is another one. Read this: "Grandma's soup was so delicious that everyone asked for a second bowl." If everyone wanted a second bowl, the soup must have tasted very good. So delicious means very tasty. The clues were right there in the sentence.

Let's Explore

Try this sentence on your own. "The path was so narrow that we had to walk one behind the other." What do you think narrow means? Look at the clue — they could not walk side by side. Yes, narrow means very thin, with little space across.

Look Around You

When you meet a new word, do not panic. Look at the words and sentences around it. They are clues that will help you guess the meaning.

The Endings That Change Words

Sometimes a small ending is added to the end of a word, and that little ending changes the word in some way. These special endings are called inflectional endings. The three you will meet most often are -s, -ed, and -ing.

We add -s to a naming word to show there is more than one. One cat becomes two cats. We also add -s to a doing word to go with he, she, or it: "She jumps," "He runs."

We add -ed to many doing words to show that the action already happened, in the past. Today I walk to school, but yesterday I walked to school. The little ending -ed tells us the action is finished and in the past.

We add -ing to show that an action is happening right now, still going on. "I am playing." "She is singing." "The baby is sleeping." Whenever you see -ing, you can picture the action happening this very moment.

Word Power

Watch how one little word can change. Start with play. Add -s and you get plays (she plays). Add -ed and you get played (we played yesterday). Add -ing and you get playing (they are playing now). The same root word, dressed up in three different endings!

Quick Check

Look at the word helping. Cover the ending -ing with your finger. What smaller word do you see? Yes — help! This is a wonderful trick for reading big words.

Words You Just Know

Some words come up so very often that you do not want to stop and sound them out every time. Words like the, was, said, come, they, and here appear on almost every page you read. These are called sight words, because you learn to read them in a single glance, just by seeing them.

The more you read, the more sight words you will collect, and the faster and smoother your reading will become. Reading smoothly, without stopping and starting too much, is called reading fluently. When you read fluently, the words flow like someone speaking, and the story comes alive. A lovely way to practise your sight words is with games, songs, and rhymes.

Did You Know?

Good readers do not read every word slowly, letter by letter. Their eyes glide quickly over the words they already know, and they slow down only for the new ones. That is why practising your sight words makes all your reading easier.

Let's Practice

Take the word cook. Add the three endings to make cooks, cooked, and cooking, and use each one in a sentence of your own. Then find three new words in your reading this week and use the context clues around them to guess what each one means.

Comprehension

What Is the Main Idea?

The main idea is what a paragraph or a story is mostly about. It is the one big thing the writer wants you to know. Everything else in the paragraph is there to tell you more about that one big idea.

Here is a simple way to think about it. Imagine a paragraph is like a small basket of fruit. The main idea is the basket itself — the thing that holds everything together. The pieces of fruit inside are the smaller details. The main idea holds all the details together neatly.

Let us read a short paragraph and find its main idea: "My dog Brownie is a wonderful pet. He runs to meet me at the gate every afternoon. He keeps me company when I do my homework. At night, he sleeps near my bed and keeps me safe." What is this whole paragraph mostly about? It is mostly about why Brownie is a wonderful pet. That is the main idea.

Look Around You

Pick up any storybook in your classroom and read one short paragraph. Ask yourself, "What is this paragraph mostly about?" The answer you give is the main idea.

Supporting Details

Look again at the paragraph about Brownie. The sentences that tell us more about the main idea — that he runs to the gate, that he keeps you company, that he keeps you safe at night — are called the supporting details. They are like helpers. Their job is to support the main idea, the way the legs of a table hold up the tabletop.

Supporting details answer little questions about the main idea, such as how, what, why, or when. When you can find the main idea and then point to the details that support it, you truly understand what you are reading.

Let's Explore

Read this little paragraph: "Saturday is my favourite day. I get to sleep a little longer in the morning. My whole family eats breakfast together. In the afternoon, I play football with my friends." The main idea is that Saturday is the writer's favourite day. Can you find the three details that support it? Yes — sleeping longer, family breakfast, and playing football.

Using a Graphic Organiser

A graphic organiser is a simple drawing or chart that helps you sort out your ideas. One very helpful organiser for the main idea looks like a table with a big idea on top and little details holding it up underneath, almost like a roof on top of pillars. Here is one way to lay out the Brownie paragraph:

PartIdea
Main IdeaBrownie is a wonderful pet.
Detail 1He runs to meet me at the gate.
Detail 2He keeps me company at homework time.
Detail 3He sleeps near my bed and keeps me safe.

When you fill in an organiser like this, the main idea and its supporting details become very easy to see. You will use graphic organisers again when you plan your own writing.

Quick Check

In the chart above, which line shows the big idea that the whole paragraph is about — the top line, or one of the detail lines?

Let's Practice

Read this paragraph: "Our school garden is a busy place. The children water the plants each morning. Bees buzz from flower to flower. Ripe tomatoes hang on the vines." Write down the main idea in one sentence, then list two details that support it.

Grammar

Verbs: The Doing Words

A verb is a doing word, an action word. It tells what someone or something does. In the sentence "The girl runs," the verb is runs. In "Birds fly," the verb is fly. In "We eat dinner," the verb is eat.

Verbs are very busy words. Almost every sentence you say or write needs a verb, because a sentence usually tells us about something happening. Run, jump, sing, read, cook, laugh, swim, write — all of these are verbs.

Look Around You

Watch the people near you for a moment. What are they doing? Maybe someone is writing, someone is talking, someone is smiling. Each of those doing words is a verb.

Telling Time with Tenses: Now and Before

Verbs can tell us when something happens. This is called the tense of the verb. The present tense tells us about something happening now: "I walk to school." "She plays netball." The past tense tells us about something that already happened. Most of the time, we make the past tense by adding -ed: "Yesterday I walked to school."

But some special verbs do not follow the -ed rule. "Eat" did not become "eated" — it became "ate." We call these irregular verbs, and you simply have to remember them.

Did You Know?

The word tense is a special grammar word, but you already use tenses perfectly every day. When you say "I am happy now" and "I was happy yesterday," you are using the present and the past without even thinking about it.

Making Verbs Agree: He, She, and It

When you talk about he, she, or it — that is, one single person or thing — you usually add an -s to the verb in the present tense. Look carefully and you will see the pattern:

"I run," but "He runs." "You play," but "She plays." "They eat," but "It eats."

So when the doer is he, she, or it, the verb takes that extra -s. We call this subject–verb agreement, because the verb must agree with the subject doing the action. A few common verbs change in their own special way: "She goes" (not "she gos"), and "He does" (not "he dos").

Word Power

Remember this little rhyme: "For he, she, it, add an s to fit." So we say "He runs," "She sings," "It jumps." The single -s makes the sentence sound correct.

Quick Check

Which one is correct: "Maria walk to school" or "Maria walks to school"? Since Maria is one person — a she — the verb needs the extra -s. The correct sentence is "Maria walks to school."

Adverbs: Words That Tell More

An adverb is a word that tells us more about a verb. It can tell us how, when, or where an action happens.

Some adverbs tell us how: "The turtle walked slowly." "She sang sweetly." Many adverbs that tell how end in the letters -ly. Some adverbs tell us when: "We will play today." "She arrived yesterday." Some adverbs tell us where: "Come and sit here." "The cat is hiding outside."

Let's Explore

Take the plain sentence "The boy ran." Now add an adverb and watch it grow. How did he run? "The boy ran quickly." When did he run? "The boy ran yesterday." Where did he run? "The boy ran outside." Each adverb adds a new piece of information.

Remember

An adverb tells us how, when, or where something happens. Words like slowly, today, and outside are all adverbs.

A Quick Word About Pronouns

A pronoun is a little word that takes the place of a naming word, so we do not have to keep repeating it. Listen to this without pronouns: "Sam likes football. Sam plays football every day. Sam is very good at football." It sounds a bit clumsy. Now listen with pronouns: "Sam likes football. He plays it every day. He is very good at it." Much smoother! The pronoun he takes the place of Sam, and it takes the place of football. Other pronouns you know are she, they, we, and I.

Think About It

In the sentence "Grandma made soup, and she shared it with us," which word is the pronoun that takes the place of "Grandma"? Yes — the word she.

Let's Practice

Change these present-tense sentences into the past tense: "I play in the yard." "She jumps the rope." "We walk to the shop." Then choose the correct verb: "The baby (cry / cries) when she is hungry." Finally, add an adverb to "The old man walked ________" to tell how he walked.

Writing

What Makes a Paragraph

A paragraph is a group of sentences that are all about one main idea. Writing a paragraph is like building one yourself. You choose one topic, and every sentence in the paragraph stays on that topic.

A good paragraph usually begins with a sentence that tells the main idea. Then come a few sentences that give the supporting details. So when you write, you are using the very same main idea and details you learned to find when reading. Reading and writing are like two sides of the same coin.

Did You Know?

When you start a brand-new paragraph, you leave a little space at the beginning of the first line. This little space is called an indent, and it tells the reader, "A new idea is beginning here."

Planning with a Graphic Organiser

The best writers do not just start writing straight away. First, they plan. Planning means thinking about your ideas and putting them in order before you write your sentences. A graphic organiser is the perfect tool for this. Suppose you want to write a paragraph about your favourite fruit, the mango. Before writing, you fill in an organiser like this:

PartIdea
Main IdeaThe mango is my favourite fruit.
Detail 1It is sweet and juicy.
Detail 2It grows on a big tree in our yard.
Detail 3I love to eat it in the summer.

Now your ideas are sorted and ready. Planning first makes the writing part so much easier, because you already know exactly what you want to say.

Building with a Paragraph Frame

A paragraph frame is a friendly helper that gives you some words to start with, and leaves spaces for you to fill in your own ideas. It is like the frame of a house: the strong shape is already there, and you fill in the rest. Here is a simple paragraph frame you can use:

My favourite ________ is ________.
One reason is that ________.
Another reason is that ________.
Finally, ________.
That is why I love ________.

Let us fill in the frame using our mango ideas: "My favourite fruit is the mango. One reason is that it is sweet and juicy. Another reason is that it grows on a big tree in our yard. Finally, I love to eat it in the cool summer days. That is why I love the mango so much." By planning with the organiser and building with the frame, you have written a neat, well-organised paragraph all about one main idea.

Look Around You

Look at something in your home that you love — a toy, a pet, a special meal. Could you fill in the paragraph frame about it? Try it in your head right now.

Making Your Writing Even Better

When you finish your first try at writing, we call it your first draft. A first draft is never meant to be perfect, and that is perfectly all right. The next step is to read it again and make it better. This is called revising.

One of the best ways to improve your writing is to add details and describing words. Plain writing tells us a little; rich writing paints a picture. Compare "I ate a mango" with "I happily ate a sweet, juicy yellow mango under the shady tree." The second sentence helps us see, taste, and feel the moment, because the writer added describing words and an adverb (happily).

It also helps to share your writing with a friend, an "elbow partner" sitting next to you. Your partner can tell you which parts they liked and which parts they did not quite understand. Then you can fix those parts in your next draft.

Remember

Good writing happens in steps. First you plan, then you write a draft, then you make it better by adding details and describing words. No one writes perfectly on the very first try.

Let's Practice

Plan and write your own paragraph about your favourite place. Use a graphic organiser first to sort your main idea and two details, then use the paragraph frame to write your sentences. Remember to add at least two describing words to make your writing come alive.

Sight word: a common word you learn to read at once, without sounding it out, like the, was, or said.
Fluently: reading smoothly and easily, without too much stopping and starting.
Main idea: what a whole paragraph or story is mostly about.
Supporting details: the smaller pieces of information that tell us more about the main idea.
Graphic organiser: a simple chart or drawing that helps you sort out your ideas.
Verb: an action or "doing" word, like run, sing, or eat.
Tense: whether an action is happening now (present) or already happened (past).
Irregular verb: a verb that does not add -ed for the past tense, like go → went or eat → ate.
