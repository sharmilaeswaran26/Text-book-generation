# Unit 2: Word by Word, Becoming a Storyteller

**Grade 3 · Language Arts · Term 1**

---

## Hello, Young Learner!

Imagine you have just come home from school, bursting with news. Something exciting happened in the schoolyard today, and you cannot wait to tell your mother all about it. So you take a deep breath and begin: "Mummy, you will never guess what happened!" In that one moment, you are doing something truly wonderful. You are using **language**.

Language is how we share what is in our hearts and our heads. We use it when we speak to a friend, when we listen to a story, when we read the words in a book, and when we pick up a pencil to write. Every single day, you use language dozens and dozens of times, often without even noticing.

In this chapter, you are going to become even better at all of these things. You will learn how to listen carefully and speak clearly. You will discover clever tricks for reading brand-new words you have never seen before. You will learn how to find the most important idea hidden inside a story. You will meet some new grammar friends, like verbs and adverbs, that make your sentences come alive. And best of all, you will learn how to plan and write your very own paragraphs, so you can tell your stories on paper too. Let us begin this word-by-word adventure together.

---

## Learning Goals

By the time you reach the last page of this chapter, you will be able to:

- Listen carefully and speak clearly to your friends, your teacher, and your class using Standard Jamaican English.
- Retell a story in the right order and share the most important parts.
- Read new and tricky words by using the words around them as clues.
- Notice how endings like **-s**, **-ed**, and **-ing** change a word.
- Find the **main idea** of a paragraph or story, and the small details that support it.
- Use **verbs** correctly in the present and the past, and make them agree with words like *he*, *she*, and *it*.
- Use **adverbs** to tell how, when, or where something happens.
- Plan your writing with a graphic organiser and a paragraph frame, then write a neat, well-organised paragraph.

## Our Big Question

Here is the big question that ties this whole chapter together. Keep it in your mind as you read:

**How do good listeners, readers, and writers understand and share ideas clearly?**

By the end of the chapter, you will be able to answer this in your own words. You will see that listening well, reading carefully, knowing your grammar, and planning your writing are all parts of one big skill — the skill of sharing ideas so that other people truly understand you.

## Word Power

Here are some important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about learning them all right now. Each one is explained gently inside the lessons, so you will understand them fully as you read.

- **Context clues** – the other words and sentences around a new word that help you guess what it means. *Example: In "The puppy was so playful it would not stop jumping," the words tell you that "playful" means full of play.*
- **Inflectional ending** – a small ending added to a word, like **-s**, **-ed**, or **-ing**, that changes the word a little. *Example: "jump" becomes "jumping."*
- **Sight word** – a common word you learn to read at once, without sounding it out, like *the*, *was*, or *said*. *Example: You read "the" instantly, without stopping.*
- **Main idea** – what a whole paragraph or story is mostly about. *Example: A paragraph all about feeding a dog has the main idea "how to take care of a dog."*
- **Supporting details** – the smaller pieces of information that tell us more about the main idea. *Example: "Give the dog water" is a detail that supports the main idea about caring for a dog.*
- **Verb** – an action or "doing" word. *Example: In "Maria runs," the verb is "runs."*
- **Tense** – whether an action is happening now or already happened. *Example: "I play" is now; "I played" already happened.*
- **Adverb** – a word that tells how, when, or where something happens. *Example: In "She sang sweetly," the adverb is "sweetly."*
- **Pronoun** – a small word that takes the place of a naming word, like *he*, *she*, *it*, or *they*. *Example: Instead of "Sam ran," we can say "He ran."*
- **Paragraph** – a group of sentences that are all about one main idea. *Example: A paragraph about your favourite game keeps to that one topic.*
- **Paragraph frame** – a helpful guide with some words already written, that you fill in to build a paragraph. *Example: "First, _____. Then, _____. Finally, _____."*

# Part One: Becoming a Good Listener and Speaker

Long before you could read a single word, you already knew how to listen and to speak. These are the very first language skills any of us learn. In this first part of the chapter, you will become even better at both, because good listening and good speaking are the foundation of everything else.

## The Gift of Good Listening

Listening is much more than simply hearing. You hear many sounds all day — a dog barking, a car passing, the wind in the trees — without really paying attention to them. **Listening** is different. Listening means you give your full attention to what someone is saying, so that you truly understand them.

A good listener does a few simple things. You look at the person who is speaking. You keep your body still and your mouth quiet. You think carefully about the words you hear. And you wait politely for your turn before you speak. When you listen like this, two good things happen: you understand the speaker, and the speaker feels respected.

Think about story time in your classroom. When your teacher reads a story aloud, the children who listen well are the ones who can answer the questions afterwards. They remember what happened, and in what order. The children who are busy fidgeting or whispering often miss the important parts. Good listening is like opening a door so that the ideas can come in.

> **Remember:** A good listener looks at the speaker, stays quiet and still, thinks about the words, and waits for their turn to talk.

## Retelling What You Hear

One of the best ways to show that you listened well is to **retell** what you heard. To retell a story means to say it again in your own words, in the right order, with the most important parts.

When you retell, it helps to put the events in the order they happened. We have some lovely little words that help us do this: *first*, *after that*, *then*, and *finally*. These are called order words, and they keep your retelling tidy.

> **Let's Explore:** Suppose you listened to a story about a boy named Andre who lost his goat. You might retell it like this: "*First*, Andre's goat ran away into the bush. *After that*, he looked everywhere for it. *Then*, he heard a little bleating sound near the river. *Finally*, he found his goat eating sweet grass by the water." See how the order words hold the whole story together?

Try this with a story you know well, perhaps one your grandmother tells, or a tale you read in class. Retell it using *first*, *after that*, *then*, and *finally*. You will be surprised how clear and neat your story sounds.

## Speaking Clearly in Standard Jamaican English

When we speak so that everyone — at school, on the radio, or far away — can understand us easily, we use **Standard Jamaican English**, which we often call SJE for short. It is the kind of careful, clear English you use when you read aloud in class, give a talk, or speak to a guest.

At home and with friends, you may speak in our warm Jamaican Creole, and that is a precious part of who we are. But it is also important to learn to speak clearly in Standard Jamaican English, because it helps you to be understood by people everywhere. A clever speaker knows both, and chooses the right one for the right moment.

To speak clearly, remember these helpers: speak in a voice loud enough to be heard, but not shouting; say your words slowly and fully, not all rushed together; and look at the people you are speaking to.

> **Think About It:** Imagine you are telling your whole class about your favourite game. How would you stand? How loudly would you speak? Would you look down at your shoes, or up at your friends?

## Giving and Following Instructions

Sometimes speaking means giving an **instruction** — telling someone exactly what to do. Good instructions are short, clear, and given in a voice everyone can hear. Think of these classroom instructions: "Stop writing." "Close your book." "Stand up." "Sit and listen carefully." Each one is short and easy to follow.

Following instructions is just as important as giving them. When you listen carefully and do exactly what was asked, you show that you are a good listener. You have practised this many times in games like *Simon Says*, *One-Two-Three Red Light*, and *Mother May I*. In those games, the player who listens best is the player who wins.

> **Quick Check:** If a friend says, "Hop on one foot, then clap twice," what are the two things you must do, and in what order?

## Asking Good Questions

When you do not understand something, the best thing you can do is **ask a question**. Asking questions is a sign of a smart learner, not a silly one. If a guest speaker visits your class and you are unsure about something they said, you might politely ask, "Could you please explain that part again?" or "What did you mean when you said...?"

Good questions help you gain clarity, which means they help you understand more clearly. A good question often begins with one of these helpful words: *who*, *what*, *when*, *where*, *why*, or *how*. Keep these words in your mind — they will become very important again when you reach the writing part of this chapter.

> **Did You Know?** The cleverest people in the world are not the ones who never have questions. They are the ones who are brave enough to ask them. Every question you ask helps your mind to grow.

# Part Two: Reading New Words Like a Detective

You already know how to read many, many words. But here is the exciting truth: there will always be new words you have never seen before. A good reader does not stop and give up at a new word. Instead, a good reader becomes a word detective, using clever clues to work out what the word means. In this part, you will learn the detective's tricks.

## Using Context Clues

Imagine you are reading a story and you come to a word you do not know. What can you do? One of the very best tricks is to use **context clues**. The context is all the other words and sentences around the tricky word. Very often, those words give you clues about what the new word means, just like the clues in a mystery.

Let us try it together. Read this sentence: "The hungry dog **gobbled** up its food in just a few seconds." Even if you have never seen the word *gobbled* before, the other words help you. The dog was *hungry*, and it ate its food in *just a few seconds*. So *gobbled* must mean to eat something very quickly. You worked it out like a true detective, without even needing a dictionary.

Here is another one. Read this: "Grandma's soup was so **delicious** that everyone asked for a second bowl." If everyone wanted a second bowl, the soup must have tasted very good. So *delicious* means very tasty. The clues were right there in the sentence.

> **Let's Explore:** Try this sentence on your own. "The path was so **narrow** that we had to walk one behind the other." What do you think *narrow* means? Look at the clue — they could not walk side by side. Yes, *narrow* means very thin, with little space across.

Context clues are everywhere. They can be in the words of a sentence, in a whole paragraph, or even in a picture beside the words. Whenever you meet a new word, remember to look around it for clues before you give up.

> **Remember:** When you meet a new word, do not panic. Look at the words and sentences around it. They are clues that will help you guess the meaning.

## The Endings That Change Words

Now here is something interesting that good readers notice. Sometimes a small ending is added to the end of a word, and that little ending changes the word in some way. These special endings are called **inflectional endings**. The three you will meet most often are **-s**, **-ed**, and **-ing**.

Let us start with **-s**. We add *-s* to a naming word to show there is more than one. One *cat* becomes two *cats*. One *book* becomes many *books*. We also add *-s* to a doing word to go with *he*, *she*, or *it*: "She *jumps*," "He *runs*," "It *barks*."

Next is **-ed**. We add *-ed* to many doing words to show that the action already happened, in the past. Today I *walk* to school, but yesterday I *walked* to school. Today the dog *barks*, but last night the dog *barked*. The little ending *-ed* tells us the action is finished and in the past.

Last is **-ing**. We add *-ing* to show that an action is happening right now, still going on. "I am *playing*." "She is *singing*." "The baby is *sleeping*." Whenever you see *-ing*, you can picture the action happening this very moment.

> **Word Power:** Watch how one little word can change. Start with *play*. Add *-s* and you get *plays* (she plays). Add *-ed* and you get *played* (we played yesterday). Add *-ing* and you get *playing* (they are playing now). The same root word, dressed up in three different endings!

When you are reading and you see a long word, look to see if it has one of these endings. If you cover the ending with your finger, you will often find a word you already know underneath. *Jumping* is just *jump* with *-ing*. *Wanted* is just *want* with *-ed*. This is a wonderful trick for reading big words.

> **Quick Check:** Look at the word *helping*. Cover the ending *-ing* with your finger. What smaller word do you see? Yes — *help*!

## Words You Just Know

Some words come up so very often that you do not want to stop and sound them out every time. Words like *the*, *was*, *said*, *come*, *they*, and *here* appear on almost every page you read. These are called **sight words**, because you learn to read them in a single glance, just by seeing them.

The more you read, the more sight words you will collect, and the faster and smoother your reading will become. Reading smoothly, without stopping and starting too much, is called reading **fluently**. When you read fluently, the words flow like someone speaking, and the story comes alive.

A lovely way to practise your sight words is with games, songs, and rhymes. Many classrooms have a Word Wall covered with sight words, and reading them aloud each day, with a friend or on your own, helps the words stick in your memory.

> **Did You Know?** Good readers do not read every word slowly, letter by letter. Their eyes glide quickly over the words they already know, and they slow down only for the new ones. That is why practising your sight words makes all your reading easier.

# Part Three: Finding the Big Idea

You can read every single word in a story and still need one more skill: understanding what you have read. The most important part of understanding is being able to find the **main idea** — the big idea that the whole piece of writing is about. In this part of the chapter, you will learn how to find it.

## What Is the Main Idea?

The **main idea** is what a paragraph or a story is *mostly* about. It is the one big thing the writer wants you to know. Everything else in the paragraph is there to tell you more about that one big idea.

Here is a simple way to think about it. Imagine a paragraph is like a small basket of fruit. The main idea is the basket itself — the thing that holds everything together. The pieces of fruit inside are the smaller details. Without the basket, the fruit would roll all over the floor. The main idea holds all the details together neatly.

Let us read a short paragraph and find its main idea.

*"My dog Brownie is a wonderful pet. He runs to meet me at the gate every afternoon. He keeps me company when I do my homework. At night, he sleeps near my bed and keeps me safe."*

What is this whole paragraph mostly about? It is mostly about **why Brownie is a wonderful pet**. That is the main idea. Every sentence helps to tell us more about it.

> **Look Around You:** Pick up any storybook in your classroom and read one short paragraph. Ask yourself, "What is this paragraph mostly about?" The answer you give is the main idea.

## Supporting Details

Now look again at the paragraph about Brownie. The sentences that tell us *more* about the main idea — that he runs to the gate, that he keeps you company, that he keeps you safe at night — are called the **supporting details**. They are like helpers. Their job is to support the main idea, the way the legs of a table hold up the tabletop.

Supporting details answer little questions about the main idea, such as *how*, *what*, *why*, or *when*. When you can find the main idea and then point to the details that support it, you truly understand what you are reading.

> **Let's Explore:** Read this little paragraph: *"Saturday is my favourite day. I get to sleep a little longer in the morning. My whole family eats breakfast together. In the afternoon, I play football with my friends."* The main idea is that **Saturday is the writer's favourite day**. Can you find the three details that support it? Yes — sleeping longer, family breakfast, and playing football.

## Using a Graphic Organiser

A **graphic organiser** is a simple drawing or chart that helps you sort out your ideas. One very helpful organiser for the main idea looks like a table with a big idea on top and little details holding it up underneath, almost like a roof on top of pillars.

Here is one way to lay out the Brownie paragraph in an organiser:

| Main Idea | Brownie is a wonderful pet. |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| Detail 1 | He runs to meet me at the gate. |
| Detail 2 | He keeps me company at homework time. |
| Detail 3 | He sleeps near my bed and keeps me safe. |

When you fill in an organiser like this, the main idea and its supporting details become very easy to see. You will use graphic organisers again later, when you plan your own writing.

> **Quick Check:** In the chart above, which line shows the big idea that the whole paragraph is about — the top line, or one of the detail lines?

# Part Four: Grammar Friends That Make Sentences Strong

Grammar is simply the set of rules that help our words fit together neatly, like the pieces of a puzzle. When you know a little grammar, your sentences become clear and strong, and people understand you easily. In this part, you will meet some very useful grammar friends: verbs, tenses, subject–verb agreement, and adverbs.

## Verbs: The Doing Words

A **verb** is a doing word, an action word. It tells what someone or something *does*. In the sentence "The girl **runs**," the verb is *runs*. In "Birds **fly**," the verb is *fly*. In "We **eat** dinner," the verb is *eat*.

Verbs are very busy words. Almost every sentence you say or write needs a verb, because a sentence usually tells us about something happening. Run, jump, sing, read, cook, laugh, swim, write — all of these are verbs. Look around you right now. Anything that someone is *doing* can be named with a verb.

> **Look Around You:** Watch the people near you for a moment. What are they doing? Maybe someone is *writing*, someone is *talking*, someone is *smiling*. Each of those doing words is a verb.

## Telling Time with Tenses: Now and Before

Here is something clever about verbs: they can tell us *when* something happens. This is called the **tense** of the verb. For now, we will look at two tenses: the present and the past.

The **present tense** tells us about something happening now, today, or again and again. "I *walk* to school." "She *plays* netball." "We *eat* lunch at noon." These actions are happening in the present.

The **past tense** tells us about something that already happened, before now. Most of the time, we make the past tense by adding **-ed** to the verb, just as you learned earlier. "Yesterday I *walked* to school." "Last week she *played* netball." "We *ate* lunch an hour ago."

Did you notice something? "Walk" became "walked," and "play" became "played," by adding *-ed*. But "eat" did not become "eated" — it became "ate." Some special verbs do not follow the *-ed* rule. We call these **irregular verbs**, and you simply have to remember them. Here are a few common ones:

| Present (now) | Past (before) |
|---------------|----------------|
| go | went |
| run | ran |
| eat | ate |
| see | saw |
| come | came |

> **Did You Know?** The word *tense* is a special grammar word, but you already use tenses perfectly every day. When you say "I am happy now" and "I was happy yesterday," you are using the present and the past without even thinking about it.

## Making Verbs Agree: He, She, and It

Now here is a rule that helps your sentences sound just right. When you talk about **he**, **she**, or **it** — that is, one single person or thing — you usually add an **-s** to the verb in the present tense.

Look carefully and you will see the pattern:

- "I **run**," but "He **runs**."
- "You **play**," but "She **plays**."
- "They **eat**," but "It **eats**."
- "We **walk**," but "He **walks**."

So when the doer is *he*, *she*, or *it*, the verb takes that extra *-s*. We call this **subject–verb agreement**, because the verb must agree with the subject doing the action. A few common verbs change in their own special way: "She **goes**" (not "she gos"), and "He **does**" (not "he dos").

> **Word Power:** Remember this little rhyme: "For *he*, *she*, *it*, add an *s* to fit." So we say "He runs," "She sings," "It jumps." The single *-s* makes the sentence sound correct.

> **Quick Check:** Which one is correct: "Maria walk to school" or "Maria walks to school"? Since Maria is one person — a *she* — the verb needs the extra *-s*. The correct sentence is "Maria **walks** to school."

## Adverbs: Words That Tell More

Our last grammar friend is the **adverb**. An adverb is a word that tells us more about a verb. It can tell us **how**, **when**, or **where** an action happens. Adverbs make your sentences much more interesting and clear.

Let us look at each kind.

Some adverbs tell us **how** something happens. "The turtle walked **slowly**." "She sang **sweetly**." "He spoke **softly**." The words *slowly*, *sweetly*, and *softly* all tell us *how* the action was done. Many adverbs that tell *how* end in the letters *-ly*.

Some adverbs tell us **when** something happens. "We will play **today**." "She arrived **yesterday**." "I will see you **tomorrow**." The words *today*, *yesterday*, and *tomorrow* all tell us *when*.

Some adverbs tell us **where** something happens. "Come and sit **here**." "The cat is hiding **outside**." "Put the ball **there**." The words *here*, *outside*, and *there* all tell us *where*.

> **Let's Explore:** Take the plain sentence "The boy ran." Now add an adverb and watch it grow. *How* did he run? "The boy ran **quickly**." *When* did he run? "The boy ran **yesterday**." *Where* did he run? "The boy ran **outside**." Each adverb adds a new piece of information.

> **Remember:** An adverb tells us **how**, **when**, or **where** something happens. Words like *slowly*, *today*, and *outside* are all adverbs.

## A Quick Word About Pronouns

Before we leave grammar, let us meet one more helper: the **pronoun**. A pronoun is a little word that takes the place of a naming word, so we do not have to keep repeating it.

Listen to this without pronouns: "Sam likes football. Sam plays football every day. Sam is very good at football." It sounds a bit clumsy, doesn't it? Now listen with pronouns: "Sam likes football. **He** plays it every day. **He** is very good at it." Much smoother! The pronoun *he* takes the place of *Sam*, and *it* takes the place of *football*. Other pronouns you know are *she*, *they*, *we*, and *I*.

> **Think About It:** In the sentence "Grandma made soup, and she shared it with us," which word is the pronoun that takes the place of "Grandma"? Yes — the word *she*.

# Part Five: Planning and Writing Paragraphs

Now we come to one of the most exciting parts of language: writing your very own ideas down on paper. When you write, you can share your thoughts with people far away, and even with people who have not yet been born. In this last part, you will learn how to plan and build a neat, well-organised paragraph.

## What Makes a Paragraph

A **paragraph** is a group of sentences that are all about one main idea. Do you remember the main idea from Part Three? Writing a paragraph is like building one yourself. You choose one topic, and every sentence in the paragraph stays on that topic.

A good paragraph usually begins with a sentence that tells the main idea. Then come a few sentences that give the supporting details. So when you write, you are using the very same main idea and details you learned to find when reading. Reading and writing are like two sides of the same coin.

> **Did You Know?** When you start a brand-new paragraph, you leave a little space at the beginning of the first line. This little space is called an *indent*, and it tells the reader, "A new idea is beginning here."

## Planning with a Graphic Organiser

The best writers do not just start writing straight away. First, they **plan**. Planning means thinking about your ideas and putting them in order before you write your sentences. A graphic organiser is the perfect tool for this.

Suppose you want to write a paragraph about your favourite fruit, the mango. Before writing, you fill in an organiser like this:

| Main Idea | The mango is my favourite fruit. |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|
| Detail 1 | It is sweet and juicy. |
| Detail 2 | It grows on a big tree in our yard. |
| Detail 3 | I love to eat it in the summer. |

Now your ideas are sorted and ready. Planning first makes the writing part so much easier, because you already know exactly what you want to say.

## Building with a Paragraph Frame

A **paragraph frame** is a friendly helper that gives you some words to start with, and leaves spaces for you to fill in your own ideas. It is like the frame of a house: the strong shape is already there, and you fill in the rest.

Here is a simple paragraph frame you can use:

> My favourite ________ is ________.
> One reason is that ________.
> Another reason is that ________.
> Finally, ________.
> That is why I love ________.

Let us fill in the frame using our mango ideas from the organiser:

> My favourite **fruit** is **the mango**.
> One reason is that **it is sweet and juicy**.
> Another reason is that **it grows on a big tree in our yard**.
> Finally, **I love to eat it in the cool summer days**.
> That is why I love **the mango so much**.

Look at that! By planning with the organiser and building with the frame, you have written a neat, well-organised paragraph all about one main idea.

> **Look Around You:** Look at something in your home that you love — a toy, a pet, a special meal. Could you fill in the paragraph frame about it? Try it in your head right now.

## Making Your Writing Even Better

When you finish your first try at writing, we call it your **first draft**. A first draft is never meant to be perfect, and that is perfectly all right. The next step is to read it again and make it better. This is called **revising**.

One of the best ways to improve your writing is to add **details** and **describing words**. Plain writing tells us a little; rich writing paints a picture. Compare these two sentences:

- Plain: "I ate a mango."
- Better: "I happily ate a sweet, juicy yellow mango under the shady tree."

The second sentence helps us see, taste, and feel the moment, because the writer added describing words and an adverb (*happily*). Do you remember adverbs from Part Four? Here they are again, making your writing come alive.

It also helps to share your writing with a friend, an "elbow partner" sitting next to you. Your partner can tell you which parts they liked and which parts they did not quite understand. Then you can fix those parts in your next draft. Helping each other improve is called peer review, and it makes everyone a better writer.

> **Remember:** Good writing happens in steps. First you plan, then you write a draft, then you make it better by adding details and describing words. No one writes perfectly on the very first try.

## Practice Together

Let us practise some of these skills together, step by step, before you try them on your own. Read each one slowly and work it out in your mind or with your teacher.

**1. Listening and retelling.** Have a friend or your teacher read you a short story. Then retell it using the order words: *first*, *after that*, *then*, and *finally*. Try to include the most important parts.

**2. Context clues.** Read this sentence: "The room was so **gloomy** that we could hardly see the table." Use the clue "we could hardly see" to guess what *gloomy* means. (It means dark.)

**3. Inflectional endings.** Take the word *cook*. Now add the three endings: *cook**s***, *cook**ed***, *cook**ing***. Use each one in a sentence of your own.

**4. Main idea.** Read this paragraph: *"Our school garden is a busy place. The children water the plants each morning. Bees buzz from flower to flower. Ripe tomatoes hang on the vines."* What is the main idea? (The main idea is that the school garden is a busy place.)

**5. Subject–verb agreement.** Choose the correct verb: "The baby (cry / cries) when she is hungry." Because the baby is one — a *she* — the correct word is *cries*.

**6. Adverbs.** Add an adverb to this sentence to tell *how*: "The old man walked ________." (You might say *slowly*.)

**7. Writing a paragraph.** Use the paragraph frame to write about your favourite game. Plan it first in an organiser, then fill in the frame.

## Try It Yourself

Now it is your turn to work on your own. Take your time, and do your best work.

1. Read a short paragraph from any book and write down its main idea in one sentence. Then list two details that support it.
2. Find three new words in your reading this week. For each one, use the context clues around it to guess the meaning, then check with a dictionary or your teacher.
3. Write three sentences about your day. In each one, underline the verb and circle any adverb that tells how, when, or where.
4. Change these present-tense sentences into the past tense: "I play in the yard." "She jumps the rope." "We walk to the shop." (Hint: remember the *-ed* ending and the irregular verbs.)
5. Plan and write your own paragraph about your favourite place. Use a graphic organiser first, then a paragraph frame. Remember to add at least two describing words.

## Chapter Summary

In this chapter, you grew stronger in all four parts of language: listening, speaking, reading, and writing.

You learned that a good listener gives full attention, and that retelling a story with order words like *first*, *after that*, *then*, and *finally* shows you understood it. You learned to speak clearly in Standard Jamaican English and to ask good questions when you need to understand more.

You became a word detective, using context clues to work out new words. You discovered that the inflectional endings *-s*, *-ed*, and *-ing* change a word, and that knowing common sight words helps you read smoothly and fluently.

You learned to find the main idea — what a paragraph is mostly about — and the supporting details that hold it up, and to sort them neatly in a graphic organiser. You met your grammar friends: verbs, which are doing words; the present and past tenses, which tell *when*; subject–verb agreement, which adds *-s* for *he*, *she*, and *it*; adverbs, which tell *how*, *when*, or *where*; and pronouns, which take the place of naming words.

Finally, you learned to plan a paragraph with an organiser, build it with a paragraph frame, and make it better by adding details and describing words. Step by step, word by word, you are becoming a storyteller.

## Remember

- A good listener looks at the speaker, stays quiet, thinks, and waits for a turn to speak.
- Retell stories in order using *first*, *after that*, *then*, and *finally*.
- Use **context clues** — the words around a new word — to guess its meaning.
- The endings **-s**, **-ed**, and **-ing** change a word; cover the ending to find the smaller word inside.
- The **main idea** is what a paragraph is mostly about; **supporting details** tell more about it.
- A **verb** is a doing word; **tense** tells whether the action is now (present) or before (past).
- For **he**, **she**, and **it**, add an **-s** to the verb: "He runs," "She sings," "It jumps."
- An **adverb** tells **how**, **when**, or **where**: *slowly*, *today*, *outside*.
- Plan your writing with a graphic organiser and a paragraph frame, then add details to make it better.

## Review Questions

1. **Think and Answer:** What are three things a good listener does while someone is speaking?
2. **Discuss:** Why is it helpful to use order words like *first*, *then*, and *finally* when you retell a story?
3. **Think and Answer:** Read this sentence: "The trail was so **rugged** that the bus could not pass." Using the context clues, what do you think *rugged* means?
4. **Compare:** What is the difference between the word *jump* and the word *jumped*? What does the ending *-ed* tell us?
5. **Think and Answer:** What is the main idea of a paragraph? How is it different from a supporting detail?
6. **Write:** Write one sentence in the present tense, then change it into the past tense.
7. **Think and Answer:** Why do we say "She **runs**" and not "She run"? Which grammar rule is this?
8. **Imagine:** Imagine you are watching a parade. Write one sentence using an adverb that tells *how* the band played, and one that tells *where* the people stood.
9. **Discuss:** What are the steps you should take when you want to write a good paragraph?
10. **Write:** Choose your favourite food. Plan a paragraph about it using a main idea and two supporting details, then write the paragraph.

## Fun Activity

**Word Detective Card Game.** With a friend or your family, make a small pile of cards. On each card, write a sentence that has one tricky word, with the tricky word underlined — for example, "The puppy was so **timid** that it hid behind the chair." Take turns drawing a card and using the context clues to guess what the underlined word means. If you guess correctly, you keep the card. The player with the most cards at the end is the Master Word Detective! You can also play a second round where each player adds an *-s*, *-ed*, or *-ing* ending to a word and uses it in a brand-new sentence.
