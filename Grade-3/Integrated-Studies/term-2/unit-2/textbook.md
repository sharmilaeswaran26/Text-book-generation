## A Letter to the Young Reader

Look down at your feet for a moment. You are standing on the island of Jamaica, a beautiful country surrounded on every side by the wide, blue Caribbean Sea. From where you stand, you might think that Jamaica is all on its own, far away from everywhere else. But that is not true at all. Even though the sea wraps around our island, Jamaica is joined to many other countries by all sorts of invisible threads.

Think about it. The shoes on your feet may have been made in another country. The cartoon you watched may have been drawn far across the ocean. When a Jamaican athlete runs in the Olympics, people in dozens of countries cheer her on. When visitors fly in to enjoy our sunshine and our beaches, they are reaching across the sea to us, and we are reaching back to them. Jamaica is a friendly island, and it has friends all around the world.

In this chapter, you are going to discover the many ways that Jamaica relates to other countries. You will learn why these relationships matter so much, and you will find out how you are both alike and different from children who live far away. Best of all, you will learn that you yourself have an important part to play in keeping Jamaica friendly with the rest of the world. So let us set off on a journey, not just around our island, but all the way around the globe.

---

## What You Will Learn

In this chapter you will learn about the different kinds of relationships, or relations, that Jamaica shares with other countries — through trade, sports, music, travel, tourism, family, and more. You will learn the meaning of important words such as *trade*, *import*, *export*, *interdependence*, and *tourist*, and you will use them correctly. You will discover why these relationships are so important to us: how they bring us goods we cannot make ourselves, how they let us share and learn, and how they help nations to become good friends. You will explore the ways in which you are like the people of other countries, and the ways in which you are different. And finally, you will learn how each of us, in our everyday actions, can help Jamaica to keep good and friendly relations with the countries of the world.

## The Big Questions

As you read, keep these four questions in your mind. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to answer each one in your own words.

1. What types of relations do we have with people of other countries?
2. Why is it important that we relate to other countries of the world?
3. In what ways am I like or different from the people outside of Jamaica with whom I relate?
4. How can I ensure that Jamaica maintains good relations with other countries?

## Words to Know

Here are some important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about memorising them now. You will understand them fully as you read, because each one is explained carefully in the lessons.

- **Country** – a land with its own people, government, flag, and name, such as Jamaica.
- **Relationship (relations)** – the way two people, or two countries, are connected to and treat each other.
- **Interdependence** – when two countries depend on and help each other, each giving something the other needs.
- **Trade** – the buying and selling of goods between people or between countries.
- **Import** – goods that come into Jamaica from another country.
- **Export** – goods that Jamaica sends out to be sold in another country.
- **Tourist** – a person who travels to visit a country for pleasure, rest, or to see new places.
- **Tourism** – the business of welcoming and caring for tourists who come to visit.
- **Culture** – the way a group of people live: their food, music, dress, language, and customs.
- **Communication** – sending and sharing messages, such as by letter, email, telephone, or the internet.
- **Cooperation** – working together with others to reach a shared goal.
- **Similarities** – the ways in which two things are alike.
- **Differences** – the ways in which two things are not the same.
- **Foreign exchange** – the money a country earns from other countries, often from visitors and from exports.

# Part One: The Many Links Between Jamaica and the World

You already know about relationships. You have a relationship with your mother and father, with your brothers and sisters, with your teacher, and with your friends at school. A **relationship** is the way two people are connected and the way they treat each other. Countries have relationships too. In this first part of the chapter, you will discover the many different ways that Jamaica is connected to other countries around the world.

## What Is a Relationship Between Countries?

When we say that Jamaica has a **relationship** with another country, we mean that the two countries are connected in some way and that they do things together. Just as you and your best friend share things, help each other, and spend time together, two countries can share goods, share ideas, help each other in hard times, and join together for sport, music, and learning.

Some relationships between countries are very close, and some are not as close. But almost every country in the world is joined to other countries in one way or another. No country lives completely on its own, just as no person lives completely on their own. We all need one another.

There is a special word for the way countries depend on and help each other. We call it **interdependence**. The word is made of two parts. "Inter" means *between*, and "dependence" means *needing help*. So interdependence means that countries need and help one another. When a country sends help to another country after a hurricane or an earthquake, that is interdependence at work. Jamaica both gives help and receives help, and that is a normal and healthy part of belonging to the world.

> **Think About It:** Think of a good friend of yours. Name two things you share with that friend, and one time that friend helped you. Countries can be friends in much the same way.

## Trade: Buying and Selling Across the Sea

One of the most important ways that Jamaica relates to other countries is through **trade**. Trade simply means the buying and selling of goods. When you go to the shop and buy a loaf of bread with money, you are trading. Countries trade with one another too, but on a much bigger scale.

Here is the important part. Some goods are sent out of Jamaica to be sold in other countries. Goods that we send away to sell are called **exports**. Jamaica exports many things that we grow and make on our island. We export bananas, coffee, sugar, and a special reddish earth called bauxite, which is used to make a metal called aluminium. People in faraway countries enjoy our Blue Mountain coffee and use products made from our bauxite.

Other goods come into Jamaica from other countries. Goods that come in from abroad are called **imports**. Jamaica imports many things that we do not grow or make here, or that we cannot make enough of. We import cars, computers, certain machines, and some kinds of food. The next time you are at home, look at the labels on things around you. You may be surprised to see how many of them say "Made in" another country. Those items are imports.

So remember the two words this way: **export** sends goods *out*, and **import** brings goods *in*. Both are part of trade, and both connect Jamaica to the wider world.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine a farmer in St. Elizabeth who grows lots of sweet bananas. Some of those bananas are packed into boxes and shipped across the sea to be sold in England. Those bananas are an export. Now imagine that the same farmer drives home in a car that was made in Japan. That car is an import. In one single day, this farmer is connected to both England and Japan through trade. That is how trade ties Jamaica to the world.

> **Did You Know?** When you collect the labels and tags from items around your home, you can find goods that came from many different countries — your toothpaste, your school bag, your toy, and your snacks may each come from a different part of the world. Each label tells a little story of trade.

## Sports: Cheering for Jamaica Around the World

Another wonderful way that Jamaica relates to other countries is through **sports**. Jamaicans love sport, and we are very good at it. Our athletes travel all over the world to compete, and people in many countries know and admire them.

Think about big sporting events such as the Olympic Games and the world athletics championships. At these events, athletes from many countries come together to compete. When a Jamaican sprinter races down the track, the whole world watches, and people everywhere learn the name of our small but mighty island. Jamaicans have made our country famous for running fast, and athletes like Veronica Campbell-Brown have proudly represented Jamaica at the Olympics and other world events.

Jamaicans also relate to other countries through games like cricket, football, and netball. In cricket, Jamaica plays alongside other Caribbean countries as part of the West Indies team. When we play and compete, we make friends, we learn from others, and we show the world the spirit of Jamaica.

> **Think About It:** Can you name a Jamaican athlete or sports team that has made you feel proud? When that person or team competes abroad, how do you think people in other countries come to feel about Jamaica?

## Music and Culture: Sharing Who We Are

Jamaica is famous all over the world for its **music**. Our reggae and dancehall are loved in country after country, far across the sea. When people in other lands hear Jamaican music, they learn a little about who we are, and they come to love our island.

Music is part of something larger called **culture**. Culture is the whole way a group of people live — their music, their food, their clothing, their language, and their customs. Jamaica shares its culture with the world, and the world shares its culture with us. Jamaican artists sometimes work together with singers from other countries, mixing our sounds with theirs to make something new and beautiful. We call this kind of working-together **collaboration**.

We also enjoy musical events right here in Jamaica that bring people together from many places. When visitors come to hear our music and we listen to theirs, we are sharing culture across the borders of the world.

> **Did You Know?** Jamaican reggae music is so important to the world that it has been honoured as a special treasure that belongs to all of humanity. People on every continent listen to and love the music born right here on our island.

## Travel and Tourism: Welcoming the World to Jamaica

Every single day, aeroplanes land at our airports and big cruise ships sail into our harbours, bringing thousands of visitors to Jamaica. A person who travels to visit a country for pleasure or rest is called a **tourist**. The business of welcoming and caring for these visitors is called **tourism**.

People come from all over the world to enjoy Jamaica's warm sunshine, our golden beaches, our blue sea, our friendly people, and our delicious food. They want to see our beautiful island and to feel our happy spirit. When tourists arrive, they stay in our hotels, eat in our restaurants, visit our beaches and waterfalls, and buy crafts made by Jamaican hands.

Tourism is one of the biggest ways that Jamaica relates to other countries, because it brings the people of the world right to our doorstep. A tourist who has a wonderful time in Jamaica will go home and tell their family and friends, and so our island makes more and more friends across the globe.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine a family from Canada who feel cold during their winter. They decide to fly to Jamaica for a holiday. They land at the airport, drive to a hotel near the sea, swim at the beach, taste ackee and saltfish for the first time, and buy a small wooden craft to take home. This family are tourists. While they are here, they meet kind Jamaicans, learn about our way of life, and fall in love with our island. When they go home, they carry good memories of Jamaica with them, and that is how new friendships between countries begin.

> **Think About It:** If a visitor from another country came to your community for the very first time, what is one beautiful thing about Jamaica you would want to show them?

## Family, Friends, and Messages Across the Sea

There is one more kind of relationship that may feel very close to your own heart. Many Jamaican families have relatives who live in other countries. Perhaps you have an aunt in New York, a grandmother in England, or a cousin in Canada. Jamaicans live and work in many parts of the world, and together they are called the **Jamaican diaspora** — the Jamaicans who live abroad.

Even when family members live far across the sea, we stay connected to them. We talk to them on the telephone, send them messages and pictures by email and over the internet, and write them letters. This sharing of messages is called **communication**. Thanks to communication, a child in Kingston can speak to a grandmother in London as though she were sitting in the next room.

You can build relationships with people in other countries too. Children sometimes write to a **pen pal** or an **e-pal** — a friend in another country whom they get to know through letters or email. By sharing news about your life and asking about theirs, you build a friendship that stretches across the ocean. This too is a real relationship between Jamaica and the world.

> **Did You Know?** Many of the people who help to build, lead, and care for Jamaica also have careers that connect us with other countries — pilots who fly between nations, hotel workers who welcome visitors, traders who buy and sell goods abroad, and athletes and musicians who represent us around the world.

# Part Two: Why These Relationships Matter

You have learned about the many ways that Jamaica is linked to other countries. But now we come to an important question. Why do these relationships matter so much? Why does it help Jamaica to be friends with countries far away? In this part of the chapter, you will discover the good things that come to us when we relate well to the rest of the world.

## We Get Things We Cannot Make Ourselves

Here is a simple but powerful reason why Jamaica needs other countries. No single country in the whole world can make everything that its people need. Not even the biggest and richest country can do that. Each country has some things it can make or grow well, and other things it cannot.

Jamaica is wonderful at growing bananas, coffee, sugar, and many other crops, and we are good at mining bauxite. But there are some things we do not make on our island. We do not build large aeroplanes or make most of our cars and computers. So we trade. We sell, or export, the goods we have plenty of, and we use the money to buy, or import, the goods we need from other countries.

This is **interdependence** in action. We give other countries what they want from us, and they give us what we want from them. Everyone is better off because of it. If countries did not trade and help one another, every country would have to do without many useful and important things.

> **Think About It:** Look around the room you are in right now. Can you spot one thing that probably came from another country? How would your day be different if that thing did not exist?

## We Share, We Learn, and We Grow

Relationships with other countries do far more than bring us goods. They also help us to learn and to grow. When Jamaica relates to other countries, we get to share our ideas with them, and we get to learn wonderful new things from them.

We learn from other countries in the area of **education**, when our students and teachers share lessons and ideas with people abroad. We learn in the area of **health**, when doctors and nurses share knowledge that helps to keep people well. We share and learn through **music**, **art**, **sport**, and **religion**, too. Each time we connect with another country, we have the chance to teach them something about Jamaica and to learn something new from them.

This sharing makes everyone richer, not in money, but in knowledge and friendship. A Jamaican child who writes to an e-pal in another country learns about a new way of life, and at the same time teaches that e-pal about Jamaica. Both children grow wiser, and both make a new friend. That is the gift of relating to the world.

> **Did You Know?** Even the religions practised in Jamaica show how connected we are to the world. Many of the faiths followed here came to our island long ago from other countries, and Jamaicans of different faiths still feel a bond with people of the same faith living all over the world.

## Tourism Brings Many Good Things

You have already learned that tourists travel to Jamaica to enjoy our island. But why is tourism so important to us? It turns out that welcoming visitors brings many good things to our country.

First, tourism brings money into Jamaica. When visitors stay in our hotels, eat our food, and buy our crafts, they spend money here. This money, earned from other countries, is called **foreign exchange**, and it helps our whole country. Second, tourism gives many Jamaicans **jobs**. Think of all the people needed to care for visitors: hotel workers, cooks, tour guides, taxi drivers, craft makers, and many more. Tourism gives work to thousands of Jamaicans and helps to feed their families.

Third, tourism lets us meet people from many different countries and cultures right here at home. When we welcome a visitor, we get to learn about their country, and they get to learn about ours. In this way, tourism opens a window to the whole world without us ever having to leave our island.

People come to Jamaica for many reasons. They are attracted by our warm weather and sunshine, our beautiful beaches, our blue sea, our green mountains and waterfalls, our friendly people, our tasty food, and our happy music. All of these things draw visitors to our shores, and all of them are gifts that Jamaica can proudly share.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine a hotel near the sea. A family of tourists arrives and stays for one week. While they are there, a Jamaican cook prepares their meals, a Jamaican cleaner keeps their room tidy, a Jamaican guide takes them to see a waterfall, and a Jamaican craft seller sells them a souvenir. The money the family spends helps to pay all of these workers, who then buy food and clothes for their own families. So one happy tourist visit helps many Jamaicans at once. That is why tourism matters so much.

> **Think About It:** Tourism brings many good things, but it can have some hard sides too, like crowded beaches or extra litter. Why do you think it is wise to think about both the good and the not-so-good sides of anything?

## Friendship and Help Between Nations

Perhaps the most beautiful reason of all for relating to other countries is friendship. When countries know each other and trade and share together, they become friends. And friends help one another in times of need.

Sometimes a country is struck by a terrible storm, a flood, or an earthquake. When this happens, friendly countries from around the world send help — food, water, medicine, and people to assist. Jamaica has received help from other countries during hard times, and Jamaica has also sent help to others. This giving and receiving of help is **interdependence** at its very best, and it is only possible because countries have built good relationships with one another over many years.

When nations are friends, the whole world becomes a safer, kinder, and happier place. This is why building good relationships with other countries is one of the most important things Jamaica can do.

> **Did You Know?** Jamaica belongs to groups of friendly countries that work together to help one another, especially our neighbours in the Caribbean. When Caribbean countries cooperate, each one becomes stronger than it could ever be alone.

# Part Three: How I Am Alike and Different

You have learned how Jamaica relates to other countries and why it matters. Now we turn to a question that is closer to home, a question about *you*. When you meet a child from another country, are you alike, or are you different? The answer is a happy surprise: you are both! In this part of the chapter, you will explore the ways you are like children all over the world, and the ways you are wonderfully different.

## In So Many Ways, We Are Alike

It is easy to think that people in faraway countries must be completely different from us. But when you look closely, you find something beautiful: in the most important ways, people all over the world are very much alike.

Every child in the world, no matter where they live, has the same basic **needs**. We all need food to eat, clean water to drink, clothes to wear, and a safe place to sleep. We all need to be loved and cared for. A child in Jamaica, a child in Nigeria, a child in India, and a child in Canada all share these very same needs.

We are alike in other happy ways too. Children everywhere have **families** they love. Children everywhere like to **play** games and have fun. Children everywhere enjoy **food**, **music**, and **stories**. Children everywhere laugh when they are happy and feel sad when they are hurt. When you remember these things, the world starts to feel like one big family, and the children of faraway countries start to feel like friends you simply have not met yet.

> **Think About It:** Name three things you need to live and be happy. Do you think a child living far away in another country needs those same three things? What does that tell you about people everywhere?

## In Other Ways, We Are Different

Even though people share so much, there are also many ways in which we are different from one another — and these differences are part of what makes the world so interesting and colourful.

People in different countries often speak different **languages**. Here in Jamaica we speak English and our own Jamaican language, but a child in France speaks French, and a child in China speaks Chinese. People wear different kinds of **clothing**, especially on special days, when many people wear traditional dress that has been passed down for many years. People eat different **foods**, follow different **customs** and celebrate different holidays, and may follow different **religions**.

The **weather** is different from place to place, too. Jamaica is warm and sunny almost all year round, but some countries have snow and very cold winters, while others have long dry seasons. Even the games children play and the music they listen to can be different from country to country.

None of these differences make one country better than another. They simply make each country special in its own way. Just as a garden is more beautiful when it has many different kinds of flowers, the world is more beautiful because it has many different kinds of people.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine you become e-pals with a child named Amara who lives in Nigeria. As you write to each other, you discover that you are alike in many ways. You both love your families. You both enjoy playing football. You both like to laugh with your friends. But you also discover differences. Amara speaks a language you have never heard, eats foods you have never tasted, and celebrates holidays you have never known. By sharing your likenesses and your differences, you and Amara become good friends and learn so much from each other.

> **Did You Know?** When tourists visit Jamaica, they clearly enjoy our culture — they love our music, our food, and our dances. This shows us that even people who come from very different places can fall in love with the special things that make Jamaica unique.

## Learning From Other Countries

One of the best ways to learn how you are alike and different from others is to study another country. When you learn about a country's **language**, its main **religion**, its **special holidays**, its **national symbols**, its **money**, its **food**, its **dress**, and its **music and dances**, you begin to see clearly which things are like Jamaica and which things are different.

You might compare the **money**, or currency, of another country with the Jamaican dollar to see how much things cost there. You might listen to another country's **national anthem** and stand in the proper, respectful posture for it, just as you do for our own. You might learn the **traditional dances** of another country and notice how they are alike or different from our own Jamaican dances. You might even play the **children's games** of another country and discover that children far away enjoy games just as much as you do.

Learning all of this does something wonderful. It helps you to understand and respect people who are different from you. And when the people of the world understand and respect one another, they can live together in peace and harmony. That is why learning about other countries is so very important.

> **Think About It:** If you could learn about any country in the whole world, which one would you choose, and why? What is one thing you would most like to find out about the children who live there?

# Part Four: Keeping Jamaica's Good Relations

You have learned so much already. You know the many ways Jamaica relates to other countries, why those relationships matter, and how you are both alike and different from people far away. Now we come to the most important question of all, and it is a question about you. How can *you* help Jamaica to keep good and friendly relations with other countries? You may be surprised to learn just how much power you have.

## Good Relations Begin With You

It is easy to think that keeping Jamaica friendly with other countries is a job only for grown-ups, like leaders and important people. But that is not true. Every single Jamaican helps to build our country's good name, and that includes you.

Think about it this way. When a tourist meets a kind and helpful Jamaican child, that visitor goes home thinking, "Jamaicans are wonderful people." But when a visitor meets someone rude or unkind, they may go home with a poor opinion of our whole island. Every time you meet a person from another country, you are not just being yourself — you are showing the whole world what Jamaica is like. You are a little ambassador for your country. We call this being a good **representative** of Jamaica.

The good news is that being a good representative is not hard at all. It is made up of small, simple, kind actions that you already know how to do. Let us look at some of them.

> **Think About It:** Have you ever had a stranger be very kind to you? How did it make you feel about that person? Now imagine you are that kind person for a visitor to Jamaica.

## Kindness, Respect, and Good Manners

The simplest way to keep Jamaica's good relations is to treat people with **kindness**, **respect**, and **good manners**. These are things you can do every single day.

When you meet a visitor, you can greet them warmly and with a smile. You can say "good morning," "please," and "thank you." You can be helpful if someone is lost or needs directions. You can be patient and friendly, even with people who speak differently or do things in a different way from you. All of these small kindnesses add up to something big. They tell visitors that Jamaicans are warm and welcoming people, and they make our island a place that people want to come back to again and again.

It is also important to **welcome visitors** and to treat tourists well. Sadly, sometimes visitors are bothered or troubled by people who are not kind to them. This is called **tourist harassment**, and it is harmful. When tourists are treated unkindly, they may go home and warn others not to visit Jamaica, and this hurts our whole country, especially the many Jamaicans whose jobs depend on tourism. So when you are friendly and respectful to visitors, you are not only being kind — you are helping to protect the jobs and the good name of your country.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine a tourist standing on a street corner in your town, looking confused and holding a map. One child walks past and ignores her. But another child, perhaps like you, stops and says, "Good morning! Are you lost? Let me help you find your way." The tourist smiles, feels welcomed, and finds where she needs to go. That evening, she tells her family how kind the people of Jamaica are. Just one small act of kindness from one child helped to make a friend for the whole of Jamaica.

> **Did You Know?** Our artists and our sports personalities are some of Jamaica's greatest helpers when it comes to making friends around the world. When they perform and compete with kindness and skill, they win admiration for our island and help to keep our good relations strong.

## Being a Good Friend to Other Countries

Keeping good relations is a lot like keeping a good friendship. Think about how you keep a friend. You are kind to them. You share with them. You help them when they are in trouble. You do not say cruel things or break your promises. Countries keep their friendships in much the same way.

When countries are kind to each other, share with each other through trade, help each other in times of need, and treat each other with respect, their friendship grows strong. But friendships between countries can be damaged too. If a country is unfriendly, breaks its promises, or treats others badly, the friendship can be hurt, and the **ties between the countries can be broken**.

When ties between countries are broken, sad things can follow. The countries may stop trading, so they can no longer get the goods they need from each other. They may stop helping each other in hard times. They may stop sharing music, sport, and learning. Everyone ends up worse off. This is why it is so important for Jamaica to be a good friend to other countries, and to work hard to keep its friendships strong.

The wonderful thing is that we keep these friendships strong through **shared values and skills** — through kindness, fairness, respect, and cooperation. These are the very same things that keep your own friendships strong. So when you practise being a good friend, you are learning exactly how to help your whole country be a good friend to the world.

> **Think About It:** Think of a time you and a friend had a disagreement, and then made up again. What did you do to fix the friendship? How might two countries fix a friendship in a similar way?

## You Have an Important Part to Play

By now you can see that keeping Jamaica's good relations is not only the work of leaders and grown-ups. It is the work of every Jamaican, including you.

Every time you are kind to a visitor, you help. Every time you show good manners, you help. Every time you welcome someone from another country with a warm smile, you help. Every time you cheer proudly and fairly for our athletes, you help. Every time you respect someone who is different from you, you help. These small actions, done by millions of Jamaicans, add up to a country that the whole world wants to be friends with.

You are a citizen of Jamaica, and you are also a citizen of the wider world. As you grow up, you will have more and more chances to build bridges of friendship between Jamaica and other countries. It all begins with the simple, kind, respectful person you choose to be each and every day.

> **Did You Know?** Conflict resolution is a grand-sounding name for something you already do — it simply means working out a quarrel in a calm and fair way instead of fighting. When you make peace with a friend, you are practising a skill that helps whole countries to stay friends too.

## Chapter Summary

In this chapter, you travelled all the way around the world and discovered how Jamaica, our small island, is joined to many other countries.

You learned that Jamaica relates to other countries in many ways: through trade, when we export goods like bananas and coffee and import goods like cars and computers; through sports, when our athletes compete and win admiration around the world; through music and culture, when we share our reggae and learn from others; through travel and tourism, when visitors come to enjoy our island; and through family, friends, and communication, when Jamaicans abroad stay connected to home.

You discovered why these relationships matter so much. They bring us goods we cannot make ourselves, they let us share and learn and grow, they bring money and jobs through tourism, and they build friendship and help between nations.

You explored the happy truth that, in the most important ways, you are alike to children all over the world, because we all share the same needs, families, games, and feelings. You also learned that we are different in our languages, dress, foods, customs, and weather, and that these differences make the world colourful and interesting.

Finally, you learned that you yourself can help Jamaica keep good relations with other countries through kindness, respect, good manners, welcoming visitors, and being a good representative of your beloved island.

## Key Takeaways

- A **relationship** between countries is the way two countries are connected and treat each other, and **interdependence** means countries need and help one another.
- Jamaica relates to other countries through **trade** (exports go out, imports come in), **sports**, **music and culture**, **tourism**, and **communication** with family and friends abroad.
- These relationships matter because they bring us goods we cannot make, help us share and learn, bring money and jobs through tourism, and build friendship and help between nations.
- In the most important ways — needs, families, games, and feelings — we are **alike** to people everywhere, while our languages, dress, food, customs, and weather make us **different**.
- Every Jamaican, including you, helps to keep Jamaica's good relations through **kindness, respect, good manners**, welcoming visitors, and being a good **representative** of our island.
