Have you ever sat down to a plate of ackee and saltfish on a Sunday morning, or heard a reggae song drift out of a passing car, or laughed at an Anancy story told by your grandmother? If you have, then you already know something wonderful about your home. You know a little piece of **Jamaican culture**.

Culture is all around you, every single day, even when you do not stop to notice it. It is in the food you eat and the words you speak. It is in the music that makes your feet want to dance, and in the games you play with your friends in the yard. It is in the proverbs your elders share, the festivals your family celebrates, and the flag that flies on Independence Day.

In this chapter, you are going to look closely at the culture of your own country and see how rich, how colourful, and how special it really is. You will learn what Jamaican culture is made of. You will discover why it is so precious and why it gives us so much to be proud of. And you will find out how our culture came to be, growing and changing over many, many years, from many different peoples into the one Jamaica we love today. So let us begin our journey through the beautiful culture of our island home.

## What You Will Learn

You will learn why this culture is so valuable, how it gives us pride, identity, and a sense of belonging, and how some people even earn a living from it. Finally, you will learn how the Jamaican culture grew out of many different peoples who came to our island, and how it has changed and evolved over time, with some old ways and some new ways living side by side.

1. What is the Jamaican culture? You will answer this by exploring all the things that make us Jamaican, from our food to our flag.

## Words to Know

Here are some important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about memorising them now. You will understand them fully as you read, because each one is explained carefully in the lessons.

- **Culture** – the way of life of a group of people, including their food, music, language, beliefs, and customs.
- **Tradition** – a custom or way of doing something that is passed down from older people to younger people over many years.
- **Heritage** – the special things passed down to us from the people who lived before us, which we keep and treasure.
- **Custom** – a usual way of doing something that a group of people share.
- **Festival** – a special time of celebration with music, food, dancing, and other activities.
- **Patois** – the everyday Jamaican Creole language that many Jamaicans speak, also called Jamaican Creole.
- **Cuisine** – the special foods and ways of cooking that belong to a place.
- **National symbols** – the special signs that stand for our country, such as the flag, the anthem, and the coat of arms.
- **Ancestors** – the people in our families and our nation who lived long before us.
- **Evolve** – to grow and change slowly over a long time.
- **Proverb** – a short, wise saying that teaches a lesson about life.

# Part One: What Is the Jamaican Culture?

Imagine someone from another country asked you, "What is Jamaica like?" What would you tell them? You might talk about the food we eat, the music we love, or the way we speak. All of these things together are part of our **culture**. In this first part of the chapter, you will explore the many wonderful pieces that make up the Jamaican culture.

## What Does Culture Mean?

Let us begin with the most important word of all. **Culture** means the way of life of a group of people. It is everything that a group of people share and do together: the food they eat, the language they speak, the music they make, the clothes they wear, the games they play, the things they believe, and the special days they celebrate.

Every country in the world has its own culture, and ours is the **Jamaican culture**. It is what makes Jamaica different from every other place on Earth. When you eat a Jamaican meal, sing a Jamaican song, or celebrate a Jamaican holiday, you are taking part in the culture of your country.

Much of our culture is made of **tradition**. A tradition is a way of doing something that is passed down from older people to younger people over many years. Your grandparents learned many things from their grandparents, and they passed those things down to your parents, who pass them down to you. In this way, a culture is like a wonderful gift that is handed down from one family member to the next, again and again, so that it is never lost.

> **Think About It:** If a visitor came to your home from far away, what is one thing about being Jamaican that you would most want to show them? Why would you choose that thing?

## The Food We Love

One of the very best parts of any culture is its food, and Jamaica has some of the most delicious food in the whole world. The special foods and ways of cooking that belong to our island make up our **cuisine**.

Think about a Jamaican breakfast. Many families enjoy **ackee and saltfish**, which is so well loved that it is often called our national dish. The bright yellow ackee is cooked together with salted fish, and it is often eaten with fried dumplings, boiled green banana, or fried breadfruit. It is a meal that tastes like home.

There are so many other Jamaican foods to enjoy. There is **rice and peas**, cooked with coconut milk and red peas, which families love to have on a Sunday. There is **run down**, made by cooking fish in rich coconut milk. There is spicy, smoky **jerk** chicken and pork, cooked slowly over a fire so that the flavour soaks all the way through. And for something sweet, there are treats like **duckunoo**, **cornmeal pudding**, and **gizzadas**. Each of these foods has been cooked in Jamaica for a very long time, and each one is part of who we are.

> **Did You Know?** Many of our favourite dishes have travelled a long way to reach our plates. The way Jamaicans cook today is a mix of cooking styles brought to the island by people from Africa, Europe, India, China, and other places. When you eat Jamaican food, you are tasting the history of many peoples all in one bite.

## The Music and Dance of Jamaica

If there is one thing Jamaica is famous for all over the world, it is our music. Our island has given the world many kinds of music, and each one has its own special sound and feeling.

Long ago, people enjoyed **Mento**, a cheerful country music with a bouncy beat. Later came **Ska**, which was fast and full of energy, and then **Rock Steady**, which was a little slower and smoother. After that came the music Jamaica is most famous for of all: **Reggae**, with its steady, soulful beat that people everywhere love. And in more recent times we have **Dancehall**, a lively music made for dancing. These five kinds of music — Mento, Ska, Rock Steady, Reggae, and Dancehall — are like five branches growing from one strong Jamaican tree.

Where there is Jamaican music, there is always dancing. We have many beautiful traditional dances that have been performed for generations. There is the graceful **Quadrille**, the lively **Bruckins**, the **Dinki Mini**, and the colourful **Maypole** dance, where dancers weave ribbons around a tall pole. Each dance has its own special steps, its own costumes, and its own story to tell.

> **Think About It:** What is your favourite kind of Jamaican music? When you hear it, how does it make you feel, and what do you feel like doing?

## The Way We Speak

Listen carefully to the people around you, and you will notice something interesting. In Jamaica, we speak in two different ways, and both of them are part of our culture.

One way of speaking is **Standard Jamaican English**, which is the kind of English you read in your books and use when you write your schoolwork. The other way is **Jamaican Creole**, which many people call **Patois**. Patois is the warm, lively, everyday language that families speak at home and friends use when they play. It has its own special words, its own rhythm, and its own way of saying things.

It is good to know both ways of speaking, because each one is useful at different times. When you are writing a letter or speaking in front of the class, Standard English helps you to be clearly understood. When you are laughing and chatting with your family, Patois feels warm and friendly. A clever Jamaican learns to use the right language for the right moment, the same way you might wear different clothes for different days.

> **Did You Know?** Jamaican Patois is spoken and understood by Jamaicans all over the world. Wherever Jamaicans travel, they carry their language with them, and hearing it spoken can make a person far from home feel close to Jamaica once again.

## Festivals, Games, and Sayings

Our culture is also full of joyful celebrations. A **festival** is a special time of celebration with music, food, dancing, and many activities. Throughout the year, Jamaica holds wonderful festivals and national celebrations.

During **National Heritage Week**, we remember our heroes and the people who helped to build our nation. On **Jamaica Day**, schools celebrate everything that makes our island special. On **Independence Day**, the whole country celebrates the day Jamaica became free to govern itself, with parades, music, and the colours black, green, and gold everywhere you look. The Maroons, a proud group of Jamaicans, even hold their own **Maroon New Year's Celebration**. At festivals like these, people wear special, colourful dress and take part in activities passed down through the years.

Our culture lives in our play, too. Jamaican children have long enjoyed **ring games**, where players hold hands in a circle and sing and move together. And our culture lives in our words. Jamaicans love **proverbs**, which are short, wise sayings that teach a lesson. When someone says, "One one coco full basket," they are reminding us that doing a little at a time will add up to a lot in the end. Proverbs are a clever way our elders pass down wisdom to us.

We also enjoy our **folk tales** and **folk songs**. Many children grow up hearing stories about Anancy, the clever spider, and singing songs like "Carry Mi Ackee Guh A Linstead Market." These stories and songs are fun to share, and they often teach us an important lesson at the same time.

> **Think About It:** Has an older person ever told you a proverb, an Anancy story, or taught you a ring game? What do you remember about it?

## Our Religion and Our National Symbols

Religion is also an important part of Jamaican culture. Many Jamaicans are Christians who attend church, pray, and follow the teachings of the Christian religion, and these beliefs shape the way many families live each day. The Maroons, who fought long ago to be free, also have their own beliefs and ways of life that they have kept for many years. Our different religions help to give people moral values and guide how they treat one another.

Finally, every Jamaican should know our **national symbols**. These are the special signs that stand for our country and belong to all of us. We have our **National Flag**, with its black, green, and gold. We have our **National Coat of Arms**, with its proud motto. And we have our **National Anthem**, our **National Pledge**, and our **National Song**, which we say and sing with pride. These symbols remind us that we are all Jamaicans together, and they should be learned by heart and treated with respect.

> **Did You Know?** Our national motto is "Out of Many, One People." It means that even though Jamaicans come from many different backgrounds, we are all joined together as one proud nation. You will learn much more about this beautiful idea later in the chapter.

# Part Two: Why Is the Jamaican Culture Valuable to Me?

Now that you have explored the many parts of Jamaican culture, you might be wondering why all of it matters so much. Why should you care about our food, our music, our language, and our customs? In this second part of the chapter, you will discover why the Jamaican culture is so valuable, not only to our whole country, but to you yourself.

## What Does It Mean to Be Valuable?

Let us start with the word **valuable**. Something that is valuable is something precious, something worth a great deal, something we would not want to lose. We might think of money or jewels as valuable, but the most valuable things of all are often things we cannot buy in a shop, such as our family, our friends, and our culture.

Think for a moment of something that is very valuable to *you*. Perhaps it is a gift from someone you love, or a photograph, or a special memory. You treasure it because it means something to you. In the very same way, our culture is one of the most valuable things we have, because it means so much to who we are.

> **Think About It:** Name one thing that is very valuable to you. Is it valuable because it cost a lot of money, or for some other reason? What makes it so special?

## Culture Gives Us Who We Are

The first reason our culture is so valuable is that it gives us our **identity**. Your identity is the answer to the question, "Who am I?" When you eat Jamaican food, speak in Patois with your friends, dance to reggae, and sing the National Anthem, you are showing the world that you are Jamaican. Our culture is like a name badge that we carry in our hearts, telling everyone, "This is who I am, and this is where I come from."

Because our culture gives us our identity, it also fills us with **pride**. It feels good to know that our little island has given the world such famous music, such delicious food, and such great heroes. When a Jamaican athlete wins a race, or a Jamaican song is loved far away, Jamaicans everywhere feel proud, because that success belongs to our culture and to all of us.

Our culture also gives us a sense of **belonging**. To belong means to feel that you are part of a group, that you fit in and are welcome. When you join in a festival, sing a folk song with your class, or celebrate Independence Day with your family, you feel that you belong to something much bigger than yourself. You belong to Jamaica.

> **Did You Know?** Jamaican culture is so well loved that it has helped people in faraway countries to learn about and understand our island. Our music, our food, and our friendly spirit have made friends for Jamaica all around the world.

## Culture Brings Us Together

Our culture is valuable for another beautiful reason: it brings people together and helps us to live in **unity**. To live in unity means to live together in peace as one people, even though we are all different.

Remember our national motto, "Out of Many, One People"? Jamaica is made up of people whose families came from many different places, including Africa, Europe, India, China, and other lands. People in Jamaica also follow different religions and have different customs. Yet our shared culture helps to join all of these different people together into one nation. When we celebrate the same festivals, sing the same songs, and feel proud of the same country, we are reminded that we are all one people, all Jamaicans together.

Our religions, too, help us to live well with one another. They teach us good moral values, such as kindness, honesty, and forgiveness. There is a famous story, the story of the **Good Samaritan**, which teaches us to be helpful and kind even to people who are different from us. When we follow lessons like these, our whole community becomes stronger, more peaceful, and more loving.

> **Think About It:** Why do you think it is a good thing for people who are different from one another to live together in unity? How does it make a country stronger?

## Earning a Living from Our Culture

Here is something that may surprise you: many Jamaicans do not only enjoy our culture, they also earn a living from it. This is another reason why our culture is so valuable.

Think of all the people whose work comes from Jamaican culture. There are **musicians** who make our music, **dancers** who perform our dances, and **artists** and **sculptors** who create beautiful works of art. There are **athletes** who make us proud in sport. There are people who cook our food in restaurants and who make crafts for visitors to buy. All of these people are able to earn money and care for their families because of our rich culture.

Our culture even helps our whole country to earn money. When visitors from other lands, called tourists, come to enjoy our music, our food, our beaches, and our friendly way of life, they bring money that helps Jamaica to grow. And many Jamaican products are sold in faraway countries, such as **Blue Mountain coffee**, Jamaican spices and sauces, and ginger products. When people in other lands buy these Jamaican goods and enjoy them, they learn about our culture, and our island earns value from sharing what we make.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine a Jamaican musician who writes a wonderful reggae song. People in Jamaica love it, and soon people in other countries are listening to it too. The musician earns a living from the song, visitors come to Jamaica hoping to hear more music like it, and our whole island becomes better known and loved around the world. All of this good came from one piece of our culture, a single song. That is how valuable our culture can be.

## The Value of Our Land and Plants

Our culture is also closely tied to the beautiful land of Jamaica and the plants that grow here. Some plants, herbs, and spices grow naturally on our island, and Jamaicans have used them for a very long time.

Many of these plants are used as **folk medicine**, which means natural remedies that families have used for years to help with small illnesses. People use **cerasee** to help with a belly ache, **fever grass** to ease a cough and cold, and many other herbs besides. Other plants and spices, like pimento and ginger, give our food its wonderful flavour. These plants are a valuable part of our culture and our way of life.

Because these plants are so valuable, it is important that we take care of them and protect them, especially the special ones that grow only in Jamaica. We must also protect our indigenous animals, the creatures that belong to our island. When we care for our plants and animals, we are caring for our culture and for the future of our country.

> **Did You Know?** Some plants and animals live only in Jamaica and nowhere else in the whole world. That makes them very special and precious, and it is our job to protect them so that they do not disappear.

# Part Three: How Has the Jamaican Culture Evolved?

The Jamaican culture you enjoy today did not appear all at once. It grew slowly, over hundreds of years, changing little by little, until it became the rich and colourful culture we know now. In this last part of the chapter, you will discover how our culture came to be and how it has changed, or **evolved**, over time.

## Out of Many, One People

To understand how our culture grew, we must remember our national motto once more: **"Out of Many, One People."** This motto holds the secret to where our culture came from.

Long ago, people came to Jamaica from many different parts of the world. Some came from **Africa**, some from **Europe**, some from **India**, some from **China**, and others from still more places. Each group of people brought their own food, their own music, their own words, their own beliefs, and their own ways of doing things.

Over many, many years, all of these different ways of life mixed together on our island, like many different ingredients stirred into one delicious pot of soup. Slowly, they blended into something brand new: the Jamaican culture. This is why our food, our music, and even our language carry pieces of so many different peoples. When something grows and changes slowly over a long time like this, we say that it has **evolved**. Our culture evolved out of many peoples into one.

> **Think About It:** Our culture is a mix of many peoples joined together. Why do you think a mix of many different peoples might create a richer and more interesting culture than just one?

## How Our Music Has Changed

One of the clearest ways to see how our culture has evolved is to listen to how our music has changed over the years.

Think back to the kinds of Jamaican music you learned about earlier. The older music, like **Mento, Ska, and Rock Steady**, came first, many years ago. Then, as time went on, the music changed and grew. **Reggae** developed from these earlier sounds, and later still came **Dancehall**. Even the instruments changed over time. Long ago, much of our music was played on simple, hand-made instruments, while today musicians often use electric instruments and computers to create new sounds.

If you listen to an old Mento song and then a modern Dancehall song, you will hear how much our music has changed. Yet both of them are still proudly Jamaican. This shows us something important about culture: it can change and grow without ever stopping being our own. The new music grew out of the old, just as a tall tree grows from a small seed.

> **Did You Know?** When we describe music, we can talk about its **pitch**, which means how high or low a sound is, and its **dynamics**, which means how loud or soft it is. We can also talk about **timbre**, which means the special sound that each instrument makes. As our music evolved, all of these things changed too.

## The People Who Shaped Our Culture

Our culture did not grow all by itself. Many talented Jamaicans helped to shape it and make it what it is today, and we remember them with great respect.

There was **The Honourable Louise Bennett-Coverley**, lovingly known as Miss Lou, who helped to make Jamaican Patois and our folk stories beloved by everyone. There was **Edna Manley**, a great artist whose sculptures and paintings became an important part of our visual art. There was **Mervyn Morris**, a fine poet who wrote beautiful words. And there was **Bob Marley**, whose reggae music carried the spirit of Jamaica all around the world. These are just a few of the many people who helped our culture grow. Each of them gave a gift to Jamaica that we still enjoy today.

> **Think About It:** People like Miss Lou and Bob Marley helped to shape our culture. What gift do you think *you* might be able to give to Jamaica one day when you grow up?

## How Everyday Life Has Changed

Our culture is not only about music and famous people. It is also about the way ordinary families live each day, and this too has changed a great deal over the years. If you could talk to your great-grandparents about their childhood, you might be amazed at how different life was.

Think about how families travelled long ago. People once rode in a **tramcar**, which was a kind of train that ran through the streets. Today, that tramcar is gone, and people travel by car, bus, and other modern ways instead. Think about how children were taught. Long ago, students learned with very few books and simple tools, while today many learn with computers and tablets.

Even work in the home has changed because of **science and technology**. Long ago, people used a heavy **coal iron** or tailor iron, heated on hot coals, to press their clothes, and they cooked on a **coal stove**. They listened to a radio for news and entertainment, and they swept and cleaned with simple tools like a **coconut brush**. Today we have electric irons, gas and electric stoves, televisions, and many machines that make work in the home much easier and faster than before.

**Let's look at an example.** Long ago, if a family wanted to send a message to a relative far away, they might have to write a letter and wait many days for it to arrive. Today, a person can send a text message on a phone and have it arrive in just a second. This shows how much science and technology have changed our everyday lives. Of course, these changes bring both good and not-so-good things. It is wonderful to send a message so quickly, but some people feel we should still make time to talk and visit one another face to face, just as families did in the past.

> **Did You Know?** Long ago, Jamaican families worked together closely to get things done in the home, with everyone helping to cook, clean, wash, and care for one another. Sharing the work was an important part of family life, and many families still treasure that spirit of working together today.

## How We Can Shape Our Culture

Here is one of the most exciting ideas in this whole chapter: culture is still changing, and you can help to shape it. Culture is not finished and locked away. It keeps growing, and the people of today, including children like you, help to decide what it will become tomorrow.

How can a child help to shape culture? One of the best ways is by getting a good **education**. When you learn and grow, you gain new ideas, and you find new ways to solve problems and to do things better. Educated people help to make their communities stronger and kinder. They find good solutions instead of breaking laws or hurting others, and they help to lead their country forward.

You can help to shape our culture in many small ways, too. You can learn our songs, dances, and stories and pass them on to others. You can treat people of all backgrounds with kindness and respect. You can be proud of being Jamaican. In all of these ways, you become part of the wonderful, never-ending story of the Jamaican culture, carrying it forward into the future.

> **Think About It:** Name one good change you would like to see in your own community one day. How might you help to make that change happen when you grow up?
