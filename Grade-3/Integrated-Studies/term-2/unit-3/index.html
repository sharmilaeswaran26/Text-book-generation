Focus Question 1: What Is the Jamaican Culture?

What Does Culture Mean?

Culture means the way of life of a group of people. It is everything that a group of people share and do together: the food they eat, the language they speak, the music they make, the clothes they wear, the games they play, the things they believe, and the special days they celebrate.

Every country has its own culture, and ours is the Jamaican culture. It is what makes Jamaica different from every other place on Earth. When you eat a Jamaican meal, sing a Jamaican song, or celebrate a Jamaican holiday, you are taking part in the culture of your country.

Much of our culture is made of tradition. A tradition is a way of doing something that is passed down from older people to younger people over many years. In this way, a culture is like a wonderful gift that is handed down from one family member to the next, again and again, so that it is never lost.

Think About It

If a visitor came to your home from far away, what is one thing about being Jamaican that you would most want to show them? Why would you choose that thing?

The Food We Love

One of the very best parts of any culture is its food, and Jamaica has some of the most delicious food in the whole world. The special foods and ways of cooking that belong to our island make up our cuisine.

Many families enjoy ackee and saltfish, which is so well loved that it is often called our national dish. It is often eaten with fried dumplings, boiled green banana, or fried breadfruit. There is also rice and peas, cooked with coconut milk; run down, made by cooking fish in rich coconut milk; and spicy, smoky jerk chicken and pork. For something sweet, there are treats like duckunoo, cornmeal pudding, and gizzadas. Each of these foods has been cooked in Jamaica for a very long time, and each one is part of who we are.

Did You Know?

Many of our favourite dishes have travelled a long way to reach our plates. The way Jamaicans cook today is a mix of cooking styles brought to the island by people from Africa, Europe, India, China, and other places. When you eat Jamaican food, you are tasting the history of many peoples all in one bite.

The Music and Dance of Jamaica

Jamaica is famous all over the world for our music. Long ago, people enjoyed Mento, a cheerful country music with a bouncy beat. Later came Ska, which was fast and full of energy, and then Rock Steady, which was slower and smoother. After that came Reggae, with its steady, soulful beat that people everywhere love, and in more recent times Dancehall, a lively music made for dancing.

Where there is Jamaican music, there is always dancing. We have many beautiful traditional dances, such as the graceful Quadrille, the lively Bruckins, the Dinki Mini, and the colourful Maypole dance, where dancers weave ribbons around a tall pole. Each dance has its own special steps, its own costumes, and its own story to tell.

Think About It

What is your favourite kind of Jamaican music? When you hear it, how does it make you feel, and what do you feel like doing?

The Way We Speak

In Jamaica, we speak in two different ways, and both are part of our culture. One way is Standard Jamaican English, the kind of English you read in your books and use for your schoolwork. The other is Jamaican Creole, which many people call Patois. Patois is the warm, lively, everyday language that families speak at home and friends use when they play.

It is good to know both ways of speaking, because each is useful at different times. When you are writing a letter or speaking in front of the class, Standard English helps you to be clearly understood. When you are laughing with your family, Patois feels warm and friendly. A clever Jamaican learns to use the right language for the right moment.

Festivals, Games, and Sayings

A festival is a special time of celebration with music, food, dancing, and many activities. During National Heritage Week, we remember our heroes. On Jamaica Day, schools celebrate everything that makes our island special. On Independence Day, the whole country celebrates with parades and the colours black, green, and gold. The Maroons even hold their own Maroon New Year's Celebration.

Our culture lives in our play, too. Jamaican children have long enjoyed ring games, where players hold hands in a circle and sing and move together. Jamaicans also love proverbs, short wise sayings that teach a lesson. When someone says, "One one coco full basket," they remind us that doing a little at a time will add up to a lot in the end. We also enjoy folk tales about Anancy the clever spider, and folk songs like "Carry Mi Ackee Guh A Linstead Market."

Think About It

Has an older person ever told you a proverb, an Anancy story, or taught you a ring game? What do you remember about it?

Our Religion and Our National Symbols

Religion is an important part of Jamaican culture. Many Jamaicans are Christians who attend church and follow the teachings of the Christian religion. The Maroons, who fought long ago to be free, also have their own beliefs and ways of life that they have kept for many years. Our different religions help to give people moral values and guide how they treat one another.

Every Jamaican should know our national symbols. We have our National Flag, with its black, green, and gold; our National Coat of Arms; and our National Anthem, National Pledge, and National Song. These symbols remind us that we are all Jamaicans together, and they should be learned by heart and treated with respect.

Did You Know?

Our national motto is "Out of Many, One People." It means that even though Jamaicans come from many different backgrounds, we are all joined together as one proud nation.

Let's Practice

Look around your own home and see how many parts of Jamaican culture you can find in just one day. Notice the food on your plate, the music you hear, the words your family speaks, and the games you play. Then draw or write about three of them and tell why each one is part of our culture.

Focus Question 2: Why Is the Jamaican Culture Valuable to Me?

What Does It Mean to Be Valuable?

Something that is valuable is something precious, something worth a great deal, something we would not want to lose. We might think of money or jewels as valuable, but the most valuable things of all are often things we cannot buy in a shop, such as our family, our friends, and our culture.

Think of something that is very valuable to you. Perhaps it is a gift from someone you love, or a special memory. You treasure it because it means something to you. In the very same way, our culture is one of the most valuable things we have, because it means so much to who we are.

Think About It

Name one thing that is very valuable to you. Is it valuable because it cost a lot of money, or for some other reason? What makes it so special?

Culture Gives Us Who We Are

Our culture gives us our identity, which is the answer to the question, "Who am I?" When you eat Jamaican food, speak Patois with your friends, dance to reggae, and sing the National Anthem, you show the world that you are Jamaican.

Because our culture gives us our identity, it also fills us with pride. It feels good to know that our little island has given the world such famous music, such delicious food, and such great heroes. Our culture also gives us a sense of belonging. When you join in a festival or celebrate Independence Day with your family, you feel that you belong to something much bigger than yourself. You belong to Jamaica.

Did You Know?

Jamaican culture is so well loved that it has helped people in faraway countries to learn about and understand our island. Our music, our food, and our friendly spirit have made friends for Jamaica all around the world.

Culture Brings Us Together

Our culture brings people together and helps us to live in unity, which means living together in peace as one people, even though we are all different. Jamaica is made up of people whose families came from many places, including Africa, Europe, India, China, and other lands. Our shared culture joins all of these different people together into one nation. This is the meaning of our motto, "Out of Many, One People."

Our religions also help us to live well together. They teach us good moral values, such as kindness, honesty, and forgiveness. The story of the Good Samaritan teaches us to be helpful and kind even to people who are different from us. When we follow lessons like these, our whole community becomes stronger and more loving.

Think About It

Why do you think it is a good thing for people who are different from one another to live together in unity? How does it make a country stronger?

Earning a Living from Our Culture

Many Jamaicans do not only enjoy our culture, they also earn a living from it. There are musicians who make our music, dancers who perform our dances, artists and sculptors who create beautiful art, and athletes who make us proud in sport. All of these people care for their families because of our rich culture.

Our culture even helps our whole country to earn money. When visitors, called tourists, come to enjoy our music, food, and friendly way of life, they bring money that helps Jamaica grow. Many Jamaican products are also sold in faraway countries, such as Blue Mountain coffee, Jamaican spices and sauces, and ginger products. When people in other lands buy these goods, they learn about our culture, and our island earns value from sharing what we make.

The Value of Our Land and Plants

Our culture is also closely tied to the beautiful land of Jamaica and the plants that grow here. Many plants are used as folk medicine, the natural remedies that families have used for years. People use cerasee to help with a belly ache and fever grass to ease a cough and cold. Other plants and spices, like pimento and ginger, give our food its wonderful flavour.

Because these plants are so valuable, it is important that we take care of them and protect them, especially the special ones that grow only in Jamaica. We must also protect our indigenous animals, the creatures that belong to our island. When we care for our plants and animals, we are caring for our culture and for the future of our country.

Did You Know?

Some plants and animals live only in Jamaica and nowhere else in the whole world. That makes them very special and precious, and it is our job to protect them so that they do not disappear.

Let's Practice

Think of one part of Jamaican culture that is valuable to you, such as a food, a song, or a festival. Write a short paragraph that explains why it is so valuable to you, and share it with your class.

Focus Question 3: How Has the Jamaican Culture Evolved?

Out of Many, One People

To understand how our culture grew, we must remember our motto: "Out of Many, One People." Long ago, people came to Jamaica from many different parts of the world. Some came from Africa, some from Europe, some from India, some from China, and others from still more places. Each group brought their own food, music, words, beliefs, and ways of doing things.

Over many years, all of these different ways of life mixed together on our island, like many ingredients stirred into one delicious pot of soup. Slowly, they blended into something brand new: the Jamaican culture. When something grows and changes slowly over a long time like this, we say that it has evolved.

Think About It

Our culture is a mix of many peoples joined together. Why do you think a mix of many different peoples might create a richer and more interesting culture than just one?

How Our Music Has Changed

One of the clearest ways to see how our culture has evolved is to listen to how our music has changed. The older music, like Mento, Ska, and Rock Steady, came first, many years ago. Then the music changed and grew. Reggae developed from these earlier sounds, and later still came Dancehall. Even the instruments changed, from simple, hand-made instruments long ago to electric instruments and computers today.

If you listen to an old Mento song and then a modern Dancehall song, you will hear how much our music has changed. Yet both are still proudly Jamaican. The new music grew out of the old, just as a tall tree grows from a small seed.

Did You Know?

When we describe music, we can talk about its pitch, which means how high or low a sound is, and its dynamics, which means how loud or soft it is. We can also talk about timbre, which means the special sound that each instrument makes.

The People Who Shaped Our Culture

Many talented Jamaicans helped to shape our culture. There was The Honourable Louise Bennett-Coverley, lovingly known as Miss Lou, who helped to make Jamaican Patois and our folk stories beloved by everyone. There was Edna Manley, a great artist whose sculptures and paintings became an important part of our visual art. There was Mervyn Morris, a fine poet, and Bob Marley, whose reggae music carried the spirit of Jamaica all around the world. Each of them gave a gift to Jamaica that we still enjoy today.

Think About It

People like Miss Lou and Bob Marley helped to shape our culture. What gift do you think you might be able to give to Jamaica one day when you grow up?

How Everyday Life Has Changed

Our culture is also about the way ordinary families live each day, and this too has changed a great deal. Long ago, people rode in a tramcar, a kind of train that ran through the streets. Today, that tramcar is gone, and people travel by car and bus instead. Long ago, students learned with very few books, while today many learn with computers and tablets.

Even work in the home has changed because of science and technology. Long ago, people used a heavy coal iron to press their clothes and cooked on a coal stove. They listened to a radio and cleaned with simple tools like a coconut brush. Today we have electric irons, gas and electric stoves, televisions, and many machines that make work in the home much easier and faster than before.

Did You Know?

Long ago, Jamaican families worked together closely to get things done in the home, with everyone helping to cook, clean, wash, and care for one another. Sharing the work was an important part of family life, and many families still treasure that spirit of working together today.

How We Can Shape Our Culture

Culture is still changing, and you can help to shape it. One of the best ways is by getting a good education. When you learn and grow, you gain new ideas, and you find new ways to solve problems and to do things better. Educated people help to make their communities stronger and kinder, finding good solutions instead of breaking laws or hurting others.

You can help to shape our culture in small ways, too. You can learn our songs, dances, and stories and pass them on to others. You can treat people of all backgrounds with kindness and respect. You can be proud of being Jamaican. In all of these ways, you become part of the never-ending story of the Jamaican culture.

Think About It

Name one good change you would like to see in your own community one day. How might you help to make that change happen when you grow up?

Let's Practice

Ask an older person in your family how something was done long ago, such as travelling, cooking, or washing clothes. Then write two sentences comparing how it was done then and how it is done today.

Glossary: Words to Know

Important Words from This Unit

Culture: The way of life of a group of people, including their food, music, language, beliefs, and customs.
Tradition: A custom or way of doing something that is passed down from older people to younger people over many years.
Heritage: The special things passed down to us from the people who lived before us, which we keep and treasure.
Custom: A usual way of doing something that a group of people share.
Festival: A special time of celebration with music, food, dancing, and other activities.
Patois: The everyday Jamaican Creole language that many Jamaicans speak.
Cuisine: The special foods and ways of cooking that belong to a place.
National symbols: The special signs that stand for our country, such as the flag, the anthem, and the coat of arms.
Ancestors: The people in our families and our nation who lived long before us.
Evolve: To grow and change slowly over a long time.
Proverb: A short, wise saying that teaches a lesson about life.
Identity: Who we are, the answer to the question "Who am I?"
Unity: Living together in peace as one people, even though we are all different.
Folk medicine: Natural remedies made from plants and herbs that families have used for years.
Pitch: How high or low a sound is.
Dynamics: How loud or soft a sound is.
Timbre: The special sound that each instrument makes.

Answer Key

Review Question Answers

1. What does the word culture mean? Give two examples.
Culture means the way of life of a group of people. Examples of Jamaican culture include our food (like ackee and saltfish) and our music (like reggae). Other examples are our language, dress, festivals, and games.

2. Name three Jamaican foods and tell which one is our national dish.
Three Jamaican foods are ackee and saltfish, rice and peas, and jerk chicken. Ackee and saltfish is often called our national dish.

3. Name the five kinds of Jamaican music, from older to newer.
Mento, Ska, Rock Steady, Reggae, and Dancehall.

4. Explain the difference between Standard Jamaican English and Patois, and when each is useful.
Standard Jamaican English is the English we use for schoolwork and writing, and it helps us to be clearly understood. Patois is the warm, everyday language we speak with family and friends. We use Standard English for formal times and Patois for friendly, everyday times.

5. Name two national symbols and explain why we should respect them.
Two national symbols are the National Flag and the National Anthem (others are the National Coat of Arms, National Pledge, and National Song). We should respect them because they stand for our country and remind us that we are all Jamaicans together.

6. Give two reasons why the Jamaican culture is valuable to you.
Our culture gives us our identity and our pride, and it gives us a sense of belonging. It also brings people together in unity. (Any two reasons are correct.)

7. What does "Out of Many, One People" mean, and how does it help us live in unity?
It means that even though Jamaicans come from many different backgrounds, we are all joined together as one nation. It helps us live in unity by reminding us that we are all Jamaicans together, no matter how different we are.

8. Describe one way a Jamaican can earn a living from our culture.
A Jamaican can earn a living as a musician, dancer, artist, sculptor, athlete, or by cooking our food or making crafts for visitors. (Any one is correct.)

9. Explain how the Jamaican culture came to be.
Long ago, people came to Jamaica from many places, including Africa, Europe, India, and China. Each group brought their own ways of life, and over many years these mixed together to create the one Jamaican culture.

10. Describe two ways everyday life has changed because of science and technology.
Long ago people used a coal iron and a coal stove, but today we use electric irons and gas or electric stoves. Long ago people travelled by tramcar, but today they travel by car and bus. Long ago messages took days to arrive by letter, but today a text message arrives in a second. (Any two are correct.)

11. Name one way you can help to shape the Jamaican culture in the future.
You can help by getting a good education, by learning and passing on our songs, dances, and stories, by treating everyone with kindness and respect, and by being proud of being Jamaican. (Any one is correct.)
