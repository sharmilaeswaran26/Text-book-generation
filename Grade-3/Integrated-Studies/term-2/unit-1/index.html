Focus Question 1: Who are the providers of goods and services that we need?

Goods and Services

The workers in your community provide two kinds of things. We call them goods and services.

Goods are things you can hold in your hands and use. A loaf of bread is a good. So is a pair of shoes, a school bag, a wooden chair, a bunch of bananas, and a bottle of water. Goods are things that have been made or grown by somebody. When you go to a shop and buy a tin of milk or a bag of rice, you are buying goods.

Services are different. A service is not a thing you can hold. A service is helpful work that one person does for another. When a teacher teaches your class, that is a service. When a bus driver carries you safely to school, that is a service. When a doctor makes you better, when a barber cuts your hair, or when a fireman puts out a fire, each of those is a service.

Here is an easy way to remember the difference. If it is a thing you can touch and keep, it is a good. If it is help that someone does for you, it is a service. We need them both every single day.

Think About It

Look around the room you are in right now. Can you point to three goods that a worker made? Can you think of one service that a worker did for you today?

Workers Are the Providers

Goods and services do not appear by magic. Behind every good and every service there is a worker. A worker is a person who does a job, and we call that worker a provider, because the worker provides — that means gives — the goods and services we need. The person who uses those goods and services is called a consumer.

Imagine a farmer named Mr Brown who grows yam and tomatoes. Mr Brown is a provider, because he provides the food he grows. He sells his food at the market to a lady named Miss Pearl, who buys it. Right now, Miss Pearl is a consumer. But Miss Pearl is a nurse, and at the hospital she provides a service by caring for sick people. So Miss Pearl is a provider too. The farmer feeds the nurse, and the nurse cares for the farmer. That is how a community works.

Meet the Workers in Our Community

If you walk around your community with your eyes open, you will see workers everywhere. Some workers grow and catch our food. Farmers plant crops like yam, cabbage, and banana, and raise animals. Fishermen sail out early in the morning to catch fish for us to eat.

Some workers sell us the things we need. The shopkeeper in the corner shop and the market vendor at the stall sell goods like rice, soap, and fruit.

Some workers build and fix things. A carpenter builds chairs and houses out of wood. A mason lays blocks for strong walls. A plumber fixes the pipes that carry our water, and an electrician makes sure our lights and fans work.

Many workers provide services that help us directly. A teacher helps you learn. A doctor and a nurse keep us healthy. A police officer keeps us safe. A fire fighter puts out fires. A bus driver and a taxi driver carry people from place to place. A postman brings letters, and a garbage collector keeps our community clean.

Did You Know?

Some workers live and work right inside your community, like the shopkeeper down the road. Other workers come into the community to do their jobs, like a doctor who travels in to the clinic. And some services can only be found outside the community, such as the workers at an airport or a big hotel. It takes workers from many places, all working together, to give a community everything it needs.

Needs and Wants

Some things we truly must have, and some things are simply nice to have. The things we must have in order to live are called our needs. Food, clean water, clothes, and a safe home are all needs. The things that are pleasant to have, but that we could live without, are called our wants. A new toy, a sweet, or a video game are all wants.

Knowing the difference helps us understand which workers are most important of all. The workers who provide our needs — like the farmer who grows our food and the worker who brings us clean water — are doing some of the most important work in the whole community.

Essential Services and Other Services

Among all the services in a community, some are so important that the community simply could not manage without them. We call these essential services. The word essential means "completely necessary."

Think about what would happen if there were no police to keep us safe, no fire fighters to put out fires, no doctors and nurses at the hospital, and no clean water flowing to our homes. That is why these are called essential services. In fact, the law of Jamaica names certain services as essential.

Other services are helpful and good, but the community could manage for a while without them. A barber who cuts hair and a tailor who sews clothes provide useful other services. A community is happiest when it has both — the essential services that keep everyone safe, and the other services that make daily life better.

Did You Know?

Religious workers and places of worship, such as churches, also provide important services in many Jamaican communities. They comfort people, help families in need, and bring people together.

Sorting the Workers

Sorting things into groups helps us understand them better. One way to sort workers is by what they provide. Some workers provide goods, like the farmer, the fisherman, the carpenter, and the baker. Other workers provide services, like the teacher, the nurse, the bus driver, and the police officer.

Another way is to sort the service workers into those who provide essential services, like the police, fire service, and hospital workers, and those who provide other services, like the barber and the tailor. When you sort workers in these ways, you start to see just how many different people it takes to keep one community running.

Think About It

Pick three workers you know in your own community. For each one, decide: does this worker provide a good or a service? Is it an essential service or another kind?

Let's Practice

Take a short walk around your school or your community with a grown-up. Try to spot at least three workers. For each one, decide whether the worker is providing a good or a service, and whether it is an essential service or another kind.

Then try these quick questions:

  1. What is the difference between a good and a service?
  2. Name two essential services and explain why they are essential.
  3. What is the difference between a need and a want?

Focus Question 2: How do workers contribute to the development of my community?

Why People Work

People work for several good reasons. The first reason is to earn income, which is the money a worker is paid for doing a job. Income is also called wages. With this money, workers can buy the goods and services their own families need, such as food, clothes, and a home.

But money is not the only reason people work. Many people work because they enjoy their job and feel proud of it. A teacher feels proud when a child learns to read. A farmer feels proud of a healthy field of crops. People also work because their work helps other people and makes the whole community a better place to live.

We All Depend on One Another

No worker in a community works alone. Every worker depends on other workers, and other workers depend on them. We call this interdependence, which simply means "depending on each other."

Think about the bread you might eat for breakfast. The farmer grows the wheat. The truck driver carries the wheat to the bakery. The baker bakes it into bread. The shopkeeper sells the bread. Your mother or father buys it and brings it home. Not one of these people could give you your bread alone. The baker cannot bake without the farmer's wheat. The shopkeeper has nothing to sell without the baker's bread. That is interdependence.

Because workers depend on one another, they must cooperate, which means working together kindly and helpfully. When you work together with your classmates to clean the classroom, you are practising the very same cooperation that grown-up workers use every day.

Did You Know?

A resource is anything useful that helps workers do their jobs, such as wood for the carpenter, water for the farmer, or land for building. Communities share their resources, and workers use them wisely so that everyone can have what they need.

How Different Workers Develop the Community

When workers do their jobs well, the community grows and improves. We say the community develops.

Workers in health and sanitation, like doctors, nurses, and garbage collectors, keep the people healthy and the streets clean. Workers in security, like the police and security guards, keep everyone safe. Workers in education, like teachers, help children learn, building the community's future. Workers who provide utilities, like those who keep the water flowing and the electricity running, make daily life possible.

When all of these workers do their part, the community becomes a better place to live. Step by step, the hard work of many people helps the whole community to develop.

Think About It

Imagine your community without one group of these workers — say, without anyone collecting the garbage. What do you think would happen over time, and why?

Fishing Communities and the Workers Who Feed Us

Jamaica is an island surrounded by the sea, so fishing has always been important work. In many places around our coast there are fishing communities, where many people earn their living by catching fish. Early each morning, fishermen take their boats out and cast their nets. When they return, their fresh catch is sold in markets and shops, and it feeds families all over the island.

There is a well-known story called "The Great Catch". In the story, a fisherman named Peter and his friends had fished all night and caught nothing. Then they were told to lower their nets once more, and this time the nets filled with so many fish that they could hardly pull them in. The story shows us that fishing is hard and patient work, and that the fishermen who keep at it provide food and life for their whole community.

Did You Know?

Old Harbour Bay and Whitehouse are just two of the many fishing communities in Jamaica where workers bring in fresh fish that feeds people across the island.

Then and Now: How Work Has Changed

Many jobs have changed a great deal over the years. Long ago, much more work was done by hand. Farmers prepared the soil with simple tools like the hoe, the machete, and the fork. Today, some farmers use machines and engines that do the work faster. Long ago, washing was scrubbed by hand at the riverside; today many people use a washing machine. The tools workers use have changed, and so the work itself has changed too.

Some jobs that were common long ago are rarely seen today, while brand-new jobs have appeared, such as jobs working with computers and mobile phones. Another important change is this: long ago, some jobs were thought to be only for men and others only for women. Today we know that most jobs can be done by both men and women, working side by side. When everyone is free to do the work they are good at, the whole community grows stronger.

Let's Practice

Talk with an older family member about the kind of work people did when they were young. Find out one job or one tool that has changed since then, and tell a friend what you learned.

Then try these quick questions:

  1. What does the word interdependence mean?
  2. Give one reason why people go to work.
  3. Choose one group of workers and explain how they help the community develop.

Focus Question 3: How do rules help workers at the workplace?

What Are Rules?

A rule is something we are expected to do, or expected not to do, so that things are safe and fair for everyone. You follow rules every day. At home, there may be a rule about bedtime, or a rule that you wash your hands before you eat. At school, there are rules about putting up your hand before you speak and walking, not running, in the corridors.

Even your games have rules. When you play cricket, football, or a board game, everyone must follow the rules of the game, or the game falls apart and stops being fun. Rules are everywhere — at home, at school, at church, on the sports field, and in every job. Wherever people do things together, rules help them do it well.

Think About It

Name one rule you follow at home and one rule you follow at school. What do you think each rule is for?

Why Do We Need Rules?

Imagine a game of football with no rules. Players could pick up the ball, push each other over, or score in any goal they liked. Very quickly the game would turn into a mess, and nobody would enjoy it. The rules are exactly what make the game fair and fun for everyone.

Rules keep us safe, by stopping people from doing dangerous things. Rules keep things fair, so that everyone is treated the same way and no one is cheated. Rules help us get along, so that people can work and play together without quarrelling. And rules keep things orderly, so that work gets done smoothly.

Rules and laws are usually made by people in charge, such as parents at home, teachers at school, and leaders in the community. When rules are broken, there are usually consequences — something that happens as a result. A player who breaks the rules may have to sit out. Consequences remind us that rules matter.

Did You Know?

When people break important rules, it can hurt others, not just themselves. A driver who breaks the rules of the road can cause an accident. This is why obeying rules is a way of caring for the people around us.

Rules at the Workplace

The workplace is the place where a worker does his or her job — a farm, a shop, a school, a hospital, a building site, or a fishing boat. Every one of these places has rules to help the workers.

The most important reason for workplace rules is safety. A worker on a building site must wear a hard hat so that nothing falling can hurt his head. A worker in a kitchen must be careful with sharp knives and hot stoves. These rules protect workers from getting hurt, so that they can go home safely to their families.

Workplace rules also keep things fair. There may be rules about arriving on time, treating customers politely, and being honest. Rules also help workers work together. When everyone follows the same rules, they can cooperate smoothly, like the players on a well-trained team.

Think About It

Think about a shop or a clinic you have visited. Can you guess one rule the workers there must follow? How does that rule help the workers or the people they serve?

When Workers Obey the Rules

Workers who obey the rules and behave responsibly are not only helping themselves. They are helping their whole workplace, their community, and even their country. When workers follow the rules, the workplace runs smoothly and safely, customers are treated well, accidents are avoided, and good work gets done.

But when rules are broken, things can go wrong. If a worker is careless, someone might get hurt. If a worker is dishonest, customers lose trust. This is why obeying rules is one of the most caring and responsible things a worker can do.

Music shows us this lesson too. When a group of people sing a song together, especially in a round, they must keep in time, keep the right beat and rhythm, and sing in harmony. If everyone sang however they liked, the song would sound like noise. But when everyone follows the rules of the music, the singing becomes beautiful. A workplace is just the same: when everyone follows the rules, everything works in harmony.

Did You Know?

Many communities have rules and signs that protect everyone, like "No Littering" signs that keep our streets clean. When workers and citizens obey these rules, the whole community stays clean, safe, and pleasant for all.

Being a Rule-Follower Yourself

You do not have to wait until you are grown up to practise following rules. When you follow your class rules, take your turn fairly, play games by the rules, and treat others kindly, you are becoming the kind of responsible person who will one day be a trusted worker. The child who learns to obey good rules today grows into the worker who keeps a workplace safe and fair tomorrow.

Let's Practice

Draw a simple picture of one worker in your community doing their job. Show the tool or thing they use most, and write one sentence under your picture telling why this worker is important.

Then try these quick questions:

  1. Why do workplaces need rules? Give two reasons.
  2. How do safety rules help a worker on a building site?
  3. How does a worker who obeys the rules help the whole community?

Glossary: Words to Know

Goods: Things that workers make or grow that you can hold and use, like bread, shoes, or a chair.
Services: Helpful work that one person does for another, like teaching, driving a bus, or putting out a fire.
Provider: A worker who gives, or provides, the goods or services we need.
Consumer: A person who uses the goods and services that workers provide.
Community: The place where people live, work, and help one another, such as a town, district, or village.
Worker: A person who does a job to provide goods or services.
Occupation: The name of the job a person does, such as farmer, nurse, or carpenter.
Needs: The things we must have to live, such as food, clean water, clothes, and shelter.
Wants: The things that are nice to have but that we can live without, like toys and sweets.
Essential services: Services so important that a community cannot do without them, like the police, fire service, and hospital.
Interdependence: The way workers and people all depend on one another to live and work.
Income: The money a worker earns for doing a job; also called wages.
Resource: Anything useful that helps workers do their jobs, such as wood, water, or land.
Rules: The things we are expected to do, and not do, so that everyone is safe and fair.
Workplace: The place where a worker does his or her job, such as a shop, a farm, or a hospital.
Cooperation: Working together kindly to get a job done.
Consequence: Something that happens as a result of an action, such as what happens when a rule is broken.
