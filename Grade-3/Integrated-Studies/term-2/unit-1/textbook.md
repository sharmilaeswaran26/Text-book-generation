**Grade 3 · Integrated Studies · Term 2**

## A Letter to the Young Reader

Think for a moment about everything that happened before you arrived at school this morning. Perhaps you ate a slice of bread for breakfast. Perhaps you put on a clean uniform and a pair of shoes. Perhaps you rode in a bus or a taxi, or walked along a road, and turned on a tap to brush your teeth. Every single one of those simple things was made possible by a worker you may never have met.

Somebody planted and reaped the wheat that became your bread. Somebody sewed your uniform and made your shoes. Somebody drove the bus, mended the road, and brought clean water to your home. All around you, all day long, people are working hard so that you and your family have the things you need. We hardly notice them, but our lives would be very different without them.

In this chapter, you are going to meet these busy people. We call them the workers of our community, and they are the providers of the goods and services we use every day. You will learn who they are and the helpful work they do. You will discover how their work helps your whole community to grow strong. And you will learn about the rules that keep workers safe and fair while they do their jobs. By the time you reach the last page, you will look at your own community with brand-new eyes. Let us begin.

---

## What You Will Learn

In this chapter you will learn what goods and services are, and how to tell the two apart. You will be able to name many of the workers in your community and to sort them into groups, including those who provide things we cannot do without, called essential services. You will learn why people work and what the word interdependence means — that workers and families all depend on one another. You will compare the jobs and tools of long ago with the jobs and tools of today. You will think about how each kind of worker helps your community to develop and grow. Finally, you will learn what workplace rules are, why every workplace needs them, and how good rules keep workers safe, fair, and able to do their best.

## The Big Questions

As you read, keep these three questions in your mind. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to answer each one in your own words.

1. Who are the providers of goods and services that we need?
2. How do workers contribute to the development of my community?
3. How do rules help workers at the workplace?

The chapter is built around these three questions. Part One meets the workers who provide what we need. Part Two shows how their work helps the whole community to grow. Part Three explores the rules that keep workplaces safe and fair.

## Words to Know

Here are some important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about learning them all at once. You will understand each one as you read, because every word is explained carefully inside the lessons.

- **Goods** – things that workers make or grow that you can hold and use, like bread, shoes, or a chair.
- **Services** – helpful work that one person does for another, like teaching, driving a bus, or putting out a fire.
- **Provider** – a worker who gives, or provides, the goods or services we need.
- **Consumer** – a person who uses the goods and services that workers provide.
- **Community** – the place where people live, work, and help one another, such as a town, district, or village.
- **Worker** – a person who does a job to provide goods or services.
- **Occupation** – the name of the job a person does, such as farmer, nurse, or carpenter.
- **Needs** – the things we must have to live, such as food, clean water, clothes, and shelter.
- **Wants** – the things that are nice to have but that we can live without, like toys and sweets.
- **Essential services** – services so important that a community cannot do without them, like the police, fire service, and hospital.
- **Interdependence** – the way workers and people all depend on one another to live and work.
- **Income** – the money a worker earns for doing a job; also called wages.
- **Rules** – the things we are expected to do, and not do, so that everyone is safe and fair.
- **Workplace** – the place where a worker does his or her job, such as a shop, a farm, or a hospital.
- **Cooperation** – working together kindly to get a job done.

# Part One: The People Who Provide What We Need

Every day you use things that other people have made, and you receive help that other people have given. In this first part of the chapter, you will learn about the two kinds of things that workers provide, and you will meet many of the busy people in your own community who provide them.

## Goods and Services

Let us begin with a simple idea. The workers in your community provide two kinds of things. We call them **goods** and **services**.

**Goods** are things you can hold in your hands and use. A loaf of bread is a good. So is a pair of shoes, a school bag, a wooden chair, a bunch of bananas, and a bottle of water. Goods are things that have been made or grown by somebody. When you go to a shop and buy a tin of milk or a bag of rice, you are buying goods.

**Services** are different. A service is not a thing you can hold. A service is helpful work that one person does for another. When a teacher teaches your class, that is a service. When a bus driver carries you safely to school, that is a service. When a doctor makes you better, when a barber cuts your hair, or when a fireman puts out a fire, each of those is a service. You cannot pick up a service and carry it home in a bag, but it helps you all the same.

Here is an easy way to remember the difference. If it is a *thing* you can touch and keep, it is a **good**. If it is *help* that someone does for you, it is a **service**. Both goods and services are important, and we need them both every single day.

> **Think About It:** Look around the room you are in right now. Can you point to three goods that a worker made? Can you think of one service that a worker did for you today?

## Workers Are the Providers

Goods and services do not appear by magic. Behind every good and every service there is a worker. A worker is a person who does a job, and we call that worker a **provider**, because the worker provides — that means gives — the goods and services we need.

The person who uses those goods and services is called a **consumer**. When you eat the bread, wear the shoes, or ride the bus, you are a consumer. Almost everyone is a consumer, because we all use goods and services. And almost every grown-up is also a provider, because they do a job that helps somebody else. In this way, we are all linked together.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine a farmer named Mr Brown who grows yam and tomatoes. Mr Brown is a provider, because he provides goods — the food he grows. He sells his food at the market to a lady named Miss Pearl, who buys it. Right now, Miss Pearl is a consumer. But Miss Pearl is a nurse, and at the hospital she provides a service by caring for sick people. So Miss Pearl is a provider too. The farmer feeds the nurse, and the nurse cares for the farmer. Each one provides, and each one consumes. That is how a community works.

## Meet the Workers in Our Community

If you walk around your community with your eyes open, you will see workers everywhere. Some make goods. Some provide services. Let us meet a few of them.

Some workers grow and catch our food. **Farmers** plant crops like yam, cabbage, and banana, and raise animals like chickens and goats. **Fishermen** sail out in their boats early in the morning to catch fish for us to eat. Without these workers, the markets and shops would be empty.

Some workers sell us the things we need. The **shopkeeper** in the corner shop and the **market vendor** at the stall sell goods like rice, soap, and fruit, so that we do not have to grow or make everything ourselves.

Some workers build and fix things. A **carpenter** builds chairs, tables, and houses out of wood. A **mason** lays the blocks that make strong walls. A **plumber** fixes the pipes that carry our water, and an **electrician** makes sure our lights and fans work.

Many workers provide services that help us directly. A **teacher** helps you learn to read, write, and count. A **doctor** and a **nurse** keep us healthy and care for us when we are sick. A **police officer** helps keep us safe. A **fire fighter** rushes to put out fires. A **bus driver** and a **taxi driver** carry people from place to place. A **postman** brings letters, and a **garbage collector** keeps our community clean.

> **Did You Know?** Some workers live and work right inside your community, like the shopkeeper down the road. Other workers come into the community to do their jobs, like a doctor who travels in to the clinic. And some services can only be found outside the community, such as the workers at an airport or a big hotel. It takes workers from many places, all working together, to give a community everything it needs.

## Needs and Wants

When we talk about the things workers provide, it helps to know that not everything is equally important. Some things we truly must have, and some things are simply nice to have.

The things we **must** have in order to live are called our **needs**. Food, clean water, clothes, and a safe place to live — a home — are all needs. We cannot stay healthy and alive without them.

The things that are pleasant to have, but that we could live without, are called our **wants**. A new toy, a sweet, a fancy bicycle, or a video game are all wants. They are lovely to have, but nobody needs them to stay alive.

Knowing the difference helps us understand which workers are the most important of all. The workers who provide our needs — like the farmer who grows our food and the worker who brings us clean water — are doing some of the most important work in the whole community.

> **Think About It:** Make a quick list in your head of three things you needed today and two things you only wanted. Which workers provided the things you needed?

## Essential Services and Other Services

Among all the services in a community, some are so important that the community simply could not manage without them. We give these very special services a name. We call them **essential services**. The word *essential* means "completely necessary."

Think about what would happen if there were no police to keep us safe, no fire fighters to put out fires, no doctors and nurses at the hospital, and no clean water flowing to our homes. Life would become difficult and even dangerous very quickly. That is why these are called essential services. In fact, the law of Jamaica names certain services as essential, because the country needs them so badly.

Other services are helpful and good, but the community could still manage for a while without them. A barber who cuts hair, a tailor who sews clothes, and a person who paints houses all provide useful services, but we call these **other services**, because they are not quite as urgent as the essential ones. A community is happiest when it has both — the essential services that keep everyone safe and well, and the other services that make daily life better and more comfortable.

> **Did You Know?** Religious workers and places of worship, such as churches, also provide important services in many Jamaican communities. They comfort people, help families in need, and bring people together. This is one more way that workers and helpers serve the people around them.

## Sorting the Workers

Now that you have met so many workers, you can begin to sort them into groups. Sorting things into groups helps us understand them better.

One way to sort workers is by what they provide. Some workers provide **goods**, like the farmer, the fisherman, the carpenter, and the baker. Other workers provide **services**, like the teacher, the nurse, the bus driver, and the police officer.

Another way is to sort the service workers into those who provide **essential services**, like the police, fire service, and hospital workers, and those who provide **other services**, like the barber and the tailor.

You can even sort workers by where they do their jobs, such as those who work in health and sanitation, those who work in security, those who work in education, and those who keep our water and electricity flowing. When you sort workers in these ways, you start to see just how many different people it takes to keep one community running smoothly.

> **Think About It:** Pick three workers you know in your own community. For each one, decide: does this worker provide a good or a service? Is it an essential service or another kind?

# Part Two: How Workers Help Our Community Grow

You have met the workers who provide our goods and services. Now we will discover something even more wonderful: how all of these workers, by doing their own small jobs, help the whole community to grow stronger and better. We call this helping the community to **develop**.

## Why People Work

Have you ever wondered why grown-ups go to work every day, even when they would rather rest? People work for several good reasons, and understanding them helps you understand your community.

The first reason is to earn **income**, which is the money a worker is paid for doing a job. Income is also called wages. With this money, workers can buy the goods and services their own families need, such as food, clothes, and a home. So a worker earns money by helping others, and then uses that money to buy help from others. It goes round and round.

But money is not the only reason people work. Many people work because they enjoy their job and feel proud of it. A teacher feels proud when a child learns to read. A farmer feels proud of a healthy field of crops. People also work because their work helps other people and makes the whole community a better place to live. Work gives people a way to use their skills and to feel that they matter.

> **Think About It:** Ask yourself what kind of work you might like to do one day. What is it about that job that you would enjoy, and how would it help other people?

## We All Depend on One Another

Here is one of the most important ideas in this whole chapter. No worker in a community works alone. Every worker depends on other workers, and other workers depend on them. We have a special word for this. We call it **interdependence**.

The word *interdependence* simply means "depending on each other." Let us see how it works.

**Let's look at an example.** Think about the bread you might eat for breakfast. The farmer grows the wheat. The truck driver carries the wheat to the bakery. The baker bakes it into bread. The shopkeeper sells the bread in the shop. Your mother or father buys it and brings it home. Not one of these people could give you your bread alone. The baker cannot bake without the farmer's wheat. The shopkeeper has nothing to sell without the baker's bread. Each worker depends on the others. That is interdependence, and it is happening all around you, all the time.

Because workers depend on one another, they must **cooperate**, which means working together kindly and helpfully. When workers cooperate, jobs get done well and the whole community benefits. When you work together with your classmates to clean the classroom or finish a project, you are practising the very same cooperation that grown-up workers use every day.

> **Did You Know?** A **resource** is anything useful that helps workers do their jobs, such as wood for the carpenter, water for the farmer, or land for building. Communities share their resources, and workers use them wisely so that everyone can have what they need.

## How Different Workers Develop the Community

When workers do their jobs well, the community grows and improves. We say the community **develops**. Let us look at how some groups of workers help their community to develop.

Workers in **health and sanitation**, like doctors, nurses, and garbage collectors, keep the people of the community healthy and the streets clean. A healthy, clean community is a happy and strong community.

Workers in **security**, like the police and security guards, keep everyone safe. When people feel safe, they can go about their work and their lives without fear.

Workers in **education**, like teachers, help children learn. The children of today will become the workers of tomorrow, so teachers are quietly building the community's future.

Workers who provide **utilities**, like those who keep the water flowing and the electricity running, make daily life possible. With clean water and electric power, families can cook, study, and stay healthy.

When all of these workers do their part, the community becomes a better place to live. New shops may open, roads may be mended, and families may grow stronger. Step by step, the hard work of many people helps the whole community to develop.

> **Think About It:** Imagine your community without one group of these workers — say, without anyone collecting the garbage. What do you think would happen over time, and why?

## Fishing Communities and the Workers Who Feed Us

Jamaica is an island surrounded by the sea, so fishing has always been an important kind of work for us. In many places around our coast there are **fishing communities**, where many of the people earn their living by catching fish.

Early each morning, fishermen take their boats out onto the water and cast their nets and lines. When they return, their fresh catch is sold in markets and shops, and it feeds families all over the island. The fishermen also create work for others: people who mend the nets, people who sell the fish, and people who cook and serve it. In this way, one fishing community helps to feed and support many other people far beyond its own shore.

There is a well-known story called **"The Great Catch"** that reminds us how important fishing work has always been. In the story, a fisherman named Peter and his friends had fished all night and caught nothing. They were tired and disappointed. Then they were told to lower their nets once more, and this time the nets filled with so many fish that they could hardly pull them in. The story shows us that fishing is hard and patient work, and that the fishermen who keep at it provide food and life for their whole community.

> **Did You Know?** Old Harbour Bay and Whitehouse are just two of the many fishing communities in Jamaica where workers bring in fresh fish that feeds people across the island.

## Then and Now: How Work Has Changed

If you could talk to your great-grandparents about the work people did fifty years ago, you might be surprised. Many jobs have changed a great deal over the years, and some old jobs have almost disappeared while new ones have appeared.

Long ago, much more work was done by hand. Farmers prepared the soil with simple tools like the hoe, the machete, and the fork, and sometimes with the help of animals. Today, some farmers use machines and engines that do the work faster. Long ago, washing was scrubbed by hand at the riverside; today many people use a washing machine. The **tools** workers use have changed, and so the work itself has changed too.

Some jobs that were common long ago are rarely seen today, while brand-new jobs have appeared that nobody could have imagined years ago, such as jobs working with computers and mobile phones. When you grow up, there may even be jobs that do not exist yet at all. The world of work is always changing, just as our community is always growing and developing.

Another important change is this: long ago, some jobs were thought to be only for men and others only for women. Today we know that most jobs can be done by both men and women, working side by side. A woman can be a doctor, a pilot, or a police officer, and a man can be a nurse, a cook, or a teacher. When everyone is free to do the work they are good at, the whole community grows stronger.

> **Think About It:** Ask an older person in your family about a job they did, or saw being done, when they were young. What tools did the worker use? How is that job different today?

# Part Three: Rules That Keep Workers Safe and Fair

You have learned who our workers are and how their work helps the whole community develop. In this last part of the chapter, we will discover something that every workplace needs in order to run well: **rules**. Rules may sound dull, but they do something wonderful — they keep workers safe, fair, and happy. Let us find out how.

## What Are Rules?

You already know about rules, even if you have never thought about them carefully. A **rule** is something we are expected to do, or expected not to do, so that things are safe and fair for everyone.

You follow rules every day. At home, there may be a rule about bedtime, or a rule that you wash your hands before you eat. At school, there are rules about putting up your hand before you speak and walking, not running, in the corridors. Even your games have rules. When you play cricket, football, or a board game, everyone must follow the rules of the game, or the game falls apart and stops being fun.

So rules are not only for the workplace. Rules are everywhere — at home, at school, at church, on the sports field, and in every job. Wherever people do things together, rules help them do it well.

> **Think About It:** Name one rule you follow at home and one rule you follow at school. What do you think each rule is for?

## Why Do We Need Rules?

You might sometimes wish there were no rules at all. But imagine, just for a moment, a game with no rules. In a game of football with no rules, players could pick up the ball, push each other over, or score in any goal they liked. Very quickly the game would turn into a mess, and nobody would enjoy it. The rules are exactly what make the game fair and fun for everyone.

The same is true of life and work. Rules are needed for some very good reasons. Rules keep us **safe**, by stopping people from doing dangerous things. Rules keep things **fair**, so that everyone is treated the same way and no one is cheated. Rules help us **get along**, so that people can work and play together without quarrelling. And rules keep things **orderly**, so that everyone knows what to do and work gets done smoothly.

Rules and laws are usually made by people in charge, such as parents at home, teachers at school, and leaders in the community and the country. When rules are broken, there are usually **consequences** — something that happens as a result. A player who breaks the rules of a game may have to sit out. A worker who breaks the rules at a job may get into trouble. Consequences remind us that rules matter and help everyone remember to follow them.

> **Did You Know?** When people break important rules, it can hurt others, not just themselves. A driver who breaks the rules of the road can cause an accident. This is why obeying rules is a way of caring for the people around us.

## Rules at the Workplace

Now let us bring all of this to the **workplace**, which is the place where a worker does his or her job. A workplace might be a farm, a shop, a school, a hospital, a building site, or a fishing boat. Every one of these places has rules, and those rules are there to help the workers.

The most important reason for workplace rules is **safety**. Many jobs can be dangerous if they are done carelessly. A worker on a building site must wear a hard hat so that nothing falling can hurt his head. A worker in a kitchen must be careful with sharp knives and hot stoves. A fisherman must follow safety rules out on the water. These rules protect workers from getting hurt, so that they can go home safely to their families at the end of the day.

Workplace rules also keep things **fair**. There may be rules about arriving at work on time, about treating customers politely, and about being honest. These rules make sure that every worker is treated fairly and that the work is done well. When customers know they will be treated fairly, they trust the workers and come back again.

Rules also help workers **work together**. When everyone in a workplace follows the same rules, they can cooperate smoothly, like the players on a well-trained team. Nobody is confused about what to do, and the job gets done well.

> **Think About It:** Think about a shop or a clinic you have visited. Can you guess one rule the workers there must follow? How does that rule help the workers or the people they serve?

## When Workers Obey the Rules

Here is the happy truth about rules: workers who obey the rules and behave responsibly are not only helping themselves. They are helping their whole workplace, their community, and even their country.

When workers follow the rules, the workplace runs smoothly and safely. Customers are treated well, accidents are avoided, and good work gets done. A worker who is honest, polite, and careful makes the business stronger, and a community full of such workers becomes a better place for everyone to live.

But when rules are broken, things can go wrong. If a worker is careless, someone might get hurt. If a worker is dishonest or unfair, customers lose trust. If everyone ignored the rules, workplaces would become unsafe and unfair, and the whole community would suffer. This is why obeying rules is one of the most caring and responsible things a worker can do.

There is even a place where music shows us this lesson. When a group of people sing a song together, especially in a round, they must follow certain rules of music. They must keep in time, keep the right beat and rhythm, and sing in harmony, which means blending their voices nicely together. If everyone sang however they liked, the song would sound like noise. But when everyone follows the rules of the music, the singing becomes beautiful. A workplace is just the same: when everyone follows the rules, everything works in harmony.

> **Did You Know?** Many communities have rules and signs that protect everyone, like "No Littering" signs that keep our streets clean. When workers and citizens obey these rules, the whole community stays clean, safe, and pleasant for all.

## Being a Rule-Follower Yourself

You do not have to wait until you are grown up to practise following rules. Right now, in your own school and home, you are learning the very same skill that good workers use every day.

When you follow your class rules, take your turn fairly, play games by the rules, and treat others kindly, you are becoming the kind of responsible person who will one day be a trusted worker. The child who learns to obey good rules today grows into the worker who keeps a workplace safe and fair tomorrow. In this way, learning to follow rules is a gift you give to your future self and to your whole community.

> **Think About It:** What is one rule at your school that you think is very important? How would your school be different if no one followed that rule?

## Chapter Summary

In this chapter, you explored the workers of your community and the important part they play in your life.

You learned that workers provide two kinds of things: goods, which are things you can hold and use, and services, which are helpful work that one person does for another. You met many workers, such as farmers, fishermen, shopkeepers, carpenters, teachers, nurses, police officers, and bus drivers. You learned to sort them into groups, and to tell our needs from our wants. You discovered that some services are so important that we call them essential services, like the police, the fire service, and the hospital.

You then learned why people work — to earn an income, to feel proud, and to help others. You met the big idea of interdependence, which means that all workers depend on one another, and you saw how cooperation helps a community grow and develop. You compared the jobs and tools of long ago with those of today, and you learned that most jobs can now be done by both men and women.

Finally, you explored the rules that keep workplaces safe and fair. You learned what rules are, why we need them, and how workplace rules protect workers, treat people fairly, and help everyone work together. You discovered that workers who obey the rules help not only themselves, but their whole community and country — and that by following good rules today, you are practising to become a trusted worker tomorrow.

## Key Takeaways

- Workers provide **goods**, which are things you can hold, and **services**, which are helpful work done for others.
- A worker who provides goods or services is a **provider**, and the person who uses them is a **consumer**.
- Our **needs** are things we must have to live, while our **wants** are things that are nice but not necessary.
- **Essential services**, like the police, fire service, and hospital, are so important that a community cannot do without them.
- People work to earn an **income**, to feel proud, and to help others, and all workers depend on one another through **interdependence**.
- Different groups of workers — in health, security, education, and utilities — help the whole community to grow and develop.
- The jobs and tools of today are often different from those of long ago, and most jobs can now be done by both men and women.
- **Rules** keep workplaces safe, fair, and orderly, and workers who obey the rules help their whole community and country.
