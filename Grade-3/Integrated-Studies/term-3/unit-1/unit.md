# Grade 3 — Integrated Studies — Term 3 — Unit 1

> **Faithful curriculum extract** from the Jamaica National Standards Curriculum (NSC), Grade 3, Integrated Studies, Term 3, Unit 1 (pp. 105–131, Version 5, June 2018). This file preserves the curriculum as written for teachers. The student-facing lesson is in `textbook.md`.

**Theme:** Living and Non-Living Things in my Environment
**Sub-theme (Grades 1–3 overarching theme):** All About Me and My Environment

**Focus Questions:**
1. How can I tell if something in my environment is living?
2. Why do living things need specific habitats?
3. How are living and non-living things affected by changes in the environment?
4. In what ways are living things dependent on non-living things?

---

## Focus Question 1: How can I tell if something in my environment is living?

### Prior Learning
Check that students can:
- Tell that they are living beings

### Attainment Targets and Objectives

**Science**
- Explore the environment in order to relate everyday experiences to simple scientific concepts and processes.

Objectives:
- Differentiate between/categorize living and non-living things
- Identify the basic characteristics of living things (move, grow, reproduce, breathe)
- Observe changes that occur to a seed as it germinates over a period of time
- Make inferences and draw conclusions about the requirements for the survival of plants and animals

**Religious Education**
- Demonstrate an awareness of some of the ways in which religion influences changes in the lifestyles of persons within society.

Objective:
- Discuss sacred stories that depict how living and non-living things were affected by changes in the environment — the Creation story, Noah and the Ark

**Mathematics**
- Interpret data presented in simple tables, pictographs and bar graphs using horizontal or vertical representation.
- Represent and interpret numerical/pictorial data.

Objectives:
- Estimate, measure and compare units of measure of plants and other living things in the environment
- Present information using graphics
- Use tally marks to record data (where appropriate)
- Present information about living things using simple charts and graphs

**Social Studies**
- Develop an understanding of the interdependent relationship between man and his environment.

Objectives:
- Identify and know the basic needs of living things
- Prove that plants and animals are living things

**Music**
- Identify, create, and respond to sounds of living things in the environment.

Objectives:
- Select the appropriate instrument, concentrating on pitch and rhythm, to illustrate the character of the animals of their choice in song composed
- Use classroom instruments and voice to reproduce the sounds made by some living things

**Physical Education**
- Successfully demonstrate motor movements appropriate to the grade level.
- Change rhythm, speed, direction of movements and develop spatial awareness.

Objective:
- Move their bodies in response to different environmental stimuli, e.g. a light breeze, flowing water (like a flowing stream/river), loud thunder, a flash of lightning, sound

**Language Arts**
- Communicate with confidence and competence for different purposes and audiences, using SJE and JC appropriately and creatively.
- Apply relevant decoding skills to the reading process.
- Write well-constructed paragraphs or sentences which have linking sentences within and between them.
- Develop approaches to the writing process.
- Use a range of punctuation correctly.

Objectives:
- Talk freely about observations
- Read fiction and nonfiction books about living and nonliving things
- Generate own sentences using techniques that show the relation between sentences
- Develop paragraphs that show the relationship between the sentences, conveying meaningful and relevant ideas
- Select and use appropriate punctuation marks to organize ideas into a coherent structure, including layout, sections and paragraphs

**Visual Arts**
- Plan and design.

Objectives:
- Create monoprints of living things found in the environment
- Use a variety of artistic forms to represent relationships between people, animals and plants

**Resource & Technology**
- Explore methods and procedures — use simple materials, tools and equipment to develop skills in cutting, assembling and decorating.

**ICT**
- Communication & Collaboration — use selected ICT tools (e.g. drawing tool, word processor, presentation software, graphic organizers) to communicate information.
- Designing & Producing — use selected ICT tools effectively to produce multimedia presentations.
- Digital Citizenship — demonstrate safe, respectful and responsible online communication.

Objectives:
- Use software applications to create digital stories about living and non-living things in their environment
- Collect pictures using an image-capturing device

**Drama**
- Respond critically and aesthetically to a variety of stimuli through dramatic presentations.

Objective:
- Perform a piece to effectively portray three main characteristics of living things

### Suggested Teaching and Learning Activities
Students will:
- Go on a field trip in groups to observe the various living or non-living things (insects, lizards, plants/stones, dead leaves, twigs) in the environment/school yard. Use an image-capturing device to capture pictures of living and non-living things being observed. Brainstorm what is a living and a non-living thing, and categorize into plants and animals. Write a definition of what a living thing is and share it with the class.
- Construct a poster with two columns, living and non-living. Examine various items/pictures. Sort objects or pictures into living and non-living and post under the correct heading.
- View a video clip / listen to / read the story "The Greedy Dog" and list the characteristics that show the basic characteristics of living/non-living things. Discuss and list the characteristics that indicate that something is alive or living. Perform a creative piece that depicts the characteristics of a living thing.
- Write in their journals a brief description of the four common characteristics of the things in each category.
- Select three peas or corn seeds and place them in a transparent container with moistened paper. Make predictions about how tall the seeds will grow and how long it will take. Record the changes over the next 10–14 days by measuring the roots/height (or capture images of each change). Measure growth every two days and record in a log book. Once grown, discuss findings and compare actual growth with predictions. Represent the data on a pictograph/bar graph.
- Interpret data, draw a conclusion and share with the class. Write sentences using SJE and answer questions in relation to their specific graph.
- Using appropriate devices/tools, create a sound picture depicting environmental sounds that describe living things, e.g. animals/insects. Compare the living things described in the music with the sounds used. Respond to questions such as: How does the music help us imagine its size, how it moves, what it looks like, how it behaves?
- Perform an increasing variety of simple songs and sound pieces with attention to correct rhythm, pitch, phrasing, articulation and expression.
- Perform various movements in response to different sounds. Discuss what they need to do in response to the different sounds and the movement made.
- Working in small groups, manipulate playdough, scissors and papier-mâché. Plan, design and create 3D models showing the environment with living and non-living things. Share models with the class.
- Discuss the creation story in class. In groups, make a diorama of the creation story in sequential order. View and critique the diorama.

### Key Skills
Observe living things · Use device · Brainstorm · Categorize living/non-living things · Construct chart · Examine pictures · Sort objects · Listen for information · List characteristics · Perform creative piece · Write descriptions · Investigate · Record · Predict · Measure · Write journal entry · Discuss · Compare · Construct bar/pictograph · Interpret data · Draw conclusion · Write sentences · Create sound picture · Perform simple songs · Perform movements · Manipulate learning materials · Create 3D model · Discuss stories · Create diorama · Sequence events · Critique diorama

### Assessment
- Definition correctly written; objects/pictures sorted correctly
- Chart constructed; pictures examined; objects sorted
- Characteristics identified correctly; performance clearly demonstrates characteristics; four common characteristics accurately described
- Measurement accurately recorded; journal entries accurately recorded; data accurately represented
- Data interpreted correctly; sentences constructed correctly; questions answered correctly
- Picture sounds satisfactorily created; pupils respond satisfactorily to questions; songs adequately depict sounds of living things
- Movement adequately represents sounds
- 3D models satisfactorily created to represent models of non-living things
- Diorama adequately represents the sequence of story events

### Learning Outcomes
Students will be able to:
- Distinguish between living and non-living things
- Categorize and classify living things
- Conduct experiments, make predictions, record observations and draw conclusions on the growth of seeds
- Perform movements that show how living things move
- Construct a graph and interpret data on the graph
- Write properly constructed sentences and paragraphs in their journals

### Points to Note
- Students must be made aware that all living things are created for a purpose. Therefore, they should take care of living things in the environment.

### Extended Learning
- Create a Science digital story entitled "Living Things in my Environment".
- Research who botanists and zoologists are.
- Read books or watch videos based on living things.

## Focus Question 2: Why do living things need specific habitats?

### Prior Learning
Check that children can:
- Distinguish between living and non-living things

### Attainment Targets and Objectives

**Science**
- Explore selected life processes in humans, the interdependence between living things in the environment, and how lifestyles determine health and well-being in humans.

Objectives:
- Compare and contrast a given group of animals and their habitats
- Collect information from a variety of sources about special habitats of living things

**Religious Education**
- Learning how religion influences change; demonstrate an awareness of some of the ways in which religion influences changes in the lifestyles of persons within society.

Objectives:
- Outline the responsibilities of humans to have dominion and make provisions for habitats for animals
- Respond to characters or situations in religious stories relating to living things and their habitats
- Identify that animals and plants were created by God to survive in specific habitats

**Mathematics**
- Collect, organize, interpret and represent data and make inferences by applying knowledge of statistics and probability.

Objectives:
- Identify geometric shapes in natural and man-made objects
- Collect and record attribute data relating to living things in their different habitats
- Use different units of measure; estimate, measure and compare living things in their environment
- Construct simple charts or graphs to illustrate which geometric shapes are prevalent in the habitats of living things

**Social Studies / Civics**
- Develop an understanding of the interdependent relationship between man and his environment.
- Demonstrate awareness of the ways in which the Jamaican identity is shown through interactions with, and responses to, the environment and natural hazards.

Objectives:
- Differentiate between plants and animals of different habitats
- Explain why we need to preserve the natural habitats of living things in the environment
- Participate in ways and make suggestions to mitigate against harm and neglect to their immediate environment at school
- Appreciate that the habitats of living things must be cared for and respected in order to preserve them
- Learn that in order to successfully thrive, living things need to live in their natural/normal habitat

**Music**
- Create musical compositions exploring varied elements of music.

Objectives:
- Utilize rhythm and melody to create a song about animals and their habitats
- Select the appropriate instrument, concentrating on pitch and rhythm, to illustrate the character of the animals of their choice in song composed

**Physical Education**
- Develop the understanding and skills that support healthy, safe and active individuals.

Objectives:
- Practice individual techniques in ball skills
- Demonstrate the importance of following rules and working collaboratively when playing games

**Language Arts**
- Communicate with confidence and competence for different purposes and audiences, using SJE and JC appropriately and creatively.
- Research activities on issues and interests by generating ideas and exploring texts using a range of strategies.
- Develop approaches to the writing process.

Objectives:
- Spell words appropriate to grade, related to habitats
- Share ideas and feelings about situations related to animals and their habitats
- Research information from a variety of sources about specific habitats and document in a journal
- Sustain expression in SJE
- Use structural analysis to understand vocabulary and communicate ideas relating to living things and their habitats
- Make factual statements about the physical environment (e.g. pollution, natural and man-made resources, habitats)
- Write to share ideas and feelings on what they have observed about the habitats of some animals

**Visual Arts**
- Create and innovate; plan and design; appreciate and respond.

Objectives:
- Identify basic shapes, forms, colours and textures observed in the physical environment
- Design and create displays depicting how they care for and protect habitats
- Use the principles of line, shapes, colours and textures in nature to produce models of different habitats

**ICT**
- Use appropriate digital tools and resources to plan and conduct research, aid critical thinking, manage projects, solve problems and make informed decisions.

Objective:
- Incorporate the use of image-capturing devices to create a photo journal of habitats of living things

**Drama**
- Explore and create, exercising critical thinking skills throughout the dramatic process.

Objective:
- Demonstrate how to use facial expression and body gestures to convey how an animal would feel if removed from its natural habitat

**Resource & Technology**
- Use simple materials, tools and equipment to develop skills in cutting, assembling and decorating.

Objective:
- Plan, design and create a habitat for an animal of their choice

### Suggested Teaching and Learning Activities
Pupils will:
- Make 3D models depicting living things and their habitats, using discarded materials. Discuss the use of lines, shapes, colours and textures in their models and suggest ways the designs could be adapted to suit the available materials and the type of habitat.
- Participate in the game "Bull in the Pen". Discuss the various types of materials needed to build a pen to keep in a ferocious bull.
- Select three or four living things and investigate the habitats they live in (e.g. lizards, humans, worms, crocodiles, ants, dogs, butterflies, fish). Use an image-capturing device to document what the habitat looks like (in trees, on leaves, in soil or in houses). Write and share findings through a photo journal in a class display.
- Go on a field trip (nature walk) and count animals found on vegetation. Tally the numbers of different types of animals and record where they were found (on trees, grass, holes or water). Discuss why particular animals are found in specific locations (indigenous) and write a brief description using SJE (1–3 paragraphs) on why a particular location was beneficial to the named animal.
- Create and perform a dramatic piece to convey how a selected animal would behave if removed from its habitat and placed in an unsuitable area. Include an original song that depicts the plight of the animal, its feelings and mannerisms, and explain why it is necessary for that animal to remain in its habitat. In groups, discuss how animals would react if removed from their habitats (e.g. a fish taken from the sea and put in a tank).
- In groups, do a project on how the habitats of people in different parts of the world differ from their own, and report findings in a display (e.g. Botswana, Alaska, India). Create a 3D model of that habitat with an accompanying narrative speaking to the country, the number of persons that can live there, the climate, and the materials used (e.g. Alaska — igloo — made of ice). Incorporate vocabulary words and appropriate grammatical structures.
- Talk about humans as living creatures and how they need exercise for healthy living. On the playground, adapt the game "Blue bird, blue bird in and out the window" using the different animals and their habitats, e.g. "Lizard, lizard on and off the tree limb". Work cooperatively in groups and incorporate the use of a ball as they sing and move.
- Investigate habitats in and around their community. Construct simple charts or graphs to illustrate which geometric shapes are prevalent in the habitats investigated (e.g. a dog kennel has rectangles and squares; a beehive has hexagon shapes). Each child sketches their favourite habitat.
- Listen to and discuss the story "The Bremen Town Musicians" or another suitable story. Discuss and describe the habitats of these animals and why their habitats are ideal for them. Create a sound collage of the sounds of the animals in the story.

### Key Skills
Create 3D models · Discuss elements of art · Discuss materials needed · Manipulate image-capturing device · Write information in journal · Counting · Tally different types of animals · Discuss · Communicate ideas through performance piece · Create song · Research habitats of the world · Report findings · Manipulate ball · Work cooperatively · Investigate habitats · Construct charts or graphs · Sketch habitats · Listening · Creating sounds

### Assessment
- Models satisfactorily created and discussed
- Materials for building adequately discussed
- Images captured and displayed creatively in a photo journal; written findings reflect accurate knowledge of relevant habitats
- Animals successfully tallied and paragraphs proficiently written
- Dramatic piece successfully conveyed the message; song included was appropriate and relevant; group discussion accurately depicts animal behaviour
- Creativity evident in the creation of the models; report adequately reflects an understanding of a variety of habitats and uses appropriate grammatical structures; models satisfactorily represent the habitat being depicted
- Game creatively adapted to reflect an understanding of a variety of habitats
- Geometric shapes well documented on charts/graphs; habitats appropriately selected; sketches satisfactorily represent favourite habitat
- Sound collage creatively done

### Learning Outcomes
Students will be able to:
- Understand that in order to successfully thrive, living things need to live in their natural/normal habitat
- Use the principles of line, shapes, colours and textures in nature to produce a mobile of different habitats
- Use facial expression and body gestures to convey how an animal would feel if removed from its natural habitat
- Recognize the meaning of the word in relation to indigenous animals that are only found in specific parts of the world
- Appreciate that animals and plants were created by God to survive in specific habitats
- Plan, design and create a habitat for an animal of their choice
- Recognize that animals and plants adapt to their different life/climatic zones
- Develop individual techniques and team tactics to direct the ball towards the target area and away from their opponent
- Use untuned and tuned instruments to recreate the sounds made by specific living things
- Communicate and collaborate safely and responsibly online

### Points to Note
- Ensure all safety rules are observed when dealing with plants and animals.
- Discuss both natural and man-made homes of animals.
- Different types of homes around the world: igloo, tepee, wigwam, log house, chickee, log cabin, stilt house, mud hut, castle, houseboat, lighthouse, mobile home, cave, penthouse.

### Extended Learning
- Reading great literature books and watching animal documentaries are great ways of promoting self-learning.
- Research the various types of habitats there are: freshwater, marine, terrestrial, etc.
- Research houses in Jamaica and around the world.

## Focus Question 3: How are living and non-living things affected by changes in the environment?

### Prior Learning
Check that students can:
- Readily identify living things around them and in literature
- Be familiar with some of the habitats of living things that they have explored or experienced

### Attainment Targets and Objectives

**Science**
- Explore the environment in order to relate everyday experiences to simple scientific concepts and processes.

Objectives:
- Identify ways in which the physical environment has changed over time in different places (locally, nationally and internationally)
- Identify the effects that changes in time and seasons have on living and non-living things
- Make inferences/draw conclusions about the effects of various environmental changes on living and non-living things
- Discuss the importance of proper disposal of waste

**Religious Education**
- Recognize that religion contributes to shaping our identity and guiding our understanding of the meaning and purpose of life.

Objective:
- Discuss biblical stories of how living and non-living things were affected by changes in the environment

**Mathematics**
- Collect, organize, interpret and represent data and make inferences by applying knowledge of statistics and probability.
- Demonstrate an awareness of time in relation to occasions or events.

Objectives:
- Use tally marks to record data (where appropriate)
- Use bar/line/picture graphs to present data
- Read a calendar and match seasonal changes to time of year

**Social Studies**
- Develop an understanding of the interdependent relationship between human beings and their environment.
- Demonstrate an understanding of the need to care for the environment.

Objectives:
- Illustrate, using drawings and/or models, ways in which humans benefit from living and non-living things in their environment
- Role-play scenarios that show how the environment is impacted positively and negatively by humans
- Explore to find evidence to support the need to care for living and non-living things in the environment

**Civics**
- Cultivate and maintain a sense of social responsibility.

Objectives:
- Explore the meaning of social responsibility
- Interpret evidence gathered from observing the environment based on an agreed meaning of social responsibility
- Reflect on personal behaviours that support caring for the environment as a member of a group or community
- Demonstrate, with increasing ease/fluency, responsible ways of using non-living materials in their community/parish
- Practice, with increasing consistency, behaviours associated with social responsibility

**Music**
- Create musical compositions exploring varied elements of music.

Objective:
- Use elements of music to perform simple musical stories that are based on the effects of environmental changes

**Physical Education**
- Develop an understanding about how our bodies move, why we move our bodies in particular ways, and what happens to our bodies when we move.

Objective:
- Alter movement of the body in response to various simulations of environmental stimuli (e.g. fast-moving cricket ball, musical sounds, a hurricane, an earthquake, rain falling, howling wind, sweltering heat of the sun, etc.)

**Language Arts**
- Communicate with confidence and competence for different purposes and audiences, using SJE and JC appropriately and creatively.
- Use deduction and inference to interpret information and ideas to predict outcomes.
- Research activities on issues and interests.
- Write to narrate, to persuade and for a range of transactional purposes.
- Develop approaches to the writing process; write sentences that are grammatically accurate and correctly punctuated; develop phonemic awareness.

Objectives:
- Talk freely about observations
- Generate own sentences using plurals
- Interpret key ideas and words in text
- Collect information from a variety of sources about changes that take place in the environment
- Write to share ideas and feelings and their observations
- Read and respond to characters or situations in a class reader or story

**Visual Arts**
- Plan and design.

Objectives:
- Plan, design and create 3D models from items that show environmental changes
- Use art as a medium for personal creative expression, communication and emotional release

**ICT**
- Use appropriate digital tools and resources to plan and conduct research, aid critical thinking, manage projects, solve problems and make informed decisions.

Objectives:
- Use technology to communicate ideas and information and work collaboratively
- With the teacher's assistance, conduct online research to aid in class presentations
- Choose from various software applications and create poems with images, design commercials, etc. related to content

**Resource & Technology**
- Use simple materials, tools and equipment to develop skills in cutting, assembling and decorating.

Objective:
- Cut, paste and assemble items that show environmental changes

**Drama**
- Express a range of feelings and emotions through the enactment of dramatic scenarios.
- Appreciate and critique dramatic works.

Objectives:
- Create and perform an aesthetic piece to depict the effect of environmental changes on lives
- Critique and respond to group presentations

### Suggested Teaching and Learning Activities
Students will:
- Individually or in groups, discuss the changes they have observed in their surroundings (home and school) that have affected them negatively or positively. Using SJE, give reasons in a paragraph as to whether they think these changes were caused by living and/or non-living things.
- Do an online/offline research on specific environmental changes, the causes of these changes and their effects. Share information with the class in ways of their choice.
- In groups, construct tables showing time/seasonal changes that affect living/non-living things in the environment (e.g. June to November — hurricane/storm). Share information in a variety of ways.
- Select a site that has experienced some physical change (e.g. an area damaged by fire or flooding) and use an image-capturing device to record changes observed. In groups, write or use ICT tools to make reports on observations. Share reports and discuss how the change might have affected living/non-living things at the site. Label and display pictures and reports.
- Collect objects from the site that show the effect of changes in the environment (e.g. a burnt stone, wood, toy). In groups, use the objects to construct a 3D assemblage depicting "Environmental Changes". Display assemblages.
- In pairs or small groups, assign characters and read a story describing animals and how they could be affected by changes/factors in their environment. The animals should interact with each other (e.g. a cat chasing a mouse, a mouse helping a lion escape from a net, a mongoose attacking a snake — Rikki-Tikki-Tavi, the hare and the tortoise). Use a story to create a musical composition, using instruments to represent animal characters, concentrating on pitch and rhythm. Make a presentation and do peer evaluation. Read and enact the Bible story of the famine in Canaan (Gen. 41:46–42:38), highlighting how people and animals were affected.
- In groups, write a fictional/non-fictional piece/poem about a named animal that survived an environmental change and the animal's response to the change. Include calendar information (date, season, etc.).
- In groups, perform a variety of movements in response to stimuli from changes, e.g. a storm or earthquake in the environment (bend, roll, hop, jump, etc.).
- In groups, choose an animal/plant and write an expository piece explaining why it is important to protect it. Post views to the class page, design a logo, and create posters and fliers. Create and hold an advertising campaign for the protection of the specific plant/animal, performing dressed as the animal or plant and using musical instruments to enhance the performance.
- Select an area in the school yard or community and tally the numbers and types of garbage seen (e.g. juice boxes, plastic containers or bags, old cans). Report this information using a bar/line/pictograph. Create posters to convey the negative implications of disposing of garbage improperly, concentrating on graphics, lines, shapes and colours.
- In groups, discuss and record what effect littering and other improper disposal practices have on living and non-living things. Compose jingles/poems about proper garbage disposal. Perform and evaluate the jingles/poems.

### Key Skills
Observe changes · Infer reason for changes · Write paragraph · Research · Communicate information · Construct tables · Observe site · Record observation · Write report · Label pictures · Collect objects · Construct and display assemblage · Compose musical story · Perform composition · Evaluate composition · Read and discuss Bible story · Dramatize Bible story · Write fictional/non-fictional pieces · Communicate ideas · Perform movements · Write expository piece · Design logo · Create posters and fliers · Develop advertisement · Record types of garbage · Compose jingles and poems · Perform and evaluate jingles and poems

- Tables present information accurately; information reported accurately
- Pictures labelled correctly; inferences about the impact of change logically made
- Assemblage constructed creatively and represents a logical presentation of facts
- Musical story arranged creatively; composition performed satisfactorily
- Dramatic presentation satisfactorily done and represents an accurate understanding of the impact of environmental changes
- Poem/story adequately details the impact of change on the chosen animal
- Movements performed satisfactorily and represent chosen stimuli
- Important information stated clearly; logo designed creatively; posters/fliers message presented clearly; performance adequately treats the intended message
- Tally done accurately; poster uses graphics to convey ideas effectively; jingles/poems composed satisfactorily

### Learning Outcomes
Students will be able to:
- Identify changes in the environment
- Infer that environmental changes affect living things
- Understand the importance of proper waste disposal
- Use tally marks to record data
- Construct a graph using data collected
- Use lines, shapes, colours and graphics for best effect when creating a poster on how and why we need to dispose of litter correctly
- Select the appropriate instrument, concentrating on pitch and rhythm, to illustrate the character of the animals of their choice in a musical story
- Understand links among the months of the year and seasonal changes
- Create and perform a musical story
- Understand how living/non-living things were affected by environmental changes in Bible times
- Create 3D models using materials collected
- Use art to express ideas
- Use digital tools to do research, communicate and design posters
- Create dramatic presentations and critique peers' presentations

### Points to Note
- Safety of pupils should be ensured when visiting sites.
- Other appropriate Bible stories can be used: Noah and the Ark, crossing of the Red Sea, Jesus walking on the sea, Jonah, and stories such as "The Wind and the Sun".

### Extended Learning
- Do a research/project on the occurrence of hurricanes in Jamaica over a ten-year period and their impact on living and non-living things in the environment.

## Focus Question 4: In what ways are living things dependent on non-living things?

### Prior Learning
Check that students can:
- Be aware of the negative and/or positive impact living and non-living things can have on the environment

### Attainment Targets and Objectives

**Science**
- Explore selected life processes in humans, the interdependence between living things in the environment, and how lifestyles determine health and well-being in humans.

Objectives:
- Draw conclusions about the requirements for the survival of plants and animals
- Explain how the physical environment affects the ways in which people meet their basic needs
- Discuss how and why non-living things are important to other living things

**Religious Education**
- Demonstrate an awareness of some of the ways in which religion influences changes in the lifestyles of persons within society.

Objectives:
- Examine biblical stories and parables that show us how to live in ways acceptable by society
- Compare the story of The Rich Man and Lazarus to identify changes that will lead to a better society
- Reflect on scriptures and popular sayings that they have memorized to promote healthy lifestyles

**Mathematics**
- Collect, organize, interpret and represent data and make inferences by applying knowledge of statistics and probability.

Objectives:
- Collect and record attribute data
- Use tally marks to record data where appropriate

**Social Studies**
- Develop an understanding of the interdependent relationship between man and his environment.

Objectives:
- Explore the food, shelter and clothing of various cultures in relation to the physical environment
- Understand the term dependence in relation to living and non-living things
- Make inferences/draw conclusions about the effects of various environmental changes on living things

**Civics**
- Demonstrate awareness of the ways in which the Jamaican identity is shown through interactions with, and responses to, the environment.

Objectives:
- Discuss the concept of Jamaica, the Land of Wood and Water, and its impact on how we live and conduct business
- Identify the four basic elements and their impact on how we live as Jamaicans
- Cite evidence of the importance of the way Jamaicans view given situations

**Music**
- Listen and appraise music to develop an understanding of musical elements.
- Create musical compositions exploring varied elements of music.
- Perform varied repertoire of music for an audience.

Objectives:
- Analyse selected songs to conclude how our songs depict the way we view life
- Discuss a current issue and compose lyrics to show their feelings on the matter
- Perform a medley of folk songs that show how dependent Jamaicans are on non-living things

**Physical Education**
- Develop the understanding and skills that support healthy, safe and active individuals.

Objectives:
- Explain how non-living things can be used to enhance physical activities
- Develop/construct improvised equipment from non-living things

**Language Arts**
- Communicate with confidence and competence for different purposes and audiences, using SJE and JC appropriately and creatively.
- Research activities on issues and interests.
- Develop approaches to the writing process.

Objectives:
- Generate own sentences using plurals
- Compose their own song
- Interpret key ideas and words in text

**Visual Arts**
- Create and innovate; plan and design; appreciate and respond.

Objective:
- Use a variety of artistic forms to represent relationships between living and non-living things

**ICT**
- Use appropriate digital tools and resources to plan and conduct research, aid critical thinking, manage projects, solve problems and make informed decisions.

Objectives:
- Use selected ICT tools (e.g. image-capturing devices, word processor, presentation software) to communicate information
- Identify and play a medley of Jamaican games

**Drama**
- Explore and create, exercising critical thinking skills throughout the dramatic process.

**Resource & Technology**
- Use simple materials, tools and equipment to develop skills in cutting, assembling and decorating.

Objective:
- Use a variety of materials to create two- and three-dimensional images representing relationships

### Suggested Teaching and Learning Activities
Pupils will:
- Design a cartoon, collaborating in a group setting, in which each animal explains why or how it depends on non-living and other living things to survive.
- Collaborate in groups and create performance pieces to express their ideas on how their lives would be affected if non-living things were removed from the environment.
- Work cooperatively to record the ways in which non-living things are important to living things, and report on these.
- Discuss information using Standard Jamaican English about how the people in biblical days were dependent on the miracle from God to feed them. Write sentences in Standard Jamaican English to explain how God has made living things to be dependent on other living things and on non-living things.
- Go on a field trip or nature walk at home/school and use an image-capturing device to capture examples of how living things depend on non-living things. Collect, organize and classify the information.

### Key Skills
Design cartoon · Collaborate in group setting · Create performance pieces · Work cooperatively · Share information · Discuss information · Use Standard Jamaican English · Manipulate image-capturing device · Organise information · Classify things

### Assessment
- Images are clear and accurately show the relationship
- Cartoon accurately depicts the dependent relationship between a named living thing and a non-living thing
- Aesthetic piece conveys correct information that accurately depicts their ideas and makes an impact on the audience
- Reports produced contain valid stated relationships between living and non-living things
- SJE appropriately used to discuss the relationship between living and non-living things in the context of the biblical account
- Relationship between named living and non-living things clearly demonstrated in images captured; report findings accurately; use tally appropriately

### Learning Outcomes
Students will be able to:
- Explain how non-living things are important to living things
- Understand the term dependence
- Write creatively to inform an audience about how their lives would be affected if/when non-living things are removed from the environment
- Explain the account of the miracle of the feeding of the five thousand and relate it to their everyday living

### Points to Note
- Ensure students operate within a safe environment when they are exploring.
- The students should understand that they should appreciate who they are and what they have, and be content until change comes.

### Extended Learning
- Do extensive research on Hurricane Gilbert / Robin's Bay / Flooding of New Market / Portland Cottage / Port Royal / Cave Valley / Rio Grande Valley / Bog Walk Gorge, noting the effects and lessons learnt.
