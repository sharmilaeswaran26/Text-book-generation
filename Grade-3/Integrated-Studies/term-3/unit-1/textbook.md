**Grade 3 · Integrated Studies · Term 3**

## A Letter to the Young Reader

Take a moment to look around you, right where you are sitting. What do you see? Perhaps you can see a tree swaying outside your window, a small lizard resting on a wall, or a bird hopping across the grass. Perhaps you can also see a stone on the ground, a chair, a pencil, or a glass of water. Some of these things are alive. Others are not. But how can you tell the difference?

That is the wonderful puzzle we are going to solve together in this chapter. The world all around you, which we call your **environment**, is full of two great families of things. One family is the living things, like plants, animals, and people. The other family is the non-living things, like rocks, water, air, and sunlight. Both families are important, and as you will soon discover, they need each other very much.

In this chapter, you will learn how to tell a living thing from a non-living thing just by watching closely. You will find out why a fish belongs in the river and a bird belongs in a tree. You will discover how the world changes around us, with sounds and movement and great storms, and how those changes touch every living and non-living thing. And, at the very end, you will understand a beautiful secret: that no living thing could survive for even a single day without the help of the non-living world. So let us open our eyes wide and begin to explore.

## What You Will Learn

In this chapter you will learn to tell living things apart from non-living things by looking for the signs of life. You will learn what a **habitat** is and why every living thing needs its own special home that gives it food, water, shelter, and safety. You will explore the changes that happen in the environment around us, from gentle sounds and moving objects to great natural events like hurricanes and earthquakes, and you will see how these changes affect both living and non-living things. Finally, you will discover the many ways that living things depend on non-living things such as sunlight, air, water, and soil in order to stay alive.

## The Big Questions

As you read, keep these four big questions in your mind. By the end of the chapter, you will be able to answer each one in your own words.

1. How can I tell if something in my environment is living?
2. Why do living things need specific habitats?
3. How are living and non-living things affected by changes in the environment?
4. In what ways are living things dependent on non-living things?

You will answer the first question by learning the signs of life that all living things share. You will answer the second by learning how the right home keeps an animal or plant safe and well fed. You will answer the third by watching how the environment changes, with sounds, movement, weather, and seasons, and how those changes touch every kind of thing. And you will answer the last by discovering how living things lean on the non-living world for everything they need.

## Words to Know

Here are some important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about learning them all at once. Each one is carefully explained in the lessons, so you will understand them fully as you read.

- **Living thing** – something that is alive, like a plant, an animal, or a person.
- **Non-living thing** – something that is not alive, like a stone, water, or air.
- **Environment** – everything that is around us, both living and non-living.
- **Characteristics of living things** – the special signs that show something is alive. They are movement, feeding, growth, reproduction, breathing, and sensitivity.
- **Movement** – the way living things can move themselves from place to place or move parts of their bodies.
- **Feeding** – the way living things take in food to get energy and to grow.
- **Growth** – the way living things get bigger and change as they get older.
- **Reproduction** – the way living things make young ones, or new plants and animals, like themselves.
- **Breathing** – the way living things take in the air they need to stay alive.
- **Sensitivity** – the way living things notice and respond to what happens around them.
- **Habitat** – the special home where a plant or animal lives and gets what it needs.
- **Shelter** – a safe place that protects a living thing from harm and bad weather.
- **Sound** – something we hear when things in our environment move or shake the air.
- **Force** – a push or a pull that can make something move, stop, or change.
- **Season** – a time of year, such as the rainy season or the dry season.
- **Depend** – to need something else in order to live or to work properly.

# Part One: How Can I Tell If Something Is Living?

Look closely at the world around you, and you will find it filled with all kinds of things. Some of them are bursting with life. Others have never been alive at all. In this first part of the chapter, you will become a careful observer, like a real scientist, and learn the clever signs that tell you whether something is a **living thing** or a **non-living thing**.

## Two Great Families of Things

Everything in your **environment** can be sorted into one of two groups. The first group is the **living things**. A mango tree, a goat, a john crow flying high in the sky, a tiny ant, and you yourself are all living things, because you are all alive. The second group is the **non-living things**. A stone in the river, a glass of water, the air around you, a desk, and a metal gate are all non-living things, because they were never alive.

At first, it might seem easy to tell them apart. Of course a dog is living and a rock is not! But sometimes it is trickier than you think. Is a dead leaf living or non-living? Is flowing water alive because it moves? To answer questions like these, scientists do not just guess. They look for special signs, and those signs are the same for every living thing in the whole world.

> **Think About It:** Look around the room you are in right now. Can you name three living things and three non-living things that you can see?

## The Signs of Life

All living things share certain signs, and we call these the **characteristics of living things**. If something shows these signs, it is alive. If it does not show them, it is not alive. There are six main signs to look for, and once you know them, you will be able to tell any living thing from a non-living thing.

The first sign is **movement**. Living things can move by themselves. A cat can run, a fish can swim, and a bird can fly. Even plants move, though they do it so slowly that we cannot see it happening. A sunflower turns to face the sun, and a vine creeps up a fence. A stone, on the other hand, cannot move itself at all. If you see a stone roll down a hill, it is only because the wind or the rain or your own hand pushed it.

The second sign is **feeding**. Living things need food to give them energy and to help them grow. Animals eat plants or other animals. A cow eats grass, and a lizard eats insects. Plants are clever, for they make their own food using sunlight. But a chair or a cup never needs to eat, because it is not alive.

The third sign is **growth**. Living things grow and change as time passes. A tiny seed grows into a tall tree. A puppy grows into a big dog. You yourself were once a small baby, and now you are much bigger. Non-living things do not grow in this way. A stone stays the same size, year after year.

The fourth sign is **reproduction**. Living things can make young ones that are like themselves. A hen lays eggs, and from the eggs come baby chicks. A mango tree makes seeds, and from the seeds grow new mango trees. This is how living things keep their families going. A bicycle can never make another little bicycle, because it is not alive.

The fifth sign is **breathing**, which scientists also call respiration. Living things take in air to stay alive. You breathe air in and out with your lungs. A fish takes in air from the water using its gills. Even plants take in air. A bottle or a brick does not breathe, because it does not need air to live.

The sixth sign is **sensitivity**. Living things can notice and respond to what is happening around them. If you touch a peenie wallie, it may light up or fly away. If you make a loud noise near a dog, it will turn its head to listen. A leaf of the shame-o-lady plant folds up when you touch it. Non-living things cannot feel or respond in this way.

> **Did You Know?** A handy way to remember the signs of life is to look for these things: a living thing **moves**, **feeds**, **grows**, makes **young**, **breathes**, and **responds** to the world around it. If something does all of these things, you can be sure it is alive.

## Putting the Signs Together

Now that you know the six signs of life, you can be a real living-things detective. Whenever you are not sure whether something is living, you simply check it against the signs. Does it move on its own? Does it feed? Does it grow? Can it make young ones? Does it breathe? Does it respond to the world? If the answer is yes, then it is a living thing.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine you find a lizard sunning itself on a wall. Is it living or non-living? Let us check the signs. It moves, because it darts away when you come close. It feeds, because it catches and eats insects. It grew from a baby lizard into a bigger one. It can lay eggs to make baby lizards. It breathes air. And it responds to you by running away. Every single sign says yes. So the lizard is most certainly a living thing.

Now imagine the wall the lizard was resting on. Does the wall move by itself? No. Does it feed, grow, make young ones, or breathe? No, no, no, and no. Does it respond when you touch it? No. The wall shows none of the signs of life, so we know that the wall is a non-living thing.

> **Think About It:** A car can move, and it needs petrol just as you need food. Yet a car is not a living thing. Using the six signs of life, can you think of which signs a car is missing? (Here is a clue: can a car grow, or make a baby car, or breathe?)

## Watching a Seed Come to Life

One of the best ways to see the signs of life with your very own eyes is to watch a seed grow. When you plant a seed and care for it, you can watch it move, feed, grow, and come alive over many days. This is something you can do yourself, and it is wonderful to see.

If you take a few peas or corn seeds and place them on damp paper inside a clear container, and then keep the paper moist, something amazing begins to happen after a few days. The seed swells up and splits open. A tiny white root pushes its way down, and a small green shoot reaches up toward the light. Day by day, the little plant grows taller. This is **growth** happening right before your eyes.

As you watch your seed each day, you can measure how tall the shoot has grown and write it down. You might draw a picture of the plant on the first day, then on the fifth day, then on the tenth day, and see how much it has changed. This careful watching and writing down is exactly what scientists do, and it helps us understand how living things grow and change over time.

> **Did You Know?** People who study plants are called **botanists**, and people who study animals are called **zoologists**. When you watch your seed grow and write down what you see, you are doing real science, just like they do.

# Part Two: Why Living Things Need Specific Habitats

You have learned how to tell a living thing from a non-living thing. Now here is something just as important: every living thing has a special place where it belongs. A fish belongs in the water, and a bird belongs in the trees. In this part of the chapter, you will learn what that special place is called and why every plant and animal needs the right one.

## What Is a Habitat?

The special place where a plant or an animal lives is called its **habitat**. A habitat is much more than just a spot on the ground. It is a home that gives a living thing everything it needs to stay alive and well.

Think about what you need in your own home. You need food to eat, water to drink, a roof to keep you safe from the rain and sun, and a place where you feel protected. A habitat does the very same thing for a plant or an animal. A good habitat gives a living thing four important gifts: **food** to eat, **water** to drink, **shelter** to keep it safe from bad weather, and **safety** from danger.

Different living things need different habitats, because they need different things. A fish needs a habitat full of water, for a fish cannot live out of the water. A bird needs trees where it can build its nest and rest. A worm needs cool, damp soil where it can dig and feed. Each living thing has its own perfect home, and that home gives it exactly what it needs.

> **Think About It:** Think about an animal you know well, such as a dog, a goat, or a fish. Where does it live? What does its home give it that helps it to stay alive and happy?

## Homes of All Kinds

If you go for a walk around your school yard or your community, you will discover habitats all around you. Living things make their homes in the most surprising places.

Some animals live in the trees. Birds build their nests high in the branches, where their eggs are safe. Some animals live in the soil. Worms and ants dig their homes underground, in the cool, dark earth. Some animals live in the water. Fish, crabs, and tadpoles make their homes in rivers, ponds, and the sea. And some animals live in and around our houses. A lizard might rest on a wall, and a spider might spin its web in a quiet corner.

Plants have their habitats too. A water lily floats on a pond, with its roots reaching down into the mud. A cactus lives happily in dry, sandy places. A great cotton tree spreads its roots deep into the rich soil. Each plant grows best in the place that gives it the right amount of water, sunlight, and soil.

People build homes too, in all shapes and sizes, to suit the places where they live. In very cold lands like Alaska, some people once built houses called igloos out of blocks of ice. In hot, sunny lands, people build homes with thick walls to stay cool. Right here in Jamaica, we build houses of wood and concrete to keep us safe from the sun and the heavy rains. Just like animals and plants, people need homes that suit the place where they live.

> **Did You Know?** A bird does not build its nest just anywhere. It chooses a safe branch, gathers twigs and grass, and weaves them together into a cosy home. Each kind of bird builds its own special kind of nest, perfectly suited to its needs.

## Why the Right Habitat Matters

A living thing needs the right habitat, and only the right one will do. If a living thing is taken away from its proper home and put somewhere it does not belong, it can become very unhappy, and it may not be able to survive at all.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine a fish swimming happily in a river. The river is its habitat. It gives the fish water to breathe through its gills, tiny creatures to eat, and cool places to hide from larger animals. Now imagine that someone takes the fish out of the river and places it on the dry grass. What would happen? The fish could not breathe out of the water. It would flap and struggle, and it could not live for long. This shows us how very important the right habitat is. The fish was not made to live on dry land; it was made for the water.

The same is true for every living thing. A bird taken from the trees and locked in a tiny box would be sad and unwell, because it could not fly and build its nest. A plant from a wet, shady place would dry out and die if it were planted in a hot, sandy spot. This is why we must take great care of the natural homes of living things. When we keep their habitats safe and clean, we help the plants and animals to live well.

This is also why we should never harm or carelessly destroy the places where animals and plants live. When people cut down too many trees, or throw rubbish into rivers, or pollute the air, they damage the habitats that living things depend on. By keeping our environment clean and protecting natural places, we help every living thing to keep its home.

> **Think About It:** Why do you think a polar animal that lives in the snow would find it very hard to live in hot, sunny Jamaica? What would it be missing from its proper habitat?

# Part Three: How Changes in the Environment Affect Living and Non-Living Things

The world around you is always changing. Some changes are small and gentle, like a soft breeze or a quiet sound. Others are large and powerful, like a roaring hurricane or a shaking earthquake. In this part of the chapter, you will learn about the changes that happen in our environment, and how those changes touch every living and non-living thing.

## The Environment Is Always Changing

Nothing in your environment stays exactly the same forever. Day turns into night, and night turns back into day. The hot, dry season gives way to the rainy season. A calm afternoon can turn into a stormy evening. These changes happen all the time, and they affect both the living things and the non-living things around us.

Some changes come from the living world, and some come from the non-living world. A change can be small, like a leaf falling from a tree, or it can be large, like a flood that covers a whole field. As you read on, you will learn about some of the changes that happen in our environment, including the sounds we hear, the things that move, the pushes and pulls of forces, and the great events of nature.

## Sounds All Around Us

Close your eyes for a moment and listen. What do you hear? Perhaps you hear birds singing, a dog barking, the wind rustling the leaves, or a car passing by. All of these are **sounds**, and our environment is full of them.

A sound is made when something moves or shakes the air around it. When a bird sings, it shakes the air with its throat. When the wind blows through the trees, the leaves rustle and make a soft, whispering sound. When rain falls on a zinc roof, it makes a loud pattering sound. Some sounds are made by living things, like the chirping of a cricket or the croaking of a frog at night. Other sounds are made by non-living things, like thunder rumbling in the sky or water rushing over rocks.

We can even make our own musical sounds to copy the sounds of the environment. By clapping our hands, tapping a drum, or shaking a shaker, we can pretend to be falling rain, a buzzing bee, or a gentle stream. Listening carefully to sounds helps us notice the many changes happening around us all the time.

> **Did You Know?** At night in Jamaica, you can hear a chorus of sounds from living things, like the chirping of crickets and the whistling of the whistling frog. Each little creature is making a sound to tell others where it is.

## Things That Move

Look around your environment, and you will see that many things are moving. A **force** is a push or a pull that makes something move, or stop, or change its direction. Forces are at work all around us, every single day.

When you kick a football, your foot gives the ball a push, and the ball flies through the air. That push is a force. When a fast-moving cricket ball comes flying towards a batsman, a force sends it speeding along, and another force from the bat sends it flying back. When you pull a cart or push a swing, you are using a force to make it move.

Forces are not only made by people. The wind is a force that can push a kite high into the sky or bend the branches of a tree. Flowing water is a force that can carry a leaf downstream. Even when you drop a stone and it falls to the ground, a force is pulling it down. Watching things move, and thinking about the pushes and pulls that move them, helps us understand the changes in our environment.

> **Think About It:** Think of a game you like to play. Where do you use a push or a pull in that game? How does the force make something move?

## The Great Events of Nature

Some of the biggest changes in our environment come from the powerful events of nature. These events can change the land, the water, and the air, and they affect both living things and non-living things in big ways.

A **hurricane** is a giant storm with howling winds and heavy rain. When a hurricane comes, the strong wind can blow down trees, the heavy rain can cause flooding, and the rough sea can wash away the shore. Hurricanes affect living things, because animals lose their homes and plants are uprooted. They affect non-living things too, because rivers swell, roads are damaged, and the land itself is changed.

An **earthquake** is a sudden shaking of the ground. Deep below us, the earth can shift and tremble, making the ground move. An earthquake can crack the land, shake down buildings, and change the shape of the coast. Like a hurricane, an earthquake affects both the living and the non-living world.

Even gentler events bring change. When the rain falls during the rainy season, the dry land soaks up the water, the rivers grow full, and the plants turn green and grow. When the hot, dry season comes, the rivers may grow low, the grass may turn brown, and the soil may become dry and cracked. These changes follow the **seasons**, which are the different times of the year. We can look at a calendar to see when each season comes. In Jamaica, for example, the months from June to November are the time when hurricanes are most likely to come.

When the environment changes, living things must respond. After a hurricane, birds find new places to nest, and farmers plant new crops. During a dry spell, animals search harder for water. This shows us that living things and non-living things are joined together, and that a change to one can bring a change to the other.

> **Did You Know?** In 1988, a very powerful hurricane called Hurricane Gilbert struck Jamaica. It blew down many trees and houses and changed parts of the land. People still remember it today, and it reminds us how strong the events of nature can be.

## Caring for Our Environment

Because the environment can change so easily, it is our job to take care of it. Sadly, people sometimes cause harmful changes without thinking. When people throw rubbish on the ground or into rivers, they make the environment dirty. This is called littering, and it harms both living and non-living things. Plastic bottles and old cans left lying about can hurt animals, block streams, and make a place ugly and unhealthy.

The good news is that we can all help to protect our environment. We can put our rubbish in bins instead of throwing it on the ground. We can pick up litter we find in the school yard. We can plant trees and care for plants. When each of us does our small part, we help to keep our environment clean and healthy for every living and non-living thing. This is what we call being responsible, and it is one of the most caring things we can do.

> **Think About It:** Look around your school yard or your home. Can you think of one thing you could do this week to help keep your environment clean and healthy?

# Part Four: How Living Things Depend on Non-Living Things

You have learned that the world is full of living things and non-living things. Now we come to the most wonderful secret of all. The living and the non-living are not really separate at all. They are joined together in a beautiful way, because living things need non-living things in order to stay alive. In this last part of the chapter, you will learn what it means to **depend** on something, and you will discover how every living thing leans on the non-living world.

## What Does It Mean to Depend on Something?

When one thing needs another thing in order to live or to work, we say that it **depends** on it. You depend on your family to care for you and feed you. A plant depends on the rain to give it water. To depend on something means you cannot do without it.

Living things depend on many non-living things every single day. The non-living world gives living things the sunlight, the air, the water, and the soil that they need to stay alive. Without these non-living gifts, no plant or animal could survive. Let us look at each one and see how important it is.

## The Gifts of the Non-Living World

The first great gift is **sunlight**. The sun is a non-living thing, far away in the sky, yet every living thing depends on it. Plants use the light of the sun to make their food. Without sunlight, plants could not grow, and if plants could not grow, the animals that eat plants would have nothing to eat. The sun also gives us warmth and light, so that we can see and stay warm. In a way, the sun feeds the whole living world.

The second great gift is **air**. The air is all around us, even though we cannot see it. Air is a non-living thing, but every living thing depends on it. You breathe in air with your lungs. A fish takes air from the water with its gills. Plants take in air too. Without air, no living thing could breathe, and no living thing could live.

The third great gift is **water**. Water is a non-living thing, and it is one of the most precious gifts of all. Every living thing needs water to stay alive. You need to drink water every day. Animals search for water in rivers and ponds. Plants drink water through their roots. Jamaica is sometimes called the "Land of Wood and Water," because it has so many rivers, springs, and streams. Without water, no plant, animal, or person could survive.

The fourth great gift is **soil**. The soil is the earth beneath our feet, and it too is non-living. Yet plants depend on the soil completely. A plant pushes its roots into the soil to hold itself up, and it drinks water and takes goodness from the soil to help it grow. And since so many animals and people depend on plants for food, we can see that the soil helps to feed the whole living world.

> **Did You Know?** Almost all the food you eat begins with a plant that grew in the soil, drank the water, breathed the air, and soaked up the sunlight. Your breakfast, your lunch, and your dinner all depend on the non-living world.

## Living Things Depend on Each Other Too

Living things do not only depend on non-living things. They also depend on one another. This makes the whole web of life fit together beautifully.

**Let's look at an example.** Think about a simple meal of rice and stewed chicken with a slice of pumpkin. The rice came from a rice plant, which grew in the soil, drank the water, breathed the air, and used the sunlight. The chicken ate grains and corn, which are also plants that grew in the soil with water, air, and sunshine. The pumpkin grew on a vine in the warm Jamaican earth. So when you eat your dinner, you are depending on living things like plants and animals, and they in turn depended on non-living things like soil, water, air, and sunlight. Everything is joined together.

This is why we say that living things and non-living things share the world together. The plants need the soil, the water, the air, and the sun. The animals need the plants and the water and the air. And people need the plants, the animals, and all the gifts of the non-living world. Each one depends on the others, like links in a chain.

> **Think About It:** Imagine if there were suddenly no water in the world at all. What would happen to the plants, the animals, and the people? Why is water such an important non-living thing?

## Being Thankful and Taking Care

When we understand how much living things depend on non-living things, we begin to feel thankful for the world around us. The sunlight, the air, the water, and the soil are gifts that keep us alive every day, and many people believe these gifts were created for us to use wisely and to share.

Because we depend so much on the non-living world, it is our job to take care of it. We should not waste water, for it is precious. We should not dirty the air or the rivers, for living things need them to be clean. We should care for the soil, so that plants can keep growing. When we look after the non-living world, we are really looking after every living thing, including ourselves. And that is one of the most important lessons of all.

> **Did You Know?** Jamaica is called the "Land of Wood and Water" because of its many trees and rivers. These are gifts of nature that we depend on, and it is up to all of us to keep them safe for the future.

## Key Takeaways

- Everything in the environment is either a living thing or a non-living thing.

- A habitat is the special home of a living thing, and it gives food, water, shelter, and safety.
- Different living things need different habitats; a fish needs water, a bird needs trees, and a worm needs soil.
