Focus Question 1: What is my physical environment?

What Is the Physical Environment?

Your physical environment is everything around you that you can see, hear, smell, and touch. It is the world you live in, right where you are. When you stand in your schoolyard, your physical environment is all the things you can sense in that place: the ground beneath your feet, the air on your skin, the trees and grass, the buildings, and the people moving about.

The physical environment is not the same everywhere. The environment of a child living high in the cool hills is different from the environment of a child living near a warm, sandy beach. A busy town has a different environment from a quiet country district. Yet every physical environment, no matter where it is, is made up of two big kinds of things: natural things and man-made things.

Think About It

Look around the place where you are sitting right now. Can you name three things you can see, one thing you can hear, and one thing you can touch in your physical environment?

Using Your Senses to Explore

How do you come to know your physical environment? You use your five senses: sight, hearing, smell, touch, and taste. Your senses are like five helpful messengers that bring news of the world to your brain all day long.

With your eyes, you see the green of the leaves, the blue of the sky, and the grey of the road. With your ears, you hear birds singing, a river rushing, a car passing, or the wind in the trees. With your nose, you smell flowers blooming, rain on the ground, or smoke in the air. With your skin, you feel the warmth of the sun, the cool of the breeze, and the rough bark of a tree. Your senses even help you to taste, such as the sweetness of a ripe mango picked from the tree.

Your senses do something very important. They help you to notice the condition of your environment, which means whether it is healthy or unhealthy, clean or dirty, safe or unsafe. If you smell something foul near a gully, your nose is warning you that the water may be polluted. If you hear birds singing and see green plants growing, your eyes and ears are telling you the place is healthy. This is why your senses are such a useful gift. They are always helping you to read the world around you.

Did You Know?

Your senses work together as a team. If you walk past a flower, your eyes might see it first, but then your nose smells its sweet scent, and your fingers feel its soft petals. Each sense adds a little more to what you know.

Natural and Man-Made Things

When you look closely at your physical environment, you will notice that some things were made by nature and some things were made by people. Learning to tell these two groups apart is one of the first steps in understanding the world around you.

The natural environment is made up of all the things that nature gives us, without any help from people. The land, the hills, the soil, and the rocks are natural. The rivers, the sea, and the rain are natural. The air and the sky are natural. And every living plant and animal, every tree, flower, bird, fish, and insect, is natural too. Nobody built these things in a factory. They were here long before us, and they make up the beautiful world of nature.

The man-made environment, sometimes called the built environment, is made up of all the things that people have made or built. Your house is man-made. So are roads, bridges, schools, shops, churches, cars, fences, and lamp posts. Everything that human hands have built or made belongs to the man-made environment.

Imagine you are walking from your home to school. Along the way you pass a tall cotton tree, a flowing river, and a green hillside. Those are all natural. You also pass a paved road, a wooden bridge, a little shop, and the school gate. Those are all man-made. So even on one short walk, your physical environment is full of both natural things and man-made things, mixed together all around you.

Natural Things (made by nature) Man-Made Things (made by people)
Trees, plants, and flowers Houses and buildings
Rivers, the sea, and rain Roads and bridges
Hills, soil, and rocks Schools and shops
Animals, birds, and fish Cars, fences, and lamp posts

Think About It

Look around your classroom or your yard. Can you point to two things that are natural and two things that are man-made?

Living and Non-Living Things

There is another helpful way to sort the things in your environment. We can ask whether a thing is living or non-living.

Living things are things that are alive. They grow bigger, they need food and water, and they breathe. Plants are living things, because they grow and need water and sunlight. Animals are living things, and so are people. A dog, a goat, a fish, a butterfly, a mango tree, and a blade of grass are all living things.

Non-living things are things that are not alive. They do not grow, they do not need food, and they do not breathe. A rock is a non-living thing. So is water, air, a chair, a stone wall, and a metal gate. Non-living things can still be very important and useful, but they are not alive in the way that a plant or an animal is.

Did You Know?

Living things and non-living things depend on each other. A living plant needs the non-living things around it, such as soil, water, air, and sunlight, in order to grow. So even though a rock is not alive, the non-living parts of the environment help to keep the living parts healthy.

Resources From the Environment

Your physical environment does more than surround you. It also gives you the things you need to live. We call these helpful things resources. A resource is anything from the environment that people use to meet their needs, such as food, water, shelter, and clothing.

Think of all the resources nature provides. Water from a river or spring is a resource we drink, cook with, and wash with. Soil is a resource that lets farmers grow food. Trees are a resource that gives us wood, shade, fruit, and clean air. Sand, stones, and clay are resources we use to build. Even sunlight is a resource, because it helps plants to grow and warms the earth. People are resources too, because the skills and work of people in your community help everyone to live well.

People use these resources to meet their basic needs, which are the things every person must have to live: food to eat, water to drink, clothes to wear, and shelter to keep them safe. The physical environment around you shapes how you meet these needs. A child who lives near the sea might eat plenty of fish, while a child who lives in the hills might grow yam and dasheen in the cool soil. In every place, people use the resources nearby to feed, clothe, and shelter themselves.

This is exactly why our resources are so precious. Because we depend on the environment for everything we need, we must use its resources wisely and never waste or spoil them.

Think About It

Name three resources from the environment that your family uses every day. What would happen if one of them ran out?

Let's Practice

Step outside and look carefully at your physical environment. Make two lists: one list of natural things you can see, and one list of man-made things. See how many you can find for each. Then use your five senses, one at a time, to explore the place: write down one thing you can see, one you can hear, one you can smell, and one you can touch, and decide whether the place is healthy or unhealthy.

Focus Question 2: Why and how should we care for our environment?

Why We Should Care for Our Environment

Caring for the environment is one of the most important jobs we all share. But why does it matter so much? There are several good reasons, and once you understand them, you will want to do your part.

The first reason is that a clean environment keeps us healthy. When the land, water, and air around us are clean, we stay well. But when they become dirty, people can fall sick. Dirty water can carry diseases. Dirty air can make it hard to breathe. Rubbish left lying about can attract flies, rats, and mosquitoes that spread illness. So keeping our environment clean keeps our bodies healthy.

The second reason is that a cared-for environment keeps us safe. When people cut down too many trees on a hillside, the soil can slide down in a landslide. When drains and gullies are blocked with rubbish, the water has nowhere to go, and floods can follow. By caring for the environment, we help to keep ourselves and our families safe from these dangers.

The third reason is that caring for the environment protects plants and animals. Plants and animals share this world with us, and they depend on a healthy environment just as we do. When we spoil a river, the fish that live in it suffer. When we cut down a forest, the birds and animals that lived there lose their homes.

The fourth reason is that the environment is a gift we must pass on. The clean rivers, green hills, and fresh air we enjoy today were here long before us. If we look after them well, they will still be here for the children who come after us.

Did You Know?

Long ago, many people believed that we are caretakers of the earth, and that it is our duty to look after the land, the water, the plants, and the animals. People in many faiths and families still teach this today. Caring for the environment is something good people all over the world believe in.

What Is Pollution?

To care for the environment, you must first understand the biggest danger that harms it. That danger is called pollution. Pollution means harmful or dirty things that spoil the land, the water, or the air. When the environment is polluted, it becomes unhealthy and unsafe for people, plants, and animals.

Land pollution happens when rubbish and waste are left on the ground instead of being thrown away properly. When people drop litter, such as plastic bottles, food wrappers, and old bags, on the street or in an empty lot, they are causing land pollution. Litter makes a place look ugly, and it can harm animals and attract pests.

Water pollution happens when dirty or harmful things get into our rivers, ponds, gullies, and the sea. When people throw rubbish into a river, or when waste flows into the water, the water becomes polluted. Polluted water can make people sick if they drink it, and it harms the fish and other creatures that live in it.

Air pollution happens when dirty smoke and harmful gases get into the air. Smoke from burning rubbish, from factories, and from vehicles can pollute the air. Polluted air is unpleasant to breathe and can make people cough and feel unwell.

Imagine a clear, pretty river where children once swam and fish darted about. Then people begin tossing their rubbish into it: plastic bottles, old tins, and bags. Soon the water turns dirty and smelly. The fish cannot live there anymore, and no one wants to swim. This sad change happened because of water pollution, and it shows just how quickly a beautiful place can be spoiled when we are careless.

Think About It

Have you ever seen a place near your home that was spoiled by litter or pollution? How did it make you feel? How do you think it could be cleaned up?

How We Can Care for Our Environment

Now for the happy part. There are many simple things that you, even as a young person, can do to care for your environment. None of them is difficult, and together they make a big difference.

The first thing you can do is keep your environment clean and not litter. Always put your rubbish in a bin, never on the ground. Pick up litter when you see it, and help to keep your home, your school, and your community tidy. Keeping drains and gullies clear of rubbish also helps to stop floods.

The second thing you can do is plant trees and care for plants. Trees are wonderful friends to the environment. They give us shade, fruit, and clean air, and their roots hold the soil in place to stop landslides. When you plant a tree or water a plant, you are helping the whole environment.

The third thing you can do is save water and electricity. This is called conservation, which means using our resources carefully so they are not wasted. Turn off the tap while you brush your teeth. Turn off lights and fans when you leave a room.

The fourth thing you can do is avoid causing pollution. Do not burn rubbish, do not throw waste into rivers or gullies, and do not drop litter. Every time you choose not to pollute, you keep the land, water, and air a little cleaner.

The fifth and very powerful thing you can do is to follow the three Rs: reduce, reuse, and recycle.

Think About It

Which of these ways of caring for the environment do you already do? Which new one could you start doing today?

The Three Rs: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle

The three Rs stand for three important words that all begin with the letter R: reduce, reuse, and recycle. Together they help us to make less rubbish and to take better care of our resources.

To reduce means to use less, so that we waste less. If you use only the paper you really need, or take only as much food as you will eat, you are reducing. The less we use and the less we throw away, the less rubbish there is to spoil the environment.

To reuse means to use something again instead of throwing it away. An empty plastic bottle does not have to become rubbish. You can wash it and use it again to carry water. You can cut it in half to make a flower pot, a funnel, or a bird feeder. An old glass jar can hold pencils. When you reuse things, you give them a new life and keep them out of the rubbish heap.

To recycle means to make something old into something new and useful again. Some materials, like certain plastics, paper, and tins, can be collected, taken to a special place, and made into brand-new things. Old paper can become new paper.

Think of a single plastic bottle. First, you could reduce by drinking from one bottle all day instead of buying many. Then, when you are finished, you could reuse it by cutting it in half to make a little flower pot for a seedling. And if it can no longer be used, it could be recycled to help make something new. One small bottle, treated with the three Rs, never has to become litter at all.

The R What It Means An Example
Reduce Use less so you waste less Use only the paper you really need
Reuse Use something again instead of throwing it away Turn a bottle into a flower pot
Recycle Make something old into something new Old paper made into new paper

Did You Know?

Many useful and beautiful things can be made from items that people once threw away. Old bottles, tins, and boxes can be turned into pots, toys, decorations, and craft pieces. When you reuse and recycle, you are turning rubbish into treasure.

Keeping Our Surroundings Safe

Caring for the environment is not only about keeping it clean. It is also about keeping it safe. A safe environment is one where people can live, play, and work without getting hurt.

You can help to make your environment safer in many ways. At school and at home, keep walkways clear so that no one trips and falls. Make sure that broken glass, sharp tins, and other dangerous rubbish are cleared away. When you play, be aware of the space around you and the safety of others. Keeping drains and gullies clear also keeps the community safe from flooding.

When everyone in a community works together to keep the environment clean and safe, wonderful things happen. The streets look beautiful, the air smells fresh, people stay healthy, and everyone feels proud of where they live. A well-cared-for community is a happy community.

Think About It

Think about your own schoolyard. Can you name one thing that could be made cleaner or safer there, and one way you and your friends could help to fix it?

Let's Practice

Find one empty plastic bottle or container at home. With an adult's help, think of a new way to reuse it instead of throwing it away. Could it become a flower pot, a pencil holder, or a bird feeder? Then make a list of three ways your family could care for the environment this week.

Focus Question 3: How can I persuade others to care for the environment?

What Does It Mean to Persuade?

To persuade someone means to use kind, convincing words and actions to help them change their mind or to encourage them to do something good. When you persuade, you are not forcing or fighting. Instead, you are sharing good reasons and showing a good example, so that other people choose, all on their own, to do the right thing.

Persuading others to care for the environment is very important, because one person alone cannot keep a whole community clean. But if you can encourage your family, your friends, your classmates, and your neighbours to care too, then everyone working together can make a big and lasting difference. The more people who care, the cleaner and safer the environment becomes for all.

When you want to persuade someone, the best words are kind, clear, and convincing. Tell people why caring for the environment matters. Remind them that it keeps everyone healthy and safe, and that it protects the plants and animals we love. Speak politely and with a smile, and most people will be glad to listen.

Did You Know?

Even grown-ups can be persuaded by children. When a child speaks up kindly about keeping the environment clean, parents, teachers, and neighbours often listen and feel proud. Your voice has more power than you might think.

Many Ways to Persuade

There are many wonderful ways to persuade people to care for the environment, and the best part is that they are creative and fun. Here are some of the most powerful ones.

You can make a poster. A bright, well-drawn poster with a clear message, such as "Keep our school clean" or "Do not litter," catches people's eyes and reminds them to do the right thing. Use bold lines, colours, and pictures to make your message strong.

You can write a song or a jingle. A catchy little song about caring for the environment is easy to remember, and people may find themselves humming it long after they hear it. Music has a special way of carrying a message into people's hearts.

You can write a poem. A poem can paint a picture of a clean, beautiful environment, or it can gently warn about the harm that pollution does. A well-written poem can move people to care.

You can give a speech or a talk. When you stand up and speak clearly to your class or your community about why we must care for the environment, you can persuade many people at once. Speak with feeling, look at your listeners, and share good reasons.

You can write a letter or an email. A kind, convincing letter to a family member, a neighbour, or even the newspaper can persuade people to care. In your letter, explain why the environment matters and ask the reader to help.

And, most powerfully of all, you can set a good example. When people see you putting your rubbish in the bin, planting a tree, or picking up litter, they are quietly persuaded to do the same. Your good actions speak even louder than your words.

Imagine a class that wants to stop litter in their schoolyard. They make colourful posters and put them up around the school. They write a short, catchy jingle and sing it at the morning assembly. One pupil gives a friendly speech asking everyone to use the bins. And every day, the whole class sets a good example by picking up any litter they see. Little by little, the other children begin to do the same, and soon the whole schoolyard is clean. That is the power of persuasion at work.

Think About It

If you could make one poster to persuade people to care for the environment, what would it say, and what picture would you draw on it?

Speaking Up for the Environment

When you persuade others to care for the environment, you are doing something brave and good. You are speaking up for the land, the water, the air, and all the living things that cannot speak for themselves. The trees, the rivers, the birds, and the fish have no voice of their own, so people who care must be their voice.

You can speak up in your own community in many ways. You can encourage your friends and family to keep your district clean. You can join or organise a clean-up day at a beach, a park, or your schoolyard. You can find out about the helpers in our country who work to protect the environment, such as the people who collect and manage our waste, and you can support their work. Every time you encourage someone else to care, you are helping the whole community to grow stronger and healthier.

Remember that caring for the environment is a job we all share. Each person, no matter how young, has a part to play. When you do your part and help others to do theirs, you become a true friend of the environment, and you help to make the world a cleaner, safer, and more beautiful place for everyone.

Did You Know?

When a whole community decides to care for its environment, amazing things can happen. A dirty lot can become a green park. A polluted gully can be cleaned. A bare hillside can be planted with trees. All of this begins when people persuade one another to care.

Let's Practice

Make a small poster with a kind message that persuades people to care for the environment. Use bold colours and a clear picture. Then think of a short jingle or a single sentence you could say to persuade a friend to stop littering.

📖 Glossary of Key Terms

The Physical Environment

Environment: Everything around you, including the land, water, air, living things, and the things people have made
Physical Environment: The part of your surroundings that you can see, hear, smell, and touch
Natural Environment: The things in your surroundings that were made by nature, not by people
Man-Made Environment: The things in your surroundings that were built or made by people (also called the built environment)
Senses: Sight, hearing, smell, touch, and taste, which help you to explore your environment
Condition: Whether the environment is healthy or unhealthy, clean or dirty, safe or unsafe
Living Things: Plants, animals, and people that grow, feed, and breathe
Non-Living Things: Things like rocks, water, and air that do not grow, feed, or breathe
Resource: Something from the environment that people use to meet their needs
Basic Needs: The things every person must have to live: food, water, clothing, and shelter

Caring for the Environment

Pollution: Harmful or dirty things that spoil the land, water, or air
Land Pollution: Rubbish and waste left on the ground instead of being thrown away properly
Water Pollution: Dirty or harmful things that get into our rivers, ponds, gullies, and the sea
Air Pollution: Dirty smoke and harmful gases that get into the air
Litter: Rubbish dropped carelessly on the ground instead of in a bin
Conservation: Using and protecting our resources wisely so they are not wasted
Reduce: To use less of something so that less is wasted
Reuse: To use something again instead of throwing it away
Recycle: To make something old into something new and useful again
The Three Rs: Reduce, reuse, and recycle, three ways to make less rubbish and care for our resources

Persuading Others

Persuade: To use kind, convincing words and actions to help someone change their mind or do something good
Poster: A bright drawing with a clear message used to remind people to do the right thing
Jingle: A short, catchy song that carries a message and is easy to remember
Speech: Speaking clearly to a group to share ideas and persuade them
Good Example: Doing the right thing yourself so that others are encouraged to do the same

Answer Key

Focus Question 1: What is my physical environment?

1. What is the physical environment? The physical environment is everything around you that you can see, hear, smell, and touch. (Example: trees, the sky, a road, your school.)

2. Natural environment vs. man-made environment: The natural environment is made by nature (trees, rivers, hills, animals). The man-made environment is built or made by people (houses, roads, schools, cars).

3. How do the senses help? The five senses (sight, hearing, smell, touch, taste) help us notice the things around us and tell whether the environment is healthy or unhealthy, clean or dirty.

4. Living vs. non-living: Living things grow, feed, and breathe (a plant, a dog). Non-living things do not grow, feed, or breathe (a rock, water).

5. Resource: A resource is something from the environment that people use to meet their needs, such as water (for drinking) and trees (for wood, fruit, and shade).

Focus Question 2: Why and how should we care for our environment?

6. Three reasons to care: A clean environment keeps us healthy; a cared-for environment keeps us safe (from floods and landslides); it protects the plants and animals that share it with us.

7. Pollution and its three kinds: Pollution is harmful or dirty things that spoil the environment. The three kinds are land pollution (rubbish on the ground), water pollution (dirty things in rivers and the sea), and air pollution (smoke and harmful gases in the air).

8. Four ways to care: Do not litter and keep things clean; plant trees and care for plants; save water and electricity; avoid causing pollution. (Following the three Rs also counts.)

9. The three Rs: Reduce (use less), reuse (use again), recycle (make old into new). A plastic bottle can be reduced (use one all day), reused (cut into a flower pot), and recycled (made into something new).

Focus Question 3: How can I persuade others to care for the environment?

10. What is persuading, and ways to persuade: To persuade means to use kind, convincing words and actions to help someone do something good. Ways to persuade include making posters, writing songs or jingles, writing poems, giving a speech, writing letters or emails, and setting a good example.
