## A Letter to the Young Reader

Stop for a moment and think back over your day. When you woke up this morning, you were in a home that kept you safe and dry through the night. You put on clothes. You probably ate something and had a drink of water. Perhaps a parent gave you a hug before school, or a friend made you laugh on the way to class.

Every one of these things is part of what we call your **needs**. Some of them keep your body alive and well. Others fill your heart and make you feel happy, safe, and loved. Here is something wonderful to remember: every single person in the world has needs, just as you do. Children and grown-ups, people in every town and every country, and people of every kind — all of them need certain things to live and to be happy. This is one of the special ways in which all people are alike.

In this chapter, you are going to learn about the needs we all share. You will discover the difference between something you truly need and something you simply want. You will find out how families, friends, and helpful groups make sure our needs are met. And you will learn how, when people help one another, life becomes better and kinder for everyone. # Part One: Understanding Our Needs

Before we can talk about how our needs are met, we must first understand what a need really is. We must also learn how a need is different from a want, and then we will sort our needs into two important groups. This is where our journey begins.

## Needs and Wants Are Not the Same

The words *need* and *want* sound rather alike, and people sometimes use them as though they mean the same thing. But they do not, and learning the difference between them is one of the most useful lessons you will ever learn.

A **need** is something you must have in order to live and to be well. It is not an extra or a treat. It is one of the important things that keeps you alive, healthy, and happy. If you do not have a need, your body or your feelings will suffer.

A **want**, on the other hand, is something you would like to have, but that you can live perfectly well without. A want is nice. It is pleasant to have. But it is not necessary. If you do not get a want, you may feel a little disappointed, yet you will still be safe, healthy, and well.

It helps to think of some everyday examples. You need clean water to drink so that your body can work properly, but you might want a sweet, fizzy drink, which your body does not need at all. You need clothes to keep you warm and covered, but you might want a brand-new pair of fancy sneakers, even though your old shoes still keep your feet perfectly safe. You need food so that your body has energy, but you might want a large slice of cake, when a simple, healthy meal is what your body truly calls for.

Why does this difference matter so much? Because when you can tell a need from a want, you can make wise choices. You learn to make sure your real needs are taken care of first, before you spend time or money on wants. You also learn to be thankful for the needs you already have, and patient about the wants that may come to you later. In fact, this is exactly how careful grown-ups manage their money: they always pay for their needs first, and they treat themselves to their wants only afterwards.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine four things laid out on a table: a cup of water, a video game, a warm bed, and a bag of sweets. The cup of water is a need, because your body cannot live without water. The video game is a want, because it is fun but you can live happily without it. The warm bed is a need, because you need rest and shelter. And the bag of sweets is a want, because, although it tastes lovely, it is not at all necessary. Sorting things this way helps you see clearly what truly matters most.

> **Think About It:** Look around your home right now. Can you point to one thing that is a need and one thing that is a want? How do you know which is which?

## Our Basic Needs

Now that you understand what a need is, let us learn about the most important kind of all: our **basic needs**.

Basic needs are the things that every single person must have in order to stay alive and healthy. They come before everything else. We call them *basic* because they are the foundation of a healthy life, in the same way that the strong base of a building must come first before anything can be built on top of it. There are four main basic needs, and it is worth getting to know each one.

The first basic need is **food**. Your body needs food to give you energy and to help you grow. Without food, the body slowly grows weak and cannot do its work. You may remember learning that a balanced diet keeps the body strong — well, food itself is a basic need for every person on earth.

The second basic need is **water**. Your body needs clean water to drink, because water helps every single part of you to work properly. People can live only a very short time without water, far shorter than they can live without food, which makes water one of the most important basic needs of all.

The third basic need is **clothing**. Clothes keep your body warm when the weather is cool, help to keep you cool and shaded when the sun is hot, and keep you covered, comfortable, and protected. Clothing is something every person needs.

The fourth basic need is **shelter**. Shelter means a safe place to live, such as a house. Your shelter keeps you safe and dry from the heavy rain, the burning sun, and the cold of night. It is the place where you rest, where you sleep, and where you feel protected together with your family.

When you stop to think about it, you use your basic needs every single day, often before you even leave for school. You wake up inside your home, which is your shelter. You get dressed, which is your clothing. You eat something and drink some water. In just a few minutes each morning, all four of your basic needs are being met. This is why, whenever people need help, others always try to make sure these four basic needs are taken care of first of all.

> **Think About It:** Which of your four basic needs did you use already today? See if you can find all four of them in your morning.

## Our Other Needs

People need much more than food and shelter in order to be truly happy. We also have what are called our **other needs**. These needs are every bit as real and important, but they are different in one special way: you cannot hold them in your hand.

Our other needs are the feelings and the care that every person needs in order to feel happy, safe, and loved. They help you to grow into a kind and confident person. Here is the surprising part: even if every one of your basic needs were met, even if you had plenty of food and a fine, comfortable house, you would still feel sad and empty inside without your other needs. That is how important they are.

There are several other needs that matter deeply to all of us. There is **love**, which means both giving love and receiving it from family and friends; love is what makes us feel that we belong and that we matter to someone. There is **friendship**, which means having friends to play with, talk to, and share with, so that we are not lonely. There is **safety**, which means feeling protected and free from danger, so that we can relax and enjoy our lives. There is **respect**, which means being treated kindly and fairly, and treating others in the same kind way. There is **trust**, which means being able to depend on others and having them depend on you. There is **comfort**, which means being cared for and made to feel better when we are sad or hurt. And for many families there is **worship**, the need to pray or to worship together, which is a deeply important part of life.

Why do these other needs matter so much? Because they are what make life truly worth living. A person can have a full belly and a fine house, and yet still feel unhappy if they have no love, no friends, and no one they can trust. But when our other needs are met, we feel whole, settled, and content.

You meet these other needs, and have them met for you, more often than you may realise. When you fall and scrape your knee, and a parent gives you a warm hug, your need for love and comfort is being met. When a classmate invites you to play at break time, your need for friendship is being met. When your home feels calm and protected, your need for safety is being met. These small, everyday moments are quietly looking after some of your most important needs.

> **Did You Know?** Kind words and a friendly smile cost nothing at all, yet they can help to meet another person's need for love, friendship, and respect. You have the power to help meet other people's needs every single day.

## We All Share the Same Needs

Here is one of the most important ideas in this whole chapter, so read it slowly: every person in the world shares the same needs.

It does not matter what country a person comes from. It does not matter what language they speak, what they look like, what kind of family they have, or how they worship. Every person, everywhere, needs food, water, clothing, and shelter. And every person, everywhere, needs love, friendship, safety, and respect. In our deepest needs, all people are exactly the same.

This idea is important because it teaches us to understand and to care about other people. Once you realise that a child far away in another part of the world wants the very same things you want — to be fed, to be safe, and to be loved — you begin to feel a warm kindness toward all people, even those who seem very different from you on the outside. It also stirs something good in your heart when you see someone whose needs are not being met, and it makes you want to help them.

You can see this truth right in your own classroom. Your friends may come from different families, eat different foods at home, or worship in different ways. Yet all of you need the very same things — to learn, to be treated kindly, and to feel that you belong. That shared need is what makes you alike, even when some things on the surface look different.

> **Think About It:** A child living far away, in a country you have never visited, needs the same things you need. Can you name two needs that you and that child both share?

# Part Two: How Our Needs Are Met

You have learned what our needs are, and you have sorted them into basic needs and other needs. Now we will explore something just as important: how those needs are actually met. The good news, which you will discover in this part, is that you are never alone. All around you, people and helpful groups are working to meet one another's needs.

## The People Who Help Us

Most of our other needs are met by the people who are closest to us. In fact, people meet each other's needs all day long, often without even noticing that they are doing it.

Your family is usually the very first to meet your needs. Your family gives you food, shelter, clothing, love, and comfort. When you are sick, your family cares for you until you are well. When you are sad, your family helps you to feel better. Day after day, quietly and faithfully, your family looks after both your basic needs and your other needs.

Your friends help to meet your needs too, especially your need for friendship and for fun. A good friend listens when you talk, plays with you, shares with you, and stands beside you. Friends help us to feel happy and to feel that we belong somewhere.

Beyond your home, there are still more people who help. At school, your teachers help to meet your need to learn, and they help you to feel safe and cared for through the day. In your community, a kind neighbour might keep a watchful eye on you, lend a helping hand, or step in to support your family during a difficult time.

When you understand that people meet one another's needs in this way, two good things happen inside you. First, you begin to feel thankful for all the people who help you. Second, you start to see that you, too, can become a helper, meeting the needs of others through your own kindness.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine that one day you forget your lunch and leave it at home. You are sitting at your desk feeling hungry, when a friend notices and quietly shares their own food with you. In that moment, your friend has helped to meet your need. Saying a warm "thank you" shows that you are grateful, and one day, when someone else is in need, you will remember that kindness and share with them.

> **Think About It:** Name one person who helps to meet your needs. What is one thing they do for you?

## Organisations That Help Us

Sometimes a need is far too big for one person or one family to handle alone. In those times, special groups called **organisations** step in to help. An organisation, which is sometimes also called an agency, is simply a group of people who work together for the purpose of helping others.

There are several important helping organisations that look after a community. The fire brigade helps when there is a fire, putting out the flames and rescuing anyone in danger. The police help to keep people safe and step in when there is trouble or danger. The hospital, together with the ambulance service, helps when someone is sick or badly hurt. And charities, churches, and community groups help families who are in need of food, clothing, shelter, or care.

Some of the most important help these organisations give is during an **emergency**. An emergency is a sudden, dangerous situation that needs help right away. In an emergency such as a fire, a flood, a sudden illness, or a child becoming lost, there is no time to waste. This is exactly why it is so useful for every family to know the emergency telephone numbers for these helpers, so that a grown-up can call for help quickly. Many of these organisations are ready to help at any hour, whether it is the middle of the day or the middle of the night.

Knowing about these helping organisations brings a great comfort: it means that you and your family are never truly alone, not even in a frightening situation. When you know who to call and what each helper does, you can stay calmer and get help more quickly when it is needed most.

**Let's look at an example.** Suppose there is a fire in a building. The right helper to call is the fire brigade. Now suppose, instead, that someone has suddenly become very sick. This time the right helper is the ambulance, or a trip to the hospital. And if a child were lost, or there were trouble, the right helper would be the police. Matching the right helper to the right emergency makes sure that people get exactly the help they need, as fast as possible.

> **Think About It:** Why do you think it is a good idea to keep a list of emergency phone numbers in a place where everyone in your home can find it?

## Helping One Another in the Right Way

It is a good and kind thing to help others meet their needs. But there is something just as important as helping, and that is *how* we help. We should always help with good **moral values**.

Moral values are the good ways of behaving toward other people. Kindness, honesty, sharing, forgiveness, and respect are all moral values. When you hold these values in your heart, you help others in a way that is loving and right, not selfish or unkind.

Families and communities all over the world teach moral values to their children, and one of the oldest and best ways of teaching them is through stories. Many of these stories, including stories from religion, show people helping one another, and they gently teach us the right way to behave.

One well-known story is the story of the **Good Samaritan**. In this story, a traveller is hurt and lying helpless by the side of the road. Several people pass by and walk straight past him, pretending not to see. But then one kind man stops. He cares for the hurt stranger and makes sure his needs are met, even though the two of them have never met before. This simple story teaches a powerful lesson: it is good and right to help others, even people we do not know. The kind man showed wonderful moral values, and that is why people still tell his story today.

Helping others is at its very best when it comes from a kind and honest heart. When people treat one another with good moral values, needs are met, friendships grow stronger, and the whole community becomes a happier and better place to live. And the best news of all is that you can show these same values every single day — by sharing, by telling the truth, by including a classmate who has been left out, and by forgiving a friend who says sorry.

> **Did You Know?** People also celebrate kindness and helping through songs, stories, and art. Across Jamaica and around the world, people sing songs and tell tales about caring for one another, and these joyful songs and stories help to pass good values from one person to the next.

# Part Three: How Meeting Needs Makes a Difference

You have learned what our needs are and how they are met. Now you will discover something truly important: when needs are met, life becomes better — and not only for one person, but for everyone around them. In this final part of the chapter, you will see how meeting needs makes a real and lasting difference, especially during the hardest of times.

## We Depend on One Another

No person in the world can do everything alone. We all **depend on** one another, which simply means that we rely on each other for help. Every day, in small ways and in large ways, people depend on the help of others.

Just think for a moment about how connected we all are. You depend on your family to care for you. Your family, in turn, may depend on a farmer or a shopkeeper to provide food. The shopkeeper depends on a driver to bring the goods to the shop. In this way, person after person is joined together, each one helping the others, like the links in a long and sturdy chain.

This depending on one another becomes especially important during a **life-threatening situation** — a moment of great danger, when someone's life or safety is truly at risk. Picture some of these dangers: a fire breaking out in a building, a serious road accident, a flood that covers the roads, lightning crackling during a heavy storm, or a home left without water when a pipe is locked off. In moments like these, no one can manage entirely alone. People must work together and depend on one another, and on the helping organisations, in order to stay safe.

Think of how this works in real life. A firefighter rescues a family from a burning house. A doctor cares for a person who has been badly hurt. Neighbours guide one another to safety, away from rising flood water, and they share food and water until the danger has passed. When people cooperate in this way, lives can be saved. Knowing that we depend on one another teaches us to be helpful, brave, and caring, and it reminds us that helping others is not only kind — sometimes it can save a life.

> **Think About It:** Can you think of a time when someone helped your family, or a neighbour, during a hard or dangerous moment? How did that help make a difference?

## Good Ways and Poor Ways of Meeting Needs

It is important not only to meet needs, but to meet them in the right way. There are positive, good ways of satisfying a need, and there are negative, poor ways, and it is worth learning to tell them apart.

A positive way of meeting a need is one that is kind, honest, and fair, and that helps everyone involved. Imagine you want a turn with the ball during a game. A positive way to meet that need is to ask politely and wait patiently for your turn. Or imagine a friend is feeling sad and needs comfort. A positive way to help is to sit with them and listen kindly. These positive actions meet the need, and at the very same time they build trust and friendship.

A negative way of meeting a need is one that is unkind or unfair, and that hurts other people. Snatching the ball, pushing to the front, or being selfish in order to get what you want are all negative ways. A negative way might get a person what they want for a single moment, but it breaks trust, causes hurt feelings, and leaves others unhappy.

This is why the way we meet a need matters so much. When needs are met in positive ways, good feelings grow, and everyone benefits. When needs are met in negative ways, problems and bad feelings grow instead. Choosing the positive way, again and again, helps a whole community to live together happily.

> **Think About It:** A classmate has a toy that you would really like to play with. What would be a positive way to ask for a turn? What would be a negative way to try to get it?

## How Meeting Needs Changes Lives

When our other needs are met, and met with kindness, people's lives truly change for the better. Let us look closely at how this happens.

When a lonely person is given friendship, they no longer feel alone in the world. They feel, perhaps for the first time in a while, that they belong. When a frightened person is given safety, they can stop worrying and finally feel calm and protected. When a person who is sad or hurting is given love and comfort, their heart grows lighter, and they begin to heal. And when a whole community comes together to solve a problem, life gets better for many people at once, all at the same time.

Meeting needs in good ways brings what we call positive results — happiness, safety, trust, and friendship. These results do not last only for a single moment. Sometimes they can change a person's whole life for the better.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine that your community has a problem: there is nowhere safe and clean for the children to play. Now imagine that the neighbours decide to come together. They clear away the rubbish from an empty lot, they make it tidy and safe, and they turn it into a proper play area. With that single act of working together, they have met an important need. From that day on, every child in the community has a safe place to play. The whole community becomes happier and healthier, all because people joined hands to meet a need. That is how satisfying a need can make a real and lasting difference to many lives at once.

The wonderful truth is that you can be part of making this kind of difference. A small act of kindness can brighten a single person's whole day. A larger act, done together with others, can change an entire community. And although you are young, you can already begin. You can share with a friend who has nothing. You can comfort someone who is sad. You can be kind and respectful to everyone you meet, include a classmate who is being left out, and tell a trusted grown-up whenever someone needs help. Small acts of kindness add up, and when many people each do a little, together they make a very big difference.

> **Did You Know?** When one person is helped, they very often go on to help someone else. In this way, a single act of kindness can spread from person to person, like ripples spreading across a pond after a stone is dropped in.

## A Friendly Idea: Symmetry

As you explore the things that help to satisfy our needs, you will sometimes make shapes, drawings, and folded paper designs, such as origami, of useful objects. While you do this, you can also discover a friendly idea from mathematics called **symmetry**, which is hidden all around you once you learn to look for it.

A shape has symmetry when it can be split into two halves that match each other exactly. The special line that splits a shape into two matching halves is called the **line of symmetry**. Here is an easy way to picture it. Imagine folding a shape exactly in half. If the two halves lie perfectly on top of each other, with no part sticking out anywhere, then the line you folded along is a line of symmetry.

Once you start looking, you will find symmetry everywhere in the world. A butterfly has beautiful symmetry, because its left wing matches its right wing exactly, and the line of symmetry runs straight down the middle of its body. A square has symmetry too, for you can fold it down the middle and watch the two halves meet perfectly. Even the letter "A" has a line of symmetry running down its centre. Many leaves, many faces, and many folded paper designs show this same lovely matching.

Learning to spot a line of symmetry helps you to see the patterns and the balance that exist in shapes and in nature, and it is a useful skill that you will carry with you. To try it yourself, take a square piece of paper and fold it exactly in half so that the edges meet. Because the two halves match perfectly, you have just found a line of symmetry. Then see whether you can fold the same square a different way and find another line of symmetry hiding inside it.

> **Think About It:** Look carefully at the objects around your classroom. Can you find one object that has a line of symmetry? How could you check whether you are right?

- Every person in the world shares the same needs, and this helps us to understand and care for one another.
