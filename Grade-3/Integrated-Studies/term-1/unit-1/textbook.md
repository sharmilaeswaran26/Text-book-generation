## A Letter to the Young Reader

Have you ever stopped to think about how wonderful your body is? At this very moment, while your eyes move across these words, your body is doing dozens of important jobs without being asked. Your heart is beating steadily. Air is flowing gently in and out of your chest. If you ate breakfast this morning, your body is quietly turning that food into the energy you are using to read and think right now.

You never have to remind your body to do these things. It simply knows how. In many ways, your body is like a clever, hard-working friend who looks after you all day and all night, never taking a rest.

In this chapter, you are going to get to know that friend much better. You will travel inside your own body to discover three of its busiest parts: your **teeth**, your **stomach**, and your **lungs**. You will learn how to keep your body clean, strong, and healthy. And you will find out how the food on your plate helps every part of you to grow. By the time you reach the last page, you will understand your body in a way you never did before. Let us begin the journey.

## Words to Know

Here are some important words you will meet in this chapter. Do not worry about memorising them now. You will understand them fully as you read, because each one is explained carefully in the lessons.

- **Incisors** – the flat, sharp front teeth that bite and cut food.
- **Canines** – the pointed teeth that tear and grip food.
- **Molars** – the wide, flat back teeth that crush and grind food.
- **Crown** – the part of a tooth you can see above the gum.
- **Root** – the part of a tooth hidden in the gum that holds it firmly.
- **Enamel** – the hard, shiny layer that protects the outside of a tooth.
- **Temporary teeth** – the first set of teeth, also called baby or milk teeth.
- **Permanent teeth** – the second set of teeth, meant to last for life.
- **Stomach** – the stretchy, bag-shaped part that holds and breaks down food.
- **Elastic** – able to stretch and then return to its first size.
- **Lungs** – the two soft, spongy parts in your chest that help you breathe.
- **Oxygen** – the part of the air your body needs to stay alive.
- **Hygiene** – keeping yourself and your things clean to stay healthy.
- **Balanced diet** – eating the right amount of food from every food group.

# Part One: The Wonderful Parts Inside Me

Your body is made of many parts. Some of them, like your hands and your teeth, you can see whenever you like. Others do their work hidden away inside you, where you cannot watch them. In this first part of the chapter, you will meet three remarkable parts of your body and learn the important jobs they do every single day.

## All About Your Teeth

Stand in front of a mirror and give yourself a big smile. Those strong, white parts inside your mouth are your **teeth**, and they are some of the hardest parts of your entire body.

Most people think teeth are only for smiling, but teeth do far more than that. Their most important job is to help you eat. When you put food into your mouth, your teeth break it into small, soft pieces. We call this **chewing**. Chewing turns a big bite of food into something soft enough to swallow safely, and it makes the food easy for your stomach to use later on.

Your teeth help you in another quiet way, too. They help you to speak clearly. Say the words "this" and "tooth" aloud, and notice how your tongue gently touches your teeth as you speak. Without teeth, many of the words you use every day would be very difficult to say.

Now imagine, just for a moment, trying to eat with no teeth at all. A crunchy apple, a slice of hard-dough bread, a roasted corn, a piece of jerk chicken — every one of these would be almost impossible to enjoy. When you think about it like this, you begin to understand why your teeth are such an important gift.

> **Think About It:** What is your favourite food to eat? Which teeth do you think you use the most when you eat it?

## The Three Kinds of Teeth

Here is something most children never notice until they look closely: your teeth are not all the same shape. If you study your smile in the mirror, you will see that you have three different kinds of teeth, and each kind has its own special job. They work together like a well-trained team.

At the very front of your mouth are your **incisors**. These are flat teeth with a thin, straight edge, a little like the edge of a small spade. Incisors are built for biting and cutting. Every time you take a bite out of a ripe mango or a piece of bread, it is your incisors that do the very first part of the work.

Just beside your incisors sit your **canines**. People sometimes call these the "eye teeth." Canines are pointed and sharp, almost like little spikes. Their job is to grip and tear. When you pull a piece of meat or chicken away from the bone, your canines are the teeth that hold on tightly and tear it loose.

Far at the back of your mouth are your **molars**, which some people call the "jaw teeth." Molars are big, wide, and flat on top. They are the grinders of your mouth. Once your front teeth have bitten off a piece of food, your tongue pushes it back to the molars, which crush and grind it into a soft mush that is ready to swallow.

Think about what happens when you eat a piece of carrot. First, your incisors bite off a piece. If the piece is tough, your canines help to grip and tear it. Then your molars take over, grinding the carrot again and again until it is soft and smooth. All of this happens in just a few seconds, every time you eat, and you barely notice it at all. That is real teamwork.

> **Did You Know?** Each kind of tooth is shaped perfectly for its own job. If you had only flat front teeth, you could bite your food but never grind it. If you had only molars, you could grind but never take a neat bite. Having all three kinds means you can eat almost any food in the world.

## A Look Inside a Single Tooth

A tooth might look like one simple, solid piece, but it is really made of several parts, and each part matters.

The part of the tooth you can see when you smile is called the **crown**. The crown is the top of the tooth, the part that rises above your gum and meets your food when you chew. But a tooth does not end at the gum. Hidden underneath, deep inside your jaw, is the **root**. You cannot see the root, but it does a very important job: it holds the tooth firmly in place, in much the same way that the roots of a mango tree hold the whole tree steady in the ground. Thanks to its strong roots, a tooth does not fall out when you bite into something hard.

Covering the outside of every tooth is a hard, shiny layer called **enamel**. Enamel is the toughest material in your whole body — it is even harder than your bones. Its job is to act like a strong helmet, protecting the softer parts deep inside the tooth from harm.

> **Did You Know?** Unlike your skin, which heals itself after a small cut, enamel cannot grow back once it is badly damaged. This is exactly why brushing your teeth and protecting your enamel every day is so important. We will learn how to do that later in this chapter.

## Why You Have Two Sets of Teeth

One of the most surprising facts about teeth is that you do not get just one set in your lifetime. You get two.

Your very first set of teeth is called your **temporary teeth**. You may also hear people call them baby teeth, milk teeth, or primary teeth. You began growing these when you were a tiny baby, and they are smaller than grown-up teeth. The word *temporary* means "lasting only for a short time," and that is exactly what these teeth do. They are never meant to stay forever.

As you grow bigger, something clever begins to happen. One by one, your baby teeth become loose and fall out. Each time a baby tooth leaves, a new and bigger tooth pushes up from underneath to take its place. This second set is called your **permanent teeth**, because the word *permanent* means "lasting" or "staying." These are the teeth that are meant to stay with you for the rest of your life.

This is why losing a tooth is nothing to be afraid of. It is a normal and happy part of growing up, and it usually means a stronger tooth is on the way. But it also carries an important reminder. Your permanent teeth are the very last set you will ever get. There is no third set waiting behind them. If you look after your permanent teeth well, they can stay healthy and strong for your whole life.

> **Think About It:** How many baby teeth have you lost so far? Can you remember what the new permanent tooth looked like when it first grew in?

## A Closer Look at Your Stomach

Place your hand gently on your tummy, a little above your belly button and slightly to the left side. Right there, hidden inside you, is your **stomach**. You cannot see it, but it is always busy.

The stomach is shaped a little like a soft, squashy bag, and it is made of strong, stretchy muscle. When you chew your food and swallow it, the food does not stay in your mouth. It slides down a long tube and drops gently into your stomach below.

Once your food arrives, the stomach gets to work on two big jobs. First, it holds the food, so that everything you have eaten does not rush through your body all at once. Second, it breaks the food down, churning and mixing it until it becomes a soft, soupy mush. This breaking-down is very important, because your body cannot use a large lump of food. It can only take the good parts out of food once the food has been turned soft and small.

In a way, your stomach is like a kitchen tucked inside your body. Just as food must be washed, cut, and cooked in a kitchen before anyone can eat it, food must be broken down in your stomach before your body can use it to grow and give you energy.

> **Think About It:** Why do you think it is important to chew your food well before it travels down to your stomach? What might happen if you swallowed a big lump without chewing?

## The Amazing Stretchy Stomach

Here is one of the most surprising things about your stomach: it can stretch. When you eat a large meal, your stomach grows bigger to make room for all the food. Then, after the food has moved on, it shrinks back down to its smaller, resting size.

We have a special word for something that can stretch and then return to its first shape. We say it is **elastic**. A rubber band is elastic. A balloon is elastic. And your stomach is elastic too.

You have felt this happen many times, even if you did not realise it. Think about a big Sunday dinner, or a time you drank two or three cups of water one after another. Did your tummy feel full and tight? That tight feeling was your stomach stretching to hold everything you put inside it. Later, when the food and water had passed on, the full feeling faded away, because your stomach had shrunk back to its usual size.

There is a very old story that helps people remember how the stomach can stretch. It is the story of **Jonah and the great fish**. In the story, a man named Jonah is swallowed by a huge fish and lives inside its belly for a time before coming out safely. The story reminds us of something that is really true about stomachs: they can stretch to make room for something large. Of course, your own stomach stretches in a much smaller way, but it does it every single time you enjoy a big meal.

> **Did You Know?** When your stomach is empty and ready for food, it sometimes makes a rumbling, growling sound. That gurgle is a friendly little message from your body, letting you know it is time to eat.

## Meet Your Lungs

Place both of your hands flat on your chest and take a slow, deep breath in. Did you feel your chest rise and grow bigger under your hands? That happens because of your **lungs**.

You have two lungs, one on each side of your chest. They are soft and spongy, a little like two pink sponges. Your lungs are precious, so your body keeps them safe inside a cage of curved bones called your ribs.

The job of your lungs is one of the most important jobs in your entire body: they help you to breathe. Every time you breathe in, your lungs fill up with fresh air. Now, the air all around us contains something very special called **oxygen**, and your body must have oxygen to stay alive and full of energy. Your lungs take the oxygen out of the air you breathe in and pass it into your body. Then, when you breathe out, your lungs push away the used air that your body no longer needs.

This is why your lungs never get a holiday. They keep working all day and all night, even while you are deep in sleep, so that your body always has the fresh air it needs. Out of all the busy parts of your body, your lungs are among the hardest workers of all.

> **Think About It:** Try holding your breath for a few seconds, then let it go. Notice how your body soon wants you to breathe again. Why do you think your body will not let you stop breathing for long?

## How Breathing Works

Breathing is made of two simple parts that repeat over and over, all day long. Understanding these two parts will help you understand your lungs completely.

The first part is breathing in, which we call **inhaling**. When you inhale, you draw fresh air into your body through your nose or mouth. The air travels down into your lungs, and your lungs fill up like two soft balloons. As they fill, your chest expands and rises upward.

The second part is breathing out, which we call **exhaling**. When you exhale, you push the used air out of your lungs. As the air leaves, your lungs become smaller again, and your chest sinks back down. So the rule is easy to remember: when you breathe in, your chest grows bigger, and when you breathe out, your chest grows smaller.

**Let's look at an example.** Sit up nice and tall and rest one hand flat on the middle of your chest. Breathe in slowly through your nose, and feel your chest rise gently under your hand. Now breathe out slowly through your mouth, and feel your chest sink back down. You have just felt your lungs filling with air and emptying again. That simple movement, which you just felt, is breathing — and it never stops.

There is an easy way to picture what your lungs are doing. Think of a balloon. When you blow air into a balloon, it swells up and gets bigger. When you let the air out, it shrinks and gets smaller. Your lungs behave in much the same way inside your chest. They fill with air and grow larger, then empty and grow smaller, again and again.

You may have noticed your lungs working extra hard at certain times. After you run a fast race or play a hard game of football, you breathe quickly and your chest moves up and down rapidly. That is your lungs racing to bring in all the extra oxygen your body needs after so much running about.

> **Did You Know?** Most children breathe in and out around twenty times every minute when resting — and many more times than that after running and playing. You do all of this breathing without ever having to think about it.

# Part Two: Caring for My Body

You have now met three of the hardest-working parts of your body, and the truth is that every part of you works just as faithfully, day after day. Because your body does so much for you, it is only fair that you do your part to look after it. When you take good care of your body, it stays healthy, strong, and happy, and it can keep doing all the wonderful things it does for you.

## Why Your Body Deserves Your Care

Your body is a gift, and it is the only body you will ever have. Every part of it — your teeth, your skin, your stomach, your lungs, your eyes, your hands — needs care in order to stay well.

Think about how carefully you would look after something precious, such as a brand-new bicycle. You would keep it clean, protect it from rust, and fix any problem before it grew worse. Your body deserves even more care than a bicycle, for one simple reason: a bicycle can be replaced, but your body cannot. It must carry you through your whole life.

When you care for your body, wonderful things happen. You fall sick less often. You have more energy to play and to learn. You feel happy and confident about yourself. But when a body is neglected, it can grow weak, become unwell, or stop working as it should. The happy news is that caring for your body is not difficult once you have learned the right habits — and that is exactly what the next few lessons will teach you.

> **Think About It:** Name one thing you already do to take care of your body. How do you think it helps you?

## Looking After Your Teeth

Of all the parts of your body, your teeth need some very special care, because, as you learned earlier, your permanent teeth must last you a whole lifetime. Looking after them is simpler than you might think.

The most important habit of all is brushing. You should brush your teeth at least twice every day — once in the morning and once at night before bed. Using a toothbrush and a little toothpaste, brush gently up and down and all around, reaching the front, the back, and the tops of every tooth. Brushing sweeps away the tiny bits of food and the germs that cling to your enamel and would otherwise harm it.

Brushing alone, however, cannot reach everywhere. Small bits of food often hide in the narrow spaces between your teeth, where a brush cannot squeeze in. To clean those hidden spaces, we use a thin thread to floss between the teeth. Brushing and flossing together keep your whole mouth clean and fresh.

It also helps your teeth a great deal to be careful with sweets. Sugary foods and drinks, such as sweets and sodas, can be unkind to your teeth. When sugar is left sitting on your teeth, it can slowly make tiny holes called **cavities**, which can ache and damage a tooth. Eating fewer sweets, and brushing after you do enjoy one, keeps cavities away.

Finally, it is wise to visit the dentist. A dentist is a special doctor who looks after teeth. The dentist checks that your teeth are healthy, cleans them, and gently fixes any small problem before it grows into a big one.

Many children find it easier to remember to brush when they sing a little song while they do it. Here is one you can try:

> *Brush your teeth, brush your teeth, give them all a treat,*
> *up and down and all around, to keep them clean and sheen.*
> *In the morning and the night, clean them twice a day,*
> *up and down and all around, keep cavities away.*

> **Think About It:** Imagine if a person never brushed their teeth at all. What do you think would slowly happen to their teeth, and why?

## Healthy Habits and Unhealthy Habits

A **habit** is something you do again and again, so often that it becomes easy and natural, like brushing your teeth or saying good morning. Some habits are good for your body, and we call those healthy habits. Other habits can slowly harm your body, and we call those unhealthy habits. Learning to tell them apart is one of the most useful things you can do for your health.

There are four main areas where good habits keep your whole body well. The first is **hygiene**, which means keeping yourself clean. Washing your hands with soap before you eat and after you use the bathroom, taking a bath, keeping your nails short, and wearing clean clothes all wash away the germs that can make you sick. The second area is your **diet**, which means the food you eat. Choosing fruits and vegetables instead of too much junk food keeps your body strong, and you will learn much more about this in the next part of the chapter. The third area is **exercise**, which means moving your body by running, jumping, dancing, and playing. The fourth area is **rest**, which means going to bed on time and sleeping well, because it is while you sleep that your body rests and grows.

When you look at habits side by side, it becomes easy to see which ones help and which ones harm. Brushing your teeth twice a day is healthy, but going to bed without brushing is not. Eating fruits and vegetables is healthy, but eating too much junk food is not. Washing your hands before a meal is healthy, but touching food with dirty hands is not. Getting a good night's sleep is healthy, but staying up very late every night is not. Playing and exercising each day is healthy, but sitting still all day long without moving is not.

The most wonderful thing about habits is that you get to choose them. If you notice you have an unhealthy habit, you do not have to keep it forever. Little by little, day by day, you can change it into a healthy one. That power belongs to you.

> **Think About It:** Think carefully about your day so far. Can you name two healthy habits you have already done, and one unhealthy habit you would like to change tomorrow?

## The Power of Exercise

**Exercise** simply means moving your body in active ways. When you run, skip, swim, climb a tree, dance, or play games like cricket and football, you are exercising — and your body loves it.

Exercise keeps your heart, your lungs, and your muscles strong. It helps you grow, fills you with energy, and even helps you sleep more soundly at night. Children who exercise regularly are usually healthier and happier than those who sit still all day.

Something interesting happens to your body whenever you exercise hard. For a short time, your body changes, and these changes are perfectly normal. They are simply signs that your body is working and getting stronger. You may begin to breathe faster, because your lungs are working harder to bring in extra oxygen. You may feel your heart beating more quickly, because it is pumping more blood to your busy muscles. Your skin may grow warm and start to sweat, which is the clever way your body cools itself down. And afterwards, you may feel pleasantly tired. Once you rest a while and drink some water, you soon feel good again, and your breathing and heartbeat slow back to normal.

**Let's look at an example.** Sit quietly for a moment and notice how calm and slow your breathing is. Now run on the spot for half a minute, then bend and touch your toes ten times. Stop and pay attention to your body. Are you breathing faster? Can you feel your heart beating harder? Has your skin grown a little warm? Now rest for a minute and feel your body slowly settle back to calm. You have just watched your own body respond to exercise, exactly as it is meant to.

This is also a good moment to remember something you learned about food and your lungs. When you exercise, your body uses up energy and loses water through sweat, which is why you often feel thirsty and hungry afterwards. Good food and clean water give your body the fuel it needs to stay active and strong.

> **Did You Know?** When you exercise often, your heart and lungs slowly grow stronger and better at their jobs, just as your arm muscles grow stronger when you lift and carry things each day.

## Wise Words About Staying Healthy

People all over the world care about keeping their bodies healthy, and for a very long time families and communities have shared wise sayings and beliefs about it. Some of these wise words come from religion and from the teachings passed down within families.

In many homes, children are taught that the body is special and should be kept clean and healthy. You can find simple, caring sayings from different faiths and families that remind everyone to eat well, keep clean, and look after their health. When you hear these sayings, you are reminded that caring for the body is something good and important to people everywhere, not only in your own home but all around the world.

This shows us that taking care of your body is not only a science lesson. It is also part of how families and whole communities live well together. When everyone makes the effort to stay healthy, the entire community grows stronger.

> **Think About It:** Has an older person in your family ever given you advice about staying healthy, such as "wash your hands" or "eat your vegetables"? Why do you think they care enough to remind you?

# Part Three: Food and a Healthy Body

You cannot grow, run, think, or even smile without food. Food is the fuel that keeps your body going, in the very same way that fuel keeps a car or a bus moving along the road. But here is something important: not every food does the same job inside you. In this last part of the chapter, you will learn about the different kinds of food and discover exactly how each one helps your body.

## Food Is the Body's Fuel

Every time you feel hungry, your body is sending you a message. It is telling you that it needs more fuel to keep working well. When you eat, the food is broken down inside you, and your body takes from it the good parts it needs. These helpful parts of food are called **nutrients**, and they are what your body uses to grow, to move, and to stay healthy.

This is why the food you choose matters so much. Some foods give your body plenty of good nutrients, while others give very little. Learning which foods help your body the most is one of the smartest things you can do, and it begins with understanding food groups.

## The Five Food Groups

To make food easier to understand, we sort foods into **food groups**. Foods that help your body in similar ways are placed in the same group. There are five main food groups, and once you know them, you will look at your plate in a whole new way.

The first group is the **staples**. These are foods such as rice, bread, yam, green banana, potato, and dumpling. Staples are usually the largest part of a meal, and their main gift to your body is energy, which lets you move, work, and play.

The second group is the **legumes**, which simply means peas and beans. Red peas, gungo peas, lentils, and broad beans all belong here. Legumes help your body to grow and to build its strong parts.

The third group is **fruits and vegetables**. This is a large and colourful group that includes mango, orange, pawpaw, callaloo, carrot, pumpkin, and tomato. These foods help to keep your body working well and help you to fight off sickness.

The fourth group is **fats and oils**, which includes cooking oil, butter, margarine, and nuts. Fats and oils do give your body energy, but here is the important part: your body needs only a small amount of them, so we eat these foods sparingly.

The fifth group is **foods from animals**. This group includes chicken, fish, egg, milk, cheese, and meat. These foods help your body to grow strong and to repair itself when it is hurt.

Here is a simple table to help you keep the five groups in mind:

| Food group | Some examples | How it helps your body |
|------------|---------------|------------------------|
| Staples | Rice, yam, bread, green banana | Gives energy |
| Legumes | Red peas, gungo peas, lentils | Helps the body grow |
| Fruits and vegetables | Mango, callaloo, carrot, orange | Keeps the body healthy |
| Fats and oils | Cooking oil, butter, nuts | Gives energy (only a little is needed) |
| Foods from animals | Fish, chicken, egg, milk | Helps the body grow and repair |

> **Think About It:** Picture a meal you ate today. Can you find foods from at least two different food groups on that plate?

## Go, Grow, and Glow Foods

There is a friendly and easy way to remember what foods do for you. Many foods can be sorted into three cheerful groups: the go foods, the grow foods, and the glow foods. Each name tells you exactly what that food does.

**Go foods** are the ones that give you energy, so you can run, jump, and play. They are like the fuel that makes you "go." Rice, bread, yam, green banana, and potato are all go foods. On a busy school day when you need plenty of energy, it is your go foods that keep you moving.

**Grow foods** are the ones that help your body to grow bigger and stronger, and they also help to mend your body when it is hurt. Fish, chicken, egg, milk, cheese, peas, and beans are grow foods. When you are growing taller or healing a scraped knee, your grow foods are quietly at work.

**Glow foods** are the ones that keep your body healthy and "glowing," and that help you fight off illness. These are mostly the fruits and vegetables, like orange, mango, callaloo, carrot, and pumpkin. When you want bright eyes, healthy skin, and a strong body, it is your glow foods that help.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine your lunch is rice, stewed fish, and a slice of ripe mango. The rice is a go food, giving you energy for the afternoon. The fish is a grow food, helping your body grow strong. The mango is a glow food, keeping you healthy. Because this lunch has a go food, a grow food, and a glow food all together, it is a very good and well-balanced meal indeed.

Your body needs all three kinds of food every day. If you ate only go foods, you would have energy but you would not grow well. If you ate only glow foods, you would stay healthy but you would lack energy. It is when you eat go, grow, and glow foods together that your whole body is properly looked after.

> **Did You Know?** The colourful fruits and vegetables on your plate are some of the best glow foods of all. In general, the more colours you see on your plate, the more kinds of helpful nutrients your body is getting.

## Eating a Balanced Diet

When your meals contain the right amount of food from every food group, we say that you are eating a **balanced diet**. The word *balanced* means that everything is in its proper amount — not too much of one thing, and not too little of another.

A balanced plate has go foods to give you energy, grow foods to build your body, and plenty of colourful glow foods to keep you healthy, with only a small amount of fats and oils. When your meals look like this most of the time, your body receives everything it needs to do its many jobs.

This matters because every part of your body, including the teeth, stomach, and lungs you learned about earlier, depends on good food to stay strong. A balanced diet gives each part what it needs. It also gives you the steady energy to learn and play through the whole day, and it helps your body grow properly as you get older.

> **Think About It:** Why do you think it is healthier to eat many different foods than to eat the very same food at every meal?

## When Food Is Not Balanced

Just as the right foods keep your body healthy, poor eating habits can lead to problems. Your body needs the proper amount of good food — neither too little, nor too much of the wrong things — and it is worth understanding what happens when that balance is lost.

When a person does not get enough good food, their body cannot grow or work as it should. Over time they may become thin, weak, and tired, and they may fall sick more easily, because their body simply does not have the nutrients it needs. A body that goes without enough grow foods and glow foods for a long time cannot stay strong and well.

There is another side to this, too. Eating too much of the wrong foods — too many sweets, fried snacks, and sugary drinks — is also unhealthy. It can cause a person to gain too much weight in an unhealthy way, which forces the body to work much harder than it should.

The lesson here is a simple but powerful one. Good health is not only about eating, but about eating the right foods in the right amounts. A balanced diet protects you from both kinds of problem at once. When you choose healthy foods most of the time, you give your body its very best chance to be strong, active, and well.

> **Think About It:** Sweets taste lovely, but why would it be a poor idea to eat only sweets all day and nothing else?

## From the Market to Your Plate

Food does not appear on your plate by magic. Someone has to buy it, and someone has to prepare it. Understanding this journey helps you understand food even better.

When grown-ups buy food at the market or shop, they need to know how much they are buying. To do this, they use **units of measure**. Fruits and vegetables, for example, might be sold by the pound, or by the dozen, which means twelve, or by the bag. Saltfish might be sold by the pound. Using units of measure lets people buy exactly the right amount of food and pay a fair price for it.

**Let's look at an example.** Imagine you go to the market with your aunt. She asks the seller for "a dozen oranges" and "two pounds of yam." When she says a dozen, she means twelve oranges, no more and no less. When she says two pounds of yam, she is telling the seller exactly how much yam to weigh out for her. Because she used clear units of measure, the shopping was simple, quick, and fair for everyone.

It also helps to know that some foods are usually eaten after they have been cooked, while others are eaten fresh and raw. Rice, chicken, and yam are nearly always cooked before we eat them. A ripe banana, a washed carrot, or a slice of mango, on the other hand, are usually eaten just as they are. Knowing which foods to cook and which to enjoy fresh keeps your meals both tasty and safe.

Whenever food is being prepared, it is very important to keep everything clean, so that germs cannot creep in and make anyone sick. Hands should always be washed well with soap before touching food. The food itself, along with the bowls, the utensils, and the table, should be clean. Fruits and vegetables should be washed before they are eaten. Even the healthiest food can make a person ill if it is dirty or handled with dirty hands, so clean preparation is just as important as choosing good food in the first place.

> **Did You Know?** Washing your hands before you prepare or eat food is one of the easiest and most powerful ways in the whole world to stop germs from spreading and to keep your family healthy.

## Food in Our Traditions

Food is far more than fuel. It is also a treasured part of how families and communities live, celebrate, and show respect. Different families, and different religious groups, have their own special foods and food customs, and many of these are closely connected to staying healthy and living well.

Some groups, for example, choose to eat certain foods and not others, and they may prepare their meals in particular, careful ways as part of their beliefs. People follow these customs out of respect for their faith and as part of keeping a healthy and meaningful life. When you learn about the food customs of others, you grow in understanding and respect for the people around you, and you come to see that, all over the world, people care about eating in ways that are both healthy and full of meaning.

> **Think About It:** Does your family have a special food that you enjoy on holidays or other special days? What makes that food so special to you?

## Chapter Summary

In this chapter, you explored your own remarkable body and learned how to keep it healthy and strong.

You discovered that you have three kinds of teeth that work together as a team: the incisors that bite and cut, the canines that tear and grip, and the molars that crush and grind. You learned that each tooth has a crown you can see, a root that anchors it firmly, and a hard coat of enamel that protects it. You also learned that you receive two sets of teeth in your life — first your temporary baby teeth, and then your permanent teeth, which must last for the rest of your life.

You travelled inside your body to meet your stomach, which holds and breaks down your food and is elastic enough to stretch around a big meal. You met your two lungs, which fill with air so that your chest rises when you breathe in and falls when you breathe out, bringing your body the oxygen it must have to live.

You then learned why and how to care for your body through clean hygiene, healthy eating, regular exercise, and good rest, and you learned how to tell a healthy habit from an unhealthy one. Finally, you explored the five food groups and the cheerful idea of go, grow, and glow foods, and you came to understand why a balanced diet keeps every part of you well.

## Key Takeaways

- Your teeth — incisors, canines, and molars — work together to bite, tear, and grind food, and each tooth has a crown, a root, and a protective layer of enamel.
- You grow two sets of teeth in your life: temporary baby teeth first, then permanent teeth that must last forever.
- Your stomach holds and breaks down food, and it stretches like elastic to hold a meal.
- Your two lungs help you breathe, filling with air and growing larger when you inhale, and emptying and growing smaller when you exhale.
- Caring for your body means keeping clean, eating well, exercising, and resting, and choosing healthy habits over unhealthy ones.
- Foods belong to five food groups, and the simple idea of go, grow, and glow foods helps you remember how each food helps your body.
- A balanced diet gives your whole body what it needs, and preparing food cleanly keeps you safe and well.
