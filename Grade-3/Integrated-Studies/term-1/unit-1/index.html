🦷 Lesson 1: All About Teeth

What are teeth? Teeth are hard, white structures in your mouth that help you eat food. They break food into smaller pieces so your stomach and digestive system can process it more easily. Teeth also help you speak clearly and give shape to your face.

Did you know? You will have two sets of teeth in your lifetime! The first set is called temporary teeth (also called baby teeth, milk teeth, or primary teeth). These fall out naturally as you grow. The second set is called permanent teeth, which stay with you for the rest of your life.

Key Terms to Know:

Crown: The visible white part of the tooth above the gum
Root: The part of the tooth under the gum that holds it in place
Enamel: The hard, shiny coating that protects the tooth
Temporary Teeth: Baby teeth that fall out (also called milk teeth or primary teeth)
Permanent Teeth: Adult teeth that replace baby teeth and last a lifetime

Types of Teeth and Their Functions

Type of Tooth Common Name What It Does Shape
Incisors Front teeth Cut and bite food Flat and sharp
Canines Eye teeth Tear and grip food Pointed and sharp
Molars Jaw teeth Crush and grind food Flat and wide

🍽️ Lesson 2: The Stomach

What is the stomach? The stomach is a muscular organ (part of your body that has a special job) located inside your body. It looks like a small bag or pouch. After you swallow food, it goes down your food pipe and into your stomach.

Where is the stomach? Your stomach is located in the upper part of your belly, just below your ribs. If you place your hand on your belly button and move it up a little, you're near where your stomach is.

What does the stomach look like? The stomach is about the size of a closed fist when it's empty. It has a reddish-brown color and wrinkled walls.

Key Terms to Know:

Stomach: A muscular bag-shaped organ that stores and breaks down food
Elastic: Able to stretch and return to its original size
Muscles: Body tissues that can contract and relax to create movement
Digest: To break down food so your body can use it

Where Is the Stomach?

The stomach is positioned just below your ribcage, slightly to the left side of your body. Put your hand a little above your belly button and slightly to the left — your stomach is working away inside, right there.

The Stomach's Important Jobs

  1. Stores food: When you eat, food goes into your stomach where it stays while being broken down. Your elastic stomach can stretch to hold more food!
  2. Mixes food: The muscles of the stomach squeeze and move the food around, mixing it with special juices.
  3. Breaks down food: These special juices help break the food into very tiny pieces that your body can use.
  4. Pushes food along: When the food is ready, the stomach pushes it into the small intestine to continue digestion.

The Elastic Stomach: Did you know your stomach is very stretchy? It can expand (get bigger) when you eat. When your stomach is empty, it looks wrinkled and flat. But when you eat, it stretches out like a balloon to hold the food. This is why the story of Jonah and the Whale is sometimes used to explain how stomachs can be elastic and stretch to hold things!

Activity 3: Locate Your Stomach

What to do:

  1. Stand or sit comfortably
  2. Place your hand on your belly button
  3. Move your hand up toward your ribs—this is near your stomach
  4. Point to show where you think your stomach is
  5. Drink 2-3 cups of water slowly
  6. Notice how your stomach feels fuller
  7. In your notebook, describe how your body feels

Activity 4: Demonstrate Stomach Elasticity

What you need: Balloons, water

What to do:

  1. Take an empty balloon and observe its size and shape
  2. Slowly fill it with water or air
  3. Watch how it stretches and changes shape
  4. Notice the wrinkles disappearing as it fills
  5. This is similar to how your stomach stretches when you eat!

Write about it: In a paragraph, describe what happens to the balloon and explain how this shows what happens to your stomach when you eat.

💨 Lesson 3: The Lungs

What are lungs? Lungs are organs (body parts with special jobs) that help you breathe. They take in fresh air and give out used air. Every time you breathe in and breathe out, your lungs are working!

Where are your lungs? Your lungs are located in your chest, protected by your ribcage (the bones around your chest). You have two lungs—one on each side of your heart. They are spongy and light, filled with tiny air sacs.

What do lungs look like? Lungs are pink and spongy organs. They have a bumpy, textured surface. When they fill with air, they get bigger. When they push out the air, they shrink smaller.

Key Terms to Know:

Lungs: Organs that take in oxygen and release carbon dioxide
Breathing: Taking air in (inhaling) and letting air out (exhaling)
Oxygen: A gas in the air that your body needs to live
Diaphragm: A muscle below your lungs that helps them expand and contract
Ribcage: The bones in your chest that protect your lungs and heart

Where Are the Lungs?

The lungs fill most of your chest, with the heart resting between them. You have one lung on each side of your chest, and your ribcage forms a strong cage around them to keep them safe.

The Lungs' Important Jobs

  1. Take in oxygen: When you breathe in, fresh air with oxygen enters your lungs through your nose or mouth.
  2. Release carbon dioxide: Your body creates a waste gas called carbon dioxide. Your lungs push this out when you breathe out.
  3. Exchange gases: Oxygen from the air goes into your blood, and carbon dioxide comes out of your blood into your lungs.
  4. Help you speak: When you push air out of your lungs through your voice box, you can make sounds and speak!

How do lungs work? A large muscle called the diaphragm sits below your lungs. When you breathe in, this muscle moves down and pulls your lungs open, filling them with air. Your ribcage also moves out. When you breathe out, the diaphragm moves up and your lungs shrink, pushing the used air out. This happens automatically—you don't have to think about it!

Activity 5: Observe Your Breathing

What to do:

  1. Stand with your hands on your chest
  2. Take a deep breath in and notice your chest expanding
  3. Hold the breath for a second
  4. Slowly breathe out and notice your chest getting smaller
  5. Repeat this 5-10 times while observing the changes
  6. In your notebook, describe the changes you felt in your chest
  7. Now try the same activity with your hands on your back and sides to feel the movement

Activity 6: Build a Lung Model

What you need: Plastic bottle, 2 balloons, straw, tape, scissors, elastic band

What to do:

  1. Cut the bottom off the plastic bottle
  2. Stretch one balloon across the bottom opening and secure with elastic band or tape
  3. Place one balloon inside the bottle and feed the end of the straw through the neck
  4. Secure the straw with tape so air doesn't escape
  5. Pull down on the stretched balloon at the bottom (this represents the diaphragm)
  6. Watch the balloon inside fill with air (representing your lungs)
  7. Push the stretched balloon up to watch the balloon inside deflate

Write about it: In a paragraph, explain how this model shows how your lungs work.

Activity 7: Breathing Exercises

What to do:

  1. Breathe normally and count how many breaths you take in one minute
  2. Now run on the spot for 30 seconds
  3. Count your breaths again—notice how they've increased!
  4. Notice the short, quick breaths (staccato breathing)
  5. Now try slow, smooth, deep breaths (legato breathing)
  6. Try to sing or hum while making long sounds
  7. Discuss: Why do athletes need to practice good breathing?

💪 Lesson 4: Taking Care of Our Bodies

Why should we take care of our bodies? Your body is like a machine. Just as you need to maintain a bicycle by oiling the chain and checking the tires, your body needs care to work well and keep you healthy and happy.

What does "taking care of our bodies" mean? It means making healthy choices every day in four main areas:

  1. Hygiene: Keeping your body clean
  2. Diet: Eating healthy foods
  3. Exercise: Moving your body and being active
  4. Rest: Getting enough sleep

Key Terms to Know:

Hygiene: Keeping yourself and your surroundings clean to prevent illness
Healthy habit: A good thing you do regularly that keeps you healthy
Unhealthy habit: A bad thing you do that can harm your health
Exercise: Physical activity that makes your body stronger
Nutrition: Getting the right foods your body needs to grow and be healthy

🦷 Lesson 5: Taking Care of Our Teeth

Why is it important to take care of our teeth? Healthy teeth help us chew food properly, smile confidently, and speak clearly. Taking care of teeth now prevents problems like cavities and tooth decay later in life.

What are cavities? Cavities are holes that form in teeth when sugar and bacteria damage the enamel. They can cause pain and problems eating.

How can we prevent cavities? The best way to prevent cavities is to:

  1. Brush teeth twice a day (morning and night)
  2. Floss between teeth once a day
  3. Limit sugary foods and drinks
  4. Drink water instead of soda
  5. Visit the dentist every 6 months

How to Brush Your Teeth Properly

  1. Get your brush ready: Use a soft-bristled toothbrush and a pea-sized amount of toothpaste
  2. Brush the outer surfaces: Hold the brush at a 45-degree angle and brush gently from the gum line down
  3. Brush the inner surfaces: Angle the brush and brush the inside of your teeth
  4. Brush the chewing surface: Use small back-and-forth motions on the tops of your teeth
  5. Brush your tongue: Gently brush your tongue to remove bacteria
  6. Rinse well: Rinse your mouth with water
  7. Time it right: Spend at least 2-3 minutes brushing

🏃 Lesson 6: Exercise and Physical Activity

What is exercise? Exercise is physical activity that makes your body move and work. It includes sports, dancing, running, walking, or any activity that gets your heart beating faster.

Why is exercise important? Regular exercise:

  • Keeps your heart and lungs strong
  • Makes your muscles stronger
  • Helps you grow properly
  • Improves your mood and confidence
  • Helps you sleep better
  • Reduces stress and worry
  • Prevents some diseases

What are the short-term effects of exercise? When you exercise, you immediately notice:

  • Your heart beating faster
  • Faster breathing
  • Sweating
  • Feeling warm
  • Muscles feeling tired

How much exercise do we need? Children your age need at least 60 minutes of physical activity every day. This can include playing, sports, dancing, or any movement that makes you active.

Activity 13: Exercise and Body Changes

What to do:

  1. Before starting: Feel your pulse (count heartbeats for 15 seconds and multiply by 4). Record this number.
  2. Exercise 1: Run on the spot for 30 seconds
  3. Exercise 2: Do 10 bending and toe-touching movements
  4. Exercise 3: Stretch arms above head and touch toes 10 times
  5. After exercise: Feel your pulse again. Notice the difference!
  6. Record observations: breathing rate, sweating, tiredness, temperature

Discussion: Talk about how the body responds to exercise. Why do you think these things happen?

🍎 Lesson 9: The Five Food Groups

What are food groups? Food groups are categories of foods that have similar nutrients. There are five main food groups, and eating from all of them helps keep your body healthy and strong.

Why do we need all five food groups? Different foods provide different nutrients that your body needs. By eating from all five groups, you make sure your body gets everything it needs to grow, have energy, and fight off germs.

The Five Food Groups

Food Group Examples Nutrients Provided What It Does
Staples
(Carbohydrates)		 Rice, bread, potatoes, pasta, cornmeal, yams, plantains, crackers Carbohydrates, fiber, B vitamins Gives you energy for playing and learning (ENERGY - "Go" foods)
Legumes
(Proteins)		 Beans, peas, peanuts, chickpeas, lentils, tofu Protein, fiber, iron, B vitamins Builds and maintains muscles and bones (GROWTH - "Grow" foods)
Fruits & Vegetables Oranges, bananas, apples, carrots, lettuce, spinach, tomatoes, broccoli, mangoes, pineapples Vitamins, minerals, fiber Keeps body working well and healthy (REGULATION - "Glow" foods)
Animal Protein Chicken, fish, eggs, beef, goat meat, milk, cheese, yogurt Protein, calcium, iron, B vitamins Builds body and bones; provides calcium for strong teeth (GROWTH - "Grow" foods)
Fats & Oils Coconut oil, butter, olive oil, nuts, avocado, seeds Fat-soluble vitamins, essential fatty acids Helps absorb vitamins and provides energy (small amounts needed)

Key Terms to Know:

Nutrients: Substances in food that your body needs to grow and be healthy
Protein: A nutrient that builds and repairs muscles and bones
Carbohydrates: A nutrient that gives your body energy
Vitamins: Nutrients that help your body work properly
Minerals: Nutrients like calcium and iron that keep bones and blood healthy
Balanced Diet: Eating foods from all groups in the right amounts

The "Go," "Grow," "Glow" Rule:

  • "Go" Foods (Energy): Staples like rice, bread, and pasta give you energy to run, play, and think
  • "Grow" Foods (Building): Legumes and animal proteins help you grow tall and strong
  • "Glow" Foods (Health): Fruits and vegetables keep your body working well and your skin, hair, and eyes healthy

⚖️ Lesson 10: What is a Balanced Diet?

What is a balanced diet? A balanced diet means eating the right amounts of food from each food group every day. It's like having the right mix of ingredients in a recipe—too much of one thing and not enough of another doesn't work as well.

How do I create a balanced plate? A good way to think about a balanced diet is to imagine dividing your plate into sections:

  • 1/4 of your plate: Staples (rice, bread, pasta)
  • 1/4 of your plate: Protein (legumes or animal protein)
  • 1/2 of your plate: Fruits and vegetables
  • Small amounts: Fats and oils for cooking
  • Drink water or milk

Why is a Balanced Diet Important?

A balanced diet helps you:

  • ✓ Grow to your full height and weight
  • ✓ Have energy for school, play, and activities
  • ✓ Build strong bones and muscles
  • ✓ Have healthy skin, hair, and teeth
  • ✓ Fight off sickness and germs
  • ✓ Think better in school
  • ✓ Feel happy and healthy

🚫 Lesson 11: Problems from Unhealthy Eating

What happens when we don't eat a balanced diet? When people don't eat the right foods in the right amounts, their bodies can develop health problems. Two common problems are malnutrition and obesity.

What is malnutrition? Malnutrition happens when a person doesn't get enough of the nutrients their body needs. This can be because:

  • They don't eat enough food
  • They eat foods that lack important nutrients
  • Their body can't absorb nutrients properly

What are signs of malnutrition? People who are malnourished may:

  • Look very thin or have a swollen belly
  • Have weak muscles and bones
  • Have skin that looks different (pale or dull)
  • Have hair that looks thin or discolored
  • Get sick often
  • Have trouble learning in school

What is obesity? Obesity happens when a person eats too much, especially too many unhealthy foods high in sugar and fat. This can lead to:

  • Being overweight
  • Heart problems
  • Diabetes (a disease affecting blood sugar)
  • Difficulty moving and exercising

What is rickets? Rickets is a disease that develops when a child doesn't get enough calcium and vitamin D. It causes:

  • Weak bones that can bend or break easily
  • Delayed growth
  • Bowed legs
  • Swollen joints

How can we prevent these problems? The answer is simple: eat a balanced diet with foods from all five groups!

👨‍🍳 Lesson 13: Food Preparation

Why is food preparation important? How we prepare food can affect its nutrition and safety. Learning to prepare food properly helps us stay healthy.

What are food safety practices? When preparing food, we must:

  • Wash our hands with soap and water before starting
  • Keep our work area clean
  • Use clean utensils and cutting boards
  • Wash fruits and vegetables before use
  • Avoid touching ready-to-eat foods with bare hands
  • Cook meat thoroughly

Activity 28: Prepare a Simple Healthy Dish

What you'll make: Fruit salad or a simple cheese sandwich

Before you start:

  1. Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water
  2. Gather all ingredients and utensils
  3. Clean your work area

For Fruit Salad:

  1. Wash each fruit under running water
  2. Peel fruits as needed (teacher assistance if required)
  3. Cut fruit into bite-sized pieces
  4. Combine in a clean bowl
  5. Optional: Add a little honey or lime juice
  6. Mix gently
  7. Serve in clean bowls

For Cheese Sandwich:

  1. Wash your hands again
  2. Gather bread, cheese, lettuce, tomato
  3. Wash vegetables
  4. Slice vegetables if needed (teacher assistance)
  5. Layer bread, cheese, and vegetables
  6. Cut diagonally if desired
  7. Serve on clean plate

Writing Activity: Write a paragraph describing your preparation process, emphasizing hygiene practices.

Activity 29: Demonstrate Hand Washing

Task: Show the proper way to wash hands for food preparation:

  1. Use warm water
  2. Apply soap
  3. Scrub palms and backs of hands
  4. Clean between fingers
  5. Clean under nails
  6. Wash wrists
  7. Rinse thoroughly
  8. Dry with clean towel

Time it right: The whole process should take about 20 seconds (the time it takes to sing "Happy Birthday" twice!).

Teacher Notes

  • Have hand-washing supplies available in the classroom
  • Conduct all food preparation in a clean, safe area
  • Supervise students carefully with any cutting activities
  • Consider students with food allergies
  • Use this activity to teach both nutrition and safety
  • Allow students to taste the food they prepared (if safe and all agree)
  • Discuss cultural variations in food preparation

🌍 Lesson 14: Food and Religion

Do religions have special food rules? Yes! Many religions around the world have practices related to food. These practices often help followers stay healthy while honoring their beliefs.

Why would religions have food rules? Religious dietary practices often have health benefits along with spiritual meanings. For example, many religions avoid certain meats or encourage eating certain foods.

📖 Glossary of Key Terms

Body Parts and Functions

Teeth: Hard structures in the mouth used for biting, tearing, and crushing food
Incisors: Front teeth with flat edges used for cutting food (also called front teeth)
Canines: Pointed teeth used for tearing and gripping food (also called eye teeth)
Molars: Large flat teeth at the back used for crushing and grinding food (also called jaw teeth)
Temporary Teeth: Baby teeth that fall out and are replaced by adult teeth (also called milk teeth or primary teeth)
Permanent Teeth: Adult teeth that last a lifetime
Crown: The visible white part of a tooth above the gumline
Root: The part of a tooth below the gumline that anchors it in place
Enamel: The hard, shiny protective coating on the surface of teeth
Cavity: A hole in a tooth caused by decay
Stomach: A muscular bag-shaped organ that stores and digests food
Elastic: Able to stretch and return to original size without tearing
Lungs: Organs in the chest that take in oxygen and release carbon dioxide
Breathing: The process of taking in air (inhaling) and releasing air (exhaling)
Oxygen: A gas in air that the body needs to survive
Carbon Dioxide: A waste gas produced by the body and released through breathing
Diaphragm: The large muscle below the lungs that controls breathing
Ribcage: The bones in the chest that protect the lungs and heart

Health and Care

Hygiene: Keeping yourself and your surroundings clean to stay healthy
Healthy Habit: A regular practice that keeps you healthy and strong
Unhealthy Habit: A regular practice that can harm your health
Exercise: Physical activity that makes your body stronger and healthier
Diet: The foods and drinks a person normally consumes
Nutrition: The process of getting the nutrients your body needs from food
Nutrient: A substance in food that your body needs to grow and function

Food and Nutrition

Food Group: A category of foods that have similar nutrients
Staples: Foods rich in carbohydrates like rice, bread, and pasta (energy foods)
Legumes: Plants that produce protein-rich seeds like beans and peas (growth foods)
Protein: A nutrient that builds and repairs muscles and bones
Carbohydrates: Nutrients that provide energy for the body
Vitamins: Nutrients needed in small amounts to keep the body healthy
Minerals: Nutrients like calcium and iron needed for body functions
Balanced Diet: Eating the right amounts of foods from all food groups
Junk Food: Foods high in sugar and fat that aren't nutritious
Malnutrition: A condition caused by not getting enough nutrients
Obesity: A condition of being significantly overweight
Rickets: A disease caused by not getting enough calcium and vitamin D

Music and Performance Terms

Jingle: A short, catchy song, often used in advertising
Rhythm: The pattern of sounds and silence in music
Beat: The regular pulse in music that you can tap or clap to
Tempo: How fast or slow music is played
Staccato: Short, detached sounds in music
Legato: Long, smooth sounds in music
Ostinato: A short rhythmic or melodic pattern that repeats throughout a piece
Body Percussion: Using your body (clapping, snapping, tapping) to create rhythm

General Learning Terms

Observation: Carefully watching and noticing details
Investigation: A scientific experiment to discover something
Conclusion: A decision or opinion based on facts and evidence
Data: Information collected during a survey or experiment
Graph: A visual way to show and compare data
Tableau: A frozen scene or picture made with people's bodies
Multimedia: Using more than one type of media (text, pictures, video, sound) together

✅ Answer Key and Assessment Guide

Assessment 1: Teeth Knowledge

Expected Answers:

  • Types of teeth and functions: Incisors (cut food), Canines (tear food), Molars (grind food)
  • Parts of a tooth: Crown (visible part), Root (below gum), Enamel (protective coating)
  • Difference between temporary and permanent teeth: Temporary fall out and are replaced; permanent last lifetime
  • Why different shapes: Different shapes help perform different functions in eating

Assessment 2: Stomach Knowledge

Expected Answers:

  • Location: Upper belly, just below the ribs
  • Appearance: Reddish-brown, spongy, bag-shaped, wrinkled when empty
  • Functions: Stores food, mixes food, breaks down food with special juices, pushes food to small intestine
  • Elastic: Can stretch to hold more food, like a balloon filling with water

Assessment 3: Lungs Knowledge

Expected Answers:

  • Location: In the chest, protected by ribcage, one on each side of heart
  • Breathing changes: Chest expands when breathing in, shrinks when breathing out
  • Functions: Take in oxygen, release carbon dioxide, help with speaking and singing
  • Lung model: Shows how diaphragm pulls lungs open (like stretching balloon at bottom of bottle) to fill with air

Assessment 4: Body Care Knowledge

Expected Answers:

  • Why we care for our bodies: To keep healthy, prevent disease, have energy, grow properly, feel good
  • Healthy habits examples: Brushing teeth, eating fruits, exercising, sleeping 8 hours, washing hands
  • Unhealthy habits examples: Eating too much sugar, not sleeping, not exercising, poor hygiene
  • Tooth care: Brush twice daily, floss, limit sugar, visit dentist every 6 months
  • Exercise benefits: Stronger heart and lungs, stronger muscles, better mood, helps sleep, prevents disease
