# Chapter 4: Transformations

## Introduction

Look around you. The tiles on a floor repeat the same shape over and over. The two sides of a butterfly's wings match perfectly. When a minibus slides forward along Half-Way-Tree Road, every part of it moves the same distance in the same direction. When you look into a mirror, your raised right hand appears as a raised left hand. These everyday experiences are all examples of **transformations** — the mathematical study of how shapes move and change position on a flat surface.

In this chapter we study three special movements: the **translation** (a slide), the **reflection** (a flip), and the **rotation** (a turn). In all three, the shape we start with is called the **object**, and the shape we end with is called the **image**. A useful feature of these three transformations is that they never stretch, shrink, or bend the shape — the image is always exactly the same size and shape as the object. We will learn what this means, how to perform each transformation on a coordinate grid, and how to describe the result precisely. Along the way we will also look closely at the **symmetry** and the **angle and side properties** of common triangles and quadrilaterals.

## Why This Topic Matters

Transformations are the mathematics behind the patterns and designs we see all around us. A tile-layer fitting tiles, a seamstress cutting a symmetrical dress, an artist creating a repeating border, and a designer arranging a logo all use the ideas in this chapter, whether they name them or not. Jamaican craft — the repeating patterns in a woven basket, the bandana (madras) plaid, or the carved designs on furniture — depends on sliding, flipping, and turning a basic shape to fill a space.

Beyond art and craft, transformations are central to modern technology. Video games move characters across the screen using translations, flip them using reflections, and spin them using rotations. Animation, robotics, and the graphics on your phone all rest on these ideas. Learning transformations now also prepares you for more advanced geometry, where you will use them to prove that shapes are equal in size and shape.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- Describe the relationship between an object and its image after a **reflection**, and reflect a point or shape in a mirror line such as the x-axis or y-axis.
- Describe the relationship between an object and its image after a **rotation**, and rotate a point or shape through 90° or 180° about a given centre.
- Explain the difference between **congruent** and **similar** shapes.
- Identify and use the **angle, side, and symmetry properties** of common triangles and quadrilaterals.

## Key Vocabulary

- **Geometry** — the branch of mathematics that studies shapes, lines, angles, and space.
- **Transformation** — a change in the position (or sometimes size) of a shape.
- **Object** — the original shape, before the transformation.
- **Image** — the new shape, after the transformation. We mark the image with dashes: the image of point A is written A′ (read "A dash" or "A prime").
- **Plane** — a flat surface that extends in all directions, such as a sheet of graph paper.
- **Translation** — a transformation that slides every point the same distance in the same direction.
- **Reflection** — a transformation that flips a shape over a mirror line.
- **Rotation** — a transformation that turns a shape about a fixed point.
- **Mirror line (line of reflection)** — the line that a shape is flipped over.
- **Centre of rotation** — the fixed point about which a shape is turned.
- **Vector** — an instruction for a translation, written as a column showing movement across and up/down.
- **Congruent** — exactly the same size and the same shape.
- **Similar** — the same shape but not necessarily the same size.
- **Symmetry** — a balanced, matching arrangement of a shape.
- **Line of symmetry** — a line that divides a shape into two matching halves that are mirror images.
- **Rotational symmetry** — a shape has this if it looks the same as it turns part-way around a centre.
- **Perpendicular** — meeting at a right angle (90°).
- **Parallel** — always the same distance apart and never meeting.
- **Bisector** — a line that cuts something exactly in half. A *perpendicular bisector* cuts a line segment in half at a right angle.
- **Orientation** — the way a shape "faces"; whether it reads forwards or backwards, clockwise or anticlockwise.

## Main Lesson

A **transformation** moves a shape from one position to another on a plane. The starting shape is the **object** and the result is the **image**. In this chapter we meet three transformations that move a shape without changing its size or shape at all: the **translation**, the **reflection**, and the **rotation**. Mathematicians call these movements *rigid* transformations, because the shape stays "rigid" — as if it were a stiff cut-out of cardboard that you simply slide, flip, or turn.

We will use a coordinate grid throughout, because it lets us describe positions exactly using ordered pairs. We label the corners of the object with capital letters such as A, B, C, and the matching corners of the image with the same letters and a dash: A′, B′, C′. The corner A always maps to A′, B to B′, and so on. Reading the object and image carefully — corner by corner — is the key skill of this chapter.

## Concept Explanations

### Translation (the slide)

A **translation** slides a shape across the plane. Every single point of the shape moves the **same distance** in the **same direction** — nothing turns and nothing flips. Imagine pushing a domino across a table without spinning it: that is a translation.

We describe a translation with a **column vector**, written like this:

$$\binom{4}{3}$$

The **top** number tells you how far to move **across** (right if positive, left if negative). The **bottom** number tells you how far to move **up or down** (up if positive, down if negative). So the vector $\binom{4}{3}$ means "4 units right and 3 units up", while $\binom{-2}{-5}$ means "2 units left and 5 units down".

The curriculum reminds us that translations come in three kinds:

- **Horizontal** — only left or right, for example $\binom{5}{0}$.
- **Vertical** — only up or down, for example $\binom{0}{-3}$.
- **Slant (diagonal)** — across *and* up/down together, for example $\binom{4}{3}$.

**Relationship between object and image:** after a translation the image is the **same size, the same shape, and the same orientation** (it faces the same way) as the object. The object and image are **congruent**, and corresponding sides of the object and image are **parallel**.

### Reflection (the flip)

A **reflection** flips a shape over a straight line called the **mirror line** (or **line of reflection**). The image appears on the opposite side of the mirror line, like your reflection in a still pool of water.

Three facts describe a reflection exactly:

1. Every point of the object and its matching image point are the **same distance** from the mirror line (they are *equidistant* from it).
2. The line joining a point to its image is **perpendicular** to the mirror line.
3. Any point that already sits **on** the mirror line does not move.

**Relationship between object and image:** the image is the **same size and the same shape** as the object, so they are **congruent** — but the **orientation is reversed**. A shape that "faces right" will face left after reflection, and lettering appears back-to-front. This reversal of orientation is the special feature that makes reflection different from translation and rotation.

### Rotation (the turn)

A **rotation** turns a shape about a fixed point called the **centre of rotation**. To describe a rotation fully you must give three things:

- the **centre** of rotation (the fixed point the shape turns around);
- the **angle** of turn (for example 90° or 180°);
- the **direction** of turn — **clockwise** (the way a clock's hands move) or **anticlockwise** (the opposite way).

Picture the hands of a clock turning about the centre of the clock face: every point sweeps around that fixed centre.

**Relationship between object and image:** the image is the **same size and the same shape** as the object, so they are **congruent**. The shape keeps its handedness (it is not flipped), but it is turned to face a new direction. Every point of the object stays the same distance from the centre of rotation as it turns. A turn of 180° is special: clockwise and anticlockwise give the same result, so we do not need to state a direction.

### Congruence and similarity

Two shapes are **congruent** when they are **exactly the same size and exactly the same shape**. You could cut one out and place it perfectly on top of the other (turning or flipping it if needed). Because translations, reflections, and rotations never change size or shape, **the object and image are always congruent** under these three transformations.

Two shapes are **similar** when they have **the same shape but not necessarily the same size** — one is an enlarged or reduced copy of the other. All the angles match, but the side lengths are scaled up or down. For example, a small photograph and a large poster print of the same picture are similar.

A helpful way to remember the difference: *congruent shapes are identical twins; similar shapes are a parent and child — the same family shape, but different sizes.* Note that all congruent shapes are also similar (a scale factor of 1), but most similar shapes are not congruent.

### Symmetry of triangles and quadrilaterals

**Line symmetry.** A shape has a **line of symmetry** if a straight line can divide it into two halves that are mirror images of each other. If you folded the shape along that line, the two halves would match exactly. A shape may have no lines of symmetry, one, or several.

**Rotational symmetry.** A shape has **rotational symmetry** if it looks exactly the same after being turned part of the way around its centre (less than a full turn). The **order of rotational symmetry** is the number of times the shape looks the same during one full 360° turn. For example, a square looks the same four times as it turns once around, so it has rotational symmetry of order 4. Every shape returns to itself after a full turn, so an order of 1 means the shape has *no* true rotational symmetry.

**Properties of common triangles:**

| Triangle | Side property | Angle property | Lines of symmetry | Rotational symmetry |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Equilateral | all three sides equal | all three angles 60° | 3 | order 3 |
| Isosceles | two sides equal | two angles equal (base angles) | 1 | order 1 (none) |
| Scalene | all sides different | all angles different | 0 | order 1 (none) |
| Right-angled | (sides may vary) | one angle is exactly 90° | 0, or 1 if also isosceles | order 1 (none) |

In **every** triangle the three angles add up to **180°**.

**Properties of common quadrilaterals** (every quadrilateral has angles adding to **360°**):

| Quadrilateral | Sides | Angles | Lines of symmetry | Rotational symmetry |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Square | all 4 sides equal; opposite sides parallel | all 4 angles 90° | 4 | order 4 |
| Rectangle | opposite sides equal and parallel | all 4 angles 90° | 2 | order 2 |
| Rhombus | all 4 sides equal; opposite sides parallel | opposite angles equal | 2 | order 2 |
| Parallelogram | opposite sides equal and parallel | opposite angles equal | 0 | order 2 |
| Trapezium | one pair of parallel sides | (vary) | 0 (1 if isosceles trapezium) | order 1 (none) |
| Kite | two pairs of adjacent equal sides | one pair of opposite angles equal | 1 | order 1 (none) |

## Mathematical Reasoning

**Why do translations, reflections, and rotations preserve size and shape?** In each of these movements we treat the shape as a single, rigid object. A translation moves every point by the very same vector, so the distance between any two points of the shape never changes — if A and B were 5 units apart, then A′ and B′ are still 5 units apart. A rotation keeps every point at its original distance from the centre and turns the whole shape together, so again no two points get closer or farther apart. A reflection places each point at an equal distance on the other side of the mirror, and this mirroring preserves all the distances within the shape. Because every distance is preserved, every side length and every angle is preserved too. That is exactly what it means for the image to be **congruent** to the object.

**Why does a reflection reverse orientation, while a translation and a rotation do not?** Think of going round the corners of a triangle and noticing whether you travel clockwise or anticlockwise. A translation simply slides this journey to a new place — you still travel the same way round, so orientation is unchanged. A rotation turns the whole journey, but you still travel the same way round it, so orientation is again unchanged. A reflection, however, swaps left and right: what was a clockwise journey becomes an anticlockwise one. This is the same reason the writing on a shirt appears back-to-front in a mirror. So reflection is the one transformation of the three that reverses orientation.

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### How to translate a point by a vector

To translate a point by the vector $\binom{a}{b}$:

1. Start with the point's coordinates, (x, y).
2. **Add** the top number of the vector to the x-coordinate: new x = x + a.
3. **Add** the bottom number of the vector to the y-coordinate: new y = y + b.
4. The image is the point (x + a, y + b).

To translate a whole shape, apply these steps to **each** corner in turn, then join the image corners in the same order.

### How to reflect a point in a line

**In the y-axis** (the vertical axis): keep the y-coordinate the same and change the **sign** of the x-coordinate. The point (x, y) maps to (−x, y).

**In the x-axis** (the horizontal axis): keep the x-coordinate the same and change the **sign** of the y-coordinate. The point (x, y) maps to (x, −y).

**In any vertical mirror line x = k:** the y-coordinate stays the same. Find how far the point is from the line, then place the image the same distance on the other side. The point (x, y) maps to (2k − x, y).

**In any horizontal mirror line y = k:** the x-coordinate stays the same. The point (x, y) maps to (x, 2k − y).

A reliable general method for *any* mirror line: from each point, draw a line perpendicular to the mirror, measure the distance to the mirror, and mark the image the same distance beyond on the other side.

### How to rotate a point about a point

For rotations about the **origin** (0, 0):

- **90° anticlockwise:** the point (x, y) maps to (−y, x).
- **90° clockwise:** the point (x, y) maps to (y, −x).
- **180° (either direction):** the point (x, y) maps to (−x, −y).

If you ever forget these rules, you can rotate using tracing paper: trace the object and the centre, hold the centre still with a pencil point, turn the paper through the required angle, and read off the new positions.

## Rule / Property Explanations

The table below collects the coordinate rules used in this chapter. Keep it close while you work through the examples.

| Transformation | Rule for the point (x, y) |
|---|---|
| Translation by $\binom{a}{b}$ | (x + a, y + b) |
| Reflection in the x-axis | (x, −y) |
| Reflection in the y-axis | (−x, y) |
| Reflection in x = k | (2k − x, y) |
| Reflection in y = k | (x, 2k − y) |
| Rotation 90° anticlockwise about O | (−y, x) |
| Rotation 90° clockwise about O | (y, −x) |
| Rotation 180° about O | (−x, −y) |

In every case the image is **congruent** to the object. The reflection rules reverse orientation; the translation and rotation rules do not.

## Fully Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Translating a single point

Translate the point A(2, 1) by the vector $\binom{4}{3}$.

*Solution.* Add the vector to the coordinates:

- new x = 2 + 4 = 6
- new y = 1 + 3 = 4

So A′ = (6, 4).

*Check:* the move was 4 right and 3 up. From (2, 1), 4 right gives x = 6, and 3 up gives y = 4. Correct.

*Figure 1: Point A(2, 1) and its image A′(6, 4) on a grid, with an arrow showing the slide of 4 right and 3 up.*

### Example 2 — Translating a triangle

Triangle ABC has vertices A(1, 1), B(4, 1), C(1, 3). Translate it by $\binom{-3}{2}$ (3 left, 2 up).

*Solution.* Apply (x − 3, y + 2) to each vertex:

- A(1, 1) → A′(1 − 3, 1 + 2) = A′(−2, 3)
- B(4, 1) → B′(4 − 3, 1 + 2) = B′(1, 3)
- C(1, 3) → C′(1 − 3, 3 + 2) = C′(−2, 5)

*Check:* side AB ran from (1,1) to (4,1), a length of 3 units. Side A′B′ runs from (−2,3) to (1,3), also 3 units. The image is the same size, confirming congruence.

*Figure 2: Triangle ABC and its image A′B′C′ after a translation by the vector (−3, 2). The image faces the same way as the object.*

### Example 3 — Reflecting a point in the y-axis

Reflect the point P(3, 2) in the y-axis.

*Solution.* Reflection in the y-axis changes the sign of x and keeps y: (x, y) → (−x, y).

P(3, 2) → P′(−3, 2).

*Check:* P is 3 units to the right of the y-axis; P′ is 3 units to the left, at the same height. Equidistant from the mirror line. Correct.

### Example 4 — Reflecting a triangle in the y-axis

Triangle ABC has vertices A(2, 1), B(5, 1), C(2, 4). Reflect it in the y-axis.

*Solution.* Using (x, y) → (−x, y):

- A(2, 1) → A′(−2, 1)
- B(5, 1) → B′(−5, 1)
- C(2, 4) → C′(−2, 4)

*Check on orientation:* reading the object A → B → C we go to the right then up (anticlockwise). The image A′ → B′ → C′ goes to the left then up — the turning sense is reversed, exactly as expected for a reflection.

*Figure 3: Triangle ABC and its image A′B′C′ after a reflection in the y-axis. The image is the same size and shape, but its orientation is reversed.*

### Example 5 — Reflecting in the x-axis

Reflect the point Q(−4, 5) in the x-axis.

*Solution.* Reflection in the x-axis: (x, y) → (x, −y).

Q(−4, 5) → Q′(−4, −5).

*Check:* Q is 5 units above the x-axis; Q′ is 5 units below it, at the same horizontal position. Correct.

### Example 6 — Reflecting in a vertical mirror line

Reflect the point R(1, 2) in the line x = 4.

*Solution.* The y-coordinate stays the same. R is 4 − 1 = 3 units to the left of the line, so R′ is 3 units to the right: x = 4 + 3 = 7.

R(1, 2) → R′(7, 2).

Using the formula (2k − x, y) with k = 4: (2 × 4 − 1, 2) = (8 − 1, 2) = (7, 2). The two methods agree.

### Example 7 — Rotating a point 90° anticlockwise

Rotate the point A(3, 1) by 90° anticlockwise about the origin.

*Solution.* The rule is (x, y) → (−y, x).

A(3, 1) → A′(−1, 3).

*Check with tracing paper reasoning:* A points mostly to the right and a little up; after a quarter-turn anticlockwise it should point mostly up and a little left. The image (−1, 3) does exactly that. Correct.

### Example 8 — Rotating a triangle 90° clockwise

Triangle ABC has vertices A(1, 1), B(4, 1), C(1, 3). Rotate it 90° clockwise about the origin.

*Solution.* The rule is (x, y) → (y, −x).

- A(1, 1) → A′(1, −1)
- B(4, 1) → B′(1, −4)
- C(1, 3) → C′(3, −1)

*Check:* side AB had length 3 (from (1,1) to (4,1)). Side A′B′ runs from (1,−1) to (1,−4), length 3. Size is preserved, so the image is congruent. Correct.

*Figure 4: Triangle ABC and its image A′B′C′ after a rotation of 90° clockwise about the origin O.*

### Example 9 — Rotating a point 180°

Rotate the point P(2, 5) by 180° about the origin.

*Solution.* The rule is (x, y) → (−x, −y).

P(2, 5) → P′(−2, −5).

*Check:* a half-turn sends a point to the opposite side of the origin, the same distance away. P was 2 right and 5 up; P′ is 2 left and 5 down. Correct. (A direction was not needed because 180° clockwise and anticlockwise are the same.)

### Example 10 — Congruent or similar?

A triangle has sides 3 cm, 4 cm, 5 cm. A second triangle has sides 6 cm, 8 cm, 10 cm. Are they congruent, similar, or neither?

*Solution.* Each side of the second triangle is exactly **twice** the matching side of the first (3→6, 4→8, 5→10). The shape is the same but the size is different. The triangles are **similar** but **not congruent**.

### Example 11 — Identifying symmetry

How many lines of symmetry and what order of rotational symmetry does a rectangle (that is not a square) have?

*Solution.* A rectangle folds onto itself along the line through the midpoints of its two long sides, and along the line through the midpoints of its two short sides — so it has **2 lines of symmetry**. Turning it once around its centre, it looks the same after a half-turn (180°) and after a full turn — so its rotational symmetry is **order 2**.

### Example 12 — Using angle properties

An isosceles triangle has a top angle of 40°. Find the two equal base angles.

*Solution.* The three angles of a triangle add to 180°. The two base angles are equal; call each one x.

40 + x + x = 180
40 + 2x = 180
2x = 140
x = 70

Each base angle is **70°**. *Check:* 40 + 70 + 70 = 180. Correct.

## Real-Life Applications

- **Tessellations and tiling.** A *tessellation* is a pattern of identical shapes that fit together with no gaps and no overlaps, covering a surface completely. Floor tiles, brick walls, and honeycomb are tessellations. They are built by translating, reflecting, and rotating a single tile — and they only work because these transformations keep every copy congruent.
- **Jamaican craft and design.** The repeating motifs in woven straw baskets, the plaid of the bandana (madras) cloth, and carved patterns on wooden furniture all repeat a basic shape by sliding, flipping, and turning it.
- **Borders and logos.** A wallpaper border or a stamped pattern around a notebook is usually one shape translated again and again. Many company logos are designed using reflection so that the two halves balance.
- **Computer graphics and games.** When a character runs across the screen it is being translated; when it faces the other way it is reflected; when a spinning coin or steering wheel turns, it is rotated.
- **Nature.** A butterfly's wings show line symmetry; the petals of many flowers and the arms of a starfish show rotational symmetry.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing the parts of a vector.** The top number is *across*, the bottom is *up/down*. Writing $\binom{3}{4}$ when you mean "3 up, 4 right" is a common slip.
- **Getting signs wrong in reflections.** Reflecting in the **y-axis** changes the sign of **x** (not y); reflecting in the **x-axis** changes the sign of **y**. Many students swap these.
- **Forgetting to give a direction for a rotation.** A rotation of 90° must say *clockwise* or *anticlockwise* — these give different images. (Only 180° needs no direction.)
- **Thinking a reflection just "moves" a shape.** A reflection also **reverses the orientation**. If your image faces the same way as the object, you have probably done a translation by mistake.
- **Mixing up congruent and similar.** Congruent = same size *and* shape; similar = same shape, possibly different size.
- **Mismatching corners.** Always send A to A′, B to B′, C to C′ — in order. Joining the image points in a different order gives the wrong shape.
- **Counting a full turn as rotational symmetry.** Every shape matches itself after 360°. Order 1 means *no* real rotational symmetry.

## Helpful Tips

- Use **tracing paper** to check any transformation: trace the object, then slide, flip, or turn the tracing to test your image.
- For rotations, lightly mark the **centre** first and keep your pencil tip on it as you turn.
- Plot points **carefully and one at a time**, and label both object and image clearly.
- After any rigid transformation, do a quick **length check** on one side — object and image sides should be equal, since the shapes are congruent.
- To test for a **line of symmetry**, imagine folding the shape along the line: do the halves match exactly?
- Remember the memory hook: **T**ranslation = slide, **R**eflection = flip, **R**otation = turn.

## Chapter Review

In this chapter you studied three rigid transformations. A **translation** slides a shape by a vector, leaving its size, shape, and orientation unchanged. A **reflection** flips a shape over a mirror line, keeping size and shape the same but **reversing orientation**, with object and image points equidistant from the mirror. A **rotation** turns a shape through a stated angle and direction about a centre, again keeping size and shape unchanged. Because all three preserve size and shape, the object and image are always **congruent** — unlike **similar** shapes, which share a shape but may differ in size. You also learned to read the **angle, side, and symmetry properties** of common triangles (equilateral, isosceles, scalene, right-angled) and quadrilaterals (square, rectangle, rhombus, parallelogram, trapezium, kite), and to count their lines of symmetry and order of rotational symmetry. Most importantly, you can now carry out each transformation on a coordinate grid and **check** your work.

## Summary

- **Object** → transformation → **image** (image points are marked A′, B′, C′).
- **Translation** (slide): by a vector $\binom{a}{b}$; rule (x + a, y + b); same size, shape and orientation.
- **Reflection** (flip): over a mirror line; same size and shape but orientation reversed; points equidistant from the line.
- **Rotation** (turn): about a centre, through an angle and a direction; same size and shape.
- **Congruent**: same size and shape (always true for these three transformations). **Similar**: same shape, possibly different size.
- Triangle angles add to **180°**; quadrilateral angles add to **360°**.
- **Symmetry**: lines of symmetry (fold to match) and order of rotational symmetry (matches in one full turn).

## Key Takeaways

- The three rigid transformations — translation, reflection, rotation — never change a shape's size or shape, so object and image are **congruent**.
- A vector's **top number is across**, **bottom number is up/down**.
- **Reflection in the y-axis** changes the sign of x; **reflection in the x-axis** changes the sign of y.
- Only **reflection** reverses orientation.
- A rotation needs a **centre, an angle, and a direction** (except 180°).
- **Congruent** means same size and shape; **similar** means same shape, different size.
- Always **check** your image: corresponding sides should be equal in length.
