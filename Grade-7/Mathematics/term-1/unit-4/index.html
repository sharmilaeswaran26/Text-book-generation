Translations (the Slide)

A transformation moves a shape from one place to another on a flat surface, or plane. The shape we begin with is the object, and the shape we end with is the image. We label the corners of the image with a small dash, so the image of A is written A′ (read "A dash"). In this unit we meet three transformations that never change a shape's size or shape at all: the translation, the reflection, and the rotation.

A translation is a slide. Every single point of the shape moves the same distance in the same direction. Nothing turns and nothing flips. Picture pushing a domino across a table without spinning it — that is a translation.

Describing a Translation with a Vector

A translation is described by a column vector, written as two numbers stacked in brackets.
The top number tells you how far to move across — right if positive, left if negative.
The bottom number tells you how far to move up or down — up if positive, down if negative.
So (4, 3) written as a column means "4 right and 3 up", and (−2, −5) means "2 left and 5 down".

Translations come in three kinds: horizontal (only left or right), vertical (only up or down), and slant or diagonal (across and up/down together). To translate a point (x, y) by the vector (a, b), simply add: the image is (x + a, y + b). To translate a whole shape, apply this to every corner and join them in the same order.

Worked Example: Translating a Point

Translate the point A(2, 1) by the vector (4, 3). Add the vector to the coordinates: new x = 2 + 4 = 6, and new y = 1 + 3 = 4. So A′ = (6, 4). Check: from (2, 1) move 4 right to x = 6, then 3 up to y = 4. Correct.

Worked Example: Translating a Triangle

Triangle ABC has vertices A(1, 1), B(4, 1), C(1, 3). Translate it by (−3, 2), meaning 3 left and 2 up. Apply (x − 3, y + 2) to each vertex: A(1, 1) → A′(−2, 3); B(4, 1) → B′(1, 3); C(1, 3) → C′(−2, 5). Check: side AB ran 3 units long; side A′B′ also runs 3 units long, so the image is the same size as the object.

Figure 2

Triangle ABC and its image A′B′C′ after a translation by the vector (−3, 2). The image faces the same way as the object.

Object and Image: What Stays the Same?

  • The image is the same size and the same shape as the object — they are congruent.
  • The image has the same orientation: it faces exactly the same way.
  • Corresponding sides of the object and image are parallel.
  • The image is preserved because every point moves by the very same vector, so no distances change.

Practice

1. Translate the point (−1, 4) by the vector (5, −6).

2. Triangle PQR has vertices P(0, 0), Q(2, 0), R(0, 3). Translate it by (3, 1) and write the image coordinates.

3. A tile has its bottom-left corner at (0, 0). The next tile is an exact copy slid 2 units right. Write the vector and the new corner.

Reflections (the Flip)

A reflection flips a shape over a straight line called the mirror line (or line of reflection). The image appears on the opposite side of the mirror line, just as your reflection appears in a still pool of water. Reflection is the one transformation in this unit that reverses orientation: a shape that faces right will face left, and writing appears back-to-front.

Three Facts That Describe a Reflection

Every point of the object and its image are the same distance from the mirror line (they are equidistant from it).
The line joining a point to its image is perpendicular to the mirror line.
A point that already lies on the mirror line does not move.

Rules for Reflecting in the Axes

Reflecting in the y-axis (the vertical axis) keeps the y-coordinate and changes the sign of x: the point (x, y) maps to (−x, y). Reflecting in the x-axis (the horizontal axis) keeps the x-coordinate and changes the sign of y: the point (x, y) maps to (x, −y). For a vertical mirror line x = k, the rule is (2k − x, y); for a horizontal line y = k, it is (x, 2k − y).

Worked Example: Reflecting in the y-axis

Reflect triangle ABC with A(2, 1), B(5, 1), C(2, 4) in the y-axis. Use (x, y) → (−x, y): A(2, 1) → A′(−2, 1); B(5, 1) → B′(−5, 1); C(2, 4) → C′(−2, 4). Orientation check: reading A → B → C we go right then up; reading A′ → B′ → C′ we go left then up — the turning sense is reversed, exactly as a reflection should do.

Figure 3

Triangle ABC and its image A′B′C′ after a reflection in the y-axis. The image is the same size and shape, but its orientation is reversed.

Worked Example: Reflecting in a Mirror Line

Reflect the point R(1, 2) in the line x = 4. The y-coordinate stays the same. R is 4 − 1 = 3 units to the left of the line, so R′ is 3 units to the right: x = 4 + 3 = 7. Therefore R′ = (7, 2). Using the formula (2k − x, y) with k = 4 gives (8 − 1, 2) = (7, 2). Both methods agree.

Reasoning: Why Does Reflection Reverse Orientation?

  • The image is congruent to the object: same size and shape, since every distance to the mirror is preserved.
  • A reflection swaps left and right, so a clockwise journey round a shape becomes anticlockwise.
  • This is the same reason writing on a shirt appears back-to-front in a mirror.
  • If your image faces the same way as the object, you have done a slide, not a flip.

Practice

1. Reflect the point (5, −2) in the y-axis.

2. Reflect the point (−4, 5) in the x-axis.

3. Reflect triangle D(1, 2), E(3, 2), F(1, 5) in the x-axis and give the image coordinates.

Rotations (the Turn)

A rotation turns a shape about a fixed point called the centre of rotation. Picture the hands of a clock turning about the centre of the clock face: every point sweeps around that fixed centre, always staying the same distance from it.

Describing a Rotation Fully

The centre of rotation — the fixed point the shape turns around.
The angle of turn — for example 90° or 180°.
The direction — clockwise (the way a clock's hands move) or anticlockwise (the opposite way).
A turn of 180° is special: clockwise and anticlockwise give the same image, so no direction is needed.

Rules for Rotating About the Origin

For rotations about the origin (0, 0): a turn of 90° anticlockwise sends (x, y) to (−y, x); a turn of 90° clockwise sends (x, y) to (y, −x); and a turn of 180° sends (x, y) to (−x, −y). If you forget a rule, you can trace the object and centre onto tracing paper, hold the centre still, and turn the paper through the required angle.

Worked Example: Rotating a Point 90° Anticlockwise

Rotate A(3, 1) by 90° anticlockwise about the origin. Use (x, y) → (−y, x): A(3, 1) → A′(−1, 3). Check: A points mostly right and a little up; after a quarter-turn anticlockwise it should point mostly up and a little left — and (−1, 3) does exactly that.

Worked Example: Rotating a Triangle 90° Clockwise

Triangle ABC has vertices A(1, 1), B(4, 1), C(1, 3). Rotate it 90° clockwise about the origin using (x, y) → (y, −x): A(1, 1) → A′(1, −1); B(4, 1) → B′(1, −4); C(1, 3) → C′(3, −1). Check: side AB had length 3; side A′B′ runs from (1, −1) to (1, −4), also length 3, so the image is congruent to the object.

Figure 4

Triangle ABC and its image A′B′C′ after a rotation of 90° clockwise about the origin O.

Object and Image Under a Rotation

  • The image is the same size and shape as the object — they are congruent.
  • The shape is not flipped; it keeps its handedness but faces a new direction.
  • Every point stays the same distance from the centre as it turns.
  • For 180°, clockwise and anticlockwise give the same result.

Practice

1. Rotate the point (2, 3) by 90° anticlockwise about the origin.

2. Rotate the point (2, 5) by 180° about the origin.

3. A Ferris wheel seat is at (0, 6), with the centre at the origin. After a 90° clockwise turn, where is the seat?

Symmetry & Properties of Shapes

Because translations, reflections, and rotations never change a shape's size or shape, the object and image are always congruent — exactly the same size and the same shape. This is different from shapes that are similar, which have the same shape but may be a different size, like a small photograph and a large poster of the same picture. A useful way to remember it: congruent shapes are identical twins; similar shapes are a parent and child — the same family shape, but different sizes.

Line Symmetry and Rotational Symmetry

A shape has a line of symmetry if a straight line divides it into two halves that are mirror images — if you folded along that line, the halves would match exactly. A shape has rotational symmetry if it looks the same after being turned part-way around its centre. The order of rotational symmetry is the number of times it looks the same during one full 360° turn. A square, for example, looks the same four times, so it has order 4. Order 1 means a shape has no true rotational symmetry, because every shape matches itself after a full turn.

Worked Example: Using Angle Properties

An isosceles triangle has a top angle of 40°. Find the two equal base angles. The angles add to 180°, and the two base angles are equal, so 40 + x + x = 180, giving 2x = 140 and x = 70. Each base angle is 70°. Check: 40 + 70 + 70 = 180. Correct.

Real-Life Applications

  • Tessellations — patterns of identical shapes that fit with no gaps, built by sliding, flipping, and turning a single tile.
  • Jamaican craft — repeating motifs in straw baskets, bandana (madras) plaid, and carved furniture.
  • Computer graphics and games — characters are translated as they run, reflected as they turn around, and rotated as they spin.
  • Nature — a butterfly's wings show line symmetry; a starfish shows rotational symmetry.

Practice

1. A triangle has sides 3 cm, 4 cm, 5 cm; another has sides 6 cm, 8 cm, 10 cm. Are they congruent, similar, or neither?

2. How many lines of symmetry and what order of rotational symmetry does a rectangle (not a square) have?

3. One angle of a parallelogram is 110°. Find the other three angles.

Key Vocabulary

Geometry: the branch of mathematics that studies shapes, lines, angles, and space.
Transformation: a change in the position (or sometimes size) of a shape.
Object: the original shape, before the transformation.
Image: the new shape, after the transformation, marked with a dash such as A′.
Plane: a flat surface that extends in all directions, such as graph paper.
Translation: a transformation that slides every point the same distance in the same direction.
Reflection: a transformation that flips a shape over a mirror line.
Rotation: a transformation that turns a shape about a fixed point.
Mirror line (line of reflection): the line a shape is flipped over.
Centre of rotation: the fixed point a shape is turned about.
Vector: an instruction for a translation, showing movement across and up/down.
Angle: the amount of turn between two lines that meet, measured in degrees.
Congruent: exactly the same size and the same shape.
Similar: the same shape but not necessarily the same size.
Symmetry: a balanced, matching arrangement of a shape.
Line of symmetry: a line dividing a shape into two matching mirror-image halves.
Rotational symmetry: a shape has this if it looks the same as it turns part-way around its centre.
Triangles: three-sided shapes; their angles add to 180°.
Quadrilaterals: four-sided shapes; their angles add to 360°.
Line / segment: a straight path; a segment is the part between two endpoints.
Perpendicular: meeting at a right angle (90°).
Parallel: always the same distance apart and never meeting.
Bisector: a line that cuts something exactly in half; a perpendicular bisector cuts a segment in half at a right angle.
Orientation: the way a shape faces — whether it reads forwards or backwards.
