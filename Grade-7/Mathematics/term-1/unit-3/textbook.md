# Chapter 3: Solids — Faces, Edges, Vertices, Nets and Euler's Formula

## Introduction

Look around you right now. The box your morning cereal came in, a die from a board game, a tin of Milo, an ice-cream cone, a football, a juice carton — every one of these is a **solid**. A solid is a three-dimensional (3-D) object. Unlike a flat shape drawn on paper, a solid takes up space: it has length, width and height. You can hold it, turn it over and look at it from many sides.

In this chapter you will learn to look at solids the way a mathematician does. You will learn the proper names for the parts of a solid, how to sort solids into families, how to unfold a solid into a flat pattern called a **net**, and how to fold a net back up into a solid. Finally, you will discover a beautiful rule connecting the parts of a solid — a rule found by a mathematician named Leonhard Euler more than 250 years ago — and you will check that it works for yourself.

## Why This Topic Matters

Solids are everywhere in real life, and understanding them is genuinely useful.

- Every box, carton and packet in a supermarket started life as a flat sheet of cardboard cut into a **net** and folded up. People who design packaging need to understand nets so that boxes fold neatly with no gaps and no wasted material.
- Builders, architects and engineers describe buildings, water tanks and pipes using the language of solids — cuboids, prisms, cylinders and pyramids.
- Artists, animators and video-game designers build everything on screen out of 3-D shapes.
- Learning to count faces, edges and vertices carefully trains you to be organised and accurate — skills that help in all of mathematics.

By the end of this chapter you will be able to look at a flat cardboard pattern and say exactly which solid it makes, and look at a solid and describe it precisely.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

1. Explain what a solid is and name the families of solids (cube, cuboid, prism, pyramid, cylinder, cone, sphere).

4. Construct (fold up) a solid from its net.

## Concept Explanations

### 1. What is a solid?

A **solid** is any object that takes up space in three directions: length, width and height. This is why we call solids **three-dimensional**, or **3-D** for short. A drawing on paper is **two-dimensional** (2-D) — it has only length and width — but the real object it represents is a solid.

Every ordinary solid is built from three kinds of part.

- A **face** is a surface of the solid. On a box, each flat side is a face. Most solids we study have flat faces, but a cylinder, cone and sphere have **curved** surfaces too.
- An **edge** is the straight line where two faces meet. Run your finger along the sharp line at the side of a box — that is an edge.
- A **vertex** is a corner, the point where edges come together. The plural of vertex is **vertices**. The pointed corners of a die are its vertices.

*Figure 1: A cube with one face shaded, one edge marked with a bold line, and one vertex circled, to show the difference between a face, an edge and a vertex.*

### 2. Families of solids

We sort solids into families by looking at their faces.

**Polyhedra (flat-faced solids).** A **polyhedron** is a solid whose faces are *all* flat polygons. There are no curved surfaces, no round parts. Cubes, cuboids, prisms and pyramids are all polyhedra. The word comes from Greek: *poly* means "many" and *hedron* means "face", so a polyhedron is a "many-faced" solid.

- A **cube** has 6 faces, and every face is an identical square. A game die is a perfect cube.
- A **cuboid** (also called a rectangular prism) is box-shaped. It has 6 faces, and each face is a rectangle. Opposite faces are equal. A cereal box and a brick are cuboids. A cube is a special cuboid in which all the faces happen to be squares.
- A **prism** has two identical end faces that are parallel to each other, joined by rectangles. The prism is named after the shape of its ends. A **triangular prism** has two triangular ends (think of a Toblerone bar or a tent); a **rectangular prism** is just a cuboid. If you slice a prism straight across, the cut is always the same shape as the ends.
- A **pyramid** has one flat base and triangular faces that rise to meet at a single point called the **apex**. A pyramid is named after the shape of its base. A **square pyramid** has a square base (like the famous Pyramids of Egypt); a **triangular pyramid** has a triangular base.

**Curved solids (not polyhedra).** Some solids have curved surfaces, so they are *not* polyhedra.

- A **cylinder** has two flat circular ends joined by one curved surface. A tin of Milo and a battery are cylinders.
- A **cone** has one flat circular base and one curved surface narrowing to a point. An ice-cream cone is a cone.
- A **sphere** is perfectly round, like a football or a marble. It has one curved surface, no flat faces, no edges and no vertices.

*Figure 2: A row of seven solids labelled cube, cuboid, triangular prism, square pyramid, cylinder, cone and sphere.*

> **Why cylinders, cones and spheres are special:** because they have curved surfaces, they are not polyhedra. This matters later, because Euler's Formula works only for polyhedra (solids with flat faces). Keep cylinders, cones and spheres in a separate group in your mind.

### 3. Faces, edges and vertices in detail

Counting the parts of a solid is the heart of this chapter, so let us be careful and systematic.

Take a **cube**. Hold a die.
- **Faces:** top, bottom, front, back, left, right — that is **6 faces**, each a square.
- **Vertices:** the corners. There are 4 on the top and 4 on the bottom — that is **8 vertices**.
- **Edges:** the sharp lines. There are 4 around the top, 4 around the bottom, and 4 going up the sides — that is **12 edges**.

So for a cube: F = 6, V = 8, E = 12. We will use these numbers again very soon.

### 4. Nets of solids

A **net** is a flat pattern that you can fold up to make a solid. If you carefully cut open a cardboard box along its edges and flatten it out, the flat shape you get is a net of that box.

Nets are the link between flat (2-D) shapes and solids (3-D). Every polyhedron can be unfolded into one or more nets, and every correct net folds back up into exactly one solid. Packaging designers work backwards: they design the net first, print it flat, then fold it into the box.

*Figure 3: The net of a cube — six squares arranged in a cross (a vertical strip of four squares with one square attached to each side of the second square).*

A cube can be unfolded in several different ways, so it has more than one net. The "cross" pattern in Figure 3 is the most familiar, but there are others. What every correct net of a cube has in common is that it is made of exactly **six squares** that fold up without overlapping and without leaving a gap.

*Figure 4: The net of a cuboid — six rectangles, with opposite rectangles equal in size.*

*Figure 5: The net of a triangular prism — two triangles and three rectangles.*

*Figure 6: The net of a square pyramid — one square in the centre with a triangle attached to each of its four sides.*

### 5. Euler's Formula

Here is the exciting part. More than 250 years ago, a Swiss mathematician named **Leonhard Euler** (his name is said "Oiler") noticed that for every polyhedron, the number of faces, vertices and edges are always linked by the same rule:

> **F + V − E = 2**

In words: the number of **faces** plus the number of **vertices**, minus the number of **edges**, always equals **2**. This is the same as saying **F + V = E + 2** (faces plus vertices equals edges plus two). It works for every polyhedron — every cube, every cuboid, every prism and every pyramid — no matter how big or small.

Let us test it on the cube, where F = 6, V = 8, E = 12:

$$F + V - E = 6 + 8 - 12 = 14 - 12 = 2 \checkmark$$

It works. In the worked examples you will check it again and again, and you will see it never fails for a polyhedron.

## Mathematical Reasoning

### Why does a net fold up into its solid?

A net is simply the surface of a solid, opened out flat. Imagine peeling an orange in one piece and pressing the peel onto a table — the flat pattern *is* the surface of the orange, just unfolded. In the same way, a net contains exactly the faces of the solid, joined along the edges where they will fold. When you fold along those lines, each face swings up into its proper position and the free edges meet. That is why a net made of six squares folds into a cube: the six squares are exactly the six faces, and the folds put them in the right places.

This is also why an *incorrect* net does not work. If you arrange the six squares so that, when folded, two of them would land on top of each other while another spot is left open, the pattern cannot close into a cube. A correct net must have every face in a position that folds neatly to cover the solid with no overlaps and no gaps.

### The pattern behind Euler's Formula

Euler's Formula is not magic — it is a pattern you can discover yourself by collecting data. The mathematician's method is: gather examples, organise them in a table, and look for what stays the same. When you fill in the faces, vertices and edges for several different polyhedra and then work out F + V − E each time, you will always get 2. Spotting that "always 2" is exactly what Euler did. Discovering a rule that holds for *every* case, by reasoning from examples, is called making a **generalisation**, and it is one of the most powerful things a mathematician can do.

Notice that the formula does **not** work for curved solids. A cylinder, for instance, has 2 flat faces and 1 curved surface, and the simple count breaks down because it is not a polyhedron. This is why we are careful to apply Euler's Formula only to flat-faced solids.

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### How to draw the net of a solid

1. Pick one face to be the "base" and draw it.
2. Think about each face that touches the base. Draw each one attached to the correct side of the base, as if it has been folded down flat.
3. Continue adding the remaining faces, each joined along the edge it shares with a face already drawn.
4. Check your count: the net must contain exactly as many faces as the solid (a cube needs 6 squares; a square pyramid needs 1 square and 4 triangles).
5. Mentally fold it back up. If every face reaches its place with no overlap and no gap, your net is correct.

### How to fold a net into a solid

1. Cut out the net along its outer edges.
2. Score and fold gently along every internal line (the lines between faces). Folding the same way each time keeps the solid neat.
3. Bring the free edges together so that matching edges meet.
4. Tape or glue the edges. The faces should now enclose a space — you have built the solid.

### How to count F, V and E systematically

Counting carefully, in a fixed order, prevents mistakes.

1. **Faces (F):** count the flat surfaces. A good trick is "top + bottom + sides". For a cuboid that is 1 + 1 + 4 = 6.
2. **Vertices (V):** count the corners. Counting the top corners and the bottom corners separately helps. For a cuboid that is 4 + 4 = 8.
3. **Edges (E):** count the lines where faces meet. Count the edges around the top, around the bottom, and the upright edges joining them. For a cuboid that is 4 + 4 + 4 = 12.
4. **Check with Euler:** work out F + V − E. If the solid is a polyhedron, the answer should be 2. If it is not 2, recount.

## Fully Worked Examples

### Example 1 — The cube

Count the faces, vertices and edges of a cube and check Euler's Formula.

- Faces: top, bottom and 4 sides → **F = 6**
- Vertices: 4 on top, 4 on the bottom → **V = 8**
- Edges: 4 on top, 4 on the bottom, 4 upright → **E = 12**
- Euler check: F + V − E = 6 + 8 − 12 = **2** ✓

### Example 2 — The cuboid

A cereal box is a cuboid. Count its parts and check Euler.

- Faces: top, bottom and 4 sides → **F = 6**
- Vertices: 4 on top, 4 on the bottom → **V = 8**
- Edges: 4 top, 4 bottom, 4 upright → **E = 12**
- Euler check: 6 + 8 − 12 = **2** ✓

(A cuboid has the same counts as a cube. That makes sense, because a cube is just a cuboid whose faces are all squares.)

### Example 3 — The triangular prism

A triangular prism (like a Toblerone bar) has two triangular ends joined by three rectangles.

- Faces: 2 triangular ends + 3 rectangles → **F = 5**
- Vertices: 3 corners on one triangular end, 3 on the other → **V = 6**
- Edges: 3 around one triangle, 3 around the other, 3 joining them → **E = 9**
- Euler check: F + V − E = 5 + 6 − 9 = 11 − 9 = **2** ✓

### Example 4 — The square pyramid

A square pyramid has a square base and four triangular faces meeting at an apex.

- Faces: 1 square base + 4 triangles → **F = 5**
- Vertices: 4 around the base + 1 apex on top → **V = 5**
- Edges: 4 around the base + 4 rising to the apex → **E = 8**
- Euler check: F + V − E = 5 + 5 − 8 = 10 − 8 = **2** ✓

### Example 5 — The triangular pyramid (tetrahedron)

A triangular pyramid has a triangular base and three triangular faces, four triangles in all.

- Faces: 1 base + 3 sides → **F = 4**
- Vertices: 3 around the base + 1 apex → **V = 4**
- Edges: 3 around the base + 3 rising to the apex → **E = 6**
- Euler check: F + V − E = 4 + 4 − 6 = 8 − 6 = **2** ✓

### The Faces–Vertices–Edges table

Collect the results from the examples into one table. This is the table you complete to discover Euler's Formula.

| Solid | Faces (F) | Vertices (V) | Edges (E) | F + V | F + V − E |
|-------|:---------:|:------------:|:---------:|:-----:|:---------:|
| Cube | 6 | 8 | 12 | 14 | **2** |
| Cuboid | 6 | 8 | 12 | 14 | **2** |
| Triangular prism | 5 | 6 | 9 | 11 | **2** |
| Square pyramid | 5 | 5 | 8 | 10 | **2** |
| Triangular pyramid (tetrahedron) | 4 | 4 | 6 | 8 | **2** |

Look down the last column: every single answer is **2**. That is Euler's Formula in action. Notice also that in the "F + V" column the value is always exactly 2 more than the number of edges — another way of seeing the same rule.

### Example 6 — Using Euler to find a missing number

A polyhedron has F = 6 faces and E = 12 edges. How many vertices does it have?

Use Euler's Formula F + V − E = 2 and substitute the known values:

$$6 + V - 12 = 2$$
$$V - 6 = 2$$
$$V = 8$$

The solid has **8 vertices**. (These numbers match a cube or cuboid.)

### Example 7 — Naming a solid from its net

A net is made of one square with a triangle attached to each of its four sides. Which solid does it make?

The square is the base, and the four triangles fold up to meet at a point above it. This is the net of a **square pyramid**.

## Real-Life Applications

**Packaging.** Almost every box on a shop shelf is a net that has been printed flat on cardboard, cut out, folded and glued. A juice carton, a tube of toothpaste box and a shoebox are all designed as nets first. Designers must make the net fold neatly with flaps that overlap to be glued, and they try to fit as many nets as possible onto one sheet of cardboard so that little is wasted. This saves money and material.

**Building and storage.** A water tank may be a cylinder; a storage room is a cuboid; the roof of a house may be shaped like a prism or a pyramid. Knowing how many faces and edges a shape has helps in working out how much material is needed to build or cover it.

**Design and games.** Dice are cubes (and some games use other polyhedra, such as the tetrahedron). Designers of toys, buildings and computer games all build their creations out of the families of solids you have studied here.

## Common Mistakes

> **Watch out for these errors:**
>
> - **Confusing edges and vertices.** An edge is a *line* where two faces meet; a vertex is a *corner point*. Do not count them as the same thing.
> - **Losing count of hidden parts.** When you draw a solid on paper, some faces, edges and vertices are at the back and hard to see. Count carefully — hold a real object, or count the top, the bottom and the sides separately.
> - **Applying Euler's Formula to curved solids.** Euler's Formula works only for polyhedra (flat faces). It does not apply to cylinders, cones or spheres.
> - **Thinking a solid has only one net.** Many solids, such as the cube, can be unfolded in several different ways, so they have several correct nets.
> - **Drawing a net with the wrong number of faces.** A cube net must have six squares; a square pyramid net must have one square and four triangles. Always check the count.

## Helpful Tips

> **Tips for success:**
>
> - Whenever you can, hold the real solid and turn it as you count. It is far easier than counting from a flat drawing.
> - Count in a fixed order every time — faces, then vertices, then edges — and break each count into "top, bottom, sides".
> - After counting, always do the Euler check (F + V − E). If you do not get 2 for a polyhedron, you have miscounted, so recount.
> - To test a net, mentally fold it up face by face and ask: does every face reach its place with no overlap and no gap?
> - Make a model. Cutting and folding a real net teaches you more than any number of pictures.

## Chapter Review

In this chapter you learned that a **solid** is a 3-D object with length, width and height, and that every solid is built from **faces** (surfaces), **edges** (lines where faces meet) and **vertices** (corner points). You learned to sort solids into families: the flat-faced **polyhedra** (cube, cuboid, prism, pyramid) and the curved solids (cylinder, cone, sphere), which are not polyhedra. ## Summary

- A **solid** is three-dimensional: it has length, width and height.
- **Face** = a surface; **edge** = a line where two faces meet; **vertex** = a corner point (plural: vertices).
- **Polyhedra** have only flat faces (cube, cuboid, prism, pyramid). **Cylinders, cones and spheres** have curved surfaces and are not polyhedra.
- A **net** is a flat pattern that folds into a solid; a solid may have several different nets.
- **Euler's Formula** for any polyhedron: **F + V − E = 2** (equivalently F + V = E + 2).
- Cube and cuboid: F = 6, V = 8, E = 12. Triangular prism: F = 5, V = 6, E = 9. Square pyramid: F = 5, V = 5, E = 8. Each gives F + V − E = 2.

## Key Takeaways

1. Every solid can be described precisely by counting its faces, edges and vertices.
2. Count systematically — top, bottom, sides — and you will not lose track of hidden parts.
3. A net is the bridge between flat shapes and solids; packaging and design depend on it.
4. Euler's Formula, F + V − E = 2, is a generalisation that holds for every polyhedron, and it is a powerful check on your counting.
5. Mathematicians discover rules like Euler's by collecting examples in a table and looking for the pattern that stays the same.
