Solids: Faces, Edges and Vertices

Look around you. A cereal box, a die from a board game, a tin of Milo, an ice-cream cone, a football — every one of these is a solid. A solid is a three-dimensional (3-D) object: it has length, width and height, and it takes up space. Unlike a flat shape drawn on paper, you can hold a solid and turn it over to look at it from many sides.

Every ordinary solid is built from three kinds of part. A face is a surface of the solid. An edge is the straight line where two faces meet. A vertex is a corner, the point where edges come together. The plural of vertex is vertices.

The Three Parts of a Solid

Face: a flat (or curved) surface of a solid. A cube has 6 flat square faces.
Edge: a line where two faces meet. Run your finger along the sharp line at the side of a box.
Vertex (plural vertices): a corner point where edges meet. The pointed corners of a die are its vertices.

Families of Solids

We sort solids into families by looking at their faces. A polyhedron is a solid whose faces are all flat polygons — no curved surfaces. Cubes, cuboids, prisms and pyramids are all polyhedra.

The Solid Families

Cube: 6 equal square faces (a game die).
Cuboid: box-shaped, 6 rectangular faces (a cereal box, a brick). A cube is a special cuboid.
Prism: two identical parallel ends joined by rectangles (a Toblerone bar is a triangular prism).
Pyramid: a flat base with triangular faces meeting at an apex (the Pyramids of Egypt).
Cylinder: two circular ends joined by a curved surface (a tin of Milo). Not a polyhedron.
Cone: a circular base narrowing to a point (an ice-cream cone). Not a polyhedron.
Sphere: perfectly round (a football). No flat faces, edges or vertices. Not a polyhedron.

Figure 2

A row of seven solids labelled cube, cuboid, triangular prism, square pyramid, cylinder, cone and sphere.

Counting the Parts of a Cube

Counting carefully, in a fixed order, prevents mistakes. Hold a die and count.

Faces: top, bottom, front, back, left, right — that is 6 faces, each a square.
Vertices: 4 corners on the top and 4 on the bottom — that is 8 vertices.
Edges: 4 around the top, 4 around the bottom, and 4 going up the sides — that is 12 edges.

So for a cube: F = 6, V = 8, E = 12. We will use these numbers again in the next section.

Helpful Tips

  • Whenever you can, hold the real solid and turn it as you count.
  • Count in a fixed order every time: faces, then vertices, then edges.
  • Break each count into "top, bottom, sides" so you do not miss hidden parts.
  • An edge is a line; a vertex is a corner point — do not confuse them.

Practice

Find a real box (a cuboid) at home. Count its faces, vertices and edges. Then find a tin (a cylinder) and decide: is it a polyhedron? Why or why not?

Euler's Formula

More than 250 years ago, a Swiss mathematician named Leonhard Euler (his name is said "Oiler") noticed that for every polyhedron the number of faces, vertices and edges are always linked by the same rule.

Euler's Formula

F + V − E = 2
F is the number of faces.
V is the number of vertices.
E is the number of edges.
In words: faces plus vertices, minus edges, always equals 2. This is the same as F + V = E + 2.
Important: Euler's Formula works only for polyhedra (flat-faced solids). It does not apply to cylinders, cones or spheres.

The Reasoning Behind the Formula

  • Euler's Formula is not magic — it is a pattern you can discover by collecting data.
  • Fill in F, V and E for several polyhedra, work out F + V − E each time, and you always get 2.
  • Discovering a rule that holds for every case is called making a generalisation.
  • The formula fails for curved solids, which is why we apply it only to polyhedra.

Worked Example: Checking the Cube

For a cube, F = 6, V = 8 and E = 12. Substitute into the formula:

F + V − E = 6 + 8 − 12 = 14 − 12 = 2. It works.

Worked Example: Finding a Missing Number

A polyhedron has F = 6 faces and E = 12 edges. How many vertices does it have?

Use F + V − E = 2: 6 + V − 12 = 2, so V − 6 = 2, which gives V = 8. (These numbers match a cube or cuboid.)

Common Mistake

Do not apply Euler's Formula to a cylinder, cone or sphere. These have curved surfaces, so they are not polyhedra and the simple count of F, V and E does not give 2.

Practice

A triangular prism has F = 5 and E = 9. Use Euler's Formula to find the number of vertices, then check your answer against the table above.

Nets of Solids

A net is a flat pattern that you can fold up to make a solid. If you carefully cut open a cardboard box along its edges and flatten it out, the flat shape you get is a net of that box. Nets are the link between flat (2-D) shapes and solids (3-D).

Every polyhedron can be unfolded into one or more nets, and every correct net folds back up into exactly one solid. Packaging designers work backwards: they design the net first, print it flat, then fold it into the box.

Why a Net Folds Into Its Solid

  • A net is simply the surface of a solid, opened out flat — like an orange peel pressed onto a table.
  • The net contains exactly the faces of the solid, joined along the edges where they fold.
  • When you fold along those lines, each face swings into place and the free edges meet.
  • A correct net folds up with no overlaps and no gaps.

Figure 3

The net of a cube — six squares arranged in a cross (a vertical strip of four squares with one square attached to each side of the second square).

A cube can be unfolded in several different ways, so it has more than one net. What every correct net of a cube has in common is that it is made of exactly six squares that fold up without overlapping and without leaving a gap.

Figure 4

The net of a cuboid — six rectangles, with opposite rectangles equal in size.

Figure 5

The net of a triangular prism — two triangles and three rectangles.

Figure 6

The net of a square pyramid — one square in the centre with a triangle attached to each of its four sides.

How to Draw a Net

1. Pick one face to be the base and draw it.
2. Draw each face that touches the base attached to the correct side, as if folded down flat.
3. Add the remaining faces, each joined along the edge it shares with a face already drawn.
4. Check the count: a cube needs 6 squares; a square pyramid needs 1 square and 4 triangles.
5. Mentally fold it back up. If every face reaches its place with no overlap and no gap, the net is correct.

How to Fold a Net Into a Solid

1. Cut out the net along its outer edges.
2. Fold gently along every internal line (the lines between faces).
3. Bring the free edges together so that matching edges meet.
4. Tape or glue the edges. The faces now enclose a space — you have built the solid.

Worked Example: Naming a Solid From Its Net

A net is made of one square with a triangle attached to each of its four sides. The square is the base, and the four triangles fold up to meet at a point above it. This is the net of a square pyramid.

Real-Life Application: Packaging

Almost every box on a shop shelf is a net printed flat on cardboard, then cut out, folded and glued. Designers fit as many nets as possible onto one sheet so that little material is wasted — this saves money.

Practice

Carefully open out an empty matchbox or juice carton along its edges and flatten it. Draw the net you get, then count the faces. Can you fold it back into the box?

Key Vocabulary

Solid: a three-dimensional object that has length, width and height and takes up space. Example: a box of matches, a football.
Face: a flat (or curved) surface of a solid. Example: a cube has 6 flat square faces.
Edge: a line where two faces meet. Example: the straight line along the side of a box.
Vertex (plural vertices): a corner point where edges meet. Example: the pointed corner of a die.
Polyhedron: a solid whose faces are all flat polygons (no curved surfaces). Example: cube, cuboid, prism, pyramid.
Cube: a solid with 6 equal square faces. Example: a game die, a sugar cube.
Cuboid: a box-shaped solid with 6 rectangular faces. Example: a cereal box, a brick.
Prism: a solid with two identical, parallel end faces joined by rectangles. Example: a Toblerone bar (triangular prism).
Pyramid: a solid with a flat base and triangular faces meeting at one apex. Example: the Pyramids of Egypt.
Cylinder: a solid with two circular ends joined by a curved surface. Example: a tin of Milo, a battery.
Cone: a solid with one circular base narrowing to a point. Example: an ice-cream cone.
Sphere: a perfectly round solid; every point on its surface is the same distance from the centre. Example: a football, a marble.
Net: a flat pattern that folds up to make a solid. Example: six squares fold into a cube.
Apex: the single point at the top of a pyramid where the triangular faces meet.
Euler's Formula: the rule for polyhedra, F + V − E = 2. Example: cube: 6 + 8 − 12 = 2.
Generalisation: a rule found to hold for every case, discovered by reasoning from examples.
