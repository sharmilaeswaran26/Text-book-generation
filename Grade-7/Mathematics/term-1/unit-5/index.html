Instruments & Circles

A construction is an accurate drawing made using only two basic tools: a ruler (used as a straight-edge) and a pair of compasses. We do not guess where the lines go and we do not estimate by eye. Instead we follow an exact procedure that gives a correct figure every time. This is the same careful drawing that architects, builders, and engineers use when they design houses, bridges, and machines.

What You Will Learn in This Section

  • Name the geometric instruments and use each one correctly and safely.
  • Construct a circle of a given radius using a pair of compasses.
  • Form the habit of leaving construction arcs visible as proof of your method.

Lesson 1: The Geometric Instruments

The ruler (straight-edge) does two jobs. As a measuring tool it gives length in centimetres and millimetres; as a straight-edge it lets you draw a straight line between two points. In a pure construction we use it only as a straight-edge, except to set the compasses or to check the answer at the end. Remember there are 10 mm in 1 cm, so 4.5 cm is the same as 45 mm.

The pair of compasses is the most important construction tool. One arm has a sharp point and the other holds a pencil; the gap between them sets the radius. Keep the pencil sharp, press lightly, and turn the compasses by the handle at the top so the gap does not change. Once a gap is set, it stays the same until you change it — this is how compasses copy a length perfectly from one place to another.

The protractor is a semicircular instrument marked from 0° to 180°. We use it mainly to check our right angles and to measure angles, not to make the figure.

The set square is a flat plastic triangle containing a right angle. It is a quick, practical way to draw perpendicular and parallel lines. We learn the compasses-only methods first because they work even when no set square is available.

Safety First

Handle the compasses carefully: the point is sharp. Never wave it about, never point it at another student, and store it pointing downward in your geometry set.

Lesson 2: Constructing a Circle of a Given Radius

A circle is the set of all points that are exactly the same distance from a fixed centre. That fixed distance is the radius. Because the compasses keep a constant gap as they turn, they draw a circle naturally. To construct a circle of radius 4 cm:

  1. Use the ruler to open the compasses to a gap of 4 cm (40 mm).
  2. Mark and label the centre point O.
  3. Place the sharp point firmly on O.
  4. Hold the compasses by the handle and turn the pencil all the way around in one smooth movement.
  5. Label the circle. Check: measure from O to any point on the curve — it should equal the radius.

Figure 2

A circle of radius 4 cm — the compass point fixed at centre O, and the pencil sweeping a full curve, every point 4 cm from O.

Helpful Tip

  • Sharpen your pencil before you begin, work on a flat surface, and steady the paper with your free hand so the compass point does not slip.

Practice

1. Construct a circle of radius 5 cm. Label the centre O and check the radius in several directions with your ruler.

2. Construct a circle of radius 3.5 cm inside your first circle, using the same centre. (Two circles with the same centre are called concentric.)

3. Name the four geometric instruments and state one job each one does.

Segments & Bisectors

A line segment is a straight piece of line with two endpoints and a definite length, such as a segment AB of length 6 cm. In this section you will draw a segment, copy a segment with compasses only, and construct the perpendicular bisector — the line that cuts a segment exactly in half at a right angle.

Lesson 3: Drawing and Copying a Line Segment

To draw a segment of a given length, use the ruler to measure and mark, then join the marks. To copy a segment whose length you are not told, use the compasses: open them to match the given segment exactly, then move them — without changing the gap — to a new line and mark off the same length. This shows the real power of the compasses; they transfer a length perfectly even when you never measure it in numbers.

To copy segment AB onto a new line:

  1. Draw a new straight line (a ray) and mark a starting point P on it.
  2. Place the compass point on A and open the pencil to reach exactly to B. Do not change this gap.
  3. Move the compasses to P, place the point on P, and draw a small arc that crosses the new line. Label the crossing point Q.
  4. PQ is now equal in length to AB. Check by measuring both with the ruler.

Figure 3

Copying segment AB onto a ray from P — compasses opened to the length of AB, then the point placed at P to mark off PQ equal to AB.

Lesson 4: The Perpendicular Bisector

The perpendicular bisector of a segment AB does two things at once: it passes through the exact midpoint of AB (so it bisects it), and it crosses AB at a right angle (so it is perpendicular).

To construct the perpendicular bisector of AB:

  1. Draw segment AB.
  2. Open the compasses to more than half the length of AB. Keep this gap for the whole construction.
  3. Place the point on A and draw an arc above AB and an arc below AB.
  4. Without changing the gap, place the point on B and draw an arc above and an arc below, crossing the first two arcs. Label the upper crossing point C and the lower crossing point D.
  5. Use the ruler to draw the straight line through C and D.
  6. The line CD is the perpendicular bisector. Where it crosses AB is the midpoint M. Check: AM and MB are equal, and angle AMC measured with a protractor is 90°.

Figure 4

The perpendicular bisector of AB — equal arcs from A and from B meet at points C above and D below; the line CD crosses AB at right angles through its midpoint M.

Why This Construction Works

  • An arc drawn from A puts every point on it the same distance from A; an arc of the same gap from B puts every point the same distance from B. Where they cross, the point is the same distance from A and from B — it is equidistant.
  • Every point equidistant from A and B lies on the perpendicular bisector of AB, so the line through the two crossing points is the perpendicular bisector.
  • Join the four arc-ends and all four sides are equal, forming a rhombus. The diagonals of a rhombus always cross at right angles and cut each other in half — and AB and CD are those diagonals.
  • This is also why the gap must be more than half of AB: if it is too small, the arcs never reach far enough to cross.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Rubbing out the arcs: the arcs are the proof of your method — leave them faint but visible.
Changing the gap halfway: when a step says "same gap," the gap must not change; hold the compasses by the handle.
Opening too small: for a perpendicular bisector always open to more than half the segment, or the arcs will not cross.

Practice

1. Draw a segment AB = 7 cm and construct its perpendicular bisector. Mark the midpoint M and check that AM = MB.

2. Copy a 5.5 cm segment onto a new line using compasses only, then check with a ruler.

3. Draw a strip of paper, then find its exact midpoint by construction without measuring its length.

Perpendicular & Parallel Lines

A perpendicular is a line that meets another at a right angle (90°). Parallel lines stay exactly the same distance apart and never meet, no matter how far they are extended. The rails of a train track are parallel; the corners of a building are perpendicular. In this section you will construct both, using only a ruler and compasses.

Lesson 5: Perpendicular from a Point on a Line

When the perpendicular must pass through a point P that lies on the line:

  1. With the compass point on P, draw an arc that cuts the line on both sides of P. Label these points X and Y, so that PX = PY.
  2. Open the compasses wider, to more than the distance PX.
  3. With the point on X draw an arc above the line; with the same gap and the point on Y draw another arc crossing it. Label the crossing point Z.
  4. Draw the straight line through P and Z. PZ is perpendicular to the line at P. Check with a protractor — it should read 90°.

Figure 5

Perpendicular erected at point P on a line — equal points X and Y, arcs meeting at Z, line PZ at right angles.

Lesson 6: Perpendicular from a Point off the Line

When the point P lies off the line (away from it) — for example, when drawing the height of a triangle from a vertex to the opposite side:

  1. With the compass point on P, draw an arc that cuts the line at two points. Label them X and Y.
  2. With the point on X and a gap of more than half XY, draw an arc on the far side of the line from P.
  3. With the same gap and the point on Y, draw another arc crossing it. Label the crossing point W.
  4. Draw the straight line through P and W. This line is perpendicular to the given line. Check with a protractor.

Figure 6

Perpendicular dropped from a point P above a line — arc from P cuts the line at X and Y, arcs from X and Y meet at W, line PW at right angles.

Lesson 7: Constructing Parallel Lines

Two lines are parallel if they slope the same way, which we guarantee by copying an angle. To construct a line through a point P parallel to a given line:

  1. Draw any straight line (a transversal) through P that crosses the given line at a point Q.
  2. With the compass point on Q, draw an arc that crosses both the given line and the transversal. Keep this gap.
  3. With the same gap and the point on P, draw a matching arc crossing the transversal above P.
  4. Open the compasses to measure the width of the arc you drew at Q (where it crossed the two lines).
  5. With that gap, mark across the P-arc to locate a point R.
  6. Draw the straight line through P and R. It is parallel to the given line, because you have copied the angle at Q to P (equal corresponding angles). Check by measuring the gap between the lines at each end — it should be the same.

Figure 7

A line through P constructed parallel to a given line by copying the angle at Q — equal corresponding angles show the lines are parallel.

Reasoning and Tips

  • "Equal arcs make equal distances" is the single idea behind bisectors, perpendiculars, and parallels — once you see it, the steps make sense.
  • To make a 45° angle, first construct a 90° angle and then bisect it.
  • Always finish with a check: equal lengths with the ruler, right angles with the protractor.

Practice

1. Draw a line, mark a point P on it, and construct the perpendicular to the line at P.

2. Draw a line and mark a point P about 4 cm above it; construct the perpendicular from P to the line.

3. Construct a line parallel to a given line through a point 3 cm away, then check the distance is equal at both ends.

Key Vocabulary

Construction Terms

Construction: An accurate drawing made using only a ruler (straight-edge) and a pair of compasses.
Ruler / straight-edge: An instrument used to draw straight lines and to measure length.
Pair of compasses: An instrument with a sharp point on one arm and a pencil on the other, used to draw circles and arcs.
Protractor: An instrument used to measure and draw angles, marked from 0° to 180°.
Set square: A flat triangular instrument with a built-in right angle, used to draw perpendicular and parallel lines.
Arc: A part of a circle — a curved line drawn by the compasses.

Shape and Line Terms

Geometry: The branch of mathematics that studies shape, space, and position.
Plane: A flat surface that extends in all directions, such as the page you draw on.
Circle: The set of all points that are the same distance (the radius) from a fixed centre.
Radius: The distance from the centre of a circle to any point on the circle.
Line segment: A straight line with two endpoints and a fixed length.
Angle: The amount of turn between two lines that meet at a point, measured in degrees.
Triangles: Three-sided plane shapes.
Quadrilaterals: Four-sided plane shapes.

Relationship Terms

Perpendicular: Two lines that meet at a right angle (90°).
Parallel: Two lines that stay the same distance apart and never meet.
Bisector: A line that cuts a segment or an angle into two equal parts.
Perpendicular bisector: A line that cuts a segment into two equal parts and meets it at a right angle.
Equidistant: The same distance from two or more points.

2. Any two, for example: builders use it for square corners on houses; engineers use it so holes and edges line up on machine parts.

3. The arcs are the proof that the figure was constructed correctly, not estimated by eye; an examiner wants to see them.

4. Perpendicular lines meet at a right angle; parallel lines stay the same distance apart and never meet.

5. Measure the angle with a protractor; it should read exactly 90°.

Part E — Construct These

1. Circle correct if every point measures 5 cm from O.

2. Bisector correct if AM = MB = 3.5 cm and angle at M = 90°, with arcs visible.

3. Perpendicular at P correct if the protractor reads 90°.

4. Perpendicular from P correct if PW meets the line at 90°.

5. Parallel line correct if the distance between the two lines is equal at both ends.

Challenge Problems

2. The distance between the two lines is the same at every point; this proves the two lines are parallel.

3. Construct a 90° angle, then bisect it to make 45°.

4. Draw the radius to P, then construct the perpendicular to that radius at P; that perpendicular is the tangent.
