# Chapter 5: Geometric Construction

## Introduction

Mathematics is not only about numbers and calculations. It is also about shape, space, and the careful drawing of figures that must be exactly right. In this chapter you will learn how to *construct* geometric figures. A **construction** is a drawing made using only two basic tools: a **ruler** (used as a straight-edge) and a **pair of compasses**. Sometimes we also use a protractor and a set square to check our work or to make it easier.

When you construct a figure, you do not guess where the lines should go and you do not estimate by eye. Instead, you follow an exact procedure that produces a figure that is correct every time. This is the same kind of careful, accurate drawing that architects, builders, and engineers use when they design houses, bridges, and machines.

By the end of this chapter you will be able to draw a circle of any radius you choose, copy a line segment exactly, cut a segment into two equal halves, draw a line that meets another at a perfect right angle, and draw lines that are perfectly parallel — all with just a ruler and a pair of compasses.

## Why This Topic Matters

Imagine a builder in Kingston laying the foundation for a house. The corners of the building must be exact right angles, or the walls will not stand straight and the roof will not fit. Imagine a craftsman in Montego Bay making a wooden gate; the bars must be perfectly parallel and evenly spaced. None of this is done by guessing — it is done by construction.

Geometric construction teaches you to:
- Work with **accuracy** and care, a skill useful in every subject and trade.
- Understand *why* shapes have the properties they have, not just *that* they have them.
- Use simple tools to solve problems that look complicated.

Long before computers and design software existed, every map, building plan, and machine drawing was made by hand using a ruler and compasses. The methods you learn here are over two thousand years old, first written down by the Greek mathematician Euclid, and they are still exactly correct today.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

## Prior Knowledge

Before starting this chapter, you should already be able to:
- Use a pair of compasses to draw a circle.
- Use a ruler to measure lengths in centimetres and millimetres.
- Use a protractor to draw and measure angles.
- Recognise a right angle (an angle of 90°).

A quick reminder: there are 10 millimetres (mm) in 1 centimetre (cm). So 4.5 cm is the same as 45 mm. Reading your ruler carefully to the nearest millimetre is the first habit of good construction.

## Key Vocabulary

- **Construction:** An accurate drawing made using only a ruler (straight-edge) and a pair of compasses.
- **Ruler / straight-edge:** An instrument used to draw straight lines and to measure length.
- **Pair of compasses:** An instrument with two arms — one with a sharp point and one holding a pencil — used to draw circles and arcs.
- **Protractor:** An instrument used to measure and draw angles.
- **Set square:** A flat triangular instrument with a built-in right angle, used to draw perpendicular and parallel lines.
- **Radius:** The distance from the centre of a circle to any point on the circle.
- **Circle:** The set of all points that are the same distance (the radius) from a fixed centre point.
- **Arc:** A part of a circle — a curved line drawn by the compasses.
- **Line segment:** A straight line with two endpoints and a fixed length.
- **Perpendicular:** Two lines that meet at a right angle (90°).
- **Parallel:** Two lines that stay the same distance apart and never meet.
- **Bisector:** A line that cuts a segment or an angle into two equal parts.
- **Perpendicular bisector:** A line that cuts a segment into two equal parts *and* meets it at a right angle.
- **Equidistant:** The same distance from two or more points.

## Concept Explanations

### Concept 1: The Geometric Instruments

Good construction begins with knowing your tools.

**The ruler (straight-edge).** A ruler does two jobs. As a *measuring tool*, it tells you length in centimetres and millimetres. As a *straight-edge*, it lets you draw a straight line between two points. In a pure construction we use the ruler only as a straight-edge — we do not measure with it except to set the compasses or to check the answer at the end. Always keep the ruler still with one hand and draw with a sharp pencil held against its edge.

**The pair of compasses.** This is the most important construction tool. It has a sharp metal point on one arm and a pencil on the other. The gap between them sets the radius. To use it well:
- Keep the pencil sharp.
- Press lightly so the point does not slip and the paper does not tear.
- Turn the compasses by the handle at the top, not by the arms, so the gap does not change.

Once you set a gap on the compasses, that gap stays the same until you change it. This is the secret behind every construction: the compasses let you copy a length from one place to another perfectly.

**The protractor.** A semicircular instrument marked from 0° to 180°. We use it mainly to *check* our right angles and to measure angles. In a true ruler-and-compasses construction we do not use the protractor to make the figure — but we are glad to use it afterwards to prove that our right angle really is 90°.

**The set square.** A flat plastic triangle containing a right angle. It is a quick, practical way to draw perpendicular and parallel lines, and tradespeople use it constantly. We will learn the compasses-only methods first, because they work even when no set square is available, and then see how a set square does the same job faster.

*Figure 1: The four geometric instruments — a ruler, a pair of compasses (with sharp point and pencil arm), a semicircular protractor, and a right-angled set square.*

> **Tip — Safety first.** The point of a pair of compasses is sharp. Never wave it about, never point it at another student, and store it pointing downward in your geometry set.

### Concept 2: Constructing a Circle of a Given Radius

A **circle** is the set of all points that are exactly the same distance from a fixed centre. That fixed distance is the **radius**. Because the compasses keep a constant gap as they turn, they draw a circle naturally.

To construct a circle of, say, radius 4 cm, you set the compasses to a gap of 4 cm using your ruler, place the point at the centre, and sweep the pencil all the way around. Every point on the curve is then exactly 4 cm from the centre.

*Figure 2: A circle of radius 4 cm — the compass point fixed at centre O, and the pencil sweeping a full curve, every point 4 cm from O.*

### Concept 3: Constructing and Copying a Line Segment

A **line segment** is a straight piece of line with two endpoints and a definite length, such as a segment AB of length 6 cm.

There are two tasks here. The first is to *draw* a segment of a given length — for example, "draw AB = 6 cm." For this you simply use the ruler to measure and mark, then join the marks.

The second task is more interesting: to *copy* a segment whose length you are not told, using only the compasses. You open the compasses to match the given segment exactly, then move them — without changing the gap — to a new line and mark off the same length. This shows the real power of the compasses: they transfer a length perfectly even when you never measure it in numbers.

*Figure 3: Copying segment AB onto a ray from P — compasses opened to the length of AB, then the point placed at P to mark off PQ equal to AB.*

### Concept 4: The Perpendicular Bisector of a Segment

The **perpendicular bisector** of a segment AB is a line that does two things at once: it passes through the exact midpoint of AB (so it *bisects* it), and it crosses AB at a right angle (so it is *perpendicular*).

We construct it by opening the compasses to *more than half* the length of AB, and drawing arcs from A and then from B, above and below the segment. The arcs cross at two points. The straight line through those two crossing points is the perpendicular bisector. (We explain *why* this works in the next section.)

*Figure 4: The perpendicular bisector of AB — equal arcs from A and from B meet at points C above and D below; the line CD crosses AB at right angles through its midpoint.*

### Concept 5: Perpendiculars and Parallels

A **perpendicular** is a line that meets another at a right angle. We will construct a perpendicular in two situations: when the new line must pass through a point *on* the given line, and when it must pass through a point *off* the given line (away from it).

**Parallel** lines stay exactly the same distance apart and never meet, no matter how far they are extended. The rails of a train track are parallel; so are the opposite edges of this page. We construct a parallel line by using the idea that if two lines are both perpendicular to the same third line, they are parallel to each other — or by copying an angle so that the new line slopes the same way as the old one.

*Figure 5: Two parallel lines crossed by a third line, showing equal (corresponding) angles — the sign that the two lines are parallel.*

## Mathematical Reasoning: Why the Constructions Work

Constructions are not magic and they are not lucky drawings. Each one works for a clear geometric reason. Understanding the reason helps you remember the steps and trust your answer.

### Why the perpendicular bisector construction works

When you set the compasses to a fixed gap and draw an arc from A, every point on that arc is exactly that distance from A. When you draw an arc with the *same* gap from B, every point on it is the same distance from B. Where the two arcs cross — call the crossing point C — the point C is therefore the same distance from A as it is from B. We say C is **equidistant** from A and B.

You do this twice, getting a point C above the segment and a point D below it. Both C and D are equidistant from A and B. Here is the key fact of geometry: **every point that is the same distance from A and from B lies on the perpendicular bisector of AB.** So the line through C and D *is* the perpendicular bisector.

Another way to see it: join A to C, C to B, B to D, and D back to A. Because all four arcs had the same radius, all four sides AC, CB, BD, DA are equal in length. A four-sided figure with all sides equal is a **rhombus**. And the diagonals of a rhombus always cross each other at right angles, and each diagonal cuts the other exactly in half. The segment AB and the line CD are the two diagonals of this rhombus — so CD must cut AB in half and at a right angle. That is exactly the definition of the perpendicular bisector.

This is also why the arcs must be opened to *more than half* of AB: if the gap were too small, the arc from A and the arc from B would never reach far enough to cross, and there would be no rhombus and no crossing points.

### Why "equal arcs" locate the right points

In every construction in this chapter, the compasses do one job: they guarantee that two distances are equal. By choosing where to put the point and how wide to open the gap, we force certain points to be equidistant from certain others. Right angles, midpoints, and parallels all come out of this single idea of equal distances. Once you see that, constructions stop being a list of steps to memorise and become a set of sensible moves you could almost work out yourself.

## Step-by-Step Procedures

In every procedure below, use a sharp pencil and **do not rub out your construction arcs.**

### Procedure A — Construct a circle of a given radius

1. Use the ruler to open the compasses so the gap between the point and the pencil equals the required radius (for example, 4 cm).
2. Mark and label the centre point O on your paper.
3. Place the sharp point firmly on O.
4. Hold the compasses by the handle and turn the pencil all the way around in one smooth movement.
5. Label the circle. Check: measure from O to any point on the circle — it should equal the radius.

### Procedure B — Construct (draw) a line segment of a given length

1. Draw a straight line longer than the segment you need, using the ruler.
2. Mark a point near the left end and label it A.
3. From A, measure the required length along the line with the ruler and mark the point B.
4. Go over the part from A to B firmly. Label it. Check the length with the ruler.

### Procedure C — Copy a given line segment AB onto a new line

1. Draw a new straight line (a ray) and mark a starting point P on it.
2. Place the compass point on A and open the pencil to reach exactly to B. Do not change this gap.
3. Move the compasses to P, place the point on P, and draw a small arc that crosses the new line. Label the crossing point Q.
4. PQ is now equal in length to AB. Check by measuring both with the ruler.

### Procedure D — Construct the perpendicular bisector of segment AB

1. Draw segment AB.
2. Open the compasses to more than half the length of AB. Keep this gap for the whole construction.
3. Place the point on A and draw an arc above AB and an arc below AB.
4. Without changing the gap, place the point on B and draw an arc above and an arc below, crossing the first two arcs. Label the upper crossing point C and the lower crossing point D.
5. Use the ruler to draw the straight line through C and D.
6. The line CD is the perpendicular bisector. The point where it crosses AB is the midpoint M. Check: measure AM and MB (they should be equal) and check angle AMC with a protractor (it should be 90°).

### Procedure E — Construct a perpendicular to a line from a point P *on* the line

1. With the compass point on P, draw an arc that cuts the line on both sides of P. Label these points X and Y. (Now PX = PY.)
2. Open the compasses wider, to more than the distance PX.
3. With the point on X, draw an arc above the line. With the same gap and the point on Y, draw another arc crossing it. Label the crossing point Z.
4. Draw the straight line through P and Z.
5. PZ is perpendicular to the line at P. Check angle with a protractor — it should be 90°.

### Procedure F — Construct a perpendicular to a line from a point P *off* the line

1. With the compass point on P, draw an arc that cuts the line at two points. Label them X and Y.
2. With the point on X and a gap of more than half XY, draw an arc on the far side of the line from P.
3. With the same gap and the point on Y, draw another arc crossing it. Label the crossing point W.
4. Draw the straight line through P and W. This line is perpendicular to the given line. Check with a protractor.

### Procedure G — Construct a line parallel to a given line through a point P

1. Draw the given line, and mark the point P off the line.
2. Draw any straight line through P that crosses the given line. Label the crossing point Q. (This crossing line is called a transversal.)
3. With the compass point on Q, draw an arc that crosses both the given line and the transversal. Keep this gap.
4. With the same gap and the point on P, draw a matching arc crossing the transversal above P.
5. Open the compasses to measure the width of the arc you drew at Q (the distance between where it crossed the two lines).
6. With that gap, place the point where the P-arc crosses the transversal and mark across the P-arc. Label this crossing point R.
7. Draw the straight line through P and R. This line is parallel to the given line, because you have copied the angle at Q to the point P (equal corresponding angles). Check by measuring the perpendicular distance between the two lines at each end — it should be the same.

## Fully Worked Examples

### Worked Example 1 — Draw a circle of radius 3.5 cm

**Task:** Construct a circle with centre O and radius 3.5 cm.

1. Set the compasses against the ruler to a gap of 3.5 cm (that is, 35 mm).
2. Mark and label the centre O.
3. Place the point on O and sweep the pencil right around.
4. **Check:** Place the ruler so one end is at O. The curve should cross the ruler at the 3.5 cm mark in every direction. ✓

*Figure 6: A completed circle, centre O, radius 3.5 cm.*

### Worked Example 2 — Copy a line segment

**Task:** A segment AB is drawn on the board. Copy it onto your page as PQ, using compasses only.

1. Draw a ray and mark the start point P.
2. Place the compass point on A and open the pencil exactly to B.
3. Without changing the gap, place the point on P and strike a small arc cutting the ray at Q.
4. Join nothing else — PQ is your copy.
5. **Check:** Measure AB and PQ with a ruler. They should be identical. ✓

### Worked Example 3 — Perpendicular bisector of a 6 cm segment

**Task:** Draw AB = 6 cm and construct its perpendicular bisector.

1. Using the ruler, draw AB = 6 cm.
2. Half of AB is 3 cm, so open the compasses to about 4 cm (more than half).
3. Point on A: draw arcs above and below AB.
4. Point on B (same gap): draw arcs above and below, crossing the first arcs at C (top) and D (bottom).
5. Draw the line CD with the ruler.
6. **Check:** CD crosses AB at the midpoint M. Measure: AM = 3 cm and MB = 3 cm. With a protractor, angle AMC = 90°. ✓

*Figure 7: AB = 6 cm with its perpendicular bisector CD crossing at midpoint M; arcs from A and B left visible.*

### Worked Example 4 — Find the midpoint of a segment

**Task:** Find the exact midpoint of a segment XY without measuring.

1. Construct the perpendicular bisector of XY using Procedure D.
2. The point where it crosses XY is the midpoint.
3. **Check:** Measure each half — they are equal. ✓ (Notice you never had to measure XY itself; this is how we bisect a segment of any length.)

### Worked Example 5 — Perpendicular from a point on a line

**Task:** A line is drawn with a point P on it. Construct the perpendicular to the line at P.

1. Point on P: draw an arc cutting the line at X and Y (so PX = PY).
2. Open wider. Point on X: arc above the line. Point on Y (same gap): arc crossing it at Z.
3. Draw line PZ.
4. **Check:** Angle ZPX measured with a protractor = 90°. ✓

*Figure 8: Perpendicular erected at point P on a line — equal points X and Y, arcs meeting at Z, line PZ at right angles.*

### Worked Example 6 — Perpendicular from a point off the line (height of a triangle)

**Task:** Point P lies above a line. Construct the perpendicular from P down to the line.

1. Point on P: draw an arc cutting the line at X and Y.
2. Point on X, gap more than half XY: arc below the line. Point on Y (same gap): arc crossing it at W.
3. Draw line PW. It is perpendicular to the line.
4. **Check:** Protractor reads 90° where PW meets the line. ✓ This is exactly how you would draw the height of a triangle from a vertex to the opposite side.

### Worked Example 7 — Draw a pair of parallel lines

**Task:** Given a line and a point P above it, construct a line through P parallel to the given line.

1. Draw a transversal from P crossing the given line at Q.
2. Point on Q: draw an arc crossing the given line and the transversal. Keep the gap.
3. Point on P (same gap): draw a matching arc crossing the transversal.
4. Measure the width of the Q-arc with the compasses; copy that width onto the P-arc to locate R.
5. Draw line PR.
6. **Check:** Measure the gap between the two lines at the left and at the right — both equal. ✓ The corresponding angles at P and Q are equal, which proves the lines are parallel.

*Figure 9: A line through P constructed parallel to a given line by copying the angle at Q (equal corresponding angles).*

### Worked Example 8 — Construct a square corner for a garden plot

**Task:** A student is marking out a rectangular vegetable plot and needs a true right angle at corner A on a baseline.

1. On the baseline, mark corner A.
2. Treat A as "a point on the line" and use Procedure E to erect a perpendicular at A.
3. Along the perpendicular, measure the side length needed and mark the next corner.
4. **Check:** Protractor at A reads 90°. ✓ The plot will have square corners.

## Real-Life Applications

- **Architecture and building.** Builders use right-angle constructions (often with the related "3-4-5" method and set squares) to make sure the corners of houses and walls are square. A house with corners that are not true right angles cannot be built safely.
- **Engineering drawing and design.** Before a machine part is cut, an engineer draws it accurately. Perpendiculars and parallels make sure holes line up and edges fit. Drawing software such as AutoCAD performs these same constructions electronically.
- **Bridges and structures.** The arches and triangular supports you see in bridges are designed using exact geometric constructions, because the shape decides how strong the structure is.
- **Craft and trades.** A carpenter making a gate, a tiler laying a floor, and a dressmaker cutting cloth all rely on parallel lines and right angles.
- **Maps and surveying.** Land surveyors mark out plots with perpendicular and parallel boundaries, the same constructions you are learning, scaled up to a whole field.

> **Activity idea (Extended Learning).** Take photographs of interesting buildings or structures in your community. Find the right angles and parallel lines in them, and explain in a short presentation how construction made each design possible.

## Common Mistakes

- **Rubbing out the construction arcs.** The arcs are the proof. Leave them faint but visible.
- **Changing the compass gap halfway.** When a step says "same gap," the gap must not change. Hold the compasses by the handle so they do not slip.
- **Opening the compasses too small for a perpendicular bisector.** If the gap is less than half the segment, the arcs never cross. Always open to *more than half*.
- **Using a blunt pencil.** A thick line hides exactly where points cross, making the figure inaccurate.
- **Letting the compass point slip.** Press the point in firmly but gently so it stays fixed while you draw.
- **Measuring with the ruler instead of constructing.** When a question says "ruler and compasses only," using a protractor to make the right angle is not a construction — use the compasses method, then check with the protractor.

## Helpful Tips

- Sharpen your pencil before you begin and keep a sharpener handy.
- Work on a flat surface and steady the paper with your free hand.
- Make arcs long enough to be sure they cross — a tiny arc is hard to read.
- Label every important point (A, B, C, M, and so on) as you go.
- Always finish with a **check**: measure equal lengths with the ruler and test right angles with the protractor.
- Practise the perpendicular bisector first; almost every other construction uses the same "equal arcs" idea.

## Chapter Review

In this chapter you learned that a **construction** is an accurate drawing made with a ruler and a pair of compasses. You met the four instruments and learned to use each safely and precisely. You can now construct a **circle of a given radius**, **draw and copy a line segment**, build the **perpendicular bisector** of a segment, erect **perpendiculars** from a point on a line and from a point off a line, and draw **parallel lines**.

Most importantly, you learned *why* these constructions work. The compasses guarantee equal distances; equal distances create equidistant points; and equidistant points lie on perpendicular bisectors. The perpendicular bisector construction even forms a rhombus, whose diagonals are known to cross at right angles and to bisect each other.

## Summary

- A **construction** uses only a ruler (straight-edge) and compasses; leave the arcs visible.
- A **circle** is drawn by fixing the compass gap to the radius and sweeping around the centre.
- A **line segment** can be drawn to a measured length or copied exactly with compasses.
- The **perpendicular bisector** is made with equal arcs from each end; it cuts the segment in half at 90° and gives the midpoint.
- A **perpendicular** can be constructed from a point on a line or from a point off a line.
- **Parallel** lines are constructed by copying an angle or by making two perpendiculars to the same line.
- Always **check** your work: equal lengths with a ruler, right angles with a protractor.

## Key Takeaways

1. Accuracy and care matter more than speed in construction.
2. The compasses' constant gap is the tool behind every construction.
3. "Equal arcs make equal distances" is the single idea underneath bisectors, perpendiculars, and parallels.
4. Construction arcs are proof of your method — never rub them out.
5. These same constructions are used every day in architecture, engineering, and the trades.
