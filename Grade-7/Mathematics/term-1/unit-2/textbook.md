# Chapter 2: Measuring Concepts

## Introduction

Measurement is one of the oldest and most useful branches of mathematics. Long before people had calculators or computers, farmers needed to mark out fields, builders needed to cut beams to the right length, and traders needed to weigh out goods fairly in the market. Every one of these tasks depends on the same idea: comparing something we want to know about — a length, a mass, an amount of time — against a **unit** that everyone agrees on.

In this chapter you will sharpen the measuring skills you already have and add powerful new ones. You will learn how to choose the right instrument for each kind of measurement, how to express a measurement to a sensible degree of accuracy, and how to convert from one unit to another — including the slightly tricky business of converting **square units** such as square centimetres and square metres. Finally, you will learn how to find the **perimeter** and **area** of composite (compound) figures: shapes that are built up from rectangles and right-angled triangles. By the end, you will be able to work out, for example, how much fencing is needed to enclose an L-shaped yard, or how many square metres of tiles are needed to cover a verandah.

## Why This Topic Matters

Measurement is everywhere in everyday Jamaican life. When a seamstress in Spanish Town cuts cloth, when a mason in Mandeville mixes concrete, when a nurse at the health centre records a patient's temperature, or when a market vendor in Coronation Market weighs out a pound of yam — all of them are measuring. Getting a measurement wrong can be costly: too little fence leaves a gap, too much tile is wasted money, and a wrong dose of medicine can be dangerous.

The skills in this chapter also support your other subjects. In Science you measure mass, temperature and volume in experiments. In Technical and Vocational subjects you measure and cut materials. In Physical Education you measure distances and times. Strong measuring skills make you more accurate, more confident, and more employable.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter you should be able to:

- Identify the measurable attribute of an object (length, mass, time, temperature, volume or capacity) and choose the correct instrument to measure it.
- Read measuring instruments carefully and record measurements to a stated degree of accuracy.
- Convert measurements from one metric unit to another within a quantity (for example, centimetres to metres).
- Convert between square units (for example, square metres to square centimetres) correctly.
- Find the perimeter of composite figures built from rectangles and right-angled triangles.
- Find the area of composite figures by splitting them into, or removing, simpler shapes.

## Prior Knowledge

Before starting this chapter you should already be able to:

- Name the common metric units of length, mass and capacity (millimetre, centimetre, metre, kilometre; gram, kilogram; millilitre, litre).
- Recall the basic metric relationships, for example 1 m = 100 cm and 1 kg = 1000 g.
- Find the perimeter of a rectangle and of simple polygons by adding the side lengths.
- Find the area of a rectangle using area = length × width.
- Find the area of a right-angled triangle using area = ½ × base × height.
- Recognise the units of area: square centimetre (cm²), square metre (m²), hectare (ha) and square kilometre (km²).

If any of these feel shaky, revise them now — every new idea in this chapter builds on them.

## Part 1 — Measuring and Instruments

### What does it mean to measure?

To measure something is to ask, "How many units does it contain?" When you say a rope is 4 metres long, you mean the rope is as long as 4 of the standard length called the metre, laid end to end. Every measurement therefore has two parts: a **number** and a **unit**. A number without a unit is almost useless — if a recipe says "add 2 of flour", you would not know whether to add 2 grams, 2 cups or 2 kilograms.

### Measurable attributes and the right instrument

Different objects can be measured in different ways. The particular property we measure is called the **attribute**. The most common attributes, and the correct instrument for each, are shown below.

| Attribute | What it tells us | Common instrument | Common units |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Length | How long, wide, tall or far | Ruler, measuring tape | mm, cm, m, km |
| Mass | How much matter | Balance, scale | g, kg |
| Time | How long an event lasts | Clock, stopwatch | s, min, h |
| Temperature | How hot or cold | Thermometer | °C |
| Capacity / Volume | How much a container holds | Measuring cylinder, measuring jug | mL, L |

*Figure 1: A table linking each measurable attribute to the correct instrument and units.*

**Choosing wisely.** Part of being a good measurer is matching the instrument to the job. You would use a ruler to measure the width of this book, but a measuring tape to measure the length of a classroom, because a tape is long and flexible. You would use a kitchen scale to weigh flour, but a bathroom scale to find your body mass. A clinical thermometer measures body temperature, while a cooking thermometer measures the much higher temperature of hot oil.

### Estimation

Before you measure exactly, it is good practice to **estimate** — to make a sensible, reasoned guess. Estimation is not careless guessing; it uses what you already know. If you know your hand-span is about 20 cm, you can estimate the width of a desk by imagining how many hand-spans fit across it. Estimating first helps you notice mistakes: if you estimate a doorway to be about 2 m and then "measure" it as 20 m, you know at once that something has gone wrong.

### Degree of accuracy

No measurement is ever perfectly exact, so we measure to a stated **degree of accuracy**. This means we say how precise the measurement is. For example, the length of a pencil might be recorded as 17 cm (to the nearest centimetre) or, more precisely, as 17.4 cm (to the nearest millimetre). The right degree of accuracy depends on the task. A carpenter building furniture may need accuracy to the nearest millimetre, while someone estimating how far it is to school might be happy to the nearest hundred metres.

### Temperature scales

Temperature is measured with a **thermometer**. The scale used most around the world, and in Jamaican schools, is the **Celsius** scale (°C). On the Celsius scale, water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C, and normal human body temperature is about 37 °C. Several other scales exist. The **Fahrenheit** scale (°F) is used in some countries; on it water freezes at 32 °F and boils at 212 °F. The **Kelvin** scale (K) is used by scientists and begins at absolute zero, the coldest temperature possible. The **Rankine** scale is another scientific scale related to Fahrenheit in the way Kelvin is related to Celsius. In this course we will work in Celsius, but it is useful to know that these other scales exist.

> **Common Mistake.** Writing a measurement without its unit, or mixing up units. "The desk is 120" tells us nothing — is it 120 mm, 120 cm or 120 m? Always write the unit.

> **Helpful Tip.** Before measuring, ask three questions: *What attribute am I measuring? Which instrument is best? To what degree of accuracy do I need the answer?*

## Part 2 — Unit Conversions

### Why convert?

We often need to change a measurement from one unit to another. A length might be given in centimetres but needed in metres; a distance in metres might be needed in kilometres. **Converting** means writing the same measurement using a different unit. The quantity itself does not change — only the way we express it. Saying 200 cm and saying 2 m describe exactly the same length.

### The metric system and its prefixes

The metric system is beautifully simple because it is based on **powers of ten**. Each unit is related to the next by multiplying or dividing by 10, 100 or 1000. Special word-parts called **prefixes** tell us how big a unit is. The most important prefixes are:

| Prefix | Meaning | Example with the metre |
|--------|---------|------------------------|
| kilo- | one thousand (×1000) | 1 kilometre (km) = 1000 m |
| (base unit) | one | 1 metre (m) |
| deci- | one tenth (÷10) | 1 decimetre (dm) = 0.1 m |
| centi- | one hundredth (÷100) | 1 centimetre (cm) = 0.01 m |
| milli- | one thousandth (÷1000) | 1 millimetre (mm) = 0.001 m |

The same prefixes work with the gram (mass) and the litre (capacity). So 1 kilogram = 1000 grams, and 1 millilitre = 0.001 litres.

The key relationships for length are:

- 1 cm = 10 mm
- 1 m = 100 cm = 1000 mm
- 1 km = 1000 m

### The golden rule of converting

There are two directions you can convert in, and one simple rule keeps you right:

- **Going to a smaller unit, you will have more of them, so multiply.**
- **Going to a larger unit, you will have fewer of them, so divide.**

Think about it with money first. A larger coin is worth more, so it takes many small coins to make one big one. In the same way, a centimetre is smaller than a metre, so it takes many centimetres (100 of them) to make one metre.

### Worked Example 1 — converting to a smaller unit

Convert 3.5 m into centimetres.

We are going from metres to the *smaller* unit centimetres, so we **multiply**. Since 1 m = 100 cm:

$$3.5 \text{ m} = 3.5 \times 100 \text{ cm} = 350 \text{ cm}$$

**Check:** 350 cm is a larger number than 3.5, which makes sense because centimetres are smaller, so we need more of them.

### Worked Example 2 — converting to a larger unit

Convert 2500 m into kilometres.

We are going from metres to the *larger* unit kilometres, so we **divide**. Since 1 km = 1000 m:

$$2500 \text{ m} = 2500 \div 1000 \text{ km} = 2.5 \text{ km}$$

**Check:** 2.5 is a smaller number than 2500, which makes sense because kilometres are larger.

### Worked Example 3 — a two-step conversion

Convert 45 000 mm into metres.

Method A — in one step, using 1 m = 1000 mm (divide, because the metre is larger):

$$45\,000 \text{ mm} = 45\,000 \div 1000 \text{ m} = 45 \text{ m}$$

Method B — in two steps: first mm to cm (÷10), then cm to m (÷100):

$$45\,000 \text{ mm} = 4500 \text{ cm} = 45 \text{ m}$$

Both methods give the same answer, 45 m.

### Converting square (area) units

This is the part students most often get wrong, so read it carefully. Area is measured in **square units**, such as square centimetres (cm²) and square metres (m²). A square unit is a square whose sides are one unit long.

Here is the key idea. We know that:

$$1 \text{ m} = 100 \text{ cm}$$

A square metre is a square measuring 1 m by 1 m. Replacing each side with 100 cm gives a square measuring 100 cm by 100 cm. Its area is:

$$1 \text{ m}^2 = 100 \text{ cm} \times 100 \text{ cm} = 10\,000 \text{ cm}^2$$

So **1 m² = 10 000 cm²**, *not* 100 cm². The reason is that area has two dimensions, so the conversion factor between the linear units must be used **twice** — once for each side.

*Figure 2: A 1 m by 1 m square shown as a grid of 100 columns and 100 rows of 1 cm squares, giving 10 000 small squares in total.*

A useful way to remember it: **square the linear conversion factor.**

| Linear relationship | Square relationship |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| 1 cm = 10 mm | 1 cm² = 100 mm² |
| 1 m = 100 cm | 1 m² = 10 000 cm² |
| 1 km = 1000 m | 1 km² = 1 000 000 m² |

### Worked Example 4 — square units, small to large

A floor tile has an area of 2500 cm². What is its area in square metres?

We are going from cm² to the larger unit m², so we **divide**. Since 1 m² = 10 000 cm²:

$$2500 \text{ cm}^2 = 2500 \div 10\,000 \text{ m}^2 = 0.25 \text{ m}^2$$

**Check:** 0.25 m² is a quarter of a square metre, which is reasonable for a single tile.

### Worked Example 5 — square units, large to small

A garden bed has an area of 3 m². What is its area in square centimetres?

We are going from m² to the smaller unit cm², so we **multiply**. Since 1 m² = 10 000 cm²:

$$3 \text{ m}^2 = 3 \times 10\,000 \text{ cm}^2 = 30\,000 \text{ cm}^2$$

> **Common Mistake.** Using 100 instead of 10 000 when converting between m² and cm². Remember that 1 m² = 10 000 cm² because the factor 100 is used twice (once for length, once for width).

> **Helpful Tip.** When you convert an area, first decide the linear factor, then square it. For m to cm the linear factor is 100, so the area factor is 100 × 100 = 10 000.

## Part 3 — Perimeter of Composite Figures

### What is perimeter?

The **perimeter** of a closed flat figure is the total distance around its outside edge. If you walked all the way around the boundary of a field and ended where you began, the distance you walked would be the perimeter. Because perimeter is a length, it is measured in length units such as centimetres or metres — never in square units.

For a rectangle with length *l* and width *w*, the perimeter is found by adding all four sides:

$$P = l + w + l + w = 2l + 2w = 2(l + w)$$

### What is a composite figure?

A **composite** (or **compound**) **figure** is a shape made by joining together simpler shapes, such as rectangles and right-angled triangles. An L-shaped room, a T-shaped flower bed and a house-shaped outline (a rectangle with a triangle on top) are all composite figures. Many real objects have composite outlines, which is why this skill is so useful.

### Finding the perimeter of a composite figure

The good news is that perimeter is always found the same way, no matter how complicated the shape: **add up the lengths of all the sides that form the outside boundary.** The only challenge with composite figures is that some side lengths may not be marked, and you must work them out from the others.

### Working out a missing side

Look at an L-shaped figure. Because the shape is made of rectangles, opposite parts must match up. The two horizontal lengths along the top must together equal the single horizontal length along the bottom, and likewise for the vertical sides. We use this fact to find any missing length by subtraction.

*Figure 3: An L-shaped figure with outer width 10 cm, outer height 8 cm, a notch removed from the top-right corner, with two missing inner sides to be found.*

### Worked Example 6 — perimeter of an L-shaped figure

An L-shaped figure has these labelled sides, going around: a bottom edge of 10 cm, a right edge of 3 cm, then a step inwards of 6 cm, then up 5 cm, then a top edge of 4 cm, then down the left edge of 8 cm back to the start. Find the perimeter.

First, let us check the missing lengths are consistent.

- Horizontal sides on the right (going in): 6 cm. The top edge is 4 cm. Together 4 + 6 = 10 cm, which equals the bottom edge of 10 cm. Good.
- Vertical sides: the right edge 3 cm plus the inner rise 5 cm gives 3 + 5 = 8 cm, which equals the left edge of 8 cm. Good.

Now add every outside edge:

$$P = 10 + 3 + 6 + 5 + 4 + 8 = 36 \text{ cm}$$

The perimeter of the L-shaped figure is **36 cm**.

### Worked Example 7 — finding a missing side first

A rectangle has a smaller rectangle attached to make a composite figure. The overall outline has a bottom of 12 m and a left side of 7 m. Along the top there is a horizontal piece of 8 m, then the boundary steps down 3 m, then runs across an unmarked length, then down to the bottom. Find the unmarked horizontal length, then the perimeter.

The two horizontal pieces along the top must add up to the bottom length:

$$8 \text{ m} + \text{(unmarked)} = 12 \text{ m} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{unmarked} = 12 - 8 = 4 \text{ m}$$

The vertical drop on the right must make up the full height: the step of 3 m plus the remaining drop equals the 7 m left side, so the remaining drop is 7 − 3 = 4 m.

Now add all outside edges:

$$P = 12 + 7 + 8 + 3 + 4 + 4 = 38 \text{ m}$$

The perimeter is **38 m**.

> **Common Mistake.** Forgetting to include the "hidden" inner edges of a notch, or counting a side twice. Trace the boundary with your finger, marking each side as you include it, and stop only when you return to your starting point.

> **Helpful Tip.** For an L-shape (and many composite shapes), the sum of all the horizontal top edges equals the bottom edge, and the sum of all the vertical edges on one side equals the height. Use these facts to find missing lengths.

## Part 4 — Area of Composite Figures

### What is area?

The **area** of a flat shape is the amount of surface it covers. We measure it in **square units** such as square centimetres (cm²) and square metres (m²), because we are counting how many unit squares fit inside the shape.

You already know two essential formulas:

- **Rectangle:** area = length × width, written $A = l \times w$.
- **Right-angled triangle:** area = ½ × base × height, written $A = \tfrac{1}{2} \times b \times h$. The base and height are the two sides that meet at the right angle.

### Why the triangle formula works

A right-angled triangle is exactly half of a rectangle. If you draw a rectangle and then draw one diagonal, you split it into two equal right-angled triangles. Each triangle is therefore half the area of the rectangle, which is why we multiply by ½.

*Figure 4: A rectangle divided by a diagonal into two equal right-angled triangles, showing that each triangle is half the rectangle.*

### The method for composite figures

To find the area of a composite figure, we use one of two strategies:

1. **Splitting (adding).** Divide the figure into rectangles and right-angled triangles, find the area of each piece, then **add** the pieces together.
2. **Surrounding and removing (subtracting).** Imagine a large rectangle that surrounds the whole figure, find its area, then **subtract** the area of the part that is missing.

Both methods give the same answer, so you may choose whichever is easier for the shape in front of you.

### Step-by-step procedure for splitting

1. Look at the figure and draw lines to divide it into rectangles and right-angled triangles.
2. Work out any missing lengths you will need.
3. Find the area of each separate piece using the correct formula.
4. Add all the pieces together.
5. Write the answer with the correct square unit.

### Worked Example 8 — area of an L-shaped figure by splitting

Take the L-shaped figure from Worked Example 6. Its overall outline is 10 cm wide and 8 cm tall, with a rectangular notch removed from the top-right corner; the notch is 6 cm wide and 5 cm tall.

**Method 1 — splitting into two rectangles.** Draw a horizontal line to cut the L into a bottom rectangle and a top rectangle.

- Bottom rectangle: full width 10 cm by height 3 cm.
  $$A_1 = 10 \times 3 = 30 \text{ cm}^2$$
- Top rectangle: this is the part above the cut, width 4 cm (that is 10 − 6) by height 5 cm.
  $$A_2 = 4 \times 5 = 20 \text{ cm}^2$$

Total area:

$$A = A_1 + A_2 = 30 + 20 = 50 \text{ cm}^2$$

**Method 2 — surround and subtract (check).** Surround the L with the full 10 cm by 8 cm rectangle, then remove the notch (6 cm by 5 cm).

$$A = (10 \times 8) - (6 \times 5) = 80 - 30 = 50 \text{ cm}^2$$

Both methods give **50 cm²**, confirming the answer.

### Worked Example 9 — rectangle with a right-angled triangle (a house shape)

A figure is shaped like the outline of a house: a rectangle 6 m wide and 4 m tall, with a right-angled triangle sitting on top. The triangle has a base of 6 m (the same as the rectangle) and a vertical height of 3 m. Find the total area.

- Rectangle:
  $$A_1 = 6 \times 4 = 24 \text{ m}^2$$
- Triangle:
  $$A_2 = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 6 \times 3 = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 18 = 9 \text{ m}^2$$

Total area:

$$A = A_1 + A_2 = 24 + 9 = 33 \text{ m}^2$$

The total area is **33 m²**.

*Figure 5: A house-shaped composite figure — a 6 m by 4 m rectangle with a right-angled triangle of base 6 m and height 3 m on top.*

### Worked Example 10 — a composite figure needing a unit conversion

A verandah floor is shaped like an L. The two rectangles that make it up measure 300 cm by 200 cm and 150 cm by 200 cm. Find the total area in square metres.

First find the area in square centimetres:

- Rectangle 1: $300 \times 200 = 60\,000 \text{ cm}^2$
- Rectangle 2: $150 \times 200 = 30\,000 \text{ cm}^2$
- Total: $60\,000 + 30\,000 = 90\,000 \text{ cm}^2$

Now convert to square metres. Since 1 m² = 10 000 cm², we divide:

$$90\,000 \text{ cm}^2 = 90\,000 \div 10\,000 = 9 \text{ m}^2$$

The verandah covers **9 m²**.

(You could also convert the lengths to metres first — 3 m by 2 m and 1.5 m by 2 m — giving 6 m² + 3 m² = 9 m². The answer is the same, which is a good check.)

> **Common Mistake.** Mixing units within one calculation — for example multiplying a length in metres by a width in centimetres. Always convert every length to the *same* unit before you calculate.

> **Helpful Tip.** After splitting a shape, write down the dimensions of each piece separately before doing any arithmetic. This keeps your work neat and makes mistakes easy to spot.

## Mathematical Reasoning

Good mathematicians do not just follow steps; they understand *why* the steps work, which lets them solve problems they have never seen before. Three big ideas tie this whole chapter together.

First, **a measurement is a count of units**, so the larger the unit, the fewer you need — this single idea explains the whole "multiply or divide" rule for conversions.

Second, **area is two-dimensional**, which is why converting square units uses the linear factor *twice*. Whenever a quantity is built from two lengths multiplied together, its unit conversion factor is the linear factor squared.

Third, **complicated shapes are built from simple ones**. Because area and perimeter can be added piece by piece, any composite figure can be broken into rectangles and right-angled triangles whose areas and sides we already know how to find. This idea of breaking a hard problem into easy pieces appears throughout mathematics.

## Real-Life Applications

- **Fencing a yard.** To buy the right amount of fencing for an L-shaped yard, you find its perimeter.
- **Tiling and painting.** To buy tiles for a floor or paint for a wall, you find the area of the surface.
- **Buying cloth.** A dressmaker calculates the area of fabric pieces, often converting between centimetres and metres.
- **Farming.** A farmer measures a field in metres but may state its size in hectares or square kilometres, requiring square-unit conversion.
- **Health care.** A nurse reads a thermometer to a sensible degree of accuracy and acts on the result.
- **Cooking.** A chef measures mass and capacity, converting between grams and kilograms, or millilitres and litres.

## Common Mistakes (Summary)

- Writing a number without its unit.
- Multiplying when you should divide (or the reverse) during a conversion. Remember: to a smaller unit, multiply; to a larger unit, divide.
- Using 100 instead of 10 000 when converting between m² and cm².
- Forgetting the inner edges of a notch when finding a perimeter.
- Mixing units (cm and m) in the same calculation.
- Giving an area in length units (cm) instead of square units (cm²).

## Helpful Tips (Summary)

- Estimate before you measure, then check your exact answer against the estimate.
- For square-unit conversions, square the linear conversion factor.
- Trace a composite boundary with your finger so you include every side exactly once.
- Split a shape neatly, label each piece, then add (or surround and subtract).
- Always include the correct unit in your final answer.

## Chapter Review

In this chapter you learned to identify the measurable attribute of an object and to choose the correct instrument — ruler or tape for length, balance or scale for mass, clock or stopwatch for time, thermometer for temperature, and measuring cylinder or jug for capacity and volume. You learned to estimate first and to record measurements to a sensible degree of accuracy, and you met the temperature scales Celsius, Fahrenheit, Kelvin and Rankine.

You then mastered conversions in the metric system, using the prefixes kilo-, deci-, centi- and milli-, and the rule that converting to a smaller unit means multiplying while converting to a larger unit means dividing. Most importantly, you learned that square units convert by squaring the linear factor, so that 1 m² = 10 000 cm².

Finally, you learned to find the perimeter of a composite figure by adding every outside edge (working out missing lengths first), and to find its area by splitting it into rectangles and right-angled triangles and adding the pieces, or by surrounding and subtracting.

## Summary

- Every measurement has a **number and a unit**.
- Choose the **right instrument** and a sensible **degree of accuracy**.
- Metric conversions: to a **smaller** unit **multiply**; to a **larger** unit **divide**.
- **Square units**: square the linear factor, so 1 m² = 10 000 cm².
- **Perimeter** = total distance around the outside (a length).
- **Area of a rectangle** = l × w; **area of a right-angled triangle** = ½ × b × h.
- **Composite area** = split and add, or surround and subtract.

## Key Takeaways

1. A unit makes a measurement meaningful — never leave it out.
