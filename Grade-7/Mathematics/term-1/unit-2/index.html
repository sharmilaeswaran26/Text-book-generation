Measuring & Instruments

To measure something is to ask, "How many units does it contain?" When you say a rope is 4 metres long, you mean the rope is as long as 4 of the standard length called the metre, laid end to end. Every measurement therefore has two parts: a number and a unit. A number without a unit is almost useless — if a recipe says "add 2 of flour", you would not know whether to add 2 grams, 2 cups or 2 kilograms.

The particular property we measure is called the attribute. Part of being a good measurer is matching the instrument to the job: a ruler for the width of a book, but a measuring tape for the length of a classroom; a kitchen scale for flour, but a bathroom scale for body mass.

Lesson 2: Estimation and Degree of Accuracy

Before you measure exactly, it is good practice to estimate — to make a sensible, reasoned guess using what you already know. If your hand-span is about 20 cm, you can estimate a desk's width by imagining how many hand-spans fit across it. Estimating first helps you notice mistakes.

No measurement is ever perfectly exact, so we measure to a stated degree of accuracy. A pencil might be recorded as 17 cm (to the nearest centimetre) or, more precisely, 17.4 cm (to the nearest millimetre). A carpenter may need accuracy to the nearest millimetre, while someone judging the distance to school may be happy to the nearest hundred metres.

Think About It

  • What attribute am I measuring?
  • Which instrument is best for this job?
  • To what degree of accuracy do I need the answer?

Lesson 3: Temperature Scales

Temperature is measured with a thermometer. The scale used most around the world, and in Jamaican schools, is the Celsius scale (°C). Water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C, and normal body temperature is about 37 °C.

The Fahrenheit scale (°F) is used in some countries; on it water freezes at 32 °F and boils at 212 °F. The Kelvin scale (K) is used by scientists and begins at absolute zero, the coldest temperature possible. The Rankine scale is another scientific scale, related to Fahrenheit in the way Kelvin is related to Celsius. In this course we work in Celsius, but it is useful to know these other scales exist.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

No unit: "The desk is 120" tells us nothing — is it mm, cm or m? Always write the unit.
Wrong instrument: A clinical thermometer measures body temperature; a cooking thermometer measures much higher temperatures.

Practice

1. Name the best instrument and a suitable unit for measuring: (a) the length of a netball court, (b) the mass of a bunch of bananas, (c) the temperature of a sick child, (d) the amount of juice in a jug.

2. Estimate, then state to a sensible degree of accuracy, the length of your own desk.

Unit Conversions

To convert is to write the same measurement using a different unit. The quantity itself does not change — only how we express it. Saying 200 cm and saying 2 m describe exactly the same length.

The metric system is simple because it is based on powers of ten. Special word-parts called prefixes tell us how big each unit is.

Lesson 1: Metric Prefixes and the Golden Rule

PrefixMeaningExample with the metre
kilo-one thousand (×1000)1 km = 1000 m
(base unit)one1 metre (m)
deci-one tenth (÷10)1 dm = 0.1 m
centi-one hundredth (÷100)1 cm = 0.01 m
milli-one thousandth (÷1000)1 mm = 0.001 m

The Golden Rule

  • Going to a smaller unit, you need more of them, so multiply.
  • Going to a larger unit, you need fewer of them, so divide.

Lesson 2: Converting Linear Units

Worked Example 1. Convert 3.5 m into centimetres. We are going to the smaller unit, so we multiply. Since 1 m = 100 cm: 3.5 × 100 = 350 cm. (350 is larger than 3.5, which makes sense — centimetres are smaller, so we need more.)

Worked Example 2. Convert 2500 m into kilometres. We are going to the larger unit, so we divide. Since 1 km = 1000 m: 2500 ÷ 1000 = 2.5 km.

Worked Example 3. Convert 45 000 mm into metres. Since 1 m = 1000 mm, divide: 45 000 ÷ 1000 = 45 m. (Check in two steps: 45 000 mm = 4500 cm = 45 m.)

Lesson 3: Converting Square (Area) Units

This is the part students most often get wrong, so read carefully. A square metre is a square measuring 1 m by 1 m. Replacing each side with 100 cm gives a square measuring 100 cm by 100 cm, so its area is 100 × 100 = 10 000 cm². Therefore 1 m² = 10 000 cm², not 100 cm². Because area has two dimensions, the linear conversion factor must be used twice.

Figure 2

A 1 m by 1 m square shown as a grid of 100 columns and 100 rows of 1 cm squares, giving 10 000 small squares in total.

Linear relationshipSquare relationship
1 cm = 10 mm1 cm² = 100 mm²
1 m = 100 cm1 m² = 10 000 cm²
1 km = 1000 m1 km² = 1 000 000 m²

Worked Example 4. A tile has area 2500 cm². In square metres: 2500 ÷ 10 000 = 0.25 m².

Worked Example 5. A garden bed has area 3 m². In square centimetres: 3 × 10 000 = 30 000 cm².

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Wrong factor: Using 100 instead of 10 000 for m² to cm². Remember the factor 100 is used twice.
Wrong direction: Multiplying when you should divide. To a larger unit, divide.

Practice

1. Convert 6.4 m to centimetres.   2. Convert 7200 m to kilometres.   3. Convert 5 m² to square centimetres.   4. Convert 40 000 cm² to square metres.

Perimeter of Composite Figures

The perimeter of a closed flat figure is the total distance around its outside edge. Because perimeter is a length, it is measured in length units such as centimetres or metres — never in square units. For a rectangle with length l and width w, the perimeter is P = 2(l + w).

A composite (or compound) figure is a shape made by joining or removing simpler shapes, such as rectangles and right-angled triangles. An L-shaped room and a house-shaped outline are composite figures.

Lesson 1: Adding All the Outside Edges

Perimeter is always found the same way, no matter how complicated the shape: add up the lengths of all the sides that form the outside boundary. The only challenge is that some side lengths may not be marked, and you must work them out from the others.

Because the shape is made of rectangles, the sum of the horizontal top edges equals the bottom edge, and the sum of the vertical edges on one side equals the height. We use these facts to find any missing length by subtraction.

Figure 3

An L-shaped figure with outer width 10 cm, outer height 8 cm, a notch removed from the top-right corner, with two missing inner sides to be found.

Lesson 2: Worked Examples

Worked Example 6 — L-shaped figure. Going around, the sides are: bottom 10 cm, right 3 cm, step in 6 cm, up 5 cm, top 4 cm, left 8 cm. First check the missing lengths: the top 4 cm plus the step-in 6 cm equals the bottom 10 cm; the right 3 cm plus the inner rise 5 cm equals the left 8 cm. Now add every edge: 10 + 3 + 6 + 5 + 4 + 8 = 36 cm.

Worked Example 7 — finding a missing side first. An outline has bottom 12 m and left side 7 m. Along the top there is a piece of 8 m, then the boundary steps down 3 m, then runs across an unmarked length. The two top pieces add to the bottom: 8 + unmarked = 12, so unmarked = 4 m. The vertical step 3 m plus the remaining drop equals 7 m, so the drop is 4 m. Perimeter: 12 + 7 + 8 + 3 + 4 + 4 = 38 m.

Helpful Tip

  • Trace the boundary with your finger, marking each side as you include it.
  • Stop only when you return to your starting point — this avoids missing or double-counting sides.

Common Mistake to Avoid

Hidden edges: Forgetting the inner edges of a notch, or counting a side twice. Every outside edge counts exactly once.

Practice

1. Find the perimeter of a rectangle 9 m by 4 m.

2. An L-shaped figure going around has sides: 12 cm, 4 cm, 7 cm, 6 cm, 5 cm, 10 cm. Find its perimeter.

Area of Composite Figures

The area of a flat shape is the amount of surface it covers. We measure it in square units such as cm² and m². You already know two essential formulas: a rectangle has area l × w, and a right-angled triangle has area ½ × b × h, where the base and height are the two sides that meet at the right angle.

Lesson 1: Why the Triangle Formula Works

A right-angled triangle is exactly half of a rectangle. If you draw a rectangle and then draw one diagonal, you split it into two equal right-angled triangles. Each triangle is therefore half the area of the rectangle, which is why we multiply by ½.

Figure 4

A rectangle divided by a diagonal into two equal right-angled triangles, showing that each triangle is half the rectangle.

Lesson 2: Splitting, or Surrounding and Subtracting

To find the area of a composite figure, use one of two strategies. Splitting (adding): divide the figure into rectangles and right-angled triangles, find the area of each piece, then add the pieces. Surrounding and removing (subtracting): imagine a large rectangle that surrounds the figure, find its area, then subtract the missing part. Both give the same answer.

Worked Example 8 — L-shaped figure. The outline is 10 cm wide and 8 cm tall, with a 6 cm by 5 cm notch removed from the top-right corner. Splitting: bottom rectangle 10 × 3 = 30 cm²; top rectangle 4 × 5 = 20 cm²; total 30 + 20 = 50 cm². Surround and subtract (check): (10 × 8) − (6 × 5) = 80 − 30 = 50 cm². Both agree.

Figure 5

A house-shaped composite figure — a 6 m by 4 m rectangle with a right-angled triangle of base 6 m and height 3 m on top.

Worked Example 9 — house shape. A rectangle 6 m by 4 m with a right-angled triangle (base 6 m, height 3 m) on top. Rectangle: 6 × 4 = 24 m². Triangle: ½ × 6 × 3 = 9 m². Total: 24 + 9 = 33 m².

Worked Example 10 — with a unit conversion. An L-shaped verandah is made of rectangles 300 cm × 200 cm and 150 cm × 200 cm. Areas: 60 000 cm² and 30 000 cm², total 90 000 cm². Convert: 90 000 ÷ 10 000 = 9 m². (Check in metres: 3 × 2 + 1.5 × 2 = 6 + 3 = 9 m².)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mixed units: Never multiply a length in metres by a width in centimetres. Convert everything to one unit first.
Wrong unit on the answer: Area is given in square units (cm², m²), not length units.

Practice

1. Find the area of an L-shaped figure made from a rectangle 8 cm by 5 cm and a rectangle 3 cm by 2 cm.

2. A house-shaped sign is a rectangle 40 cm by 30 cm with a right-angled triangle (base 40 cm, height 15 cm) on top. Find its total area.

Key Vocabulary

Measurement: The process of finding the size of a quantity by comparing it with a unit.
Metric: The decimal system of units based on powers of ten (for example, 1 km = 1000 m).
Attribute: The particular property of an object being measured, such as length or mass.
Unit: An agreed amount used as the basis for measuring, such as the metre.
Length: How long something is — the distance between two points.
Mass: The amount of matter in an object, measured in grams and kilograms.
Capacity: The amount a container can hold, measured in millilitres and litres.
Volume: The amount of space an object or substance occupies.
Temperature: How hot or cold something is, measured in degrees Celsius.
Time: The measured duration of an event, in seconds, minutes and hours.
Thermometer: An instrument used to measure temperature.
Metre: The base metric unit of length.
Litre: The base metric unit of capacity.
Gram: The base metric unit of mass (1000 g = 1 kg).
Milli-: A prefix meaning one thousandth (1 mm = 0.001 m).
Centi-: A prefix meaning one hundredth (1 cm = 0.01 m).
Kilo-: A prefix meaning one thousand (1 km = 1000 m).
Deci-: A prefix meaning one tenth (1 dm = 0.1 m).
Celsius: The temperature scale on which water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C.
Surface: The flat outer area of an object that can be measured.
Fahrenheit: A temperature scale on which water freezes at 32 °F and boils at 212 °F.
Perimeter: The total distance around the outside of a closed figure.
Area: The amount of surface a flat shape covers, in square units.
Kelvin: A scientific temperature scale that begins at absolute zero.
Rankine: A scientific temperature scale related to Fahrenheit as Kelvin is related to Celsius.
Composite (compound) figure: A shape made by joining or removing simpler shapes such as rectangles and right-angled triangles.
Estimate: A sensible, reasoned guess of a measurement before measuring exactly.
Degree of accuracy: How precisely a measurement is given, such as "to the nearest millimetre".
